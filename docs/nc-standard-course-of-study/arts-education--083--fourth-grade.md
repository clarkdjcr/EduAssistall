# Arts Education - Fourth Grade

Parent Guide for Fourth Grade Dance

The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K-5 standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed dance educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students explore how customs, traditions, and history of indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina have influenced and been influenced by dance styles and dancers. They also learn to compare dance concepts with other content areas and demonstrate ways to address physical and mental challenges common to dance.
CN.2 Explore - Students explore dance careers and organizations in NC and examine the impact emerging technologies, innovations, and digital media have had on the field of dance.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students explore spontaneous movement invention using multiple dance elements and how to use chorographic structures and abstract movement of everyday actions to create dance phrases. Students also learn how to represent how dancers are positioned in relation to one another in dance by drawing pictures or using symbols.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to adapt dance sequences by changing how the dancers are positioned to one another and learn how to responsibly access dance and music online.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Perform - Students learn to use feedback from instructors and peers to set personal goals to improve performance and to demonstrate movement skills with stamina and coordination. Students also strengthen performing with focus when performing short dance phrases.
PR.2 Develop - Students learn how to use instructional support to compare themes, topics, or music choices for a small group performance and examine similarities and differences in safe and respectful behavioral expectations when performing and observing performances in various cultures and settings. Students also compare how a variety of production elements strengthen the artistic intent and overall experience of the audience.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students describe movement phrases that demonstrate how multiple elements of dance (body, space, time, effort quality) are used in dance, and also compare their interpretations of dance with those of peers.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students examine differences between their personal preferences for performing and viewing dances with those of peers and use criteria to provide feedback in self and peer-evaluation of work.

How to Support Your Dance Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your dance student:
* Clear a little space so your child can move freely and safely.
* Look up videos or attend local events with dances influenced by Native American, African American, or Appalachian traditions and talk about how the movements reflect culture.
* Encourage stretching breaks, deep breathing before a performance, and positive self-talk when your child feels nervous.
* Research a local dance studio, company, or university program.
* Play music and ask your child to make up a dance that uses more than one element (body, space, time, or effort).
* Ask your child to turn a daily action into part of a dance.
* Try the same dance move facing different directions or standing closer/farther from your child, then have them sketch how you were positioned to one another, such as side by side or facing each other.
* Remind your child to use safe, responsible sites when exploring dance videos or music online.
* Have your child set personal movement goals and encourage practice sessions to strengthen stamina and coordination.
* Invite them to perform a short sequence while concentrating on energy and expression.
* Brainstorm story ideas for dances.
* Watch a short performance and compare what you and your child think the dance is about.
* Talk about favorite dance styles and why others may prefer something different.
