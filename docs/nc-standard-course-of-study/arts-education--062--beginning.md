# Arts Education - Beginning

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Visual Arts Standards Unpacking Beginning
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Visual Arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Visual Arts build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Skills progress at different levels due to a number of factors, including continuity and quality of instruction; informal exposure to Visual Arts through community interaction; opportunities to access Visual Arts programs in K-12 education; student developmental level and ability; motivation and resources available for individual practice; etc. Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a students’ choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed Visual Arts educator. Credit earned from middle and high school courses meeting these minimums satisfies the Arts Education Graduation requirement and applies toward the Arts Education Diploma Endorsement. Courses that do not meet these minimums are not designed to build proficiency and are often used to spark interest in more intensive proficiency-based study later in a student’s education.
To account for variations in skill progression, as well as to embed multiple entry points across the K-12 spectrum, the objectives in the high school NCSCOS for Visual Arts are organized by proficiency level, rather than grade level. Minimum proficiency outcomes are provided at each level to show what a student should know and be able to do by the end of a course or program when they create, present, respond to, and make connections with Visual Arts. Beginning High School standards are designed for students with no or limited K-8 progression in Visual Arts education.
The North Carolina Visual Arts Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Visual Arts programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

B.CN.1.1 Describe how the emergence of visual arts as an art form has influenced society.
Glossary
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Society - An enduring and cooperating social group whose members have developed organized patterns of relationships through interaction with one another; a community, nation, or broad grouping of people having common traditions, institutions, and collective activities and interests.14
* Visual Arts - As defined by the National Art Education Association, include the traditional fine arts, such as, drawing , painting, printmaking, photography, and sculpture; media arts, such as, animation, video and emerging digital technologies; design, such as, communication, product, and interactive; environmental, such as, architecture, landscape architecture, interior and urban planning; conceptual, performance, participatory, street, and folk arts and works of art in clay, glass, metal, wood, fiber, paper and other materials. (Revised March 2017).17
Vertical Alignment
This objective begins the EQ-throughline at the foundational level for high school aged students. The beginning level is designed for students without a K-8 visual arts continuum. Students with a K-8 continuum in visual arts should progress to the intermediate level. At the current level, students describe how the emergence of visual arts as an art form has influenced society and will explain the impact of historical, societal, and cultural perspectives on the visual arts in the next level.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a visual artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of techniques, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Understanding and defining connections in how art communicates cultural ideas
* How artists use visual arts to communicate ideas about society
* How visual arts is a record of human history and documents societal norms, beliefs, and practices of a particular time and place
* How visual arts contributes to societies cultural identity preserving traditions and heritage
* How the emergence of visual arts as an art form was influential in shaping the ideas, beliefs, and daily life practices of individuals and groups in society
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of how the emergence of visual arts as an artform influenced society
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Art plays an integral role in shaping history as it reflects society, culture, and community perspectives on key issues and human experiences
* This objective can be paired with other CR objectives as students use ideas learned of in this CN objective in the creation of personal and derivative art and with RE objectives in the analysis and evaluation of art
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Provide examples of how art has been used to influence societal perspectives, beliefs, and ideas throughout history
* Have students explore how art is used to shape perspectives about cultural or societal concerns throughout history
* Guide student investigation along timeline of key art movements across various cultures and geographic locations
* Provide opportunity for students to describe how society has been influenced by the development and recognition of visual art as an art form
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to describe how the emergence of visual arts as an art form has influenced society
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Examples of how art was used to symbolize royalty and power and helped shape scientific and philosophical discovery during early periods including the Renaissance and Age of Enlightenment
* Create a multi-modal timeline that uses image and sound in which students can describe the influence of visual art on society
* Describing findings from a visual study of social media, print campaigns, commercials, street art, etc. that were used to influence societal norms, beliefs, and practices
* Describe how artists can use memorials and/or public works to express social injustices, political issues, and societal concerns

B.CR.2.2 Compare artistic adaptation, appropriation, fair use, copyright, public domain, open source, and creative commons in the visual arts.
Glossary
* Adapt(ation) - To change, or to change something, to suit different conditions or uses.4
* Appropriation - Intentional borrowing, copying, and alteration of preexisting images and objects.17
* Compare - To locate and/or list similarities and differences.14
* Copyright - Form of protection grounded in the U.S. Constitution and granted by law for original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium of expression, covering both published and unpublished works.17
* Creative Commons - Copyright license templates that provide a simple, standardized way to give the public permission to share and use creative work on conditions of the maker’s choice.6

* Scaffold activities for students to write a response to art
* Students can write or audio/video record themselves describing their personal reaction or engage in pair/share or small group reflection to verbally share their thoughts

B.RE.2.2 Critique artwork based on given-, self-, and peer-generated criteria.
Glossary
* Criteria - The rules or guidelines used for categorizing or judging; in arts assessment, the rules or guidelines used to judge the quality of a student’s performance.28
* Critique - Individual or collective reflective process by which artists or designers experience, analyze, and evaluate a work[s] of art or design.17
* Generate - to create by means of a defined process : produce.14
* Given - Qualities or traits for assessing achievement level that are provided to students.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective begins the EQ-throughline at the foundational level for high school aged students. The beginning level is designed for students without a K-8 visual arts continuum. Students with a K-8 continuum in visual arts should progress to the intermediate level. At the current level, students critique artwork based on given-, self-, and peer-generated criteria. In the next level, students will critique personal artwork or the works of others with self-generated criteria.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to generate criteria to analyze and evaluate art
* How to use given, peer-, and self-generated criteria to analyze and identify strengths and areas for improvement in the quality of specific elements of the methods, media, techniques, and tools used in artmaking
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Analyzing and evaluating a work for how well it meets given-, self-, and peer-generated criteria.
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Note the distinction in the definition of “critique” - students should be objectively ANALYZING and EVALUATING the quality of any given aspect of the work
* Criteria should be appropriate to the work and determined based on the cultural context, purpose, and goal of the work being evaluated
* This can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students revise work based on given criteria. This objective can be paired with other CR, PR objectives when using student artwork for critique
* This objective can also be paired with CN objectives as students learn about art in personal, societal, cultural, historical, and daily life contexts along with other RE objectives in analyzing art and understanding personal preferences
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students should be able to show mastery of their ability to critique artwork based on given, peer-generated, AND self-generated criteria. This can be done collectively using all three sets of criteria in one critique or assessed separately using each type of criteria independently on different occasions over the course of study
* Specificity is the hallmark of an effective critique
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on the critique of artwork using criteria
* Provide examples of artwork for student critique (these can be professional works or works created by students in mastery of CR and PR objectives)
* Design criteria to be used in evaluation of artwork (given)
* Guide students in developing criteria to be used in peer and self evaluation of work
* Guide students in using visual literacy skills to evaluate art based on peer-generated, self-generated, and given criteria
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to critique artwork based on given-, self-, and peer-generated criteria
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Objective response to art through critiques
* The role of critiques in VA
* Develop evaluation criteria as a class (given)
* Use given criteria as the basis to generate personal and peer criteria for critique
* Decide on a subset of vocabulary that is the focus of the evaluation - Provide students with a list of possible descriptive terms or questions that the student can use to critique artwork
* Critique work as whole class, then as small group, then individual - Students can write a critique or record a video blog
