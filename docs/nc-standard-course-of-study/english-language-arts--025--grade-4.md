# English Language Arts - Grade 4

Introduction
Unpacking the ELA Standard Course of Study
Grade 4

On January 8, 2026, the State Board of Education unanimously approved the 2026 North Carolina English Language Arts (ELA) Standard Course of Study (SCOS) for implementation in the 2027-2028 school year. To support successful implementation of these standards, the NCDPI ELA Team has created Unpacking Documents to deepen the understanding of the NC ELA SCOS and its Fundamental Principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center, 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling, and 3) Variety of Texts.

The Unpacking Documents include the ELA Standards and Objectives as well as a glossary, clarifications, standards-based question stems and sentence frames, classroom considerations, and supplemental resources.  Please note that these unpacking documents are not meant to be exhaustive.

Because North Carolina is a local-control state, all curricular decisions (e.g., texts, tasks, activities, etc.) are approved at the local level. Prior to implementing any of the materials in this resource, teachers should follow appropriate policies and procedures to have their curriculum vetted and approved by their school and district leaders.

Acknowledgements: The NCDPI ELA Team consulted with educators in the field as well as the Offices of Early Learning and Exceptional Children during the creation of this resource.

CT.1
Overview and Purpose

The Complex Text Strand (CT) ensures students are developing into proficient, independent readers and listeners, outlining essential skills that students should be practicing every day, in every lesson, and with every text. This strand deepens students’ understanding of how to become skilled, confident readers who can sustain attention and build stamina with increasingly challenging texts. The CT standards and objectives are designed to speak to the complex nature of reading comprehension and provide all educators with a deeper understanding of skills students should engage in routinely to access and comprehend grade-level complex texts.

In order to place text at the center of ELA teaching and learning, CT standards and objectives should be integrated across the other two strands of the NC ELA SCOS–Comprehension Development (CD) and Communication and Writing (CW)–when designing standards-aligned instruction. This integration ensures that students practice skills within the context of grade-appropriate complex texts. As students apply these skills across a variety of such texts, they demonstrate the ability to independently construct meaning across language domains.

Through the CT Strand, students build knowledge and strengthen comprehension by reading and listening to a range of worthy, content-rich texts. This includes students
* applying a range of strategies to monitor, support, and deepen comprehension
* drawing on a wide range of reading and listening experiences to build knowledge and vocabulary
* transferring techniques from the texts to their own evolving forms of communication and writing
* independently and proficiently reading/listening to grade-level complex texts within the 4-5 text complexity band

These CT standards and objectives reflect critical, research-based components of reading comprehension and reinforce rigorous grade-level expectations across all strands of the NC ELA SCOS. The Complex Texts strand serves as a foundation for standards-based, text-centered instruction in every ELA classroom.
Complex Text Strand and the Fundamental Principles

The Complex Text Strand standards and objectives ensure the fundamental principles of 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy; and 3) a Variety of Texts are living out in the ELA classroom. Designed to be recursive in nature, CT standards and objectives make explicit and implicit references to these fundamental principles to ensure students are engaging with them routinely throughout instruction.
* Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center | Grade-level complex text is the foundation of the CT.1 standard and the primary means through which students engage with the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) to build essential academic competencies. Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation.
* Integrated Model of Literacy | CT.1 objectives support students’ ability to apply and integrate skills across language domains in various contexts and across a range of texts. For example, CT.1.1 requires students to return back to the texts they are reading to ground discussion and writing in textual evidence. CT.1.2 emphasizes repeated readings, writing in response to texts, and collaborative discussion as key drivers of comprehension. CT.1.4 promotes the reading-writing connection, ensuring students engage with language receptively through reading, and transfer that knowledge generatively to their own communication.
* Variety of Texts | Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. CT.1.3 encourages students to read widely to build knowledge and vocabulary across a range of topics, themes, and concepts.
A note on text selection and local control

North Carolina is a local-control state. Because of this, the North Carolina Department of Public Instruction (NCDPI) does not provide a recommended text list, as curricular decisions are made at the PSU (public school unit) level. NCDPI cannot endorse specific curricula or instructional materials for local use. The standards do not restrict or preclude the teaching of specific modes, types, genres, mediums, and formats of text.

PSUs should carefully analyze potential  texts for each grade by examining the qualitative, quantitative, and reader and task measures to ensure students engage with  increasingly complex texts. Through text complexity analysis, educators can determine when a particular mode, type, genre, medium, format of text best supports the instructional purpose. Multiple approaches are appropriate and should be used in alignment with the standards to ensure students experience a range of texts and text types that prepare them for postsecondary opportunities.

Classroom Considerations
* Carefully select high-quality texts by ensuring the text is appropriately complex for the grade-level. Determine overall text complexity using a variety of measures including qualitative, quantitative, and reader & task considerations. Educators should examine and weigh all three measures equally; one is not more important than another, and all three combined provide the most accurate level of complexity.
* Plan daily extensive reading instruction using increasingly complex texts within the 4-5 text complexity band, strategically integrating intentional entry points that support student access while preserving the full complexity of the text. Ensure students are exposed to a variety of text modes, types, genres, structures, and formats.
* Use think-alouds as a primary strategy to model how proficient readers make meaning from grade-level complex texts, explicitly demonstrating how to navigate challenging vocabulary, structures, and ideas. Follow this modeling with guided practice that allows students to apply these processes with support, gradually releasing responsibility as they demonstrate increasing independence.
* Equip students with tools that build their capacity to independently navigate grade-level, complex texts by teaching them to recognize when meaning breaks down, ask and answer questions as they read/listen to monitor their comprehension, and reflect on what they have read/heard. Provide regular opportunities for students to practice these behaviors so they become self-directed, strategic readers/listeners. Support students’ metacognition by engaging them before, during, and after reading to reflect on what they are learning and how they are making meaning from the text.
* Adolescents must learn from, comprehend, and analyze texts which, compared to texts in earlier grades, are significantly longer and more complex at the word, sentence, and structural levels. As this complexity increases grade-to-grade, these texts present greater conceptual challenges and demand a much higher level of information synthesis. As students move into the upper elementary grades, research-based adolescent literacy strategies should be used to teach the general content literacy skills students need to access grade-level complex texts and engage with the discipline-specific reading, writing, and discussing skills in English Language Arts. For more guidance, research, and support on adolescent literacy, see Keys to Literacy’s Adolescent Literacy: A Guide.

4.CT.1.1 Ask and answer questions about key details in a text, referring explicitly to the text when explaining what the text states and when explaining logical inferences drawn from the text.

In grade 4, students extend their grade 3 learning by increasing their use of text evidence. Students continue to ask and answer questions about keydetails–including who, what, when, where, why, and how questions–but now explain what the text says explicitly and what can be reasonably inferred based on evidence in the text. Students emphasize linking evidence to their thinking, making clear how the text supports both literal and nonliteral understanding.

Students...
* ask questions about key details
* answer questions about key details
* refer explicitly to the text when explaining what the text states
* refer explicitly to the text when explaining logical inferences drawn from the text

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	* Which detail in the text best helps you understand ___?
* According to the text, what does this [word, sentence, or section] tell us?
* What specific detail from the text supports your reasoning/inference?
* How does the text explain this reasoning/inference?
	* The detail that best helps me understand this part is ___.
* The text says ___ and I know ___ so ___.
* The text says ___, which suggests ___, so ___.
* A specific detail from the text that supports my reasoning/inference is ___.
* The text explains this reasoning/inference by ___.

Classroom Considerations
* Teach and support students with using text annotations for evidence tracking. Show students how to: mark key ideas, highlight places in the text, use margin notes, etc. to track important details as they read for specific purposes.
* Plan instruction for close reading that allows students to respond to text-dependent questions that ask them both what the text states directly (what a text states) and what it says indirectly (inferences drawn from a text).
* Through modeling and explicit instruction, show students that an inference comes from combining text clues with what they already know, not guessing. Think aloud to show students how to notice clues in the text and turn them into inferences.
* Once students have practiced explaining key details in a text, model both asking and answering questions about them during independent reading and interactive read-alouds. Use think-alouds to show how readers/ listeners rely on and refer to pictures, words, and specific parts of the text to generate questions, support answers, and make inferences, thereby building early habits of text-based reasoning rather than reliance on background knowledge alone. Students should be provided multiple and varied opportunities to practice referring to the text as the basis for answers when writing or speaking. Consider building this skill into discussion and writing norms, requiring students to provide text-based justification for their answers.

4.CT.1.2 Use self-monitoring, repeated reading, summarizing, writing in response to texts, and discussions with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension.

This is a continued emphasis on these comprehension supports from grade 3, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 4. Students utilize various supports when reading a text to strengthen comprehension. These include:
* using self-monitoring strategies to gauge and repair where their comprehension may break down
* re-reading texts multiple times for various purposes (including understanding) and levels of meaning
* summarizing the main points of what has been read, and
* engaging in writing and discussion about texts to communicate understanding and collectively reinforce learning.
Each of these approaches ensure students are accessing and comprehending grade-level complex texts with increased independence.

Students…
* use self-monitoring to strengthen comprehension
* use repeated reading to strengthen comprehension
* use summarizing to strengthen comprehension
* write in response to texts to strengthen comprehension
* use discussions with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension

Classroom Considerations
* Foster students’ active engagement with self monitoring their comprehension, explicitly teaching and supporting students to flexibly use a repertoire of comprehension strategies.
* Follow the principle of gradual release to ensure students use strategies independently.
* Provide explicit instruction and opportunities for extended discussion on text meaning and interpretation, including opportunities for whole-class interactive read-alouds and performing close reading of complex text.
* Support students with writing in response to reading for a variety of purposes, including to summarize, analyze, interpret, and apply information from texts they have read.

4.CT.1.3 Draw on a wide reading of texts to build knowledge and vocabulary.

This is a continued emphasis on wide reading to build knowledge and vocabulary from grade 3, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 4. In grade 4, students read/listen to a variety of texts on a given subject or topic–both teacher determined or student selected–in order to expand their knowledge, understanding, and vocabulary. Students draw on this information to expand their comprehension of content being studied in class and to better access grade-level complex texts.

Students…
* draw on a wide range of texts to build knowledge
* draw on a wide range of texts to build vocabulary

Classroom Considerations
* Provide text sets connected by topic/theme and ensure they include a variety of texts types, genres, modes, formats, mediums, and/or lengths. Texts in the set should reflect a range of complexity levels so students can build the knowledge and vocabulary needed to access the grade-level text being studied.
* To increase the impact of wide reading, regularly activate, connect, build, and extend students’ prior knowledge to help them bridge new learning with what they already understand and have studied. Doing so improves comprehension, supports vocabulary development, and increases engagement by making content more accessible. Writing and discussion strategies can be incorporated into instruction to help uncover what students know before introducing new material or texts (e.g., anticipation guides, cloze readings, concept sorts, ABC brainstorming, quick writes, etc).
* Create and maintain an inviting, well-resourced, and purposefully organized classroom library that offers a wide range of texts and media. Ensure the collection reflects students’ diverse interests, backgrounds, languages, and experiences; exposes students to academic language across content areas; and includes multiple genres that support knowledge-building around specific topics. Provide opportunities for students to self-select materials for independent reading at school, at home, and through digital access when available. Establish and teach clear routines for selecting, using, and caring for materials so the classroom library can be used effectively and responsibly.
* Provide regular, protected time for wide reading to allow students to explore multiple texts on the same topic. When applicable, use this time to encourage student choice within a topic to boost motivation while simultaneously supporting knowledge and vocabulary growth. Model habits of wide reading for students by doing brief book talks, highlighting how reading multiple texts deepens understanding of a topic, and allowing time for students to talk, write, or reflect about what they are learning from their reading. Explicitly draw students’ attention to how their existing banks of knowledge are expanded through additional reading about a given topic and linking newly learned information or realizations to previously established knowledge.
* Collaborate with a librarian or media specialist to help identify digital resources, databases, and multimedia that broaden the range of texts students can draw on. Involve these specialists in helping students learn how to locate, evaluate, and select texts that support their knowledge building. Consider how research or independent reading tasks can be used to get students to read more widely on a topic being studied.
* Support student vocabulary development as they read by following a comprehensive vocabulary framework that promotes word learning in a multifaceted way: teach high-impact Tier II and Tier III words related to a specific topic/text, equip students with strategies for figuring out unfamiliar words when they encounter them, foster curiosity about language by studying the way words work and relate, and provide rich and varied written and spoken language experiences so students encounter new vocabulary in meaningful contexts. Together, these components help students build stronger, more flexible word knowledge as they read.

4.CT.1.4 Recognize the choices and features used by authors and transfer those techniques to communication and writing.

This is a continued emphasis on recognizing and transferring techniques from grade 3, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 4. In grade 4, students notice and learn from how authors write and craft texts, using those same techniques to improve their own communication and writing. Students determine how an author’s choices impact a text and its effects on readers/listeners, looking at elements and features such as: word choice, structure, literary elements, figurative language, organizational patterns, etc.  Students apply similar techniques to their own writing or speaking, developing the style, complexity, and individual voice of their own communication based on their specific purpose and task.

Students…
* recognize the choices and features used by authors
* transfer those techniques to communication and writing

Classroom Considerations
* Support students with identifying and reflecting on the impact of choices authors make in texts to enhance their craft and style. These text choices and features should include elements such as organization, structure, literary elements, word choice, figurative language, etc.
* Plan instruction that integrates skills across language domains–reading, writing, speaking, and listening–so students can notice techniques in texts they are reading/listening/viewing and practice applying them to their writing and speaking in various contexts.
* Use mentor texts to help students analyze and emulate in their own communication. Select high-quality texts that clearly demonstrate different techniques across genres and styles so students develop a broader understanding of what communication can look like catered to a variety of purposes and audiences.
* Ensure instruction includes both interpretive (analysis) and expressive (creation) opportunities for students, so they can both understand and examine texts (to support reading/listening comprehension), as well as transfer techniques when producing original texts as authors/speakers with specific intentions of their own. Interpretive tasks focus on how students understand, analyze, and interpret information they receive through reading, listening, or viewing. Expressive tasks focus on how students communicate their own thoughts, ideas, and feelings through speaking, writing, or representing. Explore The Writing Guidebook and The Discourse Playbook for additional guidance and ideas for pairing reading with writing, speaking, and listening.

Glossary
	Decode - The process of translating a word from print to speech by using knowledge of sound–symbol (letter) correspondences.

Encode - The process of translating speech into print (writing) using knowledge of sound–symbol (letter) correspondences.

Morphology - The study of word structure and how words are formed by looking at their meaningful units (morphemes)—prefixes, roots, and suffixes (e.g., mis-spell-ing).

Phonics - Reading instruction that focuses on the alphabetic principle—the systematic, predictable relationship between spoken sounds (phonemes) and written letters (graphemes)—to allow readers to identify or “decode” words.

Syllabication - The act of breaking words into syllables.

4.CD.F.3.1 Use combined knowledge of letter-sound correspondences, syllabication patterns, and morphology to decode and encode unfamiliar multisyllabic words.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Maintain explicit connections between decoding and encoding by reminding students that the same knowledge they use to break apart and read multisyllabic words should also be used to spell them. Consistently model how analyzing a combination of letter-sound correspondences, syllables and affixes supports both reading and writing. Select strategies for instruction that support the combined use and application of these skills.
* Explicitly teach and regularly revisit syllabication patterns in authentic contexts, using real sentences and grade-level texts to help students practice chunking multisyllabic words and determining where syllables naturally break–bridging what they know in isolation with how they apply it in continuous text.
* Leverage morphology as a meaning-making tool–not just a decoding tool–by encouraging students to use the meanings of prefixes and suffixes to determine whether a decoded word makes sense and to refine their spelling choices.
	Example 1:
Word Decoding Routine: This strategy can be used for reading larger words, strengthening students’ understanding of how to combine their growing use of decoding strategies. Students identify any known prefixes or suffixes, then use syllabication to decode the rest of the word.
* Box and say any known affixes
* Locate and underline each vowel graphemes
* Use knowledge of syllables to mark/divide and decode the vowel sounds
* Read the whole word and see if it makes sense and sounds right
* Verify in context (if applicable)

Example 2:
Word Encoding Routine: This routine can be taught, supported, and used by students to encode words using their combined knowledge of letter-sound correspondences, syllabication patterns, and morphology.
* Say the word out loud
* Chunk the word into syllables
* Listen for meaningful word parts (i.e., prefixes, root, suffixes)
* Stretch and spell each part
* Read the whole word, checking for accuracy.
* Write a sentence using the word.

4.CD.F.3.2 Decode and encode common Latin derived words.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Emphasize meaning over memorization by connecting Latin derived words to their meanings, showing students how understanding these word parts supports both accurate decoding and deeper comprehension.
* Model word-solving talk by thinking aloud as you analyze meaningful word parts (e.g., “I see the root struct, which means to build or put together. That helps me decode and understand construction because I already know that the suffix ‘tion’ sounds like “shun” and means the act of doing something.  So, construction means the act of building something.”). Show students how breaking words apart by meaning supports both accurate reading and deeper understanding.
* Provide repeated exposure to key roots, prefixes, and suffixes across varied contexts. Use word webs, matrices, sentence-level practice, and connected texts so students encounter and apply the same meaningful word parts in multiple ways, strengthening both decoding and encoding.
* Teach roots in families by grouping related words (e.g., transport, import, export, portable, port, etc.), so students can see consistent spelling patterns and meaning connections across multiple contexts.
	Example 1:
Word Matrix Boxes Routine: In this strategy, students create real words by selecting one prefix, the derivational/Latin root, and one suffix. Then, students read the word, check its meaning, and use it in a sentence. This strategy strengthens students’ ability to decode, encode, and understand multisyllabic Latin–derived words by highlighting how meaningful parts combine to form new words.

Example 2:
Paragraph Analysis: In this strategy, students read short paragraphs or passages focusing on a specific root, highlighting words containing that root and discussing how the root's meaning contributes to the overall text. This routine supports students looking inside and outside of words to decode, determine the meaning of, and understand the impact of word parts.

Classroom Considerations
4.CD.F.4.1 Read texts for a variety of purposes.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Ensure that students build fluency through their familiarity and understanding of texts through instructional practices like close reading. When providing explicit instruction for close reading, ensure that students understand that each consecutive read has a distinct purpose with the aim to not only understand content, but transfer that knowledge to new contexts.  For example, a potential framework could be: Phase 1, determine what the text says.  Phase 2, figure out how the text works. Phase 3, determine what the text means.  Phase 4, applying learned skills/knowledge.
* Ensure that students engage in practices that encourage the oral reading of texts for a variety of purposes. For example, referring to the text in order to engage in class discussions, to solve a problem or answer a question, to present or recreate a text, to locate information, etc.
	Example 1:
Sample Reading for a Variety of Purposes Routine: This routine is designed to improve fluency and build comprehension by layering different purposes for successive readings of the same text. Students first identify and clarify unfamiliar words, then listen to a fluent model to develop understanding of the text’s overall meaning, and finally read aloud to deepen comprehension through discussion and text-dependent questions. Across all three reads, teacher modeling, partner support, and intentional pauses help students monitor understanding, resolve confusion, and practice oral reading fluency.

4.CD.F.4.2 Confirm or self-correct word recognition and understanding, as necessary.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Provide students with strategies that encourage self-reflection and questioning.  For example, strategies like text annotations encourage metacognition by having students indicate when there is a breakdown of understanding. Students, having recognized this, can then take the necessary next steps in order to confirm or self-correct.
* Model for students what to do or think about when a breakdown in understanding occurs. Do this through think alouds, providing students with strategies for different circumstances. For example, “I’m confused here.  I thought that the author said ___.  I’m going to reread this paragraph to make sure that I understand.” Consider how this could leverage word learning strategies, like using word parts, reference materials, context clues, etc.
	Example 1:
Self-Questioning:
* Does it sound right?
* Does it look right?
* Does it make sense?

Example 2:
Text Annotations:This strategy involves students using predetermined symbols to mark, elaborate on, and respond to the information provided by a text. Have students use this strategy to actively engage in and process the content of a text while they are reading for deeper comprehension. Students think metacognitively about their own understanding, making note of and acting on any lapses.

4.CD.F.4.3 Read texts with accuracy, appropriate rate, and prosody on successive readings.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Model fluent reading for students by reading aloud regularly–or using recordings of–excerpts from grade-level complex texts while students follow along in their own copies. After modeling, draw students’ attention to and discuss what cues in the text prompted pauses, increases/decreases in pitch or pace, emphasis, etc. Explain how expressive reading helps support your comprehension of the text/excerpt. Finally, remind students that the purpose of this is to provide a model that they will emulate when re-reading the same text/excerpt and will apply to independent reading of future texts.

	Example 1:
Echo reading: This strategy involves the teacher model reading a sentence or short section of a text while students follow along, having students pay close attention to pronunciations, rate, and prosody. The teacher then instructs students to read the same section aloud, “echoing” the pronunciations, rate, and prosody that was modeled for them. This strategy is useful for having students
immediately apply and internalize their learning based on a model fluent reader.

Example 2:
Partner reading: Using this routine, the teacher partners two students together
based on which students require support with a specific skill and
which student could help provide it. The students take turns reading sections of the text. Following each reading, the students provide feedback to each other and discuss their understanding. This strategy is effective because it provides models for multiple students while the teacher can circulate around the class in order to observe, monitor, and provide specific timely feedback. Because this strategy also involves comprehension, it is flexible enough to be used with any number of reading standard foci.

Example 3:
Phases of Close Reading: This strategy involves an intensive analysis of a text in order to come to terms with what it says, how it says it, and what it means. Use this resource to review the key phases of close reading, receive guidance on the purpose of each repeated read, and explore planning suggestions for how to support multiple close-reads through intentionally sequenced text-dependent questions.

Classroom Considerations
* When planning instruction, invest instructional time in learning words that are closest to the key ideas and themes of the text(s). One planning strategy to use when selecting the vocabulary to focus on is Teach, Tell, Text: teach words are those key to understanding a text and support students reading, writing, speaking, and listening; tell words can be quickly explained to students and lack meaningful context in the text; and text words are those students can determine independently using context clues, prior knowledge, or text features (footnotes, glossary, etc.).
* Using explicit instruction, model and think aloud how readers look “inside” and “outside” words to help determine their meanings. Explicitly teach “inside” strategies for how prefixes, suffixes, and roots contribute to meaning (e.g., dis-, re-, -ful, -less), and model how breaking a word apart can help clarify meaning. Emphasize the importance of confirming understanding through “outside” strategies by rereading and checking whether the inferred meaning fits logically within the broader passage.  Closely analyze context clues by attending to definitions, examples, cause-and-effect relationships, or comparisons embedded in sentences and paragraphs.  When first introducing or revisiting vocabulary strategies, focus instruction on one strategy at a time using short, more complex text excerpts that clearly support the strategy being taught.
* Once students demonstrate understanding of individual strategies, support them in flexibly combining strategies within and across texts. Model how a reader might use context clues to infer meaning, verify that meaning by analyzing word parts, and then refine understanding by consulting a glossary, dictionary, or digital reference. Engage students in creating vocabulary tools such as semantic maps, or word webs that capture definitions, characteristics, examples, nonexamples, and connections to other words. Revisit key vocabulary across multiple texts within a unit, guiding students to notice how word meanings can become more precise or nuanced as they encounter the word in different contexts. Add newly taught words to an ongoing class anchor chart so that students can reference it throughout future instruction and/or when applying newly learned vocabulary to speaking and writing.  Model and have students use strategies that emphasize these approaches, like the 4 E’s graphic organizer (breaking the word into morphemes as appropriate).
* After modeling, provide structured and varied opportunities for students to independently and collaboratively determine or clarify word meanings. Use routines such as vocabulary notebooks that track the word, inferred meaning, strategies used, and evidence from the text; Context Clue Sort and Justify where students are provided with sentences from texts that include unfamiliar words. Students sort the sentences by the type of context clue used (definition, example, comparison, or explanation) and justify their reasoning orally or in writing; or Word Strategy Choice Boards, where students are presented with an unfamiliar word and several strategy options (e.g., use context clues, analyze word parts, check a glossary). Students select the strategy they think will be most effective, apply it, and then explain why that strategy was appropriate for that word. This builds metacognitive awareness and strategic flexibility. Encourage students to justify their thinking using precise academic language (e.g., “I inferred the meaning by noticing…” or “The root ____ helped me understand…”). Reinforce that skilled readers actively monitor comprehension and strategically and flexibly apply multiple strategies to deepen understanding across a wide range of texts.

* Focus grammar instruction on a functional and rhetorical level, instead of solely focusing on memorizing the grammatical terms/concepts themselves. Consider a more student-centered approach to grammar instruction by teaching it in context, considering how it can be taught synchronously with the texts being read and produced by students. To teach grammar and usage in context, consider the following, in order:
   * Student-Level Context: Examine student writing/speaking to determine the grammar/usage concept that will benefit students.
   * Text-Level Context: Identify a mentor text (sentence, paragraph, or larger excerpt) that shows the grammar/usage concept in use. Study and discuss how the grammar/usage concept is used in the text to achieve a particular goal or effect.

   * Have students apply knowledge and use a specific concept in an actual piece of writing or communication so students can demonstrate their understanding and receive feedback.

Concepts and Example Sentences

Sentence Production & Expansion Skills

Produce complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in paragraphs

In addition to producing simple and compound sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a complex sentence includes one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses, which do not express a complete thought on their own and are often introduced by subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, when, if) or relative pronouns. When the dependent clause comes before the independent clause, it is attached with a comma. When it comes after, a comma is not needed. To produce complex sentences, students should appropriately attach a dependent clause to an independent clause.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Simple Sentence
	The wind blows gently through the towering trees.
	Compound Sentence
	The wind blows through the swaying trees, and it shakes the leaves.
	Complex Sentence
	When the strong, chilly wind blows through the trees, it shakes the leaves.

Expand complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in paragraphs

Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional clauses to simple, compound, and complex sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-4 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Expanded Simple Sentence
	Ah, how the wind whispers through the towering, swaying trees!
	Expanded Compound Sentence
	The wind whispers through the swaying trees, and it shakes the leaves like tiny dancers on a stage.
	Expanded Complex Sentence
	When the strong, chilly wind blows through the trees, it is a conductor, making the leaves create a silent performance.

Rearrange complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in paragraphs

Students need to know how to move modifiers, phrases, and clauses within simple, compound, and complex sentences to improve flow, coherence, or emphasis and without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that rearranging parts of sentences can improve flow, coherence, and emphasis without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by requiring adjustment to clause structure (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-4 grammar and convention knowledge to rearrange sentences appropriate to the grade-level.
Concept
	Example Sentence
	Rearranged Simple Sentence
	Ah, how the towering, swaying trees are whispered through by the wind!
	Rearranged Compound Sentence
	The wind whispers, and it shakes the leaves on the swaying trees like tiny dancers on a stage.

Rearranged Complex Sentence

Sentence Accuracy & Error Correction Skills

Recognize inappropriate sentence fragments

Students need to know that a fragment is an incomplete thought lacking a subject, predicate, or both. Students need to know that the simple predicate is the main verb or verb phrase that shows the action or state of being and that the complete predicate includes the simple predicate and all the words that modify or complete it, such as objects, complements, and modifiers. Students need to know how to add missing subjects, predicates, or clauses to incomplete sentences to form grammatically complete statements.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Fragment Missing a Subject
	Running through the hallway after the bell rang.
	Corrected with Added Subject and Progressive Verb Phrase
	The students were running through the hallway after the bell rang.
	Fragment Missing a Complete Predicate
	The tall trees in the park.
	Corrected with Added Verb/Complete Predicate
	The tall trees in the park blew over in the wind.
	Dependent Clause/Incomplete Thought
	Because the text said the storm was fierce.
	Corrected with Added Independent Clause
	Because the text said the storm was fierce, everyone had to evacuate.
	Corrected with Added Relative Pronoun and Independent Clause
	Because the text said the storm was fierce, the sailors, who had never faced such strong winds before, stayed below deck.

Recognize run-on sentences

Students need to know that a run-on occurs when two or more independent clauses are joined improperly. Students need to know that fused sentences are a type of run-on where two or more independent clauses are joined with no punctuation. Students also need to know that comma splices are a type of run-on where two or more independent clauses are joined only with a comma. Students need to know how to separate  independent clauses correctly using end punctuation or join them using conjunctions and commas appropriately to form grammatically correct sentences.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Fused Sentence Run-on
	The text said the hero was brave she never gave up.
	Corrected by Separating with a Period
	The text said the hero was brave. She never gave up.
	Corrected with Coordinating Conjunction and Comma
	The text said the hero was brave, and she never gave up.
	Comma Splice Run-on
	The soccer game was exciting, our team scored the last goal.
	Corrected by Separating with a Period
	The soccer game was exciting. Our team scored the last goal.
	Corrected with Coordinating Conjunction and Comma
	The soccer game was exciting, and our team scored the last goal.

Recognize and correct frequently misused words

Students need to know that some words are commonly confused or misapplied because they sound alike or have related meanings (e.g., their/there/they’re, affect/effect). Students need to know the distinct meanings and grammatical functions of these words in order to identify misuse in context. Students also need to know how to apply this knowledge when revising their own writing to ensure clarity and correctness.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Misused Their/There/They’re
	The text says the main character went their to help his friend.
	Corrected with There, Referring to Place
	The text says the main character went there to help his friend.
	Misused To/Too/Two
	Thomas Edison had to many ideas floating around in his head.
	Corrected with Too, Meaning Excessively
	Thomas Edison had too many ideas floating around in his head.
	Misused Its/It’s
	The text explains the water cycle, and it’s diagrams help me understand each step.
	Corrected with Its, Indicating Possession
	The text explains the water cycle, and its diagrams help me understand each step.

Classroom Considerations
* Support students with seeing sentences as part of an idea or paragraph, not stand-alone units. Have students practice arranging/rearranging sentences so that ideas flow logically and stay focused on one topic. Consider having students highlight or color-code to show how sentences connect.
* When teaching students to recognize and correct inappropriate fragments, run-ons, and frequently misused words, make sure to explain why this matters: they affect clarity and meaning. Show students examples of each error type, how that error impacts an audience’s understanding, and how fixing them improves readability. For example, when teaching students to recognize and correct inappropriate fragments, show students incomplete sentences from their speaking/writing that may be missing a subject or verb, model how to identify the missing element, and model how to add that missing element back in to create a complete sentence. When teaching students about run-ons, ensure that students can recognize fused sentences and comma splices. Model and have students practice splitting the independent clauses into separate sentences, adding conjunctions, or using punctuation correctly.  When teaching students to recognize and correct frequently misused words, focus on high-value words for grade 4 and those seen consistently in student speaking/writing. Consider using/co-creating an anchor chart that captures a running list of these words and provides authentic examples from student speaking/writing.  Also consider adding these elements to an editing checklist or rubric for students to review their own or other’s speaking/writing.
* Build students’ sentence writing fluency by increasing the volume and opportunities for students to write for authentic purposes across the school day and subject areas (e.g., responding to a text, reflecting on new learning, communicating a message, etc.).  Refer to Recommendation 1 of the Writing Guidebook for additional suggestions.
* When teaching cursive, introduce it gradually, focusing on letter formation, spacing, size, alignment, and connections before speed. Emphasize the importance of mode selection (manuscript, cursive, or other transcription skill), depending on the purpose and/or preference. Students should know and be able to produce these modes, choosing the one that best fits their need(s). For example, some students may prefer to use cursive when notetaking because they can do so quicker and more efficiently than in manuscript.
* Support students in using word processing tools efficiently, providing opportunities for students to practice both handwriting and typing within grade-level tasks, such as sustained drafting or note-taking and extended writing tasks that require students to maintain accuracy and efficiency over time so that transcription becomes increasingly automatic and does not interfere with sentence construction. Attention to transcription should focus on maintaining accuracy, efficiency, and readability, enabling students to concentrate on developing ideas and composing sentences with precision and sophistication.

Classroom Considerations
* Help students understand drafting as a recursive, flexible, and purposeful process rather than a one-time event. Drafting should typically be seen as the crucial step of translating ideas/information from planning into formal language (with the exception being freewriting-based strategies, like Fever Dream writing), experimenting with organization, structure and voice based on the specific purpose, task, and audience. * As part of the drafting process, ensure students have access to models of quality writing. Instruction should pair these pause points with explicit metacognitive strategies, such as self-questioning and teacher think-alouds during shared writing, that model how writers/speakers recognize uncertainty, misalignment, or stalled thinking as signals to return to pre-writing or planning in order to refine ideas and strengthen the overall composition.

Classroom Considerations
* Ensure students understand the distinction between revision and editing as related but different tasks in the composition process. Emphasize that revision focuses on meaning, structure, and effectiveness (e.g., reconsidering ideas, reorganizing sections, refining evidence, and clarifying relationships between an opinion, reasons, and evidence), while editing attends to sentence-level correctness, style, and conventions once ideas are largely in place, including considerations for formatting. As part of the revision process, students need to know how to move sentences or clauses within paragraphs to improve flow, coherence, or emphasis. Help students recognize that revision and editing are recursive rather than linear: speakers/writers may return to revision after editing reveals gaps in logic, or pause revision to edit a key passage for clarity or tone.
* Explicitly model, guide, and have practice using strategies to support revision and editing that demonstrates the conceptual differences between the two. To support revision, consider teaching the RADaR strategy, a mnemonic that outlines key revision-aligned actions. To support editing, consider having students use self and peer editing checklists. The language within these resources can/should be refined to reflect current learning and grade appropriateness.
* Support students in engaging with multiple levels of feedback–teacher, peer, and self–to strengthen revision and editing. Provide support through targeted teacher feedback, such as individual or small-group conferences that focus on high-leverage strategies rather than correcting every error. Consider grouping students based on shared instructional needs, patterns of feedback, or stage in the writing process. When working with a small group during a conference, provide other students with clearly defined revision or editing tasks–such as targeted checklists, task cards, or models–to ensure their work remains purposeful and focused. Alternatively, have students engage in structured peer feedback or independent revision/editing tasks aligned to the same instructional goals. Structure peer feedback with clear expectations, using sentence frames, checklists, or focused prompts to guide attention to specific aspects of the draft and prevent feedback from becoming overly general or surface-level. Teach students to engage in self-feedback by using strategies such as color-coding to analyze their own writing for patterns or gaps in patterns (e.g., formality of language specific to an audience, summarizing or paraphrasing as evidence to strengthen points, use of transition and linking words to signal relationships between ideas, etc.) and identify next steps independently. Ensure students understand that seeking feedback from teachers, peers, and themselves is a normal and essential part of how writers and speakers develop ideas and refine their work, and that sharing drafts at multiple stages supports growth rather than signaling weakness or failure.
* Support students in using digital tools strategically to strengthen both revision and editing as ongoing, recursive processes. Model and reinforce collaborative feedback practices by using and having students use comments, suggestions, and track-change tools to signal whether feedback targets ideas and organization (revision) or sentence-level clarity and conventions (editing). Encourage students to use automated tools (e.g., grammar and usage checkers) as diagnostic supports rather than final arbiters, guiding them to evaluate suggested changes for accuracy, purpose, and voice.

Classroom Considerations
* NCDPI does not endorse specific digital tools or companies. This objective allows for the use of a wide variety of tools and digital resources. Teachers are encouraged to expose students to multiple types of digital tools for various purposes, using 1:1 devices, computer labs, mobile carts, or group instruction as needed. * Support students with creating written and spoken products that intentionally incorporate multiple modes, such as visuals, imagery, audio, and video. These expectations should be clearly embedded in learning tasks and rubrics so students understand what high-quality multimodal work looks like. Ensure students understand the difference between using elements creatively and using them intentionally to achieve a specific purpose or impact. Students should learn that design choices should enhance their message and communication rather than distract from or weaken it. Doing so increases student motivation, accountability, and pride in their work by showing that their ideas have real purpose and reach. Collaborate with a media specialist to help identify appropriate platforms, address digital citizenship, and support safe and effective publishing. Providing structured opportunities for peers and outside audiences to view and respond to student work reinforces that learning is meant to be shared, not created solely for the teacher. 	Ensure subject-verb agreement

* Compound subjects (“and” usually = plural; “or/nor” = verb agrees with nearest subject)
* Indefinite pronouns (singular: everyone, each; plural: few, many; some can be singular or plural depending on meaning)
* Collective nouns (singular when acting as a unit, plural when acting as individuals)
* Inverted sentences (identify the true subject first, e.g., There are many books)
* Titles, sums, or amounts (usually singular, e.g., The United States is large)

Amount:

	Form and use progressive aspect
	Students need to know the progressive aspect is formed with a past, present, or future form of “be” (i.e., state of being) + a progressive verb “-ing” to show ongoing actions (e.g., is running, were eating, will be fishing). Progressive verbs function to indicate actions in progress.
	Environmentalists are cleaning up the trash that is polluting our oceans.
	4
	Use modal auxiliaries
	Students need to know modal verbs (e.g., can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would, ought to, need to, used to, have to) show ability, possibility, necessity, obligation, or permission. Modals are a specific type of auxiliary verb that add meaning to, and are always followed by, the base form of a main verb.
	People should clean up the trash that is polluting our oceans.
	4
	Form and use perfect aspect
	Students need to know the perfect aspect is formed with a past, present, or future form of “have” + a past participle to show completed actions related to another point in time (e.g., has walked, had eaten, will have fished). Perfect aspect functions to indicate actions that are finished relative to the present or past.
	Oceans currents have greatly affected our weather.
	5
	Form and use perfect progressive aspect
	Students need to know the perfect progressive aspect is formed with a past, present, or future tense of “have” + been + a progressive verb (-ing) to show an action that was ongoing for a period of time leading up to a specific point in time (e.g., has been running, had been eating, will have been fishing). This aspect emphasizes duration in relation to completion or a reference moment, highlighting both the ongoing nature of the action and its connection to another time.
	She has been texting her friend group about this evening's school dance since yesterday.
	6
	Recognize and correct inappropriate tense shifts
	Students maintain consistent verb tense within sentences or related sentences. Students need to know that verbs in a sentence or related sentences should maintain consistent tense unless the timeline changes.
	Last night he studied for the quiz, reviewed his notes, and completed the practice questions.
	6
	Distinguish between active and passive voice
	Students need to know that active voice emphasizes the subject performing the action, while passive voice emphasizes the subject receiving the action. They also need to know that passive constructions typically use a form of “be” + past participle.
	Active Voice:
The students designed the posters for Spirit Week.

Passive Voice:
The posters were designed by the students for Spirit Week.
	7
	Use active and passive voice appropriate to purpose, audience, task
	Students select active or passive voice to match their writing goal (e.g., active for directness, passive for emphasis on the action). Students need to know which form emphasizes the subject or action.
	Active Voice:
Our dedicated team won the championship game after months of practice.

Passive Voice:
The championship game was won by our dedicated team after months of practice.
	8
	Use indicative, imperative, and interrogative moods
	Students need to know that verb mood indicates the function or purpose of a sentence. In the indicative mood, verbs express statements or facts. In the imperative mood, verbs give commands or instructions. In the interrogative mood, verbs form questions. Students also need to recognize that verb forms, word order, and auxiliary verbs often signal the mood of a sentence.
	Indicative Mood:
We are taking the bus home from school tomorrow.

Imperative Mood:
Finish your meal before you can have dessert.

Interrogative Mood:
Did you upload your homework to the class website?
	8
	Use conditional and subjunctive moods
	Students need to know that the conditional mood expresses hypothetical situations and possible, but not guaranteed, outcomes by often using if/if-were clauses and modal auxiliaries. The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, demands, or possibilities that are not factual. Students need to recognize that the subjunctive often appears in clauses following verbs expressing desire, recommendation, or uncertainty and that the verb in the subjunctive is usually the base form (i.e., no -s, -ed, or -ing ending). The past subjunctive, especially with the verb to be (e.g., were), can sound unusual or “formal” because it does not follow normal past-tense patterns and is often replaced by was in casual speech; however, using were correctly signals a hypothetical or non-factual situation, which is essential for expressing wishes, contrary-to-fact statements, or imagined scenarios.
	Conditional Mood:
If the weather improves, we will go for a hike tomorrow.

If the data were more complete, the results would be more reliable.

Subjunctive Mood:
Members of the book club wished their next read were available for pick-up.

The committee recommended that the proposal be revised before approval.

	Use verbals (gerunds, participles, infinitives)

Participles act as adjectives: present participles are formed with -ing , and past participles often end in -ed for regular verbs or take an irregular form. Infinitives are formed with to + base verb and can function as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs. Students also need to recognize that verbals allow sentences to vary in structure, provide additional detail, and express actions, qualities, or purposes in different grammatical roles.

Present Participle (as an adjective):

Infinitive (as a noun):

Infinitive (as an adverb of purpose):

Infinitive (as an adjective):

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use coordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet) connect words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance. They also need to recognize that coordinating conjunctions join ideas without changing the grammatical function of the connected elements.
	She is small but strong!

	Use subordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, if, when, since, while, etc.) connect a dependent clause to an independent clause. 	Students need to know that a prepositional phrase consists of a preposition and its object (noun or pronoun) and functions as an adjective or adverb. They also need to recognize how prepositional phrases provide information about location, time, direction, cause, or manner.
	The price of toys went up because of the high demand in the store.
	3
	Use correlative conjunctions
	Students need to know that correlative conjunctions work in pairs (e.g., either–or, neither–nor, both–and, not only–but also) to connect balanced elements of equal grammatical importance. They also need to recognize that each part of the pair must attach to similar structures (e.g., noun to noun, clause to clause).
	Both the mountains and the rivers affect where people build cities and farms in the United States.
	5

	Students need to know that verbal phrases are built around verbals—gerunds (-ing forms functioning as nouns), participles (present -ing or past -ed/irregular forms functioning as adjectives), and infinitives (to + verb forms functioning as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs). They need to recognize that verbal phrases combine these verbals with other words (modifiers or objects) to create noun, adjective, or adverb phrases and that verbal phrases can appear at the beginning, middle, and end of sentences. Students also need to know that using verbal phrases allows sentences to vary in structure, add descriptive detail, and convey subtle nuances of meaning, such as timing, cause, or purpose and that verbal phrases can be layered with each other for increased sophistication.

Infinitive Phrase (as a noun):

	Use parallel structure to emphasize parity between ideas
	Students need to know that parallel structure means using the same grammatical form for words, phrases, or clauses that are joined or compared. Parallel Infinitive Phrases in a List:

When the evidence is examined carefully and when the context is considered honestly, the argument becomes more convincing.

Complex Sentence (When Dependent Clause is First):
Because of the growing list of grievances, the colonists decided to declare independence.

	Students need to know that commas help separate clauses in sentences with multiple independent and dependent clauses for clarity.
	When I finished my homework, I texted my friends because I wanted to relax, and we planned our gaming session.

	Students need to know that commas separate two or more adjectives that equally modify a noun.
	She bought a trendy, oversized hoodie at the mall.

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use semicolons to separate items in a series when items include internal commas

	Students need to know that sometimes a semicolon can be used with a coordinating conjunction to join two sentences together and preserve clarity when the sentences are long and contain additional internal punctuation. Students should recognize that in this construction, the semicolon comes before the coordinating conjunction, and no additional comma is needed immediately after it.
	Semicolons with Coordinating Conjunctions, Internal Punctuation:
Since he speaks French, Spanish, and Italian, he assumed he would have no trouble traveling in southern Europe, especially since many people in the region now speak English; but when he arrived in Sicily, his unfamiliarity with the Sicilian dialect made communication difficult.

The treaty established new boundaries, clarified trade agreements, and outlined military obligations; and although it was celebrated in the capitals, many local leaders opposed its terms.

Students need to know that a run-on occurs when two or more independent clauses are joined improperly. Students need to know that fused sentences are a type of run-on where two or more independent clauses are joined with no punctuation. Students also need to know that comma splices are a type of run-on where two or more independent clauses are joined only with a comma.
