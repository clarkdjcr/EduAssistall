# Arts Education - Beginning

Parent Guide for Beginning Visual Arts
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for visual arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with visual arts builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a student’s choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed visual arts educator.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students explore the influence of visual arts on society and apply concepts from other disciplines when making and sharing art.
CN.2 Explore - Students explore how the creative process connects to college and career opportunities and the impact new developments in technology and media have on visual arts.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students experiment with ideas and materials and create work that uses symbolism to communicate a personally meaningful theme, concept, or idea.
CR.2 Adapt - Students make new art by combining imagery from multiple artists and learn legal terminology related to copyright protection of an artist’s work.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Present - Students use specific criteria to make improvements to their work and create a statement that communicates the meaning, purpose, and techniques used to make personal art.

PR.2 Develop - Students select artwork for a presentation that demonstrates skill, use of a variety of materials, and theme, and explore how interpretations of an exhibit or collection vary based on the culture, traditions, and historical time periods.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students examine differences in artists, movements, styles, and themes in art and analyze how design elements are used to communicate meaning.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students describe personal thoughts and interpretations of art and use criteria to analyze and evaluate works of art.

How to Support Your Visual Arts Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your visual arts student:
* Talk about how art shows up in daily life, such as in advertising, logos, clothing, movies, and social media.
* Discuss how concepts from other subjects can be used when making and sharing art.
* Discuss how the creative design process can be applied to multiple college and career situations.
* Discuss how new tools and technology have changed how art is created and shared.
* Encourage your child to make original art that expresses a meaningful idea or concept.
* Provide simple materials and encourage experimentation.
* Talk about giving credit when they borrow ideas or images from other artists.
* Encourage your child to describe why they made certain choices in their art.
* Create a small home “gallery wall” or digital slideshow to display their work.
* Visit museums, galleries, or online exhibits and talk about how different people might view the same artwork differently.
* Expose your child to different works of art and have them sort them by style, media, themes, or other similarities.
* Look at art together and discuss how artistic choices were made to convey meaning.
* Encourage your child to explain what they like or don’t like about a work of art.
