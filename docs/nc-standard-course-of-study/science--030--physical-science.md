# Science - Physical Science

Physical Science: 2023 Support Document

Physical Science
Purpose:
The 2023 North Carolina K-12 Science Standards were officially approved by the State Board of Education on July 6, 2023. The Standard Course of Study is designed to help students continually grow their science knowledge and abilities. The standards are intended to foster conceptual understanding, develop scientifically literate students, and provide opportunities to build knowledge and practices within each grade band or course.

The K-12 Science Support Documents serve as resources for teachers, administrators, science specialists, instructional coaches, parents, and other stakeholders in understanding the standards and planning instruction. They do not serve as curricula nor as a means to limit instruction in the classroom, but rather are intended to serve as a guide for discerning and describing features of students and their work necessary to meet grade level proficiency.

Science Teaching and Learning:
A coherent and consistent approach throughout grades K-12 is key to realizing the vision for science education embodied in A Framework for K-12 Science Education.  Students should actively engage in science and engineering practices and apply crosscutting concepts in every grade level to deepen their understanding of each field’s disciplinary core ideas.

Science Safety:
It is the responsibility of teachers and school administrators to use appropriate legal standards and best professional practices under duty of care to make the science laboratory safe.  Follow the Public School Unit’s guidelines regarding safety in the classroom. For more information, review the National Science Teaching Association’s position statement on safety and school science instruction.

	Dimension 1: Science and Engineering Practice (SEP)
[Science and Engineering Practice included in Objective]
Practices refer to the things that scientists and engineers do and how they actively engage in their work. This section provides further clarification for what students should be doing to engage in this practice. There are various ways that each practice can be used, as articulated in A Framework for K-12 Science Education (NRC, 2012) and the NSTA’s SEP Matrix.

The Framework identifies a small number of disciplinary core ideas that all students should learn with increasing depth and sophistication, from Kindergarten through grade twelve. Key to the vision expressed in the Framework is for students to learn these disciplinary core ideas in the context of science and engineering practices. The importance of combining science and engineering practices and disciplinary core ideas is stated in the Framework as follows: Standards and performance expectations that are aligned to the framework must take into account that students cannot fully understand scientific and engineering ideas without engaging in the practices of inquiry and the discourses by which such ideas are developed and refined. At the same time, they cannot learn or show competence in practices except in the context of specific content (NRC Framework, 2012, p. 218).
The eight practices of science and engineering that the Framework identifies as essential for all students to learn and describes in detail are listed below:
1.  Asking questions (for science) and defining problems (for engineering)
2. Developing and using models
3. Planning and carrying out investigations
4. Analyzing and interpreting data
5. Using mathematics and computational thinking
6. Constructing explanations (for science) and designing solutions (for engineering)
7. Engaging in argument from evidence
8. Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information

A Framework for K-12 Science Education identifies seven crosscutting concepts that bridge disciplinary boundaries and unite core ideas. Their purpose is to help students deepen their understanding of the disciplinary core ideas and develop a coherent and scientifically based view of the world (p. 83).

The seven crosscutting concepts of the Framework are:
1. Patterns - Observed patterns of forms and events guide organization and classification, and they prompt questions about relationships and the factors that influence them.
2. Cause and effect: Mechanism and explanation - Events have causes, sometimes simple, sometimes multifaceted. A major activity of science is investigating and explaining causal relationships and the mechanisms by which they are mediated. Such mechanisms can then be tested across given contexts and used to predict and explain events in new contexts.
3. Scale, proportion, and quantity - In considering phenomena, it is critical to recognize what is relevant at different measures of size, time, and energy and to recognize how changes in scale, proportion, or quantity affect a system’s structure or performance.
4. Systems and system models - Defining the system under study—specifying its boundaries and making explicit a model of that system—provides tools for understanding and testing ideas that are applicable throughout science and engineering.
5. Energy and matter: Flows, cycles, and conservation - Tracking fluxes of energy and matter into, out of, and within systems helps one understand the systems’ possibilities and limitations.
6. Structure and function - The way in which an object or living thing is shaped and its substructure determine many of its properties and functions.
7. Stability and change - For natural and built systems alike, conditions of stability and determinants of rates of change or evolution of a system are critical elements of study.

	What does it look like to demonstrate proficiency?
Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Classify - Determining that something belongs to a category based on relevant features or patterns
* Students use models to distinguish between elements and compounds.
   * Students explain that all of the particles in a sample of an element are the same and that an element’s chemical formula contains only 1 symbol from the periodic table (e.g., K, Na, H2, O2).
   * Students explain that a compound contains 2 or more different types of element particles joined by chemical bonds and that the compound's chemical formula contains >1 element symbol from the periodic table (e.g., KBr, NaCl, H2O, MgCl2).
* Students use models to compare pure substances and mixtures.
   * Students explain that pure substances contain only 1 type of particle. (e.g., a sample of a single element, a sample of a single compound)
   * Students explain that a mixture contains >1 type of particle. (e.g., a sample containing >1 type of element particle, >1 type of compound particle, or both element particles and compound particles)
* Students use models to compare homogeneous mixtures and heterogeneous mixtures.
   * Students explain that in a heterogeneous mixture, the different types of particles are not distributed uniformly throughout the mixture giving it a nonuniform appearance.
   * Students explain that in a homogeneous mixture, the different types of particles are distributed uniformly throughout the mixture giving it a uniform appearance.
      * Students identify a solution as a homogeneous mixture.
* Students describe how solutions are formed as a result of collisions between solute and solvent particles.
   * Students explain factors that affect solubility (e.g., state of matter, nature of solute and solvent aka “Like vs Like,” temperature, pressure)
   * Students explain factors that affect the rate of solubility (e.g., changing temperature, pressure, or surface area, and stirring).
   * Students use a Solubility Curve (Amount of Solute/100 g H20 vs Temperature) to identify:
         * if a solution is saturated, unsaturated, or supersaturated.
         * the amount of solvent that could be dissolved in 100 g of water at a given temperature.
         * trends in solubility for gas vs solid solutes.
* Students use models to compare properties of solutions, colloids, and suspensions including:
   * particle size
   * particle distribution and settling (e.g. homogeneous vs heterogeneous)
   * the interaction of the mixture particles with light (Tyndall Effect)
	Academic Language

*Question/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:

* What can you look for in a particle diagram to classify a sample of particles as a pure substance or a mixture?

	Academic Language
*Question/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:
* Look at the models for each phase of matter (solid, liquid, gas). How are the particles arranged in each model?
* How does the arrangement of particles in each phase relate to its ability to flow and change shape?
* What happens to the arrangement of particles in the model when a solid changes to a liquid (or vice versa)?
* How does this change in particle arrangement explain the physical change we observed (melting/freezing)?
* How are the models for different solids similar? How are they different?
* Can you relate these similarities and differences in the models to the properties of the different solids (e.g., hardness, brittleness)?
* How does your model for a phase change (e.g., evaporation) connect the arrangement of particles in the liquid phase to the gas phase?
* Can you think of a real-world example where understanding the phases of matter and their changes is important? How does the model help us understand this application?

	What does it look like to demonstrate proficiency?
Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Compare - Finding similarities and differences between two or more objects, events, ideas, problems, or situations
* Students use models to explain the relationship between a substance’s mass, volume, and density.
* Students use the mathematical model D = m/v to predict how changes in a substance's mass or volume affect its density.
* Students use particle diagrams to compare the density of substances in terms of their masses and volumes.(e.g., Substance A is more dense than Substance B because Substance A has more mass in the same volume.)
* Students identify an unknown metal by analyzing and interpreting density data (student generated or archival).
* Students use separation techniques to separate a mixture based on the physical properties (solubility, magnetism, particle size, boiling point, etc.) of its components.
* Students analyze and interpret data to compare physical properties (melting point, solubility, boiling point, conductivity, etc.) and chemical properties (flammability, corrosion/oxidation resistance, reactivity of metals, reactivity with acids, reactivity with water, etc.) of various everyday materials such as salt, sugar, baking soda, corn starch, rubbing alcohol, water, etc.
* Students construct explanations for why physical properties are different (based on intermolecular forces) among different types of substances.
	Academic Language

*Question/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:
* Consider a simple chemical reaction like the combination of vinegar and baking soda. What are the physical and chemical changes that you observed?
* How can you use a particle diagram to compare the density of two substances?
* Looking at the data you collected during our investigations with salt, sugar, baking soda, corn starch, rubbing alcohol, and water, which of those substances have the most similar physical properties? Give examples from your data.
* What are intermolecular forces and why are they important?
* Look at the melting point data you collected during our investigations with salt, sugar, baking soda, corn starch, rubbing alcohol, and water. Can you explain in terms of intermolecular forces why some substances melt at lower temperatures than others?
* Look at the conductivity data you collected.
   * Which substances are conductive when dissolved in water but not conductive when undissolved?
   * What might be responsible for this difference in conductivity?

	What does it look like to demonstrate proficiency?
Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Interpret - Changing from one form of representation to another
* Students use modeling tools (diagrams, drawings, physical replicas, mathematical representations, analogies, and/or PhET simulations, and interactive periodic tables) to explain the subatomic structure of neutral atoms including:
   * The location, relative mass, and charges of subatomic particles (protons, neutrons, and electrons).
   * The relative size and mass of the nucleus of an atom compared to the atom as a whole.
   * Students can explain that the nucleus is a tiny fraction of the volume of an atom but makes up almost all of its mass.
   * Students can explain that even though the electron cloud makes up most of the volume of an atom, its mass is negligible (most of it is empty space).
   * Each element’s atomic number corresponds to its proton number.
   * Students use nuclear notation to represent the contents of an atom’s nucleus. (e.g.C, N )
      * Students can identify the number of protons (atomic number) and number of neutrons (mass number - atomic number) in the nucleus of an atom.
      * Students identify the number of energy levels into which electrons are arranged based on the Bohr diagram of a given neutral atom.
      * Students determine the number of valence electrons in the outermost energy level of a neutral atom based on the Bohr diagram or electron dot diagram of a given element.
      * Students understand that the dots in the electron dot diagram represent the number of valence electrons in the outermost energy level of the atom.
