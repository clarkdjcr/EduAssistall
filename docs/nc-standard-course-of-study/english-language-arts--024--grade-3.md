# English Language Arts - Grade 3

Introduction
Unpacking the ELA Standard Course of Study
Grade 3

On January 8, 2026, the State Board of Education unanimously approved the 2026 North Carolina English Language Arts (ELA) Standard Course of Study (SCOS) for implementation in the 2027-2028 school year. To support successful implementation of these standards, the NCDPI ELA Team has created Unpacking Documents to deepen the understanding of the NC ELA SCOS and its Fundamental Principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center, 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling, and 3) Variety of Texts.

The Unpacking Documents include the ELA Standards and Objectives as well as a glossary, clarifications, standards-based question stems and sentence frames, classroom considerations, and supplemental resources. Please note that these unpacking documents are not meant to be exhaustive.

Because North Carolina is a local-control state, all curricular decisions (e.g., texts, tasks, activities, etc.) are approved at the local level. Prior to implementing any of the materials in this resource, teachers should follow appropriate policies and procedures to have their curriculum vetted and approved by their school and district leaders.

Acknowledgements: The NCDPI ELA Team consulted with educators in the field as well as the Offices of Early Learning and Exceptional Children during the creation of this resource.

CT.1
Overview and Purpose

The Complex Text Strand (CT) ensures students are developing into proficient, independent readers and listeners, outlining essential skills that students should be practicing every day, in every lesson, and with every text. This strand deepens students’ understanding of how to become skilled, confident readers who can sustain attention and build stamina with increasingly challenging texts. The CT standards and objectives are designed to speak to the complex nature of reading comprehension and provide all educators with a deeper understanding of skills students should engage in routinely to access and comprehend grade-level complex texts.

In order to place text at the center of ELA teaching and learning, CT standards and objectives should be integrated across the other two strands of the NC ELA SCOS–Comprehension Development (CD) and Communication and Writing (CW)–when designing standards-aligned instruction. This integration ensures that students practice skills within the context of grade-appropriate complex texts. As students apply these skills across a variety of such texts, they demonstrate the ability to independently construct meaning across language domains.

Through the CT Strand, students build knowledge and strengthen comprehension by reading and listening to a range of worthy, content-rich texts. This includes students
* applying a range of strategies to monitor, support, and deepen comprehension
* drawing on a wide range of reading and listening experiences to build knowledge and vocabulary
* transferring techniques from the texts to their own evolving forms of communication and writing
* independently and proficiently reading/listening to grade-level complex texts at the high end of the 2-3 text complexity band

These CT standards and objectives reflect critical, research-based components of reading comprehension and reinforce rigorous grade-level expectations across all strands of the NC ELA SCOS. The Complex Texts strand serves as a foundation for standards-based, text-centered instruction in every ELA classroom.
Complex Text Strand and the Fundamental Principles

The Complex Text Strand standards and objectives ensure the fundamental principles of 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy; and 3) a Variety of Texts are living out in the ELA classroom. Designed to be recursive in nature, CT standards and objectives make explicit and implicit references to these fundamental principles to ensure students are engaging with them routinely throughout instruction.
* Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center | Grade-level complex text is the foundation of the CT.1 standard and the primary means through which students engage with the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) to build essential academic competencies. Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation.
* Integrated Model of Literacy | CT.1 objectives support students’ ability to apply and integrate skills across language domains in various contexts and across a range of texts. For example, CT.1.1 requires students to return back to the texts they are reading to ground discussion and writing in textual evidence. CT.1.2 emphasizes repeated readings, writing in response to texts, and collaborative discussion as key drivers of comprehension. CT.1.4 promotes the reading-writing connection, ensuring students engage with language receptively through reading, and transfer that knowledge generatively to their own communication.
* Variety of Texts | Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. CT.1.3 encourages students to read widely to build knowledge and vocabulary across a range of topics, themes, and concepts.
A note on text selection and local control

North Carolina is a local-control state. Because of this, the North Carolina Department of Public Instruction (NCDPI) does not provide a recommended text list, as curricular decisions are made at the PSU (public school unit) level. NCDPI cannot endorse specific curricula or instructional materials for local use. The standards do not restrict or preclude the teaching of specific modes, types, genres, mediums, and formats of text.

PSUs should carefully analyze potential texts for each grade by examining the qualitative, quantitative, and reader and task measures to ensure students engage with increasingly complex texts. Through text complexity analysis, educators can determine when a particular mode, type, genre, medium, format of text best supports the instructional purpose. Multiple approaches are appropriate and should be used in alignment with the standards to ensure students experience a range of texts and text types that prepare them for postsecondary opportunities.

Glossary
	Recount/Recounting - A clearly sequenced ordering of narrative events.

Repeated Reading - A vital comprehension tool–leveraged by the teacher or students–that entails rereading text for multiple purposes. It is frequently used in the close reading of complex texts to deepen student comprehension.

Self-Monitoring - The act of monitoring one’s own comprehension for what is understood, what is not understood, and what comprehension strategies would be appropriate to resolve issues with comprehension.

Text Complexity - Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task.

Text(s) - Texts are generated by authors to communicate to readers. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres or modalities (e.g., visual, auditory, aural, linguistic, gestural, spatial, etc.). Texts may be printed on paper or published in digital forms.

Classroom Considerations
* Carefully select high-quality texts by ensuring the text is appropriately complex for the grade-level. Determine overall text complexity using a variety of measures including qualitative, quantitative, and reader & task considerations. Educators should examine and weigh all three measures equally; one is not more important than another, and all three combined provide the most accurate level of complexity.
* Plan daily extensive reading instruction using increasingly complex texts at the high end of the 2-3 text complexity band, strategically integrating intentional entry points that support student access while preserving the full complexity of the text. Ensure students are exposed to a variety of text modes, types, genres, structures, and formats.
* Use think-alouds as a primary strategy to model how proficient readers make meaning from grade-level complex texts, explicitly demonstrating how to navigate challenging vocabulary, structures, and ideas. Follow this modeling with guided practice that allows students to apply these processes with support, gradually releasing responsibility as they demonstrate increasing independence.
* Equip students with tools that build their capacity to independently navigate grade-level, complex texts by teaching them to recognize when meaning breaks down, ask and answer questions as they read/listen to monitor their comprehension, and reflect on what they have read/heard. Provide regular opportunities for students to practice these behaviors so they become self-directed, strategic readers/listeners. Support students’ metacognition by engaging them before, during, and after reading to reflect on what they are learning and how they are making meaning from the text.

3.CT.1.1 Ask and answer questions about key details, referring explicitly to the text as the basis for the answers.

This is a continued emphasis from grade 2, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 3. Students ask and answer questions with greater independence, including who, what, when, where, why, and how questions. Referring to the text becomes more intentional. Students refer to specific parts of the text (such as a sentence, paragraph, or section) to support their answer.

Students...
* ask questions about key details
* answer questions about key details, referring explicitly to the text as the basis for the answers

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	* Which detail in the text best helps you understand ___?
* According to the text, what does this [word, sentence, or section] tell us?
* What specific detail from the text supports your thinking?
* How does the text help answer this question?
	* The detail that best helps me understand this part is ___.
* According to the text, this [word, sentence, or section] tells us ___.
* A specific detail from the text that supports my thinking is ___.
* The text helps answer this question by explaining ___.

Classroom Considerations
* Teach and support students with using text annotations for evidence tracking. Show students how to: mark key ideas, highlight places in the text, use margin notes, etc. to track important details as they read for specific purposes.
* Plan instruction for close reading that allows students to respond to text-dependent questions that ask them both what the text states directly (what a text states) and what it says indirectly (inferences drawn from a text).
* Through modeling and explicit instruction, show students that an inference comes from combining text clues with what they already know, not guessing. Think aloud to show students how to notice clues in the text and turn them into inferences.
* Once students have practiced explaining key details in a text, model both asking and answering questions about them during independent reading and interactive read-alouds. Use think-alouds to show how readers/listeners rely on and refer to pictures, words, and specific parts of the text to generate questions, support answers, and make inferences, thereby building early habits of text-based reasoning rather than reliance on background knowledge alone. Students should be provided multiple and varied opportunities to practice referring to the text as the basis for answers when writing or speaking. Consider building this skill into discussion and writing norms, requiring students to provide text-based justification for their answers.

3.CT.1.2 Use self-monitoring, repeated reading, recounting, writing in response to texts, and discussions with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension.

This is a continued emphasis on these comprehension supports from grade 2, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 3. Students utilize various supports when reading a text to strengthen comprehension. These include:
* using self-monitoring strategies to gauge and repair where their comprehension may break down
* re-reading texts multiple times for various purposes (including understanding) and levels of meaning
* recounting the main points of what has been read, and
* engaging in writing and discussion about texts to communicate understanding and collectively reinforce learning.
Each of these approaches ensure students are accessing and comprehending grade-level complex texts with increased independence.

Students…
* use self-monitoring to strengthen comprehension
* use repeated reading to strengthen comprehension
* use recounting to strengthen comprehension
* write in response to texts to strengthen comprehension
* discuss texts with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension

Classroom Considerations
* Foster students’ active engagement with self monitoring their comprehension, explicitly teaching and supporting students to flexibly use a repertoire of comprehension strategies.
* Follow the principle of gradual release to ensure students use strategies independently.
* Provide explicit instruction and opportunities for extended discussion on text meaning and interpretation, including opportunities for whole-class interactive read-alouds and performing close reading of complex text.
* Support students with writing in response to reading for a variety of purposes, including to recount, analyze, interpret, and apply information from texts they have read.

3.CT.1.3 Draw on a wide reading of texts to build knowledge and vocabulary.

This is a continued emphasis on wide reading to build knowledge and vocabulary from grade 2, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 3. In grade 3, students read/listen to a variety of texts on a given subject or topic–both teacher determined or student selected–in order to expand their knowledge, understanding, and vocabulary. Students draw on this information to expand their comprehension of content being studied in class and to better access grade-level complex texts.

Students…
* draw on a wide range of texts to build knowledge
* draw on a wide range of texts to build vocabulary

Classroom Considerations
* Provide text sets connected by topic/theme and ensure they include a variety of text types, genres, modes, formats, mediums, and/or lengths. Texts in the set should reflect a range of complexity levels so students can build the knowledge and vocabulary needed to access the grade-level text being studied.
* To increase the impact of wide reading, regularly activate, connect, build, and extend students’ prior knowledge to help them bridge new learning with what they already understand and have studied. Doing so improves comprehension, supports vocabulary development, and increases engagement by making content more accessible. Writing and discussion strategies can be incorporated into instruction to help uncover what students know before introducing new material or texts (e.g., anticipation guides, cloze readings, concept sorts, ABC brainstorming, quick writes, etc).
* Create and maintain an inviting, well-resourced, and purposefully organized classroom library that offers a wide range of texts and media. Ensure the collection reflects students’ diverse interests, backgrounds, languages, and experiences; exposes students to academic language across content areas; and includes multiple genres that support knowledge-building around specific topics. Provide opportunities for students to self-select materials for independent reading at school, at home, and through digital access when available. Establish and teach clear routines for selecting, using, and caring for materials so the classroom library can be used effectively and responsibly.
* Provide regular, protected time for wide reading to allow students to explore multiple texts on the same topic. When applicable, use this time to encourage student choice within a topic to boost motivation while simultaneously supporting knowledge and vocabulary growth. Model habits of wide reading for students by doing brief book talks, highlighting how reading multiple texts deepens understanding of a topic, and allowing time for students to talk, write, or reflect about what they are learning from their reading. Explicitly draw students’ attention to how their existing banks of knowledge are expanded through additional reading about a given topic and linking newly learned information or realizations to previously established knowledge.
* Collaborate with a librarian or media specialist to help identify digital resources, databases, and multimedia that broaden the range of texts students can draw on. Involve these specialists in helping students learn how to locate, evaluate, and select texts that support their knowledge building. Consider how research or independent reading tasks can be used to get students to read more widely on a topic being studied.
* Support student vocabulary development as they read by following a comprehensive vocabulary framework that promotes word learning in a multifaceted way: teach high-impact Tier II and Tier III words related to a specific topic/text, equip students with strategies for figuring out unfamiliar words when they encounter them, foster curiosity about language by studying the way words work and relate, and provide rich and varied written and spoken language experiences so students encounter new vocabulary in meaningful contexts. Together, these components help students build stronger, more flexible word knowledge as they read.

3.CT.1.4 Recognize the choices and features used by authors and transfer those techniques to communication and writing.

This is a continued emphasis on recognizing and transferring techniques from grade 2, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 3. In grade 3, students notice and learn from how authors write and craft texts, using those same techniques to improve their own communication and writing. Students determine how an author’s choices impact a text and its effects on readers/listeners, looking at elements and features such as: word choice, structure, literary elements, figurative language, organizational patterns, etc.  Students apply similar techniques to their own writing or speaking, developing the style, complexity, and individual voice of their own communication based on their specific purpose and task.

Students…
* recognize the choices and features used by authors
* transfer those techniques to communication and writing

Classroom Considerations
* Support students with identifying and reflecting on the impact of choices authors make in texts to enhance their craft and style. These text choices and features should include elements such as organization, structure, literary elements, word choice, figurative language, etc.
* Plan instruction that integrates skills across language domains–reading, writing, speaking, and listening–so students can notice techniques in texts they are reading/listening/viewing and practice applying them to their writing and speaking in various contexts.
* Use mentor texts to help students analyze and emulate in their own communication. Select high-quality texts that clearly demonstrate different techniques across genres and styles so students develop a broader understanding of what communication can look like catered to a variety of purposes and audiences.
* Ensure instruction includes both interpretive (analysis) and expressive (creation) opportunities for students, so they can both understand and examine texts (to support reading/listening comprehension), as well as transfer techniques when producing original texts as authors/speakers with specific intentions of their own. Interpretive tasks focus on how students understand, analyze, and interpret information they receive through reading, listening, or viewing. Expressive tasks focus on how students communicate their own thoughts, ideas, and feelings through speaking, writing, or representing. Explore The Writing Guidebook and The Discourse Playbook for additional guidance and ideas for pairing reading with writing, speaking, and listening.

Glossary
	Decode - The process of translating a word from print to speech by using knowledge of sound–symbol (letter) correspondences.

Encode - The process of translating speech into print (writing) using knowledge of sound–symbol (letter) correspondences.

Morphology - The study of word structure and how words are formed by looking at their meaningful units (morphemes)—prefixes, roots, and suffixes (e.g., mis-spell-ing).

Phonics - Reading instruction that focuses on the alphabetic principle— the systematic, predictable relationship between spoken sounds (phonemes) and written letters (graphemes)—to allow readers to identify or “decode” words.

Syllabication - The act of breaking words into syllables.

Classroom Considerations
3.CD.F.3.1 Use combined knowledge of letter-sound correspondences, syllabication patterns, and morphology to decode and encode unfamiliar multisyllabic words.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Maintain explicit connections between decoding and encoding by reminding students that the same knowledge they use to break apart and read multisyllabic words should also be used to spell them. Consistently model how analyzing a combination of letter-sound correspondences, syllables and affixes supports both reading and writing. Select strategies for instruction that support the combined use and application of these skills.
* Explicitly teach and regularly revisit syllabication patterns in authentic contexts, using real sentences and grade-level texts to help students practice chunking multisyllabic words and determining where syllables naturally break–bridging what they know in isolation with how they apply it in continuous text.
* Leverage morphology as a meaning-making tool–not just a decoding tool–by encouraging students to use the meanings of prefixes and suffixes to determine whether a decoded word makes sense and to refine their spelling choices.
	Example 1:
Word Decoding Routine: This strategy can be used for reading larger words, strengthening students’ understanding of how to combine their growing use of decoding strategies. Students identify any known prefixes or suffixes, then use syllabication to decode the rest of the word.
* Box and say any known affixes
* Locate and underline each vowel graphemes
* Use knowledge of syllables to mark/divide and decode the vowel sounds
* Read the whole word and see if it makes sense and sounds right
* Verify in context (if applicable)

Example 2:
Word Encoding Routine: This routine can be taught, supported, and used by students to encode words using their combined knowledge of letter-sound correspondences, syllabication patterns, and morphology.
* Say the word out loud
* Chunk the word into syllables
* Listen for meaningful word parts (i.e., prefixes, suffixes)
* Stretch and spell each part
* Read the whole word, checking for accuracy
* Write a sentence using the word

3.CD.F.3.2 Decode and encode words with common derivational and Latin suffixes.

Considerations
	Examples
	* When planning instruction on common derivational and Latin suffixes, intentionally integrate phonics instruction with meaning-based instruction.  Emphasize meaning over memorization by connecting suffixes to their meanings, showing students how understanding these word parts supports both accurate decoding and deeper comprehension.
* Model word-solving talk by thinking aloud as you analyze meaningful word parts (e.g., “I see the suffix ‘-tion,’ which means “the action or process of.” That helps me decode and understand construction because it means the act of constructing or building something”). Show students how breaking words apart by meaning supports both accurate reading and deeper understanding.
* Provide repeated exposure to common suffixes across varied contexts. Use word webs, matrices, sentence-level practice, and connected texts (words that are linked in sentences, phrases, or paragraphs. A group of sentences that relate to one another) so students encounter and apply the same meaningful word parts in multiple ways, strengthening both decoding and encoding.
	Example 1:
Suffix Sort: This activity supports students with recognizing the consistent sound pattern and meaning of word parts. Students cut out, read, and sort suffixes, their meaning, and a base word. Students use the Recording Mat to write the pairing, new word, and the new word’s meaning.

Example 2:
Connecting Encoding to Decoding: This routine supports students with encoding words that contain suffixes.  This routine should mirror words/suffixes taught during reading instruction.
* Say the base word
* Say the suffix
* Stretch and spell each part
* Check for spelling changes (drop e, double consonant, y → I)
* Write a sentence using the word.

Example 3:
Word Matrix: This strategy supports students in decoding and encoding words with common prefixes and suffixes by helping them understand how meaningful word parts combine to form new words. Teachers provide a base word along with a set of prefixes (e.g., un-, dis-, re-, pre-) and suffixes (e.g., -ful, -less, -ness, -hood). Students select and combine word parts to build multiple related words, read each word aloud, discuss how the prefix or suffix changes the word’s meaning, and use each word in a sentence. By visually organizing and manipulating morphemes, students strengthen their ability to decode longer words, accurately spell words with affixes, and deepen their understanding of word meaning.

3.CD.F.3.3 Decode and encode grade-appropriate, irregularly spelled words.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Ensure that students understand that parts of irregular words can still contain regular phonetic patterns and therefore still be decoded/encoded. For example, in the irregular word “said,” the “s” and “d” still assume their regular sounds; the irregular part of the word that needs to be remembered is the “ai.”  Encourage students to explain why it doesn’t match expected sound patterns.
* Provide repeated practice using a taught irregularly spelled word when reading and writing in order to reinforce the targeted sound or pattern. Use activities like word mapping, word sorts, writing/reading the word in context, etc. The concept of these activities could also be captured and co-created in anchor charts or running journals for additional reference.
	Example 1:
Heart words: This routine can be used by teachers to support students with reading irregular words by sight and spelling those words automatically.  This routine involves a teacher manipulating grapheme tiles (including a heart tile) on a magnetic whiteboard.

Example 2:
Generative Sentences: This protocol involves having students generate sentences using a specific word(s), but sticking to a criteria established by the teacher. This pushes students to form a deeper understanding of the word, forcing them to produce authentic sentences and more deeply associate the word’s spelling with its use and/or meaning. Teachers can support students with reading irregularly spelled words by building connections between their spelling and their appropriate use in context.

Classroom Considerations
3.CD.F.4.1 Read texts for a variety of purposes.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Ensure that students build fluency through their familiarity and understanding of texts through instructional practices like close reading. When providing explicit instruction for close reading, ensure that students understand that each consecutive read has a distinct purpose with the aim to not only understand content, but transfer that knowledge to new contexts. For example, a potential framework could be: Phase 1, determine what the text says.  Phase 2, figure out how the text works. Phase 3, determine what the text means. Phase 4, applying learned skills/knowledge.
* Ensure that students engage in practices that encourage the oral reading of texts for a variety of purposes. For example, referring to the text in order to engage in class discussions, to solve a problem or answer a question, to present or recreate a text or information, to locate information, etc.
	Example 1:
Sample Reading for a Variety of Purposes Routine: This routine is designed to improve fluency and build comprehension by layering different purposes for successive readings of the same text. Students first identify and clarify unfamiliar words, then listen to a fluent model to develop understanding of the text’s overall meaning, and finally read aloud to deepen comprehension through discussion and text-dependent questions. Across all three reads, teacher modeling, partner support, and intentional pauses help students monitor understanding, resolve confusion, and practice oral reading fluency.

3.CD.F.4.2 Confirm or self-correct word recognition and understanding, as necessary.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Provide students with strategies that encourage self-reflection and questioning. For example, strategies like text annotations encourage metacognition by having students indicate when there is a breakdown of understanding. Students, having recognized this, can then take the necessary next steps in order to confirm or self-correct.
* Model for students what to do or think about when a breakdown in understanding occurs. Do this using think alouds, providing students with strategies for different circumstances.  For example, “I’m confused here.  I thought that the author said ___.  I’m going to reread this paragraph to make sure that I understand.” Consider how this could leverage word learning strategies, like using word parts, reference materials, context clues, etc.
	Example 1:
Self-Questioning:
* Does it sound right?
* Does it look right?
* Does it make sense?

Example 2:
Text Annotations: This strategy involves students using predetermined symbols to mark, elaborate on, and respond to the information provided by a text. Have students use this strategy to actively engage in and process the content of a text while they are reading for deeper comprehension. Students think metacognitively about their own understanding, making note of and acting on any lapses.

3.CD.F.4.3 Read texts with accuracy, appropriate rate, and prosody on successive readings.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Model fluent reading for students by reading aloud regularly–or using recordings of–excerpts from grade-level complex texts while students follow along in their own copies. 	Example 1:
Echo reading: This strategy involves the teacher model reading a sentence or short section of a text while students follow along, having students pay close attention to pronunciations, rate, and prosody. The teacher then instructs students to read the same section aloud, “echoing” the pronunciations, rate, and prosody that was modeled for them. This strategy is useful for having students immediately apply and internalize their learning based on a model fluent reader.

Example 2:
Partner reading: Using this routine,the teacher partners two students together
based on which students require support with a specific skill and
which student could help provide it. The students take turns reading sections of the text. Following each reading, the students provide feedback to each other and discuss their understanding. This strategy is effective because it provides models for multiple students while the teacher can circulate around the class in order to observe, monitor, and provide specific timely feedback. Because this strategy also involves comprehension, it is flexible enough to be used with any number of reading standard foci.

Example 3:
Phases of Close Reading: This strategy involves an intensive analysis of a text in order to come to terms with what it says, how it says it, and what it means. This builds metacognitive awareness and strategic flexibility. Encourage students to justify their thinking using precise academic language (e.g., “I inferred the meaning by noticing…” or “The suffix ____ helped me understand…”). Concepts and Example Sentences

Phrase & Clause Use Skills

Use prepositional phrases

Students need to know that a prepositional phrase consists of a preposition and its object (noun or pronoun) and functions as an adjective or adverb. They also need to recognize how prepositional phrases provide information about location, time, direction, cause, or manner.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Prepositional phrase - cause
	The price of toys went up because of the high demand in the store.

Verb Use Skills

Use regular and irregular verbs in simple tenses

Students need to know that regular verbs form their past tense by adding -ed (e.g., walk → walked), while irregular verbs have unique past tense forms that do not follow a predictable pattern (e.g., go → went, eat → ate, am/is/are → was/were). They also need to recognize that simple tenses–past, present, and future–express actions or states at a single point in time.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Past
	We went outside to observe how much the plants had grown.
	Present
	She goes outside every morning to observe how much the plants have grown.
	Future
	We will go outside tomorrow to measure the growth of the plants.

Ensure subject–verb agreement

Students need to know that singular subjects take singular verbs and plural subjects take plural verbs, even when intervening words or phrases appear between the subject and verb. They also need to know how agreement works with:
* Compound subjects (“and” usually = plural; “or/nor” = verb agrees with nearest subject)
* Indefinite pronouns (singular: everyone, each; plural: few, many; some can be singular or plural depending on meaning)
* Collective nouns (singular when acting as a unit, plural when acting as individuals)
* Inverted sentences (identify the true subject first, e.g., There are many books)
* Titles, sums, or amounts (usually singular, e.g., The United States is large)

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Singular subject
	The teacher needs to know where you found evidence of this in the text.
	Plural subject
	They need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.
	Indefinite singular
	Everyone needs to know where you found evidence of this in the text.
	Indefinite plural
	A few (of us) need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.
	Compound subject
	John and Jane need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.
	Singular collective noun
	The class needs to know where you found evidence of this in the text.
	Plural collective noun
	The students need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.
	Inverted sentence
	Does the class need to know where you found evidence of this in the text?
	Amount
	Those two (students) need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.

Pronoun Use Skills

Ensure pronoun–antecedent agreement

Students need to know that a pronoun must match the number (singular or plural) and person of the noun it replaces. They also need to know that to determine agreement, they should first identify the antecedent–the specific noun the pronoun refers to–before choosing the correct pronoun form.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Pronoun–antecedent agreement
	Thomas Edison spent a lot of time perfecting his invention.

The miners dug for coal, and they carefully brought it to the surface.

Adjective & Adverb Use Skills

Use relative adverbs

Students need to know that relative adverbs (where, when, why) introduce clauses that provide additional information about a noun or verb. They need to know that these clauses follow the word they describe and function like adjectives or adverbs, explaining location (where), time (when), or reason (why). Students also need to recognize that these relative adverb clauses cannot stand alone and always link their clause to a specific noun or verb in the main clause.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Relative adverb - time
	I need to use a pictorial model when showing my work for the word problem.

Form and use comparative adjectives/adverbs

Students need to know comparatives are forms of adjectives or adverbs that show ranges of difference between two things (e.g., taller, more quickly). Students need to know that comparatives often use “-er” or “more” depending on word structure. They also need to recognize how to compare actions or qualities accurately using these forms.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Comparative adjective
	The area of the large rectangle is the sum of the two smaller rectangles.
	Comparative adverb
	The area of the large rectangle is found more easily by adding the two smaller rectangles.

Form and use superlative adjectives/adverbs

Students need to know superlatives are forms of adjectives or adverbs that show the highest or extreme degree among three or more things (e.g., tallest, most quickly). Students need to know that superlatives often use “-est” or “most” depending on word structure. They also need to recognize how to identify or form the “most extreme” quality in a set.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Superlative adjective

	Superlative adverb

Classroom Considerations
* Focus grammar instruction on a functional and rhetorical level, instead of solely focusing on memorizing the grammatical terms/concepts themselves. Consider a more student-centered approach to grammar instruction by teaching it in context, considering how it can be taught synchronously with the texts being read and produced by students. To teach grammar and usage in context, consider the following, in order:
   * Student-Level Context: Examine student writing/speaking to determine the grammar/usage concept that will benefit students.
   * Text-Level Context: Identify a mentor text (sentence, paragraph, or larger excerpt) that shows the grammar/usage concept in use. Study and discuss how the grammar/usage concept is used in the text to achieve a particular goal or effect.
   * Writing/Speaking-Level Context: Have students practice applying the grammar/usage concept to their own communication via a prompt, shared writing, scenario and task, etc. Ask and expect students to transfer their learning of the grammar/usage concept to their larger writing or speaking tasks. For more guidance on teaching grammar in context, see Recommendation 3 of The Writing Guidebook.
* Teach students the various conventions appropriate to whatever type of communication or writing they are doing, studying, or reading, providing a rich and varied array of examples of how these conventions are used in real-world texts and by authentic writers and speakers. Provide students mentor texts and models of quality writing that illustrate how certain constructions, conventions, and usage rules are applied in practice.
* Utilize the following practices when designing explicit instruction on grammar and usage:
   * Help students build knowledge on a specific concept by outlining what it is, how it works, and why they should know and use it.
   * Create opportunities for students to practice a specific concept to gauge and refine student understanding.
   * Have students apply knowledge and use a specific concept in an actual piece of writing or communication so students can demonstrate their understanding and receive feedback.
   * Provide feedback and additional opportunities for students to continue to apply their learning of the concept over time in more independent and sophisticated ways. Concepts should not be considered a one-and-done, but something that continues to be applied over time.
* As students master grammar/usage concepts, consider how students can experiment with them to create a unique style and voice. Find ways to intentionally move students from “using grammar correctly” to “using grammar purposefully.” Examples of this could include: helping students see that grammar choices affect style and voice (e.g., showing how comparative and superlative adjectives and adverbs establish a voice), having students emulate an author’s style (see the Copy/Change strategy in Recommendation 2 of the Writing Guidebook) using correct grammar, teaching students to adapt their grammar based on what they are trying to do (e.g., poetry writing using prepositional phrases to add detail and rhythm), providing feedback on effective grammar choices not just mistakes (e.g. “Your use of the past tense here contributes to your storytelling because I know these events already occurred.”), or having students explain their choice via quick reflection prompts to build metacognition (e.g. “Which sentence are you most proud of and why?” or “What grammar move did you try and how did it affect your voice?”).
* Intentionally activate and reinforce grammar and usage concepts from prior grades when introducing new grade-level expectations (e.g., revisiting the function of adjectives and adverbs as part of teaching the formation and use of comparative and superlative adjectives and adverbs). This will help students build knowledge cumulatively and connect past learning to more complex concepts. Maintain an ongoing anchor chart or reference list of taught concepts–with clear definitions and student-generated examples–to support students in applying conventions independently and help in providing specific, standards-aligned feedback during writing and speaking tasks, whether that feedback be to peers or themselves as part of the editing process.

	Same plural and singular

Classroom Considerations
* Focus writing conventions instruction on a functional and rhetorical level, instead of solely focusing on memorizing the terms of conventions themselves. Consider a more student-centered approach to instruction on writing conventions by teaching it in context, considering how it can be taught synchronously with the texts being read and produced by students. To teach conventions usage in context, consider the following, in order:
   * Student-Level Context: Examine student writing to determine the convention that will benefit students.
   * Text-Level Context: Identify a mentor text (sentence, paragraph, or larger excerpt) that shows the convention in use. Study and discuss how the convention is used in the text to achieve a particular goal or effect.
   * Writing-Level Context: Have students practice applying the convention to their own communication via a prompt, shared writing, scenario and task, etc. Ask and expect students to transfer their learning of conventions to their larger writing tasks. For more guidance on teaching conventions in context, see Recommendation 3 of The Writing Guidebook.
* Teach students the various conventions appropriate to whatever type of writing they are doing, studying, or reading, providing a rich and varied array of examples of how these conventions are used in real-world texts and by authentic writers. Provide students mentor texts and models of quality writing that illustrate how certain constructions, conventions, and usage rules are applied in practice (see the Copy/Change strategy in Recommendation 2 of the Writing Guidebook).
* Utilize the following practices when designing explicit instruction on convention:
   * Help students build knowledge on a specific concept by outlining what it is, how it works, and why they should know and use it.
   * Create opportunities for students to practice a specific concept to gauge and refine student understanding.
   * Have students apply knowledge and use a specific concept in an actual piece of writing so students can demonstrate their understanding and receive feedback.
   * Provide feedback and additional opportunities for students to continue to apply their learning of the concept over time in more independent and sophisticated ways. Concepts should not be considered a one-and-done, but something that continues to be applied over time.
* Intentionally activate and reinforce convention usage from prior grades when introducing new grade-level expectations (e.g., revisiting the use of apostrophes with singular possessive nouns as part of teaching the use of apostrophes for teaching plural possessive nouns). This will help students build knowledge cumulatively and connect past learning to more complex concepts. Maintain an ongoing anchor chart or reference list of taught concepts–with clear definitions and student-generated examples–to support students in applying conventions independently and help in providing specific, standards-aligned feedback during writing and speaking tasks, whether that feedback be to peers or themselves as part of the editing process (e.g., “You capitalized and underlined ‘Charlotte’s Web’ so your reader knows it is the title of a text,” or “Your quotation marks around the statement ‘I made the team!’ tells the reader that this is dialogue that the character is saying aloud”).

Concepts and Example Sentences

Sentence Production & Expansion Skills

Produce complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in a paragraph

In addition to producing simple and compound sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a complex sentence includes one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses, which do not express a complete thought on their own and are often introduced by subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, when, if) or relative pronouns. When the dependent clause comes before the independent clause, it is attached with a comma. When it comes after, a comma is not needed. To produce complex sentences, students should appropriately attach a dependent clause to an independent clause.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Simple Sentence
	Light bends slightly in clear water.
	Compound Sentence
	Light goes through the water, and it changes direction.
	Complex Sentence
	When light passes through water, it changes direction.

Expand complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in a paragraph

Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional clauses to simple, compound, and complex sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-3 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Expanded Simple Sentence
	Light bends slightly in clear water, making objects look different.
	Expanded Compound Sentence
	Light passes through the water most clearly, and it changes direction the fastest.
	Expanded Complex Sentence
	When bright light passes through water where it is clear, it bends and changes direction quickly.

Classroom Considerations
* When teaching students to produce and expand simple, compound, and complex sentences, do so in the context of speaking/writing, not in isolation.  Emphasize how sentence variety improves clarity and meaning, thinking aloud and using mentor texts as models. Model how to expand sentences using conjunctions (e.g., and, but, so, etc.), dependent clauses (e.g., because…, when…, if…, etc.) and adjectives or prepositional phrases (e.g., to…, with…, after…). Consider how the examples above could support other skills such as communicating text structure. Example: Using conjunctions and dependent clauses to indicate a cause and effect relationship or sequence of events.
* Build students’ sentence writing fluency by increasing the volume and opportunities for students to write for authentic purposes across the school day and subject areas (e.g., responding to a text, reflecting on new learning, communicating a message, etc.). Refer to Recommendation 1 of the Writing Guidebook for additional suggestions.
* When teaching cursive, introduce it gradually, focusing on letter formation, spacing, size, alignment, and connections before speed. Emphasize the importance of mode selection (manuscript, cursive, or other transcription skill), depending on the purpose and/or preference.  Students should know and be able to produce these modes, choosing the one that best fits their need(s). For example, some students may prefer to use cursive when notetaking because they can do so quicker and more efficiently than in manuscript.
* Support students in using word processing tools efficiently, providing opportunities for students to practice both handwriting and typing within grade-level tasks, such as sustained drafting or note-taking and extended writing tasks that require students to maintain accuracy and efficiency over time so that transcription becomes increasingly automatic and does not interfere with sentence construction. Attention to transcription should focus on maintaining accuracy, efficiency, and readability, enabling students to concentrate on developing ideas and composing sentences with precision and sophistication.

Classroom Considerations
* Help students understand drafting as a recursive, flexible, and purposeful process rather than a one-time event. Drafting should typically be seen as the crucial step of translating ideas/information from planning into formal language (with the exception being freewriting-based strategies, like Fever Dream writing), experimenting with organization, structure and voice based on the specific purpose, task, and audience. Support this autonomy by offering a menu of drafting options and encouraging students to reflect on which methods work best for them.
* As part of the drafting process, ensure students have access to models of quality writing. This could include mentor texts, samples of student work (student or teacher created), and rubrics and checklists that outline what drafts should include. These supports, such as copy/change, should be used to help students understand key expectations while also providing high-quality examples to inspire and promote creativity. These models should not be used to box students in or limit them to a specific approach, style, or answer. For guidance on utilizing mentor texts, see Recommendation 2 of The Writing Guidebook.
* Embed structured pause points throughout drafting to prompt students to reflect on their progress and make informed decisions about next steps in the composition process. Support students in assessing whether their draft continues to align with their purpose, audience, and intended meaning, and whether additional planning is needed before moving forward. Instruction should pair these pause points with explicit metacognitive strategies, such as self-questioning and teacher think-alouds during shared writing, that model how writers/speakers recognize uncertainty, misalignment, or stalled thinking as signals to return to pre-writing or planning in order to refine ideas and strengthen the overall composition.

* Ensure students understand the distinction between revision and editing as related but different tasks in the composition process. Emphasize that revision focuses on meaning, structure, and effectiveness (e.g., reconsidering ideas, reorganizing sections, refining evidence, and clarifying relationships between an opinion, reasons, and evidence), while editing attends to sentence-level correctness, style, and conventions once ideas are largely in place, including considerations for formatting. As part of the revision process, students need to know how to move sentences or clauses within a paragraph to improve flow, coherence, or emphasis. * Support students in engaging with multiple levels of feedback–teacher, peer, and self–to strengthen revision and editing. Provide support through targeted teacher feedback, such as individual or small-group conferences that focus on high-leverage strategies rather than correcting every error. Consider grouping students based on shared instructional needs, patterns of feedback, or stage in the writing process. When working with a small group during a conference, provide other students with clearly defined revision or editing tasks–such as targeted checklists, task cards, or models–to ensure their work remains purposeful and focused. Alternatively, have students engage in structured peer feedback or independent revision/editing tasks aligned to the same instructional goals. Structure peer feedback with clear expectations, using sentence frames, checklists, or focused prompts to guide attention to specific aspects of the draft and prevent feedback from becoming overly general or surface-level. Teach students to engage in self-feedback by using strategies such as color-coding to analyze their own writing for patterns or gaps in patterns (e.g., consistent use of tense, use of transition or linking words to signal relationships between ideas, inclusion of facts to strengthen a point being made, etc.) and identify next steps independently. Ensure students understand that seeking feedback from teachers, peers, and themselves is a normal and essential part of how writers and speakers develop ideas and refine their work, and that sharing drafts at multiple stages supports growth rather than signaling weakness or failure.

	Environmentalists are cleaning up the trash that is polluting our oceans.

	Students need to know modal verbs (e.g., can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would, ought to, need to, used to, have to) show ability, possibility, necessity, obligation, or permission. 	People should clean up the trash that is polluting our oceans.

	Form and use perfect aspect

Perfect aspect functions to indicate actions that are finished relative to the present or past.
	Oceans currents have greatly affected our weather.
	5
	Form and use perfect progressive aspect
	Students need to know the perfect progressive aspect is formed with a past, present, or future tense of “have” + been + a progressive verb (-ing) to show an action that was ongoing for a period of time leading up to a specific point in time (e.g., has been running, had been eating, will have been fishing). This aspect emphasizes duration in relation to completion or a reference moment, highlighting both the ongoing nature of the action and its connection to another time.
	She has been texting her friend group about this evening's school dance since yesterday.

	Recognize and correct inappropriate tense shifts

	6
	Distinguish between active and passive voice
	Students need to know that active voice emphasizes the subject performing the action, while passive voice emphasizes the subject receiving the action. They also need to know that passive constructions typically use a form of “be” + past participle.
	Active Voice:

Passive Voice:
The posters were designed by the students for Spirit Week.
	7
	Use active and passive voice appropriate to purpose, audience, task
	Students select active or passive voice to match their writing goal (e.g., active for directness, passive for emphasis on the action). Students need to know which form emphasizes the subject or action.
	Active Voice:

Passive Voice:
The championship game was won by our dedicated team after months of practice.
	8
	Use indicative, imperative, and interrogative moods
	Students need to know that verb mood indicates the function or purpose of a sentence. In the indicative mood, verbs express statements or facts. In the imperative mood, verbs give commands or instructions. In the interrogative mood, verbs form questions. Students also need to recognize that verb forms, word order, and auxiliary verbs often signal the mood of a sentence.
	Indicative Mood:
We are taking the bus home from school tomorrow.

Imperative Mood:
Finish your meal before you can have dessert.

Interrogative Mood:
Did you upload your homework to the class website?
	8
	Use conditional and subjunctive moods
	Students need to know that the conditional mood expresses hypothetical situations and possible, but not guaranteed, outcomes by often using if/if-were clauses and modal auxiliaries. The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, demands, or possibilities that are not factual. Students need to recognize that the subjunctive often appears in clauses following verbs expressing desire, recommendation, or uncertainty and that the verb in the subjunctive is usually the base form (i.e., no -s, -ed, or -ing ending). The past subjunctive, especially with the verb to be (e.g., were), can sound unusual or “formal” because it does not follow normal past-tense patterns and is often replaced by was in casual speech; however, using were correctly signals a hypothetical or non-factual situation, which is essential for expressing wishes, contrary-to-fact statements, or imagined scenarios.
	Conditional Mood:
If the weather improves, we will go for a hike tomorrow.

If the data were more complete, the results would be more reliable.

Subjunctive Mood:
Members of the book club wished their next read were available for pick-up.

The committee recommended that the proposal be revised before approval.

Participles act as adjectives: present participles are formed with -ing , and past participles often end in -ed for regular verbs or take an irregular form. Infinitives are formed with to + base verb and can function as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs. Students also need to recognize that verbals allow sentences to vary in structure, provide additional detail, and express actions, qualities, or purposes in different grammatical roles.

and correct dangling modifiers
	Students need to know that a dangling modifier is a descriptive word or phrase that does not clearly modify any word in the sentence, often leaving the sentence confusing or illogical. They need to know that every modifier must have a clear word it describes. To identify dangling modifiers, students should determine the intended subject or main noun for the modifier; if the intended noun is missing or unclear, the modifier is dangling and the sentence needs revision.
	Dangling Modifier:
Walking to school, the bus passed by.

Dangling Modifier Corrected:
While I was walking to school, the bus passed by.

Phrase, Clause, & Cohesion Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use coordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet) connect words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance. They also need to recognize that coordinating conjunctions join ideas without changing the grammatical function of the connected elements.
	She is small but strong!
	1
	Use subordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, if, when, since, while, etc.) connect a dependent clause to an independent clause. They also need to recognize that the dependent clause cannot stand alone and functions to provide time, cause, condition, contrast, or reason.
	I stayed inside because it was storming.

If it snows, we will build a snowman.
	2
	Use prepositional phrases
	Students need to know that a prepositional phrase consists of a preposition and its object (noun or pronoun) and functions as an adjective or adverb. They also need to recognize how prepositional phrases provide information about location, time, direction, cause, or manner.
	The price of toys went up because of the high demand in the store.
	3
	Use correlative conjunctions
	Students need to know that correlative conjunctions work in pairs (e.g., either–or, neither–nor, both–and, not only–but also) to connect balanced elements of equal grammatical importance. They also need to recognize that each part of the pair must attach to similar structures (e.g., noun to noun, clause to clause).
	Both the mountains and the rivers affect where people build cities and farms in the United States.
	5
	Form and use verbal phrases for variety, precision, and nuance
	Students need to know that verbal phrases are built around verbals—gerunds (-ing forms functioning as nouns), participles (present -ing or past -ed/irregular forms functioning as adjectives), and infinitives (to + verb forms functioning as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs). They need to recognize that verbal phrases combine these verbals with other words (modifiers or objects) to create noun, adjective, or adverb phrases and that verbal phrases can appear at the beginning, middle, and end of sentences. Students also need to know that using verbal phrases allows sentences to vary in structure, add descriptive detail, and convey subtle nuances of meaning, such as timing, cause, or purpose and that verbal phrases can be layered with each other for increased sophistication.
	Gerund Phrase (as a noun):
Analyzing primary sources requires questioning assumptions and evaluating context.

Present Participial Phrase (as an adjective):
Clutching the letter in her trembling hands, she realized the truth had arrived too late.

Past Participial Phrase (as an adjective):
Worn by decades of use, the book still held its authority.

Infinitive Phrase (as an adverb):
The city invested in more efficient public transportation to reduce energy consumption.

Infinitive Phrase (as a noun):
To forgive after such betrayal felt impossible.

Layered Verbal Phrases (participial + infinitive):
Hoping to persuade undecided voters, the candidate delivered a speech designed to highlight economic stability.
	9-12
	Use parallel structure to emphasize parity between ideas
	Students need to know that parallel structure means using the same grammatical form for words, phrases, or clauses that are joined or compared. They need to know that elements that can be made parallel include lists of words or phrases, paired ideas connected by coordinating or correlative conjunctions, and clauses within a sentence. Students also need to recognize that maintaining parallel forms ensures clarity, readability, and equal emphasis across ideas in a sentence and that when combined with extended repetition, parallelism lends itself to emphasis of ideas.
	Parallel Verbs in a List:
The researcher collected, analyzed, and interpreted the data.

Parallel Gerund Phrases in a List:
Effective study habits include reviewing notes regularly, completing practice problems, and seeking feedback.

Parallel Infinitive Phrases in a List:
She wanted to leave quietly, to avoid conflict, and to preserve her dignity.

Parallel Structure with Correlative Conjunctions:
The policy aims not only to reduce costs but also to improve accessibility.

Parallel Dependent Clauses:
When the evidence is examined carefully and when the context is considered honestly, the argument becomes more convincing.
	9-12

Comma Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use commas in dates
	Students need to know that a comma separates the day and year in full dates.
	We moved on June 26, 2023.
	1
	Use commas to separate words in a series
	Students need to know that commas separate three or more items in a list.
	My favorite colors are pink, green, and purple.
	1
	Use commas before coordinating conjunctions in compound sentences
	Students need to know that a comma comes before coordinating conjunctions (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) when joining two independent clauses.
	I wanted to play outside, but it started to rain.
	1
	Use commas in addresses
	Students need to know that in addresses written in sentences, commas separate the street address, the city, and the state. When addresses are written on separate lines, a comma is generally only needed between the city and state.
	The birthday party is at 123 Happy Place, Cary, North Carolina.

123 Happy Place
Cary, North Carolina

	Use commas in greetings and closings
	Students need to know that commas follow the greeting or closing in letters, emails, cards, etc.
	Example Greeting:

Example Closing:
Love, Mary
	2
	Use commas in dialogue
	Students need to know that commas separate the words of a speaker from dialogue tags.
	Continuous Dialogue: Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.

Interrupted Dialogue Response:  “I knew you could do it,” said Marcus, “because you practice harder than anyone else.”
	3
	Use commas to indicate direct address
	Students need to know that commas set off names or titles of people being spoken to. When direct addresses are at the beginning of a sentence, the comma comes after them. When direct addresses are at the end of a sentence, the comma comes before.
	John, I agree with what you said about the photograph helping readers understand deforestation better.

I agree with what you said about the photograph helping readers understand deforestation better, John.
	4
	Use commas to set off “yes” and “no”
	Students need to know that commas follow introductory yes or no in a sentence.
	Yes, I do believe that I included enough supporting evidence.
	4
	Use commas to indicate direct speech and quotations
	Students need to know that commas separate quoted material from the rest of the sentence unless the quoted material is integrated into the grammatical structure of the sentence. When quoted material concludes a sentence, a comma comes only before the opening quotation mark. When quoted material is introduced mid-sentence, a comma comes before the opening quotation mark and before the closing quotation mark.
	Concluding With a Quote (quoting a complete sentence): The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting a complete sentence): The author states, “Ocean currents move warm and cold water around the Earth,” to explain how they affect climate.

Integrating Quote into Grammatical Structure (quoting an incomplete sentence): The author explains that ocean currents are “powerful forces that shape Earth’s climate” and affect weather patterns around the world.
	5
	Use commas after introductory elements
	Students need to know that commas follow introductory words, phrases, or clauses.
	Single Word:
Finally, I believe that students should have longer recess because research shows that it leads to better mental health.

Prepositional Phrases as an Introductory Element:
In the second paragraph of the article, the author provides evidence that recycling helps reduce pollution.

Complex Sentence (When Dependent Clause is First):
Because of the growing list of grievances, the colonists decided to declare independence.
	5
	Use commas to set off tag questions
	Students need to know that commas separate a statement from a short question at the end of a sentence.
	The evidence for this is stated in the first paragraph, isn’t it?
	5
	Use commas in compound- complex sentences
	Students need to know that commas help separate clauses in sentences with multiple independent and dependent clauses for clarity.
	When I finished my homework, I texted my friends because I wanted to relax, and we planned our gaming session.
	6
	Use commas to separate coordinate adjectives
	Students need to know that commas separate two or more adjectives that equally modify a noun.
	She bought a trendy, oversized hoodie at the mall.
	7
	Use commas for nonrestrictive/parenthetical elements
	Students need to know that commas can set off extra information or details that are not essential to the core meaning of the sentence. This extra information often appears in recognizable structures, such as appositives, nonrestrictive relative clauses, and nonessential participial phrases.
	My best friend, who sits next to me in math class, helped me study for the test.

	Example Sentence
	Grade

Semicolons & Colons Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use semicolons to separate items in a series when items include internal commas
	Students need to know that semicolons separate complex list items that already contain commas to preserve clarity.
	For our day at the park, I packed snacks, including chips and candy; Ava made the playlists, selecting music we each like; and Leo brought the games, hoping to keep us all entertained.

I have lived in Omaha, Nebraska; Litchfield, Connecticut; and Mason City, Iowa.
	8
	Use semicolons with conjunctive adverbs or transitional phrases to link independent clauses
	Students need to know that semicolons can join two independent clauses, providing an additional way to connect complete ideas. This is especially useful when the clauses already contain internal commas or when using conjunctive adverbs (e.g., however, therefore, nevertheless, nonetheless, consequently, furthermore, moreover) or transitional phrases (e.g.,as a result, for this reason, in light of this, in contrast, on the other hand). When a conjunctive adverb or transitional phrase is used, the semicolon comes before it, and a comma always follows it, helping to maintain clarity in complex sentences.
	Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs, No Internal Punctuation:
The novel explores themes of ambition and betrayal; however, the protagonist’s personal growth remains central to the story.

Researchers have studied the effects of social media on teenagers, examining its impact on mental health, including anxiety and self-esteem, its role in shaping peer relationships and social norms, and its influence on academic motivation and time management; therefore, they argue that schools and families should guide responsible use while encouraging balance and critical thinking.

She completed the research experiment successfully; in the meantime, her classmates continued testing alternative hypotheses.

	Students need to know that sometimes a semicolon can be used with a coordinating conjunction to join two sentences together and preserve clarity when the sentences are long and contain additional internal punctuation. They also need to know that this is an uncommon construction and is most helpful when one or both sentences already include several commas (e.g., complex sentences, series of phrases, etc.). Students should recognize that in this construction, the semicolon comes before the coordinating conjunction, and no additional comma is needed immediately after it.
	Semicolons with Coordinating Conjunctions, Internal Punctuation:
Since he speaks French, Spanish, and Italian, he assumed he would have no trouble traveling in southern Europe, especially since many people in the region now speak English; but when he arrived in Sicily, his unfamiliarity with the Sicilian dialect made communication difficult.

The treaty established new boundaries, clarified trade agreements, and outlined military obligations; and although it was celebrated in the capitals, many local leaders opposed its terms.
