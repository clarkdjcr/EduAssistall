# Arts Education - Beginning

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Beginning
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Skills progress at different levels due to a number of factors, including continuity and quality of instruction; informal exposure to Dance through community interaction; opportunities to access Dance programs in K-12 education; student developmental level and ability; motivation and resources available for individual practice; etc. Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a students’ choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed Dance educator. Credit earned from middle and high school courses meeting these minimums satisfies the Arts Education Graduation requirement and applies toward the Arts Education Diploma Endorsement. Courses that do not meet these minimums are not designed to build proficiency and are often used to spark interest in more intensive proficiency-based study later in a student’s education.
To account for variations in skill progression, as well as to embed multiple entry points across the K-12 spectrum, the objectives in the high school NCSCOS for Dance are organized by proficiency level, rather than grade level. Minimum proficiency outcomes are provided at each level to show what a student should know and be able to do by the end of a course or program when they create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance. Beginning High School standards are designed for students with no or limited K-8 progression in Dance education.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

B.CN.1.1 Describe how the emergence of dance as an art form has influenced society.
Glossary
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Society - An enduring and cooperating social group whose members have developed organized patterns of relationships through interaction with one another; a community, nation, or broad grouping of people having common traditions, institutions, and collective activities and interests.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective begins the EQ-throughline at the foundational level for high school aged students. The beginning level is designed for students without a K-8 dance continuum. Students with a K-8 continuum in dance should progress to the intermediate level. In the current level, students describe how the emergence of dance as an art form has influenced society. In the next level, students will explain the impact societal, cultural, and historical events have on dance trends, styles, and dancers.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The difference between dance as an art form and social dance
* How dance has evolved as an art form over time
* How artists use dance to communicate ideas about society and culture
* How the emergence of dance as an art form was influential in shaping the ideas, beliefs, and daily life practices of individuals and groups in society
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of.how the emergence of dance as an artform influenced society
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Dance can be influenced by society and dance can also influence society
* Society can be changed and affected by artists and their art
* Art plays an integral role in shaping history as it reflects society, culture, and community perspectives on key issues and human experiences
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Present historic information on how dance emerged as an art form
* Provide examples of how the emergence of dance as an art form has influenced society
* Guide student investigation into how the emergence of dance as an art form influenced society
* Design learning activities for students to describe how the emergence of dance as an art form has influenced society
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to describe how the emergence of dance as an art form has influenced society
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* The early beginnings of dance during the Renaissance Period
* The transformation from dance in court dances and town celebrations to the proscenium stage
* The evolution of dance as an art form across Europe in the Romantic Age
* The emergence of modern dance in the 1900’s
* The influence of cultural dance forms on the art of dance (ie.jazz, tap, non-western cultural dance forms)
* The emergence of various cultural styles and genres of dance as art
* The emergence of dance as an art on stage and film
* The historic context of Modern Dance, African Dance, Ballet, Court Dancing, Social Dance, Asian Dance styles, and Dance in media

B.CR.1.1 Improvise extended movement sequences using a movement score.
Glossary
* Dance Sequence - A brief sequence of movements that are not necessarily ordered into a formal choreographic structure that communicates artistic intent but rather demonstrates a solution to a movement problem. A movement sequence could contain as few as two or movements.29
* Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14
* Movement Score - A written notation of a musical or dance composition, which allows the work to be performed at a later date or by another performer.16
Vertical Alignment
This objective begins the EQ-throughline at the foundational level for high school aged students. The beginning level is designed for students without a K-8 dance continuum. Students with a K-8 continuum in dance should progress to the intermediate level. At the current level, students use a movement score to improvise extended movement sequences. In the next level, students will improvise extended movement sequences using a variety of sources as inspiration.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of dancers and choreographers to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to create spontaneous movement phrases of significant length
* How to interpret a movement score
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating spontaneous movement phrases of significant lengths through improvisation using a movement score
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This may be a student’s first time experiencing movement improvisation in a structured dance activity. Remind students of expectations for safety and validate that there are endless possibilities for movement invention when improvising
* The elements of dance can be used to inspire and improvise movement
* Movement scores are "outlines" for the structure to be used in guiding the flow of improvisation. Basing improvisation off of movement scores keeps the student centered on exploring specific elements or concepts that exist in body movement. This helps to avoid the random strung together performance of pre-known "dance steps" or "tricks" but instead encourages a more organic exploration of movement possibilities
* The objective does not specify who created the movement score, therefore, it can be created by the teacher or in their collaboration with the class, student-generated, or peer-generated
* Scores can be individually interpreted based on personal preference to the prompt
* This objective can be paired with PR & RE objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain what it means to improv
* Teach how to use various cues and the elements of dance when improvising
* Create and share examples of movement scores with students (can also work with students on designing a movement score as a class or have students create their own scores)
* Demonstrate and call on student volunteers to show movement improvisation using extended movement sequences to fulfill a movement score
* Guide students through movement improvisation exercises using a movement score to improvise extended movement sequences
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to improvise extended movement sequences using a movement score
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Teacher-created scores that include exploration of elements of dance. The scores can be a series of different instruction sets so that students have practice exploring the set scores while varying the focus on the elements
* Advanced students may be expected to create their own score varying the elements of dance and swapping with a partner to improvise movement
* Students may be put into small groups to individually perform the improvised score for group members and then have a discussion about how each individual differently interpreted the score based on personal preference

Use a checklist to ensure all students are successful in completing this objective for both original and abstract ideas using multiple variations of dance elements
* Teachers can turn this into a game by identifying (or working as a class to identify) multiple ideas for abstraction and dance elements on two piles of index cards and then have students randomly draw from the piles. Give students time to create and practice original and abstract movements that vary the use of dance elements. Then, in charades-like fashion the class must guess the idea and focus components of the element performed by the student

Key Insights
* Students must understand that different roles, settings, and cultural contexts have varying expectations for dance participants, performers, and audience members. It is suggested that students be exposed to dance in a variety of roles, settings, cultural contexts that may have specified expectations
* Roles are inclusive of participant, performer, and observer
* This objective is one that is taught and assessed frequently, beginning with classroom behavior while the teacher is speaking, when students are dancing, and when observing informal classroom performances of others performing
* Examples of varying appropriate respectful behaviors based on cultural context include the vocal calling during competitions and pep rally performances versus only applauding when appropriate when watching a professional ballet or modern dance group
* This objective can be paired with other PR and RE objectives as students perform and observe dance
* Safe practices as dancers includes taking considerations of the space and people they are dancing with. Respectful behaviors can include giving full attention to those who are performing
* Note the conjunction “and” used twice in this objective - students need to show their ability to demonstrate safe AND respectful behaviors for a variety of dance roles, settings, AND cultural contexts. Teacher Actions
* Model appropriate behaviors in a variety of settings and cultural contexts when applicable
* Provide examples of the multiple settings in which dance can be performed and observed
* Provide examples of the appropriate behaviors for the multiple roles in which an individual can participate in dance (performer, choreographer, observer, etc)
* Lead classroom discussion on what it means to use safe and respectful behaviors and how behaviors can vary based on the roles, cultural context, and performance setting
* Aid students in identifying what safe and respectful behaviors are for various roles, dance settings and cultural contexts
* Use positive behavior support to identify behavioral expectations students should exhibit when participating, performing, and observing dances in various roles, settings and cultural contexts and praise exemplary behaviors demonstrated by students
* Provide opportunities for students to demonstrate safe and respectful behaviors for a variety of dance roles, settings, and cultural contexts
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to demonstrate safe and respectful behaviors for a variety of dance roles, settings, and cultural contexts
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* As this may be the student’s first time participating in dance program activities and learning about participant, performer and audience etiquette, it is important for them to understand common rules and expectations for informal and formal presentations including
   * Use focus when dancing
   * Respect for the presenter and other audience members. Stay quiet and seated, eyes on the presenter, applaud when appropriate
   * How to enter and exit performance spaces
   * How to safely move backstage during rehearsals and performances, staying quiet and out the way of other dancers and tech, not loitering in the wings, how to use the drapery to hide before making entrances and exits without hitting it or running into side lights or other production elements, etc
   * How to organize costumes and personal belonging in dressing room spaces
* Additional behavioral expectations will be determined by the cultural context of the performance. Have students identify culturally appropriate behaviors for performers and observers prior to participation. Remind students of behaviors before the event. The teacher can observe student behaviors or behaviors can be monitored through self-evaluation or peer observation
