# Arts Education - Fifth Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Fifth Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 Dance standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed Visual Arts educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

5.CN.1.1 Describe styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States.
Glossary
* Culture - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
* Customs - A usage or practice common to many or to a particular place or class or habitual with an individual; the whole body of usages, practices, or conventions that regulate social life.14
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Indigenous - Of or relating to the earliest known inhabitants of a place and especially of a place that was colonized by a now-dominant group.14
* Styles - Specific movement characteristics, qualities, or principles that give it distinctive identity; style may also refer to the unique artistic choices of a particular [artist].17
* Traditions - Pattern of practices and beliefs within a societal group.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students described styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina and now expand their learning to describe the influence across the United States. At the next level, students explain how dance expresses and reflects the values of civilizations around the world.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The customs and traditions of Indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States
* Dance Artists who have influenced the cultural customs or traditions of dance in the United States
* Dance Artists who have been influenced by the customs or traditions of cultural or Indigenous groups of in the United States
* Styles of dance that reflect customs and traditions of various cultural groups in the United States
* How dance styles may have shaped customs and traditions of Indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of how dance styles or the work of dance artists has influenced or been influenced by Indigenous and cultural groups in the United States
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Many popular dance styles and artists are influenced by cultural dances
* Expose students to cultural diversity of dance in the United States
* Artists and artistic styles can have an impact on the traditions and customs of cultural groups
* Note the conjunctions “and” and “or” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to describe styles AND dancers who have influenced, OR been influenced by, the customs AND traditions of indigenous AND other cultural groups in the United States. Depending on the learning activity, the teacher can decide if students are examining styles and dancers who have influenced or been influenced by. However, both styles and dancers as well as the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States must be considered. Given the scope of this objective, it may be helpful to break this objective down into multiple assessments over the course of study
* Collaborate with the Social Studies teacher to learn of units the students are studying
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Research and provide examples of cultural and indigenous dance styles in the United States
* Provide examples of artists and styles that have impacted or been impacted by customs and cultural traditions of Indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States
* Model describing styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States
* Guide student investigation into artists and styles that have influenced or been influenced by cultural customs and traditions of diverse cultural groups in the United States
* Design learning activities for students to summarize the characteristics of the styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to describe styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Contemporary or social dance that reflects indigenous movement traditions seen in Powwows, Hoop Dancing, the Grass Dance
* Artists such as Ruth St. Denis, Helen Tamiris, Lester Horton, Anna Sokolow, Alvin Ailey, Camille A. Brown, etc.
* Square Dancing was influenced by European settlers doing reels and quadrilles
* Step Dance (common at HBCUs) was influenced by traditional African dance

* Use the dance element space to explore pathways and levels that may also be used in a theatre production or effort qualities to express emotions and other character actions in theatrical and dance performance
* Analyze a piece of music for its form and create a dance sequence that mirrors the same form
* Analyzing the elements of art in famous works, using the analysis to create dance movements to show overlapping concepts

Glossary

* Psychological challenges that impact dancers
* Anatomical challenges that impact dance performance
* Strategies to improve anatomical challenges and address psychological challenges common to dancers
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Putting into practice/action appropriate strategies to address anatomical and psychological challenges encountered by dancers
Evidence of Mastery

Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students apply what they’ve learned rather than just demonstrate what they’ve learned
* Students use higher order thinking skills to find ways to address dance-related challenges
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to apply strategies to address both anatomical AND psychological challenges encountered by dancers. Depending on the learning activity, students may demonstrate strategies to meet both types of challenges in one assessment or strategies to address anatomical challenges can be assessed separately from strategies to address psychological challenges
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Activate prior student knowledge and lead classroom discussion of strategies that can be used to address anatomical and psychological challenges common to dancers
* Remind students of mobility and function approaches to address anatomical challenges in their own body
* Provide discussion opportunities for students to explore psychological challenges related to dance and identify coping strategies so that students can practice applying these strategies in their real life situations
* Support student use of strategies such as journaling and meditation, to address dance-related psychological challenges
* Guide student exploration of strategies to address anatomical and psychological challenges in dance
* Design learning activities for students to apply strategies to address anatomical and psychological challenges dancers face when creating and performing dance
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to apply strategies to address anatomical and psychological challenges encountered by dancers
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Learning about their own range of motion and how to apply those strategies to their own anatomical challenges
* Students can work in partners to help each other find strategies to address their partner’s anatomical challenges using the activities and movements
* Exercises for flexibility and mobility of the joints
* Strength and technique exercises for function of large muscle groups
* Using modifications
* Daily journaling practices reflecting dance training and creative practices
* Opportunities to expressively create movement from personal thoughts, opinions, etc.
* Using body, mind, centering techniques such as yoga, meditation, breathing exercises, positive visualization

5.CR.1.1 Improvise movement to fulfill a simple movement score using one or more dance elements.
Glossary
* Dance element - The key components of movement; movement of the body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement; see Elements of Dance Organizer by Perpich Center for Arts Education (used with permission).17
* Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14
* Movement Score - A written notation of a musical or dance composition, which allows the work to be performed at a later date or by another performer.16
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students improvised movement based on multiple dance elements and now use one or more dance elements in their improvisation to fulfill a simple movement score. In the next level, students will improvise movement using multiple dance elements in response to musical or verbal cues.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of dancers and choreographers to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The basic dance elements and their components
* How to create spontaneous movement
* How to interpret a simple movement score
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating spontaneous movement through improvisation that explores one or more of the basic dance element concepts in fulfillment of a simple movement score
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* The elements of dance can be used to inspire and improvise movement
* Movement scores are "outlines" for the structure to be used in guiding the flow of improvisation. Basing improvisation off of movement scores keeps the student centered on exploring specific elements or concepts that exist in body movement. This helps to avoid the random strung together performance of pre-known "dance steps" or "tricks" but instead encourages a more organic exploration of movement possibilities
* The objective does not specify who created the movement score, therefore, it can be created by the teacher or in their collaboration with the class, student-generated, or peer-generated
* Scores can be individually interpreted based on personal preference to the prompt
* Note the conjunction “or” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to  improvise movement to fulfill a simple movement score using one OR more dance elements. It is up to the discretion of the teacher whether the score requires using one or more elements. Depending on the activity, autonomy can also be given to the student to decide
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain what it means to improv
* Teach and review how to use various cues and the elements of dance when improvising
* Create and share examples of movement scores with students (can also work with students on designing a movement score as a class or have students create their own scores)
* Demonstrate and call on student volunteers to show movement improvisation based on simple movement score using one or more dance elements
* Guide students through movement improvisation exercises using one or more dance elements to fulfill a simple movement score
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to improvise movement to fulfill a simple movement score using one or more dance elements
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Teacher-created scores that include one or more elements of dance. The scores can be a series of different instruction sets so that students have practice exploring the set scores while varying the focus on the elements
* Advanced students may be expected to create their own score varying the elements of dance and swapping with a partner to improvise movement
* Students may be put into small groups to individually perform the improvised score for group members and then have a discussion about how each individual differently interpreted the score based on personal preference

5.CR.1.2 Create short dances using basic choreographic forms that communicate abstract ideas.
Glossary
* Abstraction - To remove movement from a particular or representative context and, by manipulating it through the elements of space, time, and energy, to create a new sequence or dance that retains the essence of the original.18
* Choreographic Form - The organization of choreography and movement to fulfill the artistic intent of a dance or dance study (for example, AB, ABA, or theme and variation); often referred to as choreographic form.2
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
* Ideas - An entity (such as a thought, concept, sensation, or image) actually or potentially present to consciousness.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students created dance phrases incorporating abstraction of everyday movement to fulfill a given choreographic form and now create short dances using basic choreographic forms that communicate abstract ideas. In the next level, students will create a short dance or dance sequence using abstracted movement that has a beginning, middle, and end.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are actualized into choreography by dancers and choreographers to intentionally fulfill specific purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to abstract ideas into movement
* How to use a basic choreographic form in the creation of a dance phrase (AB, ABA, Rondo, Natural Form, Narrative, Collage)
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Conceiving new short dances that communicate abstract ideas and use a choreographic form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Choreography is created through purposeful choices by the artist
* Abstraction can include altering the time, space, dynamics, and/or body shapes of actions of people, animals, or things that move. Abstraction of ideas can also come from images, objects, feelings, or other concepts
* Students can identify their own ideas to be communicated through abstraction or the ideas can be assigned to them
* Use interdisciplinary approaches to strengthen learning of concepts from other subject areas
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain the concept of abstraction and demonstrate how various concepts can be abstracted in choreography
* Explain how different choreographic forms are used when creating choreography (AB, ABA, Rondo, Natural Form, etc)
* Provide practice for all students to learn and perform short dances that use different choreographic forms and communicate abstract ideas
* Select a choreographic form for students to use (AB, ABA, Rondo, Natural Form, etc) or have students select a form to use in their choreography
* Design learning activities in which students can use basic choreographic forms to create short dances that communicate abstract ideas
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to create short dances using basic choreographic forms that communicate abstract ideas
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Using choreographic forms such as AB, ABA, ABCD (natural form), ABACAD (Rondo) have students create short dances about things they are familiar with or learning about in other subjects. Ideas could include science concepts or everyday situations such as a dance about getting ready for school
* Teachers can turn this into a game by identifying (or working as a class to identify) multiple ideas and choreographic forms on two piles of index cards and then have students randomly draw from the piles. Give students time to create and practice then, in charades-like fashion the class must guess the idea and choreographic form performed by the student

5.CR.1.3 Depict the movement qualities in a dance phrase by drawing a picture, writing, or using symbols.
Glossary
* Dance Phrase - A sequence of movements that are ordered based upon a preconceived choreographic intent and contains a clear beginning, middle, and end that aligns with artistic intent. A movement phrase could be compared to a paragraph of movement and would contain two or more movement sentences.29
* Depict - To represent or show something.4
* Movement Qualities - The essential nature and quantity of energy expended in a movement; its force or strengthened feeling; the intention toward the movement; the shadings in the amount of energy, intensity, or power; subtle variations in treatment of movement contrasts. In some models, this dance element is referred to as dynamics or efforts.29
* Symbol - An image, sign, or element, such as color, that is understood, by convention or context, to suggest some other meaning.22
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students depicted the spatial relationships between two or more dancers in a dance phrase by drawing a picture or using symbols and now use a picture, writing, or symbols to depict the movement qualities in a dance phrase. In the next level, students will plan an original dance sequence using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are recorded by dancers and choreographers through notation and symbolic documentation.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The movement qualities used in dance phrases
* How to visually represent movement qualities
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Representing/Showing movement qualities through written or symbolic notation
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. * The elements of dance
* How to analyze a choreographic work using the elements of dance
* How to identify the elements of dance in choreography
* How to linguistically discuss choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming the elements of dance observed in a choreographic work
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Having a video recording to observe will help facilitate conversation as it can be rewound and paused when discussing. A recording would also provide the opportunity for the student to watch it several times to help develop their analytical skills
* This is a good objective to use with different dance styles and genres to show how the elements are commonly used in choreographed works
* Note this objective specified “observation”  - whereas in earlier grades students might analyze movement phrases they are also learning and performing, it now shifts to specifically mention students’ observation of movement. The choreographic works can still be informal performances by peers in classroom activity but it is encouraged students be exposed to other professional works for analysis
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom instruction on the elements of dance
* Demonstrate identifying elements of dance in observation of choreographic works
* Provide examples of choreographic works for observation or physical embodiment (these can be generated by the teacher, student, or professionally created and performed)
* Guide students on how to observe dance for evidence of the dance elements of body, space, time, and energy/effort
* Design activities for students to identify the elements of dance observed in a choreographic work
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to identify the elements of dance observed in a choreographic work
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Students can observe a dance performed by other students or artists. The students then identify where they observed dance elements in the choreography. This can be done verbally or through a written critique. Students can also be provided with sentence stems such as “I saw use of pathways when…..” or “Examples of spatial relationships included…..”. The students can also be provided wordbanks to support their responses

5.RE.1.2 Describe alternative meanings that could be interpreted from the same choreographic work.
Glossary
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Interpret - To explain or tell the meaning of: present in understandable terms.14
* Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students compared a personal interpretation of a dance with the interpretation of peers and now describe alternative meanings that could be interpreted from the same choreographic work. In the next level, students identify multiple meanings for choreographic works, citing evidence.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer’s interpretation of a work may align with or differ from the artist’s original intent.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How meaning can be visually represented through dance movements or choreographic works
* That there can be multiple interpretations of the same artistic work
* How to analyze the artistic choices used in dance movements or choreographic work
* How to interpret meaning from artistic choices made in dance
* How to linguistically describe dance
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of interpretations of alternative meanings from the same choreographed work
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective teaches students that there are often multiple interpretations of art and not just “one” correct answer. Encourage divergent thinking in viewing dance through multiple lenses
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how dance can communicate meaning
* Demonstrate how to interpret multiple meanings from choreography that is either performed or observed (be mindful not to impart bias but allow students to have differing perspectives)
* Provide examples of age-appropriate choreography for observation or physical embodiment that can be interpreted for multiple meanings (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work)
* Guide students on how to interpret meaning from dance
* Design activities for students to describe alternative meanings that could be interpreted from the same choreographic work
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to describe alternative meanings that could be interpreted from the same choreographic work
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Students could be paired with others who have differing interpretations of works and have to work together to prepare a brief presentation in which describe the multiple interpretations of the work
* Have students give their personal interpretation of a work then challenge them to think differently when watching it again to make a different interpretation
* Have students watch a work they know little about and document their interpretation. The teacher then shares the artist’s intent and context of the work and has the students watch the work a second time through that lens. Students can then describe their multiple interpretations in a written or recorded assignment, or pair-share discussion

5.RE.2.1 Identify aspects of a dance that align and contrast with personal preference.
Glossary
* Align - To be in or come into precise adjustment or correct relative position.14
* Contrast - Compare or appraise in respect to differences.14
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students contrasted personal preferences for performing and viewing dance with those of peers and now identify aspects of a dance that align and contrast with personal preference. In the next level, students will explain how personal preferences for dance develop with increased exposure.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with dance works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to identify and describe personal preferences when observing or performing dance
* Which aspects of a dance align with personal preferences
* Which aspects of a dance contrast with personal preferences
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming aspects of a dance that are aligned with and differ from personal preferences
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective asks students to look at the aligning and differing of preferences found in one artistic work. This promotes the realization that an individual will likely find aspects of an artistic work both pleasing and displeasing
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - the student needs to demonstrate mastery of their ability to identify aspects of a dance that align AND contrast with personal preference. Both must be considered
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how preferences in dance can vary based on the individual and can range within the scope of one artistic work
* Provide examples of dance in a wide array of styles, forms, or genres for observation and physical embodiment (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work)
* Guide student discovery of preferences when observing and performing dance
* Design activities for students to identify likes and dislikes of various aspects of a dance
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to identify aspects of a dance that align and contrast with personal preference
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Use a two-column graphic organizer to identify aspects of a work that align with and differ from their personal preferences. This could include production elements, the composition of the work, the style, the way in which a dancer performed it, etc

5.RE.2.2 Generate criteria to provide feedback for self and others.
Glossary
* Criteria - The rules or guidelines used for categorizing or judging; in arts assessment, the rules or guidelines used to judge the quality of a student’s performance.28
* Feedback - A reaction or response to a particular design problem or design solution.29
* Generate - To create by means of a defined process : produce.14
* Provide - To supply or make available.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students evaluated dances using established criteria to provide feedback for self and others and now generate criteria to provide self and peer feedback. In the next level, students identify strengths and areas for improvement based on established criteria when viewing dance performance and choreography.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to generate criteria to self-evaluate dance
* How to generate criteria to evaluate dances of peers
* How to use specific criteria to generate feedback for self and others
* How to linguistically discuss movement performance and choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating/Producing criteria for evaluation of and provision of feedback on the work of self and others
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students understand that they can use feedback to improve and also give feedback to help someone else improve. It is important to identify strengths when evaluating work so students build efficacy and can continue to build on their success. Identifying areas for growth is an important aspect of constructive criticism. Students may need support in understanding the positive intention of constructive criticism
* It is important that students are guided to develop criteria that is relevant to the work being evaluated
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to generate criteria to provide self AND peer feedback. Feedback needs to be given to both self and others
* This objective can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students integrate personal goals and instructor and peer feedback to improve dance performance
* This objective can also be paired with other CR and PR objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how relevant criteria used in evaluation helps dancers to improve their skills
* Demonstrate how to generate and use criteria to evaluate students in performance
* Brainstorm with students in identifying relevant criteria to be used in evaluation of a demonstration of dance skills, choreography, or performance
* Guide students in crafting a rubric that has descriptive performance ratings for the criteria that is most essential to the dance being observed
* Design activities for students to generate criteria to provide self and peer feedback. The evaluation can be of demonstration of technical skills, choreography, or in performance of teacher or student-generated work
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to generate criteria to provide self and peer feedback
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students examine the essential elements of the work that is to be evaluated. For example, in pairing this objective with CR.1.1 students would identify if one or more dance elements were used in improvisation to fulfill a movement score, or if using CR.1.2, students would look for the use of choreographic form and communication of meaning. If using PR.1.3 students’ criteria would include performance focus. Have students use rubrics to evaluate dances they create and/or perform to generate feedback for self and others
* Use video to record dances so students can watch themselves or refer back to the video to evaluate the work of their peers
