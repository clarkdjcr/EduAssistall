# Arts Education - Beginning

Parent Guide for Beginning Dance

The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a student’s choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed dance educator.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students explore the influence of dance on society and apply concepts from other disciplines when creating and performing dance. Students also identify activities that promote physical and mental wellness in dance.
CN.2 Explore - Students explain how creativity prepares them for college and career choices and explore developments in technology and contemporary media used in dance creation and production.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students explore spontaneous movement invention in longer dance sequences in response to prompts from movement score and learn to use a variety of dance elements in original and abstract movement. Students also use pictures, writing, symbols, or media technology to plan a dance phrase.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to adapt dance phrases by changing multiple elements and also learn legal terminology related to copyright protection of dance, including the musical accompaniment.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Perform - Students make comparisons of feedback from instructors, peers, and self-assessment to improve personal and group performance and perform movement skills with strength, anatomical alignment, flexibility, stamina, coordination, and physical awareness. Students also demonstrate facial expressions, movement quality, and full-body energy when dancing.
PR.2 Develop - Students identify criteria in selection of a theme, topic, or music choices for a class performance and demonstrate safe and respectful behaviors for a variety of dance roles, settings, and cultural contexts. Students also investigate the production elements that would strengthen the meaning of a dance as it is performed in various situations.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students examine the differences of elements and choreographic structures used in different dance techniques and use the historical and cultural background of a dance or style to make interpretations of a work.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students examine how personal and cultural influences shape the way dance is viewed and evaluated, and use criteria to provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography.

How to Support Your Dance Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your dance student:
* Clear a little space so your child can move freely and safely.
* Encourage your child to create dances about concepts they are learning in other classes.
* Encourage stretching, posture exercises, mindfulness, nutrition, hydration, and rest to protect their physical and mental health.
* Discuss how creativity in dance relates to college and career skills.
* Explore videos, apps, or online tools that help create, record, or share dance and talk about how technology has changed performance and choreography.
* Encourage your child to make up movement sequences using a set of guidelines or ideas for movement that includes abstract movement, dynamics, levels, and shapes.
* Have your child draw, write, or use media technology to plan ideas for dance phrases.
* Talk about how to respectfully use inspiration from other dancers and music while understanding copyright, fair use, and Creative Commons rules.
* Encourage reflection on self, peer, and instructor feedback.
* Support flexibility,  balance, coordination, alignment, and endurance through simple home exercises.
* Encourage using facial expressions, energy, and movement clarity when performing.
* Talk about safe and respectful behavior when performing or observing dance.
* Watch dance from various genres and discuss differences in how costumes, props, or lighting were used to enhance the performance.
* Discuss possible interpretations of dances.
