# Science - Chemistry

Chemistry: 2023 Support Document
Chemistry
Purpose:
The 2023 North Carolina K-12 Science Standards were officially approved by the State Board of Education on July 6, 2023. The Standard Course of Study is designed to help students continually grow their science knowledge and abilities. The standards are intended to foster conceptual understanding, develop scientifically literate students, and provide opportunities to build knowledge and practices within each grade band or course.

The K-12 Science Support Documents serve as resources for teachers, administrators, science specialists, instructional coaches, parents, and other stakeholders in understanding the standards and planning instruction. They do not serve as curricula nor as a means to limit instruction in the classroom, but rather are intended to serve as a guide for discerning and describing features of students and their work necessary to meet grade level proficiency.

Science Teaching and Learning:
A coherent and consistent approach throughout grades K-12 is key to realizing the vision for science education embodied in A Framework for K-12 Science Education.  Students should actively engage in science and engineering practices and apply crosscutting concepts in every grade level to deepen their understanding of each field’s disciplinary core ideas.

Science Safety:
It is the responsibility of teachers and school administrators to use appropriate legal standards and best professional practices under duty of care to make the science laboratory safe.  Follow the Public School Unit’s guidelines regarding safety in the classroom. For more information, review the National Science Teaching Association’s position statement on safety and school science instruction.

	Dimension 1: Science and Engineering Practice (SEP)
[Science and Engineering Practice included in Objective]
Practices refer to the things that scientists and engineers do and how they actively engage in their work. This section provides further clarification for what students should be doing to engage in this practice. There are various ways that each practice can be used, as articulated in A Framework for K-12 Science Education (NRC, 2012) and the NSTA’s SEP Matrix.

The Framework identifies a small number of disciplinary core ideas that all students should learn with increasing depth and sophistication, from Kindergarten through grade twelve. Key to the vision expressed in the Framework is for students to learn these disciplinary core ideas in the context of science and engineering practices. The importance of combining science and engineering practices and disciplinary core ideas is stated in the Framework as follows: Standards and performance expectations that are aligned to the framework must take into account that students cannot fully understand scientific and engineering ideas without engaging in the practices of inquiry and the discourses by which such ideas are developed and refined. At the same time, they cannot learn or show competence in practices except in the context of specific content (NRC Framework, 2012, p. 218).
The eight practices of science and engineering that the Framework identifies as essential for all students to learn and describes in detail are listed below:
1. Asking questions (for science) and defining problems (for engineering)
2. Developing and using models
3. Planning and carrying out investigations
4. Analyzing and interpreting data
5. Using mathematics and computational thinking
6. Constructing explanations (for science) and designing solutions (for engineering)
7. Engaging in argument from evidence
8. Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information
	Dimension 2: Crosscutting Concepts (CCC)
Crosscutting concepts are conceptual tools used along with the SEP and DCI to help students explain and predict science phenomena. They support students in making connections between disciplines and provide a context for sense-making. Teachers should encourage students to frame their thinking around the terminology of the CCC through questions and classroom discussions.
The most relevant Crosscutting Concepts may vary in light of the specific learning objectives and how the teacher structures the topic. Teachers should choose the one(s) that best support(s) their instructional goals and help(s) students make connections across different aspects of the content.
Suggested Crosscutting Concept(s): [Crosscutting Concept Name(s): A Framework for K-12 Science Education identifies seven crosscutting concepts that bridge disciplinary boundaries and unite core ideas. Their purpose is to help students deepen their understanding of the disciplinary core ideas and develop a coherent and scientifically based view of the world (p. 83).
The seven crosscutting concepts of the Framework are:
1. Patterns - Observed patterns of forms and events guide organization and classification, and they prompt questions about relationships and the factors that influence them.
2. Cause and effect: Mechanism and explanation - Events have causes, sometimes simple, sometimes multifaceted. A major activity of science is investigating and explaining causal relationships and the mechanisms by which they are mediated. Such mechanisms can then be tested across given contexts and used to predict and explain events in new contexts.
3. Scale, proportion, and quantity - In considering phenomena, it is critical to recognize what is relevant at different measures of size, time, and energy and to recognize how changes in scale, proportion, or quantity affect a system’s structure or performance.
4. Systems and system models - Defining the system under study—specifying its boundaries and making explicit a model of that system—provides tools for understanding and testing ideas that are applicable throughout science and engineering.
5. Energy and matter: Flows, cycles, and conservation - Tracking fluxes of energy and matter into, out of, and within systems helps one understand the systems’ possibilities and limitations.
6. Structure and function - The way in which an object or living thing is shaped and its substructure determine many of its properties and functions.
7. Stability and change - For natural and built systems alike, conditions of stability and determinants of rates of change or evolution of a system are critical elements of study.

	What does it look like to demonstrate proficiency?
Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Explain - Constructing a cause-and-effect model of a system
* Students use models (diagrams, drawings, physical replicas, mathematical representations, analogies, and/or computer simulations) to explain the subatomic structure of neutral atoms (based on experimental evidence) including:
   * the relative size and mass of the nucleus of an atom compared to the atom as a whole.
   * the location, relative size, mass, and charges of subatomic particles (protons, neutrons, and electrons).
   * strengths and limitations of various models of the atom.
   * the similarities and differences between isotopes of the same element.
* Students use models to explain that each element is identified by the number of protons in its nucleus.
   * Each element’s atomic number corresponds to its proton number.
   * Students use nuclear notation to represent the contents of an atom’s nucleus.
* Students use models to explain that isotopes of an element exist in unequal abundances.
   * Students analyze mass spectroscopy data to identify isotopes and their relative abundances.
   * Students explain the relationship between an element’s atomic mass on the periodic table and the relative abundance of its isotopes.
   * Students use notation for writing isotope names and symbols (e.g., Carbon-14, C-14, 14C)
* Students use models to explain the electrostatic interactions between subatomic particles including:
   * electrons are attracted to the positive charge of the nucleus but repelled by the negative charge of the other electrons in the electron cloud.
   * neutrons contribute to the strong force that prevents the positively charged protons in the nucleus from repelling each other with enough force to break apart the nucleus.
* Students compare the various atomic models and identify/explain the evidence that led to the development of new models of the atom.

	What does it look like to demonstrate proficiency?
Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Compare - Finding similarities and differences between two or more objects, events, ideas, problems, or situations
* Students use models to compare isotopes of an element.
   * Although isotopes of an element have the same number of protons (same atomic number), their masses differ because the number of neutrons in the nucleus differs.
   * Isotopes of the same element have nearly identical chemical behavior but their nuclear stabilities differ.
* Students use models to explain the electrostatic interactions between subatomic particles including:
   * neutrons contribute to the strong force that holds the nucleus together by counteracting the repulsive electric force among all the protons in the nucleus.
* Students use models to explain characteristics and applications of spontaneous radioactive decay including that:
   * spontaneous radioactive decay is a random event and can not be predicted for individual nuclei;
   * spontaneous radioactive decay can be predicted for a large group of identical nuclei because it follows a characteristic exponential decay law;
   * the predictability of decay rate allows radiometric dating to be used to determine the ages of rocks and other materials from the isotope ratios present.
      * Students can use mathematical reasoning to determine:
         * the half-life of a radioactive isotope in a given scenario;
         * the the starting or remaining amount (in grams, as a percent, or as a fraction) of a radioactive isotope in a given scenario;
         * the amount of time passed based on the starting and remaining amounts of a radioactive isotope;
         * the number of half-lives that have passed based on the starting and remaining amounts of a radioactive isotope.
* Students use models and mathematical reasoning to compare the composition of an atom’s nucleus before and after a nuclear process to determine the type of nuclear decay (alpha, beta, gamma) that has occurred.
   * Students can explain that the total number of neutrons plus protons does not change in any nuclear process.
   * Students can compare alpha, beta, and gamma particles including applications, benefits and hazards, and penetrating ability.
   * Students can balance and solve for unknown nuclear equations using shorthand notation for nuclear particles.

	What does it look like to demonstrate proficiency?
Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Explain - Constructing a cause-and-effect model of a system
* Students use models to explain that:
   * an electron can jump to a higher energy level (excited state) if given a certain amount of energy (absorption).
   * the electron releases the same amount of energy when it returns to its ground state energy level (emission).
   * the difference between energy levels corresponds to specific wavelengths on the electromagnetic spectrum.
   * the arrangement of electrons in each atom generates a unique atomic emission spectrum, which can be used (like a barcode) to identify each element.
* Students use models to demonstrate the arrangement of electrons in energy levels around the nucleus of an atom.
   * Students translate between electron configurations and orbital diagrams (complete and Noble Gas abbreviations).
   * Students identify elements based on electron configurations and orbital diagrams (complete and Noble Gas abbreviations).
   * Students identify/explain errors in the electron configuration and/or orbital diagram of a given element.
   * Students explain how the electron configuration and/or orbital diagram for a given element would change when the neutral atom becomes an ion.
	Academic Language

*Question/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:
* What patterns do you observe in how electrons fill energy levels across different elements? What might explain these patterns?
* How does electron distribution relate to the stability of an atom? How do atoms achieve stable configurations, and how does this influence their chemical behavior?
* What are the limitations of these models in accurately representing electron distribution? What are some alternative models, and how do they complement each other?
* How can we use these models, despite their limitations, to explain cause-and-effect relationships in electron behavior? For example, how does the distribution of electrons in an atom cause it to react in a specific way with other atoms?
