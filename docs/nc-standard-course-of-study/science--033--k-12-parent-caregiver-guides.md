# Science - K-12 Parent/Caregiver Guides

Introduction: K-5

What to expect:
In elementary school, children arrive curious, eager to learn, and bring their own unique understanding of the world. They ask questions and develop their own ideas based on their experiences and prior knowledge. Watching clouds in the sky, creating shadows on a sunny afternoon, kicking or throwing balls, growing plants, and reading about animals are all experiences that inform a child’s understanding of science. Students in K-5 should continue to be active learners - doing science to learn science. Parents, extended family, caretakers, teachers and principals are all part of a community that influence how students engage in science. By working together to connect science learning between home and school, we can expand the view of where children learn science, including in museums, neighborhoods, playgrounds, grandma’s kitchen, forests, and elsewhere.

Using the Science and Engineering Practices
Children have the ability to think conceptually, generate explanations and communicate their reasoning. In science, this happens when students use the Science and Engineering Practices. By engaging in science practices, children not only have the opportunity to do science, but also build understanding of science content and share their thinking and ideas with others.

Kindergarten

By the end of Kindergarten your child will be able to:
* Describe objects by their physical proteins (e.g., size, color, shape, texture, weight).
* Understand positional words (e.g. in front of, below, between, etc.) and the way objects and organisms move (e.g., straight, zigzag, round and round, back and forth, etc.)
* Understand the characteristics of living and nonliving things.
* Investigate how organisms are alike and different
* Track daily weather conditions and seasonal changes in weather.

School and Home Connections
* Sort objects (e.g., buttons, rocks, toys) into groups and explain how they are grouped.
* Play I Spy and use positional words as clues. I spy something that is above the table…
* Act out ways different animals and objects move (e.g., rabbit-hops quickly; snake-slithers back and forth; clock hands- go round and round; elevators- go up and down).
* Go outside and count all the living things you see (e.g., people, dogs, bumblebees, trees, flowers, birds). Count all the non-living things you see (e.g., car, house, sidewalk, fence, rock, bike).
* Take a nature walk to observe birds. Tell how they are alike (e.g., wings, beak, can fly, can sing) and different (e.g., different colors, sizes, sounds or calls).
* Observe trees on your nature walk and ask your child, how are the trees alike or different from each other?
* Observe the weather on a regular basis. Ask your child what the weather is like today. Have them describe the sky conditions and the current weather (e.g., cloudy, rainy, sunny, snowy, hot, cold, etc.).

Grade 1

By the end of first grade your child will be able to:
* Investigate how pushes and pulls affect the motion of objects.
* Understand the basic needs of plants and animals.
* Observe and describe patterns of change in objects in the sky (e.g., sun,moon).
* Understand the properties of Earth materials (e.g., rocks, water, soil).
* Learn about natural resources and how they are important to humans.

School and Home Connections
* Talk about how pushes and pulls can make things move. Find ways to show pushes and pulls while playing with a ball or when using magnets. Ask your child, what effect does the type of push have on a ball?
* Plant and grow seeds from fruits, vegetables, or flowers. Talk about the needs of seeds and plants. Ask your child, how are the plant’s needs met in their environment?
* Visit a zoo, farm or an aquarium, or take care of your own pet. Describe the needs of animals. Ask your child, how are the animal’s needs met in their environment?
* Go outside and observe the day and night sky often. Ask your child to describe objects in the sky and how they change over time? What patterns do they notice?
* Take a nature walk and find examples of Earth materials. Where are rocks, water, and soil found in your environment? Describe their properties (e.g., size, shape, texture, color).
* Read a book about natural resources such as water or trees. Ask your child how we use these materials, why they are important and how we can protect them.

Grade 2

By the end of second grade your child will be able to:
* Describe the properties of solids and liquids and changes they undergo.
* Investigate the relationship between sound and vibration.
* Compare the life cycles of different animals (e.g., butterflies, frogs, crickets).
* Understand how organisms (e.g., parents, offspring) are alike and different.
* Investigate weather using observations and tools to describe weather conditions (e.g., temperature, wind direction, wind speed, precipitation).

School and Home Connections
* Make popsicles with your child. Discuss the changes from a liquid to a solid. Ask, what changes would happen if you left a popsicle on the counter?
* After a rainstorm, observe the water on the street, ask your child, what happens to it over time?
* Play an instrument (or make one out of recycled materials) to show how vibrating objects (e.g., plucking strings) make sound. Check out a library book on sound. Explain how we create sound with our vocal cords and hear it with our sense of hearing.
* Visit a science museum to observe examples of butterfly life cycles. Ask your child, how do the butterflies change?
* Observe an animal family. Ask your child, how is a puppy similar and different from its mother? How is it similar or different from its siblings?
* Track daily weather conditions together and talk about how they change over time.

Grade 3

By the end of third grade your child will be able to:
* Investigate the structure and properties of matter and how it can change.
* Understand motion and factors that affect motion.
* Describe how energy can be transferred from one object to another,
* Show how bones, muscles and skin are essential parts of human body systems.
* Investigate how plant growth is supported by the environment.
* Identify parts of the solar system and patterns in movement that occur.
* Use maps and models to understand Earth’s land and water features and locations.

School and Home Connections
* When making breakfast, ask your child, what changes do you notice when pancake batter is heated? How does bread change in a toaster? How do ice cubes placed in juice change? Are any of the changes reversible?
* Have a race, talk about who won based on the amount of time and distance traveled.
* Find examples of warmer objects transferring energy to cooler objects. Ask your child, what happens to the temperature of a mug when hot coffee is poured in it? How do warm cookies placed on a kitchen counter change the temperature of the counter?
* Grow plants in a garden or a planter. Discuss the parts of the plant, and ask your child what plants need to grow.
* Track the position of the sun in the sky throughout the day and observe patterns in how shadows change in length and direction.
* Use maps to talk about land and water features in your area of NC. Ask your child, what types of information can be found on globes, atlases, and digital maps?

Grade 4

By the end of fourth grade your child will be able to:
* Understand how magnets interact with other objects.
* Describe how energy can be transferred by sound, light, heat and electric currents.
* Investigate how light can be reflected, refracted, and absorbed.
* Understand that environmental changes can impact plants and animals.
* Compare fossils with other fossils and living organisms.
* Observe patterns in the sky including moon phases and the day/night cycle.
* Investigate properties of rocks and how the Earth’s surface changes over time.
* Understand that humans impact the environment.

School and Home Connections
* Look for things attracted to magnets. Ask your child, what do the objects have in common?
* Change the batteries in a flashlight. Talk about the parts of a flashlight and the way batteries are placed in the flashlight. Ask your child, what type of energy does a flashlight use? And what type of energy does it produce?
* Use small mirror to reflect light on a wall. Ask your child to tell you about the path of light.
* Talk about new construction or buildings and how these might change the environment for plants and animals in the area.
* Go to a local museum to observe fossils. Ask your child, how fossils are similar or different from present day plants and animals.
* Make a calendar and draw the changes in moon phases over a month. Ask your child, what patterns do you notice?
* Start a rock collection. Talk about the characteristics of the rocks. Look for areas in your neighborhood where rocks may have been moved.
* Read a book about volcanoes. Ask your child, how do volcanoes change the Earth’s surface?
* Investigate ways to reduce, reuse and recycle in your home and community.

Grade 5

By the end of fifth grade your child will be able to:
* Understand the weight of matter stays the same when it changes form.
* Determine if mixing two or more substances results in new substances.
* Understand force, motion, and the relationship between them.
* Investigate the structures and functions of human body systems.
* Understand the interdependence of plants and animals in ecosystems.
* Compare traits that are inherited and those that are not.
* Observe weather patterns and conditions in a given time and place.
* Analyze and compare weather and climate data.
School and Home Connections
* Make some trail mix. Ask your child, if we know the weight of each ingredient separately, can we know the weight of them all together?
* Bake some muffins together. Discuss how mixing substances or ingredients together sometimes creates a new substance or forms something new.
* At a rollerskating rink, ask your child, how is it different skating on carpet as compared to the rink? How does friction affect your speed of skating?
* Check out a library book on the human body systems. Read about the structures that make up the systems and what each system does for the human body.
* Take a nature walk. Observe plants and animals. Ask your child, how do plants get energy? How do animals get energy? Compare water ecosystems (e.g., ponds) with land ecosystems (e.g., forests).
* Observe similarities and differences in animals, discuss traits that are inherited and others that are learned.
* Monitor the weather on a regular basis. Look at weather maps and listen to reports to determine upcoming weather patterns and conditions.

Grade 6

 By the end of sixth grade your child will be able to:
* Describe the structure, properties, and states of matter.
* Investigate how thermal and electrical energy transfers through matter.
* Understand the properties and transfer of energy in different types of waves.
* Learn about the ways plants are able to survive and reproduce.
* Investigate the flow of energy through different ecosystems.
* Describe the earth/moon/sun system, and the structure of other celestial bodies.
* Understand how the Earth’s surface has changed over time.
* Investigate how humans depend on the Earth’s soil.

School and Home Connections
* Act out how water particles may be interacting with each other in frozen, solid, and liquid forms.
* Discuss why some cups keep drinks hotter or colder than other cups.
* Take a walk and point out examples of waves in everyday life (e.g. ripples in puddles, rhythmic pulses of sound waves from passing cars).
* Collect discarded flowers from a flower store and observe the structures and functions of the flower‘s parts that help it survive.
* Take a nature hike and discuss how plants and animals get their energy.
* Observe and record what the moon looks like for a month. Develop models of the Earth, moon, and sun to explain the changes to these patterns.
* Discuss what knowledge we might gain from observing and investigating other galaxies even though we can’t visit them.
* Identify different types of weathering and erosion happening on driveways, roads, sidewalks, walls and other rock-based structures, and look for patterns to explain what may cause the difference in rate of change. Look for possible solutions to slow weathering.
* Plant a garden of vegetables or fruits. Discuss how humans depend on the soil for survival. Identify ways for you and your child to be better stewards of your garden.

Grade 7
By the end of seventh grade your child will be able to:
* Investigate force and motion on objects and represent this data on graphical displays.
* Investigate how energy is transferred, transformed, and conserved in mechanical systems.
* Learn how the human body is made of different cells and how its organ systems interact with each other to support life.
* Understand how there are various patterns of inherited traits passed down from parent to offspring through chromosomes found in cells.
* Describe the atmosphere’s role on the Earth’s weather and climate.
* Learn about the reciprocal relationship between humans and the atmosphere.

School and Home Connections
* Build different kinds of paper airplanes using various types of material to investigate the distance they travel when dropped from the same height.
* Talk about motion and speed in relation to recreational activities and vehicles.
* How do skateboards, bikes, and boats work? What impacts their speed?
* Ride or watch a roller coaster. Notice the times when the roller coaster moves faster and other times more slowly. Discuss why this happens.
* Talk about how the body systems work together. Do you feel differently when you eat and drink certain foods? How does that impact other systems?
* Talk about how our life choices can impact our genetics and lifespans.
* Discuss similarities and differences within genetic families around you and of people that you know. Do you have neighbors whose children look alike or do you and a sibling have different traits? Why do you think that is?
* Watch the weather report together or discuss radar images from your phone’s weather app. What do you notice about the patterns? How does this connect to what your student is learning in science?
* Go outside after a rainstorm and find a puddle, then go back after the sun has come out for a period of time and ask your child what happened to the puddle.
* Talk about environmental issues that affect the atmosphere and discuss how certain choices can impact the atmosphere.

Grade 8
By the end of eighth grade your child will be able to:
* Investigate properties of matter and the physical and chemical changes that occur when matter interacts.
* Understand how diseases affect living organisms.
* Investigate how living organisms respond to living and nonliving factors in their environment.
* Describe how organisms evolve over time using evidence.
* Learn how evidence from fossil records and landforms can lead to an understanding of the history of the Earth and its life forms.
* Investigate the composition of the hydrosphere.
* Learn about the reciprocal relationship between humans and the hydrosphere.
* Understand the various methods of obtaining energy resources around the earth and environmental impacts they may have.

School and Home Connections
* Spend some time together cooking different recipes in the kitchen. Discuss ways chemical and physical changes are occurring in the foods you are preparing.
* Examine data from COVID, Flu, or other communicable viruses for your region. Talk about the differences between bacteria and viruses. Discuss what a vaccine is and why it is important to finish all of an antibiotic if you are prescribed one.
* Look around at ecosystems near your home. See if they can identify a food chain. Use this to discuss flow of energy. Talk about producers and consumers and what the difference is.
* Look through nature magazines or watch nature programs on television. Discuss the organism’s adaptations that help them survive in their environment.
* Use computer simulation programs or apps to identify and explain the effects of tectonic plate movement.
* Conduct a water analysis to estimate how much water your family uses on a weekly basis (e.g. cooking, cleaning, watering yards). Identify ways your family can conserve water.
* Check out a book at the library about renewable and nonrenewable resources.
* Discuss how to conserve these resources and be better stewards of the environment.

Working Together
To support families, communities, and teachers in realizing the goals of the 2023 North Carolina K-12 Science Standards, this guide provides an overview of the learning expectations for students studying high school science.

Graduation Requirements
The 2023 Standards for high school are designed to help students continually grow their science knowledge and abilities from the foundations built in elementary and middle grades. The standards are intended to foster conceptual understanding, develop scientifically literate students, and provide opportunities to engage in the science practices that reflect those of professional scientists and engineers. High school students will engage in all three domains of the sciences - Physical Science, Life Science, and Earth and Space Science.

Graduation requirements for science include:
* A physical science course (Chemistry, Physical Science, or Physics)
* Biology
* Earth-Environmental Science.

The North Carolina High School Graduation Requirements for all subjects can be found on the NCDPI website.
Science Standards
The 2023 North Carolina K-12 Science Standards describe objectives for student learning, but how those objectives are met is determined at the local level. Therefore, your student’s teacher(s) and school are the best sources of information about what is being learned (curriculum) and how it is being taught (instruction) in the classroom.

To review the NC 2023 K-12 Science Standards, visit the K-12 Science Standards Resource Hub. The hub provides access to a variety of resources for supporting school leaders, administrators, educators, and parents/caregivers.

How to Encourage Science Learning at Home
* Take advantage of nonformal science education opportunities in your community. Currently, there are more than 200 environmental education centers recognized in North Carolina. Visit the NC Environmental Educators website to view the directory.
* Seek out opportunities to introduce your children to individuals in your community whose work relates to science or technology. This may include trades and professions such as construction or manufacturing, public safety, medicine, natural resource management, or research.
* Participate in “Take Your Child to Work” days, and expose them to the science and technology in your workplace. Encourage your employer to promote and support these opportunities.
* Attend career fairs with your children. Help them explore a broad range of career options and learn about and understand the necessary skills and coursework required to pursue these careers.
* Look for special events and programs in your community that enable your children to meet scientists, or visit a worksite or local university where science and technology are prevalent. Support your children's participation in online academic mentorship programs that pair students and scientists to carry out STEM projects.
* Find opportunities in your community to connect science and technology businesses, schools, and non-school learning venues such as museums, libraries, and clubs. Encourage both financial and personnel investments in science learning. Ask businesses to give employees release time to support science learning at school or in the community and to become mentors for school-age youth.

Biology

Biology
Students in a high school biology course will develop an understanding of key concepts that help them make sense of life science. The ideas are building upon students’ science understanding of disciplinary core ideas and science and engineering practices from earlier grades.

Students will study content across the following strands:
1) Structure and Function
2) Inheritance and Variation of Traits
3) Matter and Energy in Organisms and Ecosystems
4) Interdependent Relationships in Ecosystems
5) Natural Selection and Evolution
These include the most fundamental concepts in life science and are  foundational for expanded study in upper-level high school courses.

Throughout Biology You May Find Students…
* Analyzing and interpreting data regarding genes; demonstrating how DNA processes are the same in all organisms; developing, communicating, and justifying an explanation of how cells form specialized tissues.
* Using a model to illustrate the role of cellular division (mitosis) and differentiation in producing and maintaining complex organisms.
* Planning and conducting an investigation to provide evidence that feedback mechanisms maintain homeostasis.
* Developing an explanation that shows how ecosystems follow the laws of conservation of matter and energy by analyzing how energy flows through trophic levels (food webs) and describing how matter cycles (carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus) through the ecosystem.
* Using mathematical representations to support and revise explanations based on evidence about factors affecting biodiversity and populations in ecosystems.
* Evaluating claims, evidence, and reasoning that the complex interactions in ecosystems maintain relatively consistent numbers and types of organisms in stable conditions, but changing conditions may result in a new ecosystem.

Earth-Environmental Science

Earth-Environmental Science
Students in Earth-Environmental Science will develop an understanding of key concepts that help them make sense of earth/space science. The ideas are building upon students’ science understanding of disciplinary core ideas and science and engineering practices from earlier grades.

Students will study content across the following strands:
1) Earth’s Place in the Universe
2) Earth’s Systems
3) Earth and Human Activity.

These include the most fundamental concepts in Earth-Environmental Science and are  foundational for expanded study in upper-level high school courses.

Throughout Earth-Environmental Science You May Find Students…
* Using a model to explain how Earth’s position relative to the sun influences life on Earth.
* Developing a model based on evidence to illustrate the life span of the sun and the role of nuclear fusion in the sun’s core to release energy that eventually reaches Earth in the form of radiation.
* Evaluating evidence of the past and current movements of continental and oceanic crust and the theory of plate tectonics to explain the ages of crustal rocks.
* Using a model to describe how variations in the flow of energy into and out of Earth’s systems result in changes in climate.
* Planning and conducting an investigation of the properties of water and its effects on Earth materials and surface processes.
* Analyzing and interpreting data to predict the location and possible occurrence of natural hazards.
* Constructing an explanation to predict how potential future changes in abiotic factors could impact biodiversity and species distribution in an ecosystem.
* Evaluating design solutions for developing, managing, and utilizing energy and mineral resources.
* Evaluating or refining a technological solution that reduces impacts of human activities on natural systems.
Physical Sciences
Physical Sciences
* Chemistry
* Physical Science
* Physics

Students studying the physical sciences in high school continue to develop their understanding of the core ideas in the discipline. These ideas include the most fundamental concepts from chemistry and physics, but are intended to leave room for expanded study in upper-level high school courses. The high school proficiency statements within the Chemistry, Physical Science, and Physics Support Documents build on the knowledge and skills developed in the middle grades and allow high school students to explain more in-depth phenomena central not only to the physical sciences, but to the life and the earth and space sciences as well.

Students will study content across the following strands:
1. Matter and Interactions (Chemistry, Physical Science)
2. Motion and Stability - Forces and Interactions (Physical Science, Physics)
3. Energy (Chemistry, Physical Science, Physics)
4. Waves and Their Applications (Physical Science, Physics)

Throughout the Physical Sciences You May Find Students…
* Using models to explain the structure of atoms and how the interactions between electric charges at the atomic scale influence the structure and interactions of matter.
* Using the periodic table as a model to predict the relative properties of elements.
* Using models to analyze chemical reactions, their rates, and their outcomes in terms of particle collisions, rearrangement of atoms, and changes in energy.
* Using mathematics and computational thinking to apply the proportional relationships inherent in chemical reactions (stoichiometry).
* Analyzing and interpreting data to predict how changing the concentration, temperature, or pressure of a chemical system would shift the equilibrium of a reversible reaction.
* Analyzing and interpreting data to determine relationships among variables and make predictions about the motion of an object. Carrying out investigations to explain how Newton’s laws of motion and the conservation of momentum can be used to predict changes in the motion of objects.
* Analyzing and interpreting data to explain how the forces experienced by two interacting objects are influenced by the fundamental properties of the objects (like mass and charge) and the distance between them.
* Using models to explain how an electric current can produce a magnetic field and that a changing magnetic field can produce an electric current.
* Using models to explain how changes in one type of energy and/or energy flows in and out of the system affect the other types of energy in the system.
* Using models to explain the interactions of positive and negative charges in electrostatic systems and in electric circuits.
* Using mathematical thinking to explain relationships among the frequency, wavelength, and speed of waves traveling in various media.
* Communicating information about how some technological devices use the principles of wave behavior and interactions with matter to transmit and capture information and energy.
