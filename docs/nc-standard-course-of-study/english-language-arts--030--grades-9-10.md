# English Language Arts - Grades 9-10

Introduction
Unpacking the ELA Standard Course of Study
Grades 9-10

On January 8, 2026, the State Board of Education unanimously approved the 2026 North Carolina English Language Arts (ELA) Standard Course of Study (SCOS) for implementation in the 2027-2028 school year. To support successful implementation of these standards, the NCDPI ELA Team has created Unpacking Documents to deepen the understanding of the NC ELA SCOS and its Fundamental Principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center, 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling, and 3) Variety of Texts.

The Unpacking Documents include the ELA Standards and Objectives as well as a glossary, clarifications, standards-based question stems and sentence frames, classroom considerations, and supplemental resources.  Please note that these unpacking documents are not meant to be exhaustive.

Because North Carolina is a local-control state, all curricular decisions (e.g., texts, tasks, activities, etc.) are approved at the local level. Prior to implementing any of the materials in this resource, teachers should follow appropriate policies and procedures to have their curriculum vetted and approved by their school and district leaders.

Acknowledgements: The NCDPI ELA Team consulted with educators in the field as well as the Offices of Early Learning and Exceptional Children during the creation of this resource.

Questions? Contact the NCDPI ELA Team.

CT.1
Overview and Purpose

The Complex Text Strand (CT) ensures students are developing into proficient, independent readers and listeners, outlining essential skills that students should be practicing every day, in every lesson, and with every text. This strand deepens students’ understanding of how to become skilled, confident readers who can sustain attention and build stamina with increasingly challenging texts. The CT standards and objectives are designed to speak to the complex nature of reading comprehension and provide all educators with a deeper understanding of skills students should engage in routinely to access and comprehend grade-level complex texts.

In order to place text at the center of ELA teaching and learning, CT standards and objectives should be integrated across the other two strands of the NC ELA SCOS–Comprehension Development (CD) and Communication and Writing (CW)–when designing standards-aligned instruction. This integration ensures that students practice skills within the context of grade-appropriate complex texts. As students apply these skills across a variety of such texts, they demonstrate the ability to independently construct meaning across language domains.

Through the CT Strand, students build knowledge and strengthen comprehension by reading and listening to a range of worthy, content-rich texts. This includes students
* applying a range of strategies to monitor, support, and deepen comprehension
* drawing on a wide range of reading and listening experiences to build knowledge and vocabulary
* transferring techniques from the texts to their own evolving forms of communication and writing
* independently and proficiently reading/listening to grade-level complex texts at the high end of the 9-10 text complexity band

These CT standards and objectives reflect critical, research-based components of reading comprehension and reinforce rigorous grade-level expectations across all strands of the NC ELA SCOS. The Complex Texts strand serves as a foundation for standards-based, text-centered instruction in every ELA classroom.

Complex Text Strand and the Fundamental Principles

The Complex Text Strand standards and objectives ensure the fundamental principles of 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy; and 3) a Variety of Texts are living out in the ELA classroom. Designed to be recursive in nature, CT standards and objectives make explicit and implicit references to these fundamental principles to ensure students are engaging with them routinely throughout instruction.
* Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center | Grade-level complex text is the foundation of the CT.1 standard and the primary means through which students engage with the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) to build essential academic competencies. Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation.
* Integrated Model of Literacy | CT.1 objectives support students’ ability to apply and integrate skills across language domains in various contexts and across a range of texts. For example, CT.1.1 requires students to return back to the texts they are reading to ground discussion and writing in textual evidence. CT.1.2 emphasizes repeated readings, writing in response to texts, and collaborative discussion as key drivers of comprehension. CT.1.4 promotes the reading-writing connection, ensuring students engage with language receptively through reading, and transfer that knowledge generatively to their own communication.
* Variety of Texts | Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. CT.1.3 encourages students to read widely to build knowledge and vocabulary across a range of topics, themes, and concepts.

A note on text selection and local control

North Carolina is a local-control state. Because of this, the North Carolina Department of Public Instruction (NCDPI) does not provide a recommended text list, as curricular decisions are made at the PSU (public school unit) level. NCDPI cannot endorse specific curricula or instructional materials for local use. The standards do not restrict or preclude the teaching of specific modes, types, genres, mediums, and formats of text.

PSUs should carefully analyze potential  texts for each grade by examining the qualitative, quantitative, and reader and task measures to ensure students engage with  increasingly complex texts. Through text complexity analysis, educators can determine when a particular mode, type, genre, medium, format of text best supports the instructional purpose. Multiple approaches are appropriate and should be used in alignment with the standards to ensure students experience a range of texts and text types that prepare them for postsecondary opportunities.

Glossary
	Evidence (Textual) - Text that can be referred to in order to support an idea, interpretation, or assertion (e.g., quote, passage, pictures or illustrations, etc.).

Repeated Reading - A vital comprehension tool—leveraged by the teacher or students—that entails rereading text for multiple purposes. It is frequently used in the close reading of complex texts to deepen student comprehension.

Self-Monitoring - The act of monitoring one’s own comprehension for what is understood, what is not understood, and what comprehension strategies would be appropriate to resolve issues with comprehension.

Text(s) - Texts are generated by authors to communicate to readers. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres or modalities (e.g., visual, auditory, aural, linguistic, gestural, spatial, etc.). Texts may be printed on paper or published in digital forms.

Text Complexity - Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task.

Classroom Considerations
* Carefully select high-quality texts by ensuring the text is appropriately complex for the grade-level. Determine overall text complexity using a variety of measures including qualitative, quantitative, and reader & task considerations. Educators should examine and weigh all three measures equally; one is not more important than another, and all three combined provide the most accurate level of complexity.
* Plan daily extensive reading instruction using increasing complex texts within the 9-10 text complexity band in grade 9 and at the high end of the 9-10 text complexity band in grade 10, strategically integrating intentional entry points that support student access while preserving the full complexity of the text. Ensure students are exposed to a variety of text modes, types, genres, structures, and formats.
* Use think-alouds as a primary strategy to model how proficient readers make meaning from grade-level complex texts, explicitly demonstrating how to navigate challenging vocabulary, structures, and ideas. Follow this modeling with guided practice that allows students to apply these processes with support, gradually releasing responsibility as they demonstrate increasing independence.
* Equip students with tools that build their capacity to independently navigate grade-level, complex texts by teaching them to recognize when meaning breaks down, ask and answer questions as they read/listen to monitor their comprehension, and reflect on what they have read/heard. Provide regular opportunities for students to practice these behaviors so they become self-directed, strategic readers/listeners. Support students’ metacognition by engaging them before, during, and after reading to reflect on what they are learning and how they are making meaning from the text.
* Adolescents must learn from, comprehend, and analyze texts which, compared to texts in earlier grades, are significantly longer and more complex at the word, sentence, and structural levels. As this complexity increases grade-to-grade, these texts present greater conceptual challenges and demand a much higher level of information synthesis. As students move into secondary grades, research-based adolescent literacy strategies should be used to teach the general content literacy skills students need to access grade-level complex texts and engage with the discipline-specific reading, writing, and discussing skills in English Language Arts. For more guidance, research, and supports on adolescent literacy, see Keys to Literacy’s Adolescent Literacy: A Guide.
9-10.CT.1.1 Cite the most relevant textual evidence to support analysis of what a text says as well as inferences drawn from a text.

This is a continued emphasis from grade 8, but with texts appropriately complex for grades 9-10. Grades 9-10 students continue to locate and use specific details and examples from the text to support interpretations and close examination of what the author/speaker says directly and what can be reasonably concluded or is indirectly implied from the text. Students refer to multiple details or pieces of evidence in a text to support their analysis. In grades 9-10, students build on prior citation skills by selecting the most relevant evidence to support their analysis.

Students…
* analyze what a text says explicitly/directly
* analyze what a text says implicitly/indirectly
* cite multiple pieces of relevant textual evidence to support analysis

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	* What information is clearly given in this section related to the main idea/theme of ___?
* What does this detail suggest about ___? What can you figure out even though it is not directly stated?
* Where in the text did you find multiple pieces of evidence to support your analysis? What quotes or details best support your thinking?
* Which evidence most strongly supports the idea/theme ___?
	* In the text, the author/speaker clearly states that ___. This supports the analysis that ___.
* The author/speaker implies that ___ by showing/saying/explaining ___. This supports the analysis that ___.
* The text says ___. Another example from the text is ___. These details support my idea that ___.
* The evidence that most strongly connects and supports the idea/theme of ___ is ___.

Classroom Considerations
* Teach and support students with text annotations for evidence tracking. Show students how to mark key ideas, highlight places in the text, use margin notes, etc. to track important details as they read for specific purposes. For secondary students, encourage annotation of common interpretive cues in literary and informational texts to support not only initial comprehension but also analytical annotation.
* Plan instruction for close reading that allows students to respond to text-dependent questions that ask them both what the text states directly (what a text states) and what it says indirectly (inferences drawn from text).
* Through modeling and explicit instruction, show students that an inference comes from combining text clues with what they already know, not guessing. Think aloud to show students how to notice clues in the text and turn them into inferences.
* Support students in citing multiple pieces of textual evidence by modeling how to select examples that are meaningful and directly connected to the ideas they are analyzing, including both direct quotations and brief summaries of key events, ideas, or passages. Encourage students to return to the text to locate evidence carefully and to note page numbers or line references so that others can verify their sources. In grades 9-10, students should expect to justify their interpretations/analyses of texts as part of authentic analytical discussion and writing, refining their own thinking in light of the justifications of their peers. Help students pay close attention to text features that often signal important ideas or nuances, such as repeated motifs, contrasts, key transitions, counterarguments, or particularly precise or loaded language, which may warrant closer examination and stronger evidence selection. To support students in selecting the most meaningful and appropriate evidence, teachers can use protocols such as Evidence Sorting, Think-Aloud Modeling, and Evidence-Connection Charts to build students’ prior skills of locating and citing relevant evidence. Highlight evidence that is not only relevant but also rich enough to reveal nuances, patterns, or connections within the text. Guide students to evaluate potential evidence by asking questions such as
   * Does this example clarify or develop my idea?
   * Does it provide insight into character, theme, or argument?
   * Does it help me analyze rather than summarize?
Help students clearly connect evidence to their analytical ideas by building in regular opportunities for writing, discussion, and explanation, where they must explicitly show how each piece of evidence supports their claims and encourage students to explain why the evidence matters, what it reveals about the text, and how it strengthens their interpretation/analysis. Reinforce that multiple pieces of evidence are necessary to support a claim and should be gathered across the text, including quotations, details, and examples that when taken together provide a deeper, more convincing analysis. Set clear expectations for the amount and quality of evidence students include in their analysis and provide structured opportunities to practice selecting, citing, and connecting evidence across a range of texts.
9-10.CT.1.2 Use self-monitoring, repeated reading, objective summarizing, writing in response to texts, and discussions with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension.

This is a continued emphasis on these comprehension supports from grade 8, but with texts appropriately complex for grades 9-10. Students utilize various supports when reading a text to strengthen comprehension. These include:
* using self-monitoring strategies to gauge and repair where their comprehension may break down
* re-reading texts multiple times for various purposes (including understanding) and levels of meaning
* summarizing the main points of what has been read, and
* engaging in writing and discussion about texts to communicate understanding and collectively reinforce learning.
Each of these approaches ensure students are accessing and comprehending grade-level complex texts with increased independence.

Students…
* use self-monitoring to strengthen comprehension
* use repeated reading to strengthen comprehension
* use summarizing to strengthen comprehension
* use writing in response to texts to strengthen comprehension
* use discussions with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension

Classroom Considerations
* Foster students’ active engagement with self monitoring their comprehension, explicitly teaching and supporting students to flexibly use a repertoire of comprehension strategies.
* Follow the principle of gradual release to ensure students use strategies independently.
* Provide explicit instruction and opportunities for extended discussion on text meaning and interpretation, including opportunities for whole-class interactive read-alouds and performing close reading of complex text.
* Support students in writing in response to reading for a variety of purposes, including to summarize, analyze, interpret, and apply information from texts they have read.
9-10.CT.1.3 Draw on a wide reading of texts, including texts on the Holocaust and genocide, to build knowledge and vocabulary.

This is a continued emphasis on wide reading to build knowledge and vocabulary from grade 8, but with texts appropriately complex for grades 9-10. In grades 9-10, students read/listen to a variety of texts on a given subject or topic–both teacher determined or student selected–in order to expand their knowledge, understanding, and vocabulary. Students draw on this information to expand their comprehension of content being studied in class and to better access grade-level complex texts. At a given point in grades 9-10, students read/listen to and engage with texts on the Holocaust and genocide to continue to build their understanding of these topics.

Students…
* draw on a wide range of texts to build knowledge generally
* draw on a wide range of texts to build vocabulary generally
* draw on a wide range of texts about genocide and the Holocaust to build knowledge and vocabulary

Classroom Considerations
* Provide text sets connected by topic/theme and ensure they include a variety of text types, genres, modes, formats, mediums, and/or lengths. Texts in the set should reflect a range of complexity levels so students can build the knowledge and vocabulary needed to access the grade-level text being studied. Consider potential models for pairing and sequencing texts in a set depending on the instructional purpose and task.
* To increase the impact of wide reading, regularly activate, connect, build, and extend students’ prior knowledge to help them bridge new learning with what they already understand and have studied. Doing so improves comprehension, supports vocabulary development, and increases engagement by making content more accessible. Writing and discussion strategies can be incorporated into instruction to help uncover what students know before introducing new material or text (e.g. anticipation guides, cloze, concept sorts, ABC brainstorming, quick writes, etc).
* Build and maintain a robust classroom library that offers students a wide range of texts–diverse authors, high-interest topics, both informational and literary works. etc. Consider organizing and promoting student selection of texts from a topic-focused collections to help students reinforce and extend learning from class through independent reading.
* Provide regular, protected time for wide reading to allow students to explore multiple texts on the same topic. When applicable, use this time to encourage student choice within a topic to boost motivation while simultaneously supporting knowledge and vocabulary growth. Model habits of wide reading for students by doing brief book talks, highlighting how reading multiple texts deepens understanding of a topic, and allowing time for students to talk, write, or reflect about what they are learning from their reading. Explicitly draw students’ attention to how their existing banks of knowledge are expanded through additional reading about a given topic and linking newly learned information or realizations to previously established knowledge.
* Collaborate with a librarian or media specialist to help identify digital resources, databases, and multimedia that broaden the range of texts students can draw on. Involve these specialists in helping students learn how to locate, evaluate, and select texts that support their knowledge building. Consider how research or independent reading tasks can be used to get students to read more widely on a topic being studied.
* Support student vocabulary development as they read by following a comprehensive vocabulary framework that promotes word learning in a multifaceted way: teach high-impact Tier II and Tier III words related to a specific topic/text, equip students with strategies for figuring out unfamiliar words when they encounter them, foster curiosity about language by studying the way words work and relate, and provide rich and varied written and spoken language experiences so students encounter new vocabulary in meaningful contexts. Together, these components help students build stronger, more flexible word knowledge as they read.
* Provide students with a carefully chosen range of literary and informational texts that focus on the Holocaust and other genocides, ensuring content is age-appropriate. Include personal narratives, historical accounts, and literary depictions to help students build knowledge of key events, historical context, and the experiences of individuals. Texts should also introduce relevant vocabulary and concepts, support critical thinking, and foster empathy while developing students’ ability to draw connections across multiple sources. Learn more about the NC Holocaust Education Act and explore sample curriculum and supporting resources by visiting the NCDPI webpage.

9-10.CT.1.4 Recognize the choices and features used by authors and transfer those techniques to communication and writing.

This is a continued emphasis on recognizing and transferring techniques from grade 8, but with texts appropriately complex for grades 9-10. In grades 9-10, students notice and learn from how authors write and craft texts, using those same techniques to improve their own communication and writing. Students determine how an author’s choices impact a text and its effects on readers, looking at elements and features such as literary and rhetorical elements, literary and rhetorical techniques, word choice, figurative language, text features, etc. Students apply similar techniques to their own writing or speaking, developing the style, complexity, and individual voice of their own communication based on their specific purpose and task.

Students…
* recognize the choices and features used by authors
* transfer authorial techniques to communication and writing

Classroom Considerations
* Support students with identifying and reflecting on the impact of choices authors make in texts to enhance their craft and style. These text choices and features should include elements such as literary and rhetorical elements, literary and rhetorical techniques, word choice, figurative language, text features, etc.
* Plan instruction that integrates skills across language domains–reading, writing, speaking/listening, and language–so students can notice techniques in texts they are reading/listening/viewing and practice applying them in their writing and speaking in various contexts.
* Use mentor texts and supports for quality writing to help students analyze and emulate in their own communication. Select high-quality texts that clearly demonstrate different techniques across genres and styles so students develop a broader understanding of what communication can look like catered to a variety of purposes and audiences.
* Ensure instruction includes both interpretive (analysis) and expressive (creation) opportunities for students, so they can both understand and examine texts (to support reading/listening comprehension), as well as transfer techniques when producing original texts as authors/speakers with specific intentions of their own. Interpretive tasks focus on how students understand, analyze, and interpret information they receive through reading, listening, or viewing. Expressive tasks focus on how students communicate their own thoughts, ideas, and feelings through speaking, writing, or representing. Explore The Writing Guidebook and The Discourse Playbook for additional guidance and ideas for pairing reading with writing, speaking, and listening.

Glossary

Adolescent Literacy
	As students progress through the secondary grades, instruction should place less emphasis on learning how to read words and more continued emphasis on efficiently recognizing familiar words and using word knowledge (morphology, syllable patterns, and meaning) to tackle increasingly complex texts and their academic vocabulary. Automatic word recognition frees cognitive space for comprehension, allowing students to focus on meaning rather than decoding. Strong word knowledge and recognition continue to directly support understanding of increasingly complex texts across disciplines and are an essential part of ensuring students continue to grow these skills throughout their secondary education. For more guidance, research, and supports on adolescent literacy, see Keys to Literacy’s Adolescent Literacy: A Guide.

Classroom Considerations
9-10.CD.F.3.1 Apply knowledge of syllabication to support word recognition.

Considerations
	Examples
	Ensure students understand that a focus on syllabication in the upper grades builds on prior learning and is intended to support flexible, strategic word recognition of increasingly complex, uncommon, or specialized multisyllabic words as text complexity increases. Define word recognition for students and explain that strong word recognition supports fluent reading by freeing cognitive resources for making meaning, which in turn strengthens comprehension of complex texts. Reinforce that syllables are organized around vowel sounds and that skilled readers may try multiple divisions, drawing on familiar syllable patterns, morphemes, and context to arrive at accurate pronunciation.

Planning for Instruction: Prior to instruction, identify a few multisyllabic words in grade-level complex texts that are key to comprehension that students may struggle understanding. Regularly utilize a routine such as Flexible Word Chunking to practice syllabication and morphological analysis. Provide students with multiple opportunities to practice this routine in the context of reading grade-level texts to integrate syllable instruction with the study of morphemes. Monitor student progress with reading multisyllabic words in order to identify students who would benefit from additional intervention.
	Example 1:
Flexible Word Chunking: This strategy helps students use syllabication and morphological analysis flexibly. Word chunking supports stronger reading comprehension, especially when encountering academic and content-area texts. Once modeled, students can begin to use this as a strategy when independently reading. As students become familiar with this routine, syllables, and morphemes over time, ask them for ideas on how to break apart a word to correctly read it.

9-10.CD.F.3.2 Apply knowledge of morphology to support word knowledge.

Considerations
	Examples
	Ensure students understand that a focus on morphology in the upper grades is meant to support their word knowledge–noticing not only how words are spelled and pronounced, but what they mean, how they’re related, and how they’re built of smaller parts. Define morphology/morphemes for students and explain their role in supporting fluent reading and, ultimately, comprehension of complex texts. Support students with strategies to utilize morphology to increase their comprehension when it breaks down while reading.
	Example 1:
Inside, Outside, Beyond Strategy: This strategy helps students analyze the meaning of unknown or unfamiliar words when reading. Note that the first step, “inside the word,” relies on looking at a word’s morphemes. Model and practice the routine for students to scaffold them toward independence.
	Support students in understanding when they encounter an unknown or unfamiliar word, they can use knowledge of morphology and individual word parts (i.e., roots, prefixes, suffixes) to determine the larger meaning of a term. This is helpful for students reading complex or difficult texts because they use an increased number of multisyllabic and discipline-specific words, a large majority of which are derived from Old-English, Latin, and Greek.
	Example 2:
Word Origins Anchor Chart: This sample anchor chart lists origin languages of modern English words and provides examples. Co-create a similar resource with students to use when introducing, practicing, or reviewing word origins. This will help students understand how morphemes lead to common meanings.

	Planning for Instruction: Identify a few morphologically rich words in grade-level complex texts that are key to comprehension or the topic/concept being studied. Explicitly teach and review the most common or relevant prefixes, Greek and Latin roots, and suffixes as part of regular vocabulary instruction, but also point out word parts to students as they naturally appear in the context of reading grade level texts. Provide explicit instruction on what a prefix, root, and suffix are and ensure students are able to isolate them in multisyllabic words.
	Example 3:
Sample Morphological Analysis Routine: This routine helps students use meaningful word parts to figure out the meaning of unfamiliar or complex words. By analyzing morphemes, students build a flexible understanding of vocabulary rather than relying on rote memorization of words. This supports stronger reading comprehension, especially when encountering academic and content-area texts. Once modeled, students can begin to use this as a strategy when independently reading.

	Provide students with multiple opportunities to practice morphological analysis in the context of reading grade-level texts. For example, practice deconstructing words into meaningful word parts, and using that knowledge to determine meaning. Note to students this strategy can help provide the gist but not always an exact definition of a word. Students can also practice building words, exploring different combinations of morphemes. Finally, students can use morphemes to build out word families, introducing a word that appears in a classroom text and brainstorming related words to support increased understanding. Monitor student progress with morphology practice in order to identify students who would benefit from additional intervention.
	Example 4:
Word Matrix Boxes: This routine helps students practice building words using morphemes. Students create real words by selecting one prefix, the root, and one suffix, then read the word, check its meaning, and use it in a sentence. This routine strengthens students’ ability to decode, encode, and understand multisyllabic Greek and Latin–derived words by highlighting how meaningful parts combine to form new words.

9-10.CD.3.1 Evaluate the approaches used to establish primary arguments or themes in texts, including explicit or implicit claims, validity of reasoning, and relevance and sufficiency of evidence.

Classroom Considerations
   * Provide repeated opportunities for students to identify the claims–explicit or implied–that authors make in both literary and informational texts. Point out places these claims often appear, such as thesis statements, topic sentences, narrator commentary, recurring motifs that imply theme, or in the resolutions of major conflicts. Model how to recognize a text’s theme, main idea, or argument and draw students’ attention to how these ideas emerge through key scenes, turning points, transitions, or rhetorical structures. To support students in communicating their interpretations, analyses, and evaluations of explicit or implied textual claims, introduce them to rhetorical and literary précis and model how to write one based on a given text. Précis can be used to support analysis of entire texts, as well as specific sections or excerpts.
   * Support students with analyzing the reasoning authors use to develop their ideas. Demonstrate how literary authors build thematic claims through character interactions, symbolic details, motifs, dialogue, or plot progression, functioning similarly to reasons and evidence in an argument. Show students where informational authors build reasoning through cause-and-effect chains, clarifying explanations, counterarguments, or linked examples. Provide side-by-side analysis of literary and informational texts to help students see that while literary evidence may not resemble evidence typically found in information texts–such as statistics or expert quotations–it still functions as justification for a broader interpretive claim.
   * Model how to evaluate the relevance, sufficiency, and validity of support. Highlight for students how, in literary texts, the strongest thematic evidence often appears in recurring imagery, shifts in tone, impactful scenes, or a character’s pattern of choices. In informational texts, guide students to examine data, cited sources, logical explanations, and examples. As students consider the relevance of supporting evidence have them ask
   * Literary: Does this excerpt/passage directly relate to the theme or is it tangential?
   * Rhetorical/Informational: Does this piece of evidence connect logically to the argument/main idea, or is it unrelated?
As students consider the sufficiency of supporting evidence have them ask
   * Literary: Are there enough instances of this element to convincingly demonstrate the theme?
   * Rhetorical/Informational: Does the author provide enough support, or are claims stated with minimal evidence?
As students consider the validity of supporting evidence have them ask
   * Literary: Does this scene, symbol, or character choice genuinely support the theme, or is it a superficial or isolated example?
   * Rhetorical/Informational: Is this evidence trustworthy and well-reasoned, or is it flawed, anecdotal, or irrelevant?
Explicitly compare strong and weak evidence in both text types so students can articulate why an author’s support is convincing or unconvincing. Select paired texts in which authors convey similar themes or arguments but through different structures, rhetorical choices, or literary techniques. Model how to track these patterns using graphic organizers, annotation routines, or text-mapping strategies that highlight which author develops their ideas more effectively.
   * Support students in forming evaluative judgments by modeling how to weigh the strength of an author’s reasoning and evidence–both informational/rhetorical and literary. For informational/rhetorical texts, guide students to consider
   * Evidence Integration: Convincing arguments are supported by a variety of relevant, interconnected evidence.
   * Appeal to Audience Understanding/Values: Convincing arguments take into account their target audience’s knowledge level, pre-existing values, and ongoing concerns about the topic at hand.
   * Complexity and Nuance: Oversimplified or one-sided reasoning (e.g., X is good; Y is bad) can reduce effectiveness, and nuanced reasoning can enhance trust (e.g., While Y has limitations such as __, there are benefits, but X is better because __).
For literary texts, guide students to consider
   * Consistency Across the Text: A theme about a certain topic feels convincing if multiple text elements relate to and develop the topic.
   * Causal or Logical Connections: A theme is less unconvincing if text elements contradict it or feel arbitrary.
   * Complexity and Nuance: Oversimplified themes (e.g., Good triumphs over evil) feel less persuasive than those that explore gray areas/nuance (e.g., Good intentions can lead to harmful consequences).
Encourage students to reference specific passages, such as pivotal scenes, rhetorical appeals, narrative decisions, word choice patterns, or quoted evidence, to justify why one author communicates their idea more effectively across multiple texts.

Students focus on which details each text highlights or omits and consider how those choices shape the development of themes, main ideas, or arguments to observe how authors manipulate source material to communicate distinct perspectives or purposes.

Scaffold annotation routines to mark where source material appears and how it is adapted or interpreted.

   * Then, showing how to connect these observations to historical or cultural context by providing brief explanations, timelines, images, or examples that illustrate the societal norms, events, or cultural practices relevant to the text; model making these connections out loud so students see the reasoning process.

Guide students to track differences in characterization, emphasis, or framing that arise due to these shifts, noting how thematic or argumentative development is affected and scaffold comparison of representations across texts. Draw attention to divergent tones, emphases, or narrative perspectives, and provide graphic organizers to record differences. Model thinking aloud about why authors might depict the same events, characters, or ideas differently based on context. Encourage students to make connections between historical/cultural context and authorial choices via discussion opportunities or recording their thinking in graphic organizers or on-going response prompts.

In informational texts, examine how word choice, selected evidence, and framing of events reflect contemporary attitudes. Model how to ask: “What does this author include or exclude because of the time period?” To encourage evaluation of how perspective shifts alter thematic or argumentative emphasis, ask students to trace how small changes in interpretation or framing reflect broader societal changes. Model how to ask: “How would this text differ if written in a different time or under different cultural values?” and facilitate discussion on implications for understanding themes or arguments.
   * Provide opportunities for students to articulate how shifting perspectives affect broader themes and arguments. In discussion and writing, encourage them to reference specific scenes, descriptive patterns, historical claims, or rhetorical shifts to support their conclusions.    * Understanding the historical/cultural context of ___ helps the reader recognize ___.
   * Because of changes in ___, the meaning of ___ is better understood as ___.
Emphasize that evaluating representation is not simply summarizing differences: it requires analyzing why authors present ideas differently and how those differences shape meaning.

Students notice distortions, omissions, or biases and assess how these influence the interpretation of themes, events, ideas, or arguments. Grades 9-10 students build on prior skills by applying them across multiple texts to determine how meaning is shaped by faithful depictions of source material.

   * evaluate the accuracy of an author’s interpretations of source material

	Sentence Frames
	   * How credible and accurate are the authors’ interpretations of source material or information in each text?
   * What distortions, omissions, or misrepresentations can you identify across the texts?
   * How do these interpretations shape understanding of shared themes, main ideas, or arguments?
   * How do differences in credibility or accuracy affect readers’ perceptions of the arguments, main ideas, or themes?
   * Which author provides the most reliable and accurate interpretation of source material? How do you know?

   * Distortions, omissions, or misrepresentations appear in [Text A/Text B]; for example ___.
   * The credible and accurate/not credible or accurate interpretations influence the understanding of the theme/main idea/argument by ___.
   * Differences in credibility or accuracy affect the reader’s perception of the arguments/themes because ___.

Classroom Considerations
   * Through think alouds, model processes for evaluating the credibility and accuracy of interpretations across texts.
   * In literary texts, guide students in examining the plausibility and faithfulness of how authors depict historical events, cultural practices, or real-world settings. Model how to pause at key passages to question whether details align with known historical or cultural contexts, and invite students to compare those depictions with brief background sources or author notes to determine whether the portrayal is grounded (closely aligned with historical or cultural records), selective (highlighting certain aspects while omitting others), or interpretive (shaped to support a particular meaning or perspective). Have students consider how these choices affect the reader’s understanding, and what alternative portrayals or additional context might change the interpretation. Highlight text features such as historical references, cultural dialogue patterns, depictions of customs, or portrayal of real groups or events.
   * In informational texts, guide students in examining the accuracy of claims by examining citations, corroboration, precision of data, and whether evidence is represented fairly. Demonstrate how to locate the source behind a cited claim, confirm whether the evidence in that source actually supports the author’s statement, and compare how other texts or sources present the same information to determine whether evidence is sufficient and accurately represented. Have students consider how the author’s selection, framing, or emphasis of evidence shapes the interpretation, and what additional evidence or alternative perspectives might lead to a different understanding. Highlight text features such as headings, subheadings, citations, data displays, examples, and the way evidence is presented, organized, or emphasized to support the author’s claims.
   * Provide instruction on comparing interpretations across texts, and model how to consider and/or annotate interpretive cues in texts. Provide text sets centered on a shared event, character, or topic that contain varying degrees of accuracy or credibility and model how to track and reflect on differences in
   * emphasized details, which reveal what the author considers important and can influence the reader’s perception of events or characters;
   * conflicting claims, which help students identify discrepancies and assess which accounts are better supported by evidence; and
   * narrative framing, which shows how the structure or point of view shapes interpretation and can highlight bias or focus.
Ask students to analyze why one interpretation may be more historically sound, logically consistent, or faithful to the source material than another. Encourage students to consider why differences exist across aspects of texts such as
   * author’s perspective and purpose, which shapes how ideas, experiences, or events are presented and can highlight certain aspects or interpretations over others;
   * intended audience, which influences tone, style, content, and the level of detail, affecting how the text communicates meaning or evokes a response;
   * author’s purpose, which clarifies why the text was created and helps explain choices in focus, structure, or emphasis;
   * use of evidence or textual support, which determines how convincingly ideas are developed, whether reasoning or storytelling is clear, and how examples or details support meaning; and
   * reliance on verifiable sources or accurate information, which affects credibility by showing whether ideas, claims, or references are grounded in fact, research, or reliable context.
As students evaluate credibility and accuracy, encourage use of relevant academic vocabulary such as credible, accurate, biased, distorted, misrepresented, corroborated, and substantiated as students communicate their analyses. Model how to use these terms when making evaluative judgments about texts and support students in applying them when writing or discussing how and why one interpretation is more trustworthy than another.
   * Support students in evaluating how credibility, accuracy, and authorial choices affect interpretation of themes, arguments, or reader perception. Guide students to notice flawed reasoning, biased selection of details, inaccurate/conflicting portrayals, altered timelines, misleading graphics, or selective use of evidence by modeling consideration of questions such as
   * Why might the author highlight or omit these details?
   * How does this choice influence my understanding of the main idea, theme, or argument?
   * Are there alternative explanations, interpretations, or perspectives that are missing?
   * How does the evidence, or lack of it, support the author’s claims or thematic focus?
   * Have the authorial choices in this text helped or hindered communication of the main idea, theme, or argument?
To support students’ use of these questions, draw their attention to common areas where differences across texts occur, including characterizations or narrative events that exaggerate traits or omit perspectives; chronological or causal distortions that affect plot or thematic understanding; and emphasis on specific scenes, dialogue, or imagery that shifts readers’ focus in literary texts, as well as statistical or data manipulation to support a claim while ignoring counterevidence; misrepresented historical events or figures that alter cause-and-effect understanding; and visuals or graphics that exaggerate trends or omit key context in informational or rhetorical texts. Have students respond to these questions in discussion or written opportunities to encourage them making explicit connections between how authors portray similar subject matter and how readers interpret main ideas, themes, or arguments as a result.

Prioritize text-derived vocabulary to support student understanding of texts and contextualize vocabulary instruction. One planning strategy to use when selecting the vocabulary to focus on is Teach, Tell, Text: teach words are those key to understanding a text and support students reading, writing, speaking, and listening; tell words can be quickly explained to students and lack meaningful context in the text; and text words are those students can determine independently using context clues, prior knowledge, or text features (footnotes, glossary, etc.).
   * Explicitly teach students the definitions of words through research-based strategies that get them to break down and actively process a word’s meaning. Strategies could include
   * Frayer models, in which students develop a deep understanding of a concept or term by examining its definition, key characteristics, examples, and non-examples, supporting precise vocabulary use and conceptual clarity.
   * Semantic mapping, in which students visually organize relationships among words and ideas by connecting a central concept to related terms, categories, or attributes, supporting meaning-making and knowledge integration.
   * Morpheme analysis routines, in which students analyze word parts such as roots, prefixes, and suffixes to determine meaning, recognize patterns across words, and support decoding and academic vocabulary development.
   * Explicitly teach, model, and practice strategies to help students derive the meaning of unfamiliar words, including using context clues, morphology, and the utilization of reference materials. An example of this strategy could be Outside, Inside, Beyond: First, have students look inside the word (word parts), then look outside the word (context clues), and if the meaning is still unknown look beyond the text (at a dictionary/thesaurus, or ask a peer).
   * Integrate vocabulary learning within reading, discussion, and writing to provide students meaningful opportunities to learn and then use new words. Examples of this could include using a text set where specific vocabulary is repeated across multiple texts, or having students complete semantic mapping, word learning journals, word sorts, etc. Ensure students use new words in class discussion and writing to reinforce their understanding.

9-10.CD.4.2 Recognize, interpret and use figurative language, as well as rhetorical and literary elements.

This is a continued emphasis from grade 8, but with texts appropriately complex for grades 9–10. In grades 9–10, students continue to recognize, interpret, and analyze figurative language, literary elements, and rhetorical elements, including their layered and nuanced uses. Students examine how authors strategically employ these elements across a range of texts–not just literature–to shape meaning, influence audiences, and enhance style. Grades 9–10 students build on prior skills from grade 8 by analyzing the interplay among figurative language, literary elements, and rhetorical elements, and transferring this understanding across reading, writing, speaking, and listening tasks.

Students…
   * recognize figurative language, as well as rhetorical and literary elements
   * interpret figurative language, as well as rhetorical and literary elements
   * use figurative language, as well as rhetorical and literary elements

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	   * How does an author’s choice of figurative language, literary elements, and rhetorical elements contribute differently to shaping meaning, influencing the audience, and achieving the author’s purpose?
   * Which figurative language/literary elements/rhetorical elements are present?
   * What does [figurative language/literary element/rhetorical element] mean in the context of the text?
   * How do figurative language/literary elements/rhetorical elements contribute to the meaning?
   * What figurative language/literary elements/rhetorical elements did you use in your writing and how did you intend it to affect meaning/the audience?
	   * The author’s use of[figurative language/literary element/rhetorical element] contributes to meaning/audience/purpose by ___.
   * This phrase/text is an example of ___ because ___.
   * [Figurative language/literary element/rhetorical element] conveys ___ because ___.
   * The author uses [figurative language/literary element/ rhetorical element] to emphasize ___ and help the audience understand ___.
   * I used ___ [figurative language/literary element/rhetorical element] in my writing to help convey ___ by ___.

Classroom Considerations
   * Support students with recognizing and analyzing figurative language in literary, argumentative, persuasive, and informational texts. Types of figurative language to highlight could include–but is not limited to–metaphor, simile, personification, hyperbole, symbolism, allusion, types of irony, oxymoron/paradox, and anaphora/repetition. Help students understand that figurative language can serve multiple purposes across text types:
   * in literary texts, it develops themes, deepens characterization, and enhances tone;
   * in persuasive and argumentative texts, it clarifies complex ideas, emphasizes points, appeals to emotion, or strengthens persuasiveness;
   * in informational texts, figurative language can make abstract or technical content more vivid, relatable, or memorable.
Model and have students practice how to identify these devices and discuss their impact on meaning, tone, and audience. Students should be encouraged to analyze how figurative language shapes meaning, tone, argument, or audience response across text types.
   * Support students with identifying and interpreting literary elements in texts. Key elements include character, plot, conflict, theme/central idea, point of view, and symbolism. Help students understand how these elements shape meaning, tone, and audience response in literary texts. Additionally, guide students to notice analogous stylistic choices in persuasive, argumentative, and informational texts such as case studies of individuals and anecdotes used to illustrate a scenario or problem. Model how to identify these elements  and discuss how they contribute to meaning, clarity, and audience understanding across text types.
   * Support students with recognizing and analyzing rhetorical elements across texts. Ensure students understand the common rhetorical appeals—ethos (credibility), logos (reasoning), pathos (emotion), and kairos (awareness of timing or context)—and model how these appear in different texts:
   * Ethos: In literary texts, a narrator’s credibility or authorial voice can establish trust; in persuasive texts, expert opinions, credentials, or fairness build credibility; in informational texts, citations, verified data, and balanced presentation establish reliability.
   * Logos: In literary texts, logical cause-and-effect in plot, coherent structure, or use of evidence within a story can guide interpretation; in persuasive texts, statistics, examples, comparisons, and reasoning support claims; in informational texts, data, charts, logical explanations, and cause/effect relationships communicate understanding.
   * Pathos: In literary texts, emotionally charged imagery, character experiences, or narrative tension evoke reader response; in persuasive texts, emotionally compelling language, anecdotes, or appeals to shared values influence audience action or belief; in informational texts, stories, vivid examples, or contextual framing may engage readers.
   * Kairos: In literary texts, timely events or cultural/historical context can heighten meaning or reader engagement; in persuasive texts, arguments or appeals may be more effective when aligned with current events, audience interests, or societal context; in informational texts, presentation of data or examples can be tailored to relevance and timeliness to enhance impact.
   * Model identifying figurative language and literary/rhetorical elements in mentor texts, then guide students to imitate those techniques in low-stakes writing practice and extended formal writing opportunities. Provide opportunities for reflection and peer feedback on how figurative language and literary/rhetorical elements affect meaning, helping students revise to clarify impact.

Students...

in texts In grades K-5, syntactical awareness is taught as part language comprehension because students are still learning how sentences work—how words are ordered, how simple sentences are formed, and how punctuation guides meaning—so they can decode and read smoothly. As students move into secondary grades, texts use longer, more complex sentences with multiple clauses, embedded phrases, and varied structures, so syntax shifts into a key comprehension skill: students must analyze how a sentence is built in order to interpret the author’s meaning, follow complex ideas, and make sense of academic or literary writing.    * interpret the syntax of clauses to comprehend texts at the high end of the 9-10 text complexity band

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	   * How could [sentence chunk]be rephrased in your own words?
   * What knowledge helps you understand or interpret [sentence chunk]?
   * Based on [sentence/sentence chunk], what should you understand about [concept, idea, topic within sentence/ source text]?
   * What does [target sentence] mean as a whole?

Classroom Considerations
   * Engage students regularly in syntax analysis to develop their awareness of how sentence structure contributes to both understanding and communicating meaning. Utilize a common, structured routine to build student familiarity with analysis and to allow students to devote more cognitive focus to the analysis of syntax and its effect on meaning, as opposed to adjusting to the norms of a new routine each time analysis takes place. A possible routine for syntax analysis could be the Juicy Sentences Protocol.
   * Ensure sentences identified for teacher-guided or modeled analysis contain a variety of phrases and clauses, as well as other elements, for students to engage with; are essential to understanding main ideas and themes within texts; have high information density; and feature grade-level academic or domain-specific language. After analysis, use text-sourced sentences as mentor sentences for students to apply different sentence constructions to their expressive communication.
   * Emphasize that phrases and clauses are units of communication separately and that they are combined in sentences to communicate meaning as a whole. Ensure students know that phrases are groups of words that function as a single part of speech in a sentence without a subject-verb pair and that they cannot stand alone. Common phrase types to draw students’ attention to include
   * noun phrases, which include a noun and its modifiers, typically structured with the modifiers preceding or following the noun;
   * verb phrases, which include a main verb and any auxiliaries, typically structured with auxiliaries before the main verb;
   * verbal phrases, which include verbs functioning as other parts of speech–infinitives, participles, or gerunds–and are typically structured with the verb form plus any objects or modifiers;
   * prepositional phrases, which include a preposition and its object, typically structured with the preposition first and the object noun or pronoun last, with any modifiers in between;
   * adjective phrases, which include an adjective and its modifiers, typically structured with modifiers before or after the adjective;
   * adverbial phrases, which include an adverb and its modifiers, typically structured with modifiers surrounding or preceding the adverb, often describing verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs;
   * absolute phrases, which include a noun and a participle with any modifiers, typically structured as a detached phrase that adds detail or context to the main clause.
Similarly, ensure that students know that clauses are groups of words that include a subject and a predicate and function as units in sentences and that they can stand alone or work within larger sentences. Common clause types to draw students’ attention to include:
   * independent clauses, which include a subject and a predicate, typically structured to express a complete thought that can stand alone;
   * dependent/subordinate clauses, which include a subject and a predicate, typically structured with a subordinating conjunction that makes it unable to stand alone;
   * relative adjective clauses, which include a subject and predicate and functions as an adjective, typically structured with a relative pronoun introducing the clause;
   * relative adverb clauses, which include a subject and predicate and functions as an adjective, typically introduced by a relative adverb like where, when, or why;
   * adverbial clauses, which include a subject and predicate and functions as an adverb, typically structured with a subordinating conjunction showing time, reason, condition, or contrast;
   * noun clauses, which include a subject and predicate and functions as a noun, typically structured with words like that, what, who, or whether introducing the clause.
   * Support students in understanding that sentence types are defined by how independent and dependent clauses are combined, and analyzing these patterns helps students interpret emphasis, hierarchy of ideas, and relationships such as cause, contrast, and condition within a text. Sentence types to draw students’ attention to include:
   * simple sentences, which consist of a single independent clause. Although they may include multiple phrases or compound subjects or verbs, they express one complete idea. Drawing attention to simple sentences supports students in identifying core ideas and recognizing moments of emphasis or clarity in a text.
   * compound sentences, which consist of two or more independent clauses joined by coordinating conjunctions or appropriate punctuation. Analyzing compound sentences helps students notice how writers link related ideas of equal importance and track shifts or continuations in meaning across clauses.
   * complex sentences, which consist of one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses. Examining complex sentences supports comprehension by helping students recognize how writers signal relationships such as cause and effect, time, condition, or contrast, and how supporting information is subordinated to a main idea.
   * compound-complex sentences, which consist of two or more independent clauses and at least one dependent clause. Analyzing these sentences builds students’ capacity to unpack layered syntax, trace multiple ideas across a sentence, and understand nuanced relationships that contribute to meaning in more complex texts.

Concepts and Example Sentences

Verb Use Skills

Use conditional and subjunctive moods

Students need to know that the conditional mood expresses hypothetical situations and possible, but not guaranteed, outcomes by often using if/if-were clauses and modal auxiliaries. The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, demands, or possibilities that are not factual. Students need to recognize that the subjunctive often appears in clauses following verbs expressing desire, recommendation, or uncertainty and that the verb in the subjunctive is usually the base form (i.e., no -s, -ed, or -ing ending). The past subjunctive, especially with the verb to be (e.g., were), can sound unusual or “formal” because it does not follow normal past-tense patterns and is often replaced by was in casual speech; however, using were correctly signals a hypothetical or non-factual situation, which is essential for expressing wishes, contrary-to-fact statements, or imagined scenarios.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Conditional Mood
	If the weather improves, we will go for a hike tomorrow.

If the data were more complete, the results would be more reliable.
	Subjunctive Mood
	Members of the book club wished their next read were available for pick-up.

The committee recommended that the proposal be revised before approval.

Use verbals (gerunds, participles, infinitives)

Students need to know that verbals are verb forms that function in ways other than as the main verb of a clause. Gerunds are formed by adding -ing to a verb and act as nouns. Participles act as adjectives: present participles are formed with -ing , and past participles often end in -ed for regular verbs or take an irregular form. Infinitives are formed with to + base verb and can function as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs. Students also need to recognize that verbals allow sentences to vary in structure, provide additional detail, and express actions, qualities, or purposes in different grammatical roles.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Gerund (as a noun)
	Writing improves with consistent practice.

She values listening in difficult conversations.
	Past Participle (as an adjective)
	The exhausted runner collapsed at the finish line.
	Present Participle (as an adjective)
	The emerging theory challenged earlier assumptions.
	Infinitive (as a noun)
	To learn requires patience and persistence.
	Infinitive (as an adverb of purpose)
	She stayed late to revise.
	Infinitive (as an adjective)
	The committee proposed a plan to implement.

Phrase & Clause Use Skills

Form and use verbal phrases for variety, precision, and nuance

Students need to know that verbal phrases are built around verbals: gerunds (-ing forms functioning as nouns), participles (present -ing or past -ed/irregular forms functioning as adjectives), and infinitives (to + verb forms functioning as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs). They need to recognize that verbal phrases combine these verbals with other words (modifiers or objects) to create noun, adjective, or adverb phrases and that verbal phrases can appear at the beginning, middle, and end of sentences. Students also need to know that using verbal phrases allows sentences to vary in structure, add descriptive detail, and convey subtle nuances of meaning, such as timing, cause, or purpose and that verbal phrases can be layered with each other for increased sophistication.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Gerund Phrase (as a noun)
	Analyzing primary sources requires questioning assumptions and evaluating context.
	Present Participial Phrase (as an adjective)
	Clutching the letter in her trembling hands, she realized the truth had arrived too late.
	Past Participial Phrase (as an adjective)
	Worn by decades of use, the book still held its authority.
	Infinitive Phrase (as an adverb)
	The city invested in more efficient public transportation to reduce energy consumption.
	Infinitive Phrase (as a noun)
	To forgive after such betrayal felt impossible.
	Layered Verbal Phrases (participial + infinitive)
	Hoping to persuade undecided voters, the candidate delivered a speech designed to highlight economic stability.
	Use parallel structure to emphasize parity between ideas

Students need to know that parallel structure means using the same grammatical form for words, phrases, or clauses that are joined or compared. They need to know that elements that can be made parallel include lists of words or phrases, paired ideas connected by coordinating or correlative conjunctions, and clauses within a sentence. Students also need to recognize that maintaining parallel forms ensures clarity, readability, and equal emphasis across ideas in a sentence and that when combined with extended repetition, parallelism lends itself to emphasis of ideas.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Parallel Verbs in a List
	The researcher collected, analyzed, and interpreted the data.
	Parallel Gerund Phrases in a List
	Effective study habits include reviewing notes regularly, completing practice problems, and seeking feedback.
	Parallel Infinitive Phrases in a List
	She wanted to leave quietly, to avoid conflict, and to preserve her dignity.
	Parallel Structure with Correlative Conjunctions
	The policy aims not only to reduce costs but also to improve accessibility.
	Parallel Dependent Clauses
	When the evidence is examined carefully and when the context is considered honestly, the argument becomes more convincing.

Classroom Considerations
   * Focus grammar instruction on a functional and rhetorical level, instead of solely focusing on memorizing the grammatical terms/concepts themselves. Consider a more student-centered approach to grammar instruction by teaching it in context, considering how it can be taught synchronously with the texts being read and produced by students. To teach grammar and usage in context, consider the following, in order:
   * Student-Level Context: Examine student writing/speaking to determine the grammar/usage concept that will benefit students.
   * Text-Level Context: Identify a mentor text (sentence, paragraph, or larger excerpt) that shows the grammar/usage concept in use. Study and discuss how the grammar/usage concept is used in the text to achieve a particular goal or effect.
   * Writing/Speaking-Level Context: Have students practice applying the grammar/usage concept to their own communication via a prompt, shared writing, scenario and task, etc. Ask and expect students to transfer their learning of the grammar/usage concept to their larger writing or speaking tasks. For additional guidance on teaching grammar in context, see Recommendation 3 of The Writing Guidebook.
   * Teach students the various conventions appropriate to the type of communication or writing they are creating, studying, or reading, providing a rich and varied array of examples of how these conventions are used in real-world texts and by authentic writers and speakers. Provide students mentor texts and models of quality writing that illustrate how certain constructions, conventions, and usage rules are applied in practice for particular effect(s).
   * Utilize the following practices when designing explicit instruction on grammar and usage:
   * Help students build knowledge on a specific concept by outlining what it is, how it works, and why they should know and use it.
   * Create opportunities for students to practice a specific concept to gauge and refine student understanding (e.g. through mini lessons, etc.).
   * Have students apply knowledge and use a specific concept in an actual piece of writing or communication so they can demonstrate their understanding and receive feedback.
   * Continue to provide feedback and additional opportunities for students to apply their learning of the concept over time in more independent and sophisticated ways. Concepts should not be considered a one-and-done, but something that continues to be applied over time.
   * As students master grammar/usage concepts, consider how students can experiment with them to create a unique style and voice. Find ways to intentionally move students from “using grammar correctly” to “using grammar purposefully.” Examples of this could include
   * helping students see that grammar choices affect tone/mood/voice (e.g. showing how punctuation can change the voice of sentence),
   * providing opportunities for students to engage in paired or small-group discussions about how specific grammar/usage concepts influence tone, voice, and style in their written/spoken products or in mentor texts,
   * having students imitate an author’s style using correct grammar,
   * teaching students to adapt their grammar based on what they are trying to do (e.g. persuasive writing using strong/direct sentences or poetic/narrative writing using dashes or parentheticals),
   * providing feedback on effective grammar choices not just mistakes (e.g., “Your use of short sentences here creates tension.”),
   * probing intention of grammar/usage via questioning during feedback (e.g., Your use of layered participial phrases slows the pace of your product. Does this match your intention?”),
   * having students explain their grammar/usage choices via quick reflection prompts to build metacognition (e.g. “Which sentence are you most proud of and why?” or “What grammar move did you try and how did it affect your voice?”), or
   * encouraging students to consider how different grammar/usage constructions in written drafts might impact how spoken products are articulated aloud and ultimately received by the intended audience.
   * Intentionally activate and reinforce grammar and usage concepts from prior grades when introducing new grade-level expectations (e.g., revisiting functions of nouns, adjectives, and adverbs when teaching use of different verbal phrase types) to help students build knowledge cumulatively and connect past learning to more complex concepts. Maintain an ongoing anchor chart or reference list of taught concepts–with clear definitions and student-generated examples–to support students in applying conventions independently and help in providing specific, standards-aligned feedback during writing and speaking tasks, whether that feedback be to peers or themselves as part of the editing process.

Dashes, Parentheses, Hyphens, Ellipses, Semicolons, & Colons Use Skills

Use colons to introduce lists

Students need to know that colons follow a complete sentence and introduce lists and explanations. Typically, lists that follow colons follow a parallel structure.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Colon Introducing a List of Nouns
	For the camping trip, we need to pack several items: a tent, a sleeping bag, a stove, and a flashlight.
	Colon Introducing a List of Gerundial Phrases

Use colons to introduce quotations

Students need to know that colons follow a complete sentence and introduce quotations, either direct (from a speaker) or text-sourced (from an authored text). Direct quotations that follow colons are indicated with an opening and closing quotation mark, and the end punctuation (e.g., period, question mark, exclamation point) is contained within the closing quotation mark. Text-sourced quotations are also indicated with an opening and closing quotation mark, but the end punctuation follows an appropriately formatted in-text citation, as needed.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Colon Introducing a Direct Quotation

	Colon Introducing a Text-Sourced Quotation

Use em dashes for nonrestrictive or parenthetical elements

Students need to know that em dashes can set off extra information or clarifications that are not essential to the main sentence meaning or indicate a break in thought. When parenthetical/nonrestrictive elements occur in the middle of a sentence, the content is enclosed in em dashes. When  parenthetical/ nonrestrictive elements occur at the end of a sentence, the content is set-off by one em dash.

Concept
	Example Sentence

	Em Dash with Break in Thought/ Interruption
	The argument seemed sound—but upon closer inspection, the evidence revealed several inconsistencies.

Semicolons & Colons Use Skills

Use semicolons with conjunctive adverbs or transitional phrases to link independent clauses

Students need to know that semicolons can join two independent clauses, providing an additional way to connect complete ideas. This is especially useful when the clauses already contain internal commas or when using conjunctive adverbs (e.g., however, therefore, nevertheless, nonetheless, consequently, furthermore, moreover) or transitional phrases (e.g.,as a result, for this reason, in light of this, in contrast, on the other hand). When a conjunctive adverb or transitional phrase is used, the semicolon comes before it, and a comma always follows it, helping to maintain clarity in complex sentences.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs, No Internal Punctuation
	The novel explores themes of ambition and betrayal; however, the protagonist’s personal growth remains central to the story.
	Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs, Internal Punctuation

	Semicolons with Transitional Phrases, Internal Punctuation

Use semicolons with coordinating conjunctions when independent clauses contain internal punctuation

Students need to know that sometimes a semicolon can be used with a coordinating conjunction to join two sentences together and preserve clarity when the sentences are long and contain additional internal punctuation. They also need to know that this is an uncommon construction and is most helpful when one or both sentences already include several commas (e.g., complex sentences, series of phrases, etc.). Students should recognize that in this construction, the semicolon comes before the coordinating conjunction, and no additional comma is needed immediately after it.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Semicolons with Coordinating Conjunctions, Internal Punctuation

Sentence Production & Expansion Skills

Use a wide variety of sentence structures to achieve particular effects

Students need to know that sentence structure is determined by how independent and dependent clauses are combined. A simple sentence contains one independent clause; a compound sentence joins two or more independent clauses through commas and coordinating conjunctions (e.g., for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) or a semicolon; a complex sentence combines an independent clause with one or more dependent clauses through subordination; and a compound-complex sentence joins at least two independent clauses with at least one dependent clause. Students should flexibly use K-10 grammar and convention knowledge to vary sentence types, lengths, and constructions intentionally to create emphasis, tone, or style in writing across a range of text types.

Concept
	Examples
	Blend of Simple, Compound, Complex, and Compound-Complex Sentences, Narrative/Poetic/ Descriptive
	The city streets buzzed with activity. Cars honked impatiently, street vendors shouted their wares, and the aroma of food drifted through the alleys. While the crowd pressed closer, a lone musician played a haunting melody on his violin. People paused, some throwing coins into his open case, and as the music continued, the chaos of the city seemed to fade.
	Blend of Simple, Compound, Complex, and Compound-Complex Sentences, Informational/Analytical/ Argumentative
	Advances in digital technology have transformed how people communicate, learn, and work. While online platforms increase access to information and collaboration, they also raise concerns about privacy, distraction, and unequal access. Because technology continues to evolve, individuals and institutions must regularly adapt their practices; they must also adapt their expectations. Schools, workplaces, and families play a critical role in teaching responsible technology use, and clear guidelines are necessary to balance innovation with well-being.

Use non-standard sentence structures for literary or rhetorical effects

Students need to know how to manipulate sentence conventions intentionally (e.g., fragments, inversion, unusual punctuation, independent lists, run-on sentences used for effect, interrupted or broken syntax, repetition for emphasis, ellipses, dashes, rhetorical questions, single-word or one-phrase sentences, deliberate sentence length variation) to create a specific literary or rhetorical impact across a range of text types.

	Example Sentence

Classroom Considerations
   * Ensure students understand that transcription skills–such as accurate handwriting or typing, spelling, and punctuation–support the fluent production and overall clarity of sentences in written and spoken products. Guide students to notice how clause order, conjunction choice, and punctuation affect emphasis and rhythm. To build understanding of how sentence variety influences audience perception, provide students with paragraphs from authentic texts, including their own prior work, and have small groups or partners experiment with sentence variation. Encourage options such as breaking long sentences for dramatic effect, combining short sentences into compound-complex forms, reordering clauses to foreground key information, converting declarative sentences to questions for emphasis, or introducing periodic and cumulative sentence structures. Provide time for groups or partners to present their reworked paragraphs, explain the intent behind each structural choice, and receive feedback on how their experimentation influenced the perception of urgency, importance, or authorial voice. Reinforce both new and previously learned grammar, convention, and sentence construction concepts, framing them as tools to shape rhetorical or literary effect. To build students’ knowledge and confidence, craft focused mini-lessons and provide regular opportunities for guided and independent practice so students gain experience with different sentence structures. Instruction should include utilizing strategies to support students in developing more elaborate and sophisticated sentences, such as: sentence combining (manipulating and rearranging multiple sentences into one), sentence deconstruction (breaking longer sentences down into smaller parts), or generating/expanding an original sentence on a topic using the “W” questions (who, what, when, where, why, which, how).
   * Model embedding a variety of phrasal modifiers (e.g., prepositional, participial, infinitive, gerund, appositive, absolute, and adjectival or adverbial phrases) into sentences to demonstrate how authors might use them to include additional detail, nuance, and stylistic effect. Emphasize that adding phrase(s) does not change the fundamental structure of a sentence, but allows authors to layer information, highlight ideas, and shape how readers experience tone, rhythm, and emphasis. Invite students to discuss with peers how phrase placement (beginning, middle, or end of a sentence) influences rhythm, pacing, and focus, and demonstrate how to stack multiple phrases for effect. For practical guidance, model steps such as
   * Start with a clear base sentence.
   * Add one phrase at a time, choosing its placement to emphasize a key idea or create a desired rhythm.
   * Read the sentence aloud after each addition to check clarity and flow.
   * Experiment with stacking phrases in different orders to see how the sequence changes the tone, pacing, or emphasis.
Have students practice by revising sentences from authentic texts or their own writing, gradually adding one or more modifiers to enhance imagery, specificity, or stylistic nuance. Encourage students to notice how the addition, placement, and sequence of modifiers shift emphasis, highlight ideas, and shape the flow of a passage, and to experiment intentionally to produce varied rhetorical or literary effects. Reinforce that phrasal modifiers are tools for enhancing style and voice.
   * Introduce students to non-standard sentence structures gradually and emphasize that non-standard structures are deliberate choices tied to purpose that appear in authentic texts across literary, informational, and professional contexts where authors/speakers/creators use them strategically to guide emphasis, pacing, and audience engagement. Model for students how to identify intentional non-standard structures in mentor texts, labeling examples such as fragments, subject-verb inversion, run-on/fused sentences, parenthetical interruptions, asyndeton, polysyndeton, and delayed main clause/periodic constructions. Guide students to explain why a writer might choose each structure by asking questions such as
   * What does this structure emphasize?
   * How does this structure affect perception or emphasis of ideas?
   * How does this structure draw or minimize attention?
Provide practice with paired excerpts, having students compare passages that use non-standard structures intentionally with those that follow conventional syntax, discussing how each choice influences tone, pacing, and reader engagement. Support students by having them experiment in low-stakes revision exercises where they start with a conventionally constructed sentence and intentionally apply one non-standard structure, then read aloud to assess effect. Encourage them to justify their choices in writing or discussion, explaining how the added/revised structure(s) enhance meaning, emphasis, or rhythm. Use small-group or partner activities where students can provide feedback on whether the intended effect is clear to the reader, helping them distinguish between purposeful experimentation and unintentional errors.

9-10.CW.5.2 Synthesize relevant information from multiple authoritative sources to maintain a clear line of reasoning.

In grades 9-10, students make insightful connections between relevant pieces of information across multiple authoritative sources and use those connections to create new understandings about grades 9-10 topics, concepts, and themes. Grades 9–10 students form broad understandings of what is already known about their research topic from reputable sources and use these insights to identify gaps, raise questions, or contribute fresh perspectives and establish and sustain a clear progression of ideas when communicating synthesized information in order to thoroughly answer their research question.

Students…
   * synthesize relevant information multiple authoritative sources
   * maintain a clear line of reasoning to communicate synthesized information

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	   * How do the ideas from [sources] connect to each other, and how do they help support your overall argument or line of reasoning?
   * What patterns, similarities, or contrasts do you notice across your sources, and how can you use them to strengthen your points?
   * How does synthesizing information from multiple sources help you develop a deeper understanding of your topic?
   * What does current expert consensus on this topic suggest, and how does that help you identify gaps, raise new questions, or develop your own perspective?
   * Which pieces of evidence from different sources can you combine to make a single, stronger point in your argument?
	   * The ideas from [source] and [source] connect because ___, and together they support my argument by ___.
   * Across my sources, I notice [patterns/similarities/contrasts], and I can use these to strengthen my points by ___.
   * Synthesizing information from multiple sources helps me understand [topic, concept, theme] more deeply because ___.
   * Current expert consensus on this topic suggests ___, which helps me identify gaps, raise new questions, or develop my own perspective by ___.
   * I can combine the evidence from [source] and [source] to make a stronger point in my argument because ___.

Classroom Considerations
   * Guide students to support each major claim with evidence drawn from multiple authoritative sources and to synthesize those sources by identifying patterns, contrasts, or relationships among ideas rather than treating each source separately. Emphasize that drawing from several credible sources strengthens a line of reasoning by showing where evidence aligns, diverges, or builds on related ideas. Teach strategies such as grouping similar findings across texts, comparing how different authors address the same issue, or using note-taking structures (e.g., comparison charts or synthesis matrices) to track connections among sources. Incorporate activities like written conversations, in which students respond in writing to one another’s observations about different sources, helping them surface patterns, tensions, or connections across texts. Model the process through think-alouds that demonstrate how an experienced researcher reads across sources–highlighting key ideas, noting where sources reinforce or challenge one another, and explaining how those relationships contribute to a clearer, more supported claim.
   * Support students in communicating new understandings by providing synthesis charts or graphic organizers that visually link ideas across sources, making patterns, contrasts, and relationships explicit. Visualizing connections helps students move beyond summarizing individual sources and instead show how ideas interact to support a larger claim or conclusion. Model how to construct and use visual tools that organize evidence and highlight relationships among sources. Examples of visualization strategies include
   * Comparison or synthesis tables that list sources across the top and key ideas, claims, or themes along the side so students can track where sources agree, differ, or extend one another.
   * Venn diagrams to highlight shared and contrasting ideas between two or more sources.
   * Concept maps or idea webs that place a central claim or topic in the middle and visually connect supporting evidence from multiple sources with labeled lines showing relationships (e.g., supports, complicates, contrasts).
When introducing visualization strategies, model how to translate notes from multiple texts into these visual formats, explaining how each representation highlights patterns, tensions, or relationships that help clarify the student’s overall understanding.
   * Support students in strategically culling sources as their research focus sharpens, helping them recognize when a source no longer contributes meaningful evidence, introduces unnecessary repetition, or no longer aligns with the evolving purpose of the research. Emphasize that removing sources is a purposeful decision that strengthens clarity and focus, not a sign that the research was wasted. During the research process, provide students with structured opportunities to revisit their sources after drafting claims or identifying emerging patterns across texts and guide them to evaluate each source based on its contribution to their current line of reasoning rather than its initial usefulness during early research. Teach students to consider questions such as
   * Does this source add a new perspective, example, or piece of evidence, or does it largely repeat information already established by other sources?
   * Does the source still align with the refined claim or research focus, or was it more relevant to an earlier direction in the inquiry?
   * Does the source deepen understanding by adding nuance, contrast, or supporting detail, or does it contribute only loosely related background information?
   * Is another source already serving this same purpose more clearly or effectively?
Introduce simple independent or collaborative review routines to help students reassess sources systematically, such as
   * Evidence-to-claim checks, where students match each source to the specific claim(s) it supports.
   * Source contribution charts, where students briefly note what unique idea or evidence each source provides.
   * Annotation reviews, where students revisit their notes and mark which sources still contribute directly to their argument.
   * Model paragraph construction with multiple pieces of evidence to support students in integrating evidence in ways that are logical and clear to readers. Teach students to introduce, explain, and connect evidence to their points so that sources work together to strengthen reasoning rather than interrupt or overwhelm it. Emphasize that each piece of evidence should clearly contribute to the paragraph’s claim/point and be explicitly connected to the writer’s explanation. Model this process by demonstrating how a paragraph moves from claim to evidence to explanation and synthesis, showing how multiple sources can reinforce, extend, or complicate an idea. Provide students with a scaffolded paragraph frame that makes these moves visible, such as:
   * Claim/Point: One important reason ___ is that ___.
   * Introduce Evidence (Source 1): According to ___, ___.
   * Explanation: This evidence shows ___ because ___.
   * Introduce Additional Evidence (Source 2): Similarly/However, ___ notes that ___.
   * Synthesis/Connection: Together, these sources suggest/show that ___ because ___.
   * Closing/Link Back to Claim: This supports the idea that ___.
When modeling with this frame, guide students to think about how many pieces of evidence are needed by considering the complexity of the claim and the expectations of the task. Emphasize that some claims may require only two strong, well-connected sources while others may need three or more to fully support nuanced reasoning. Gradually remove the scaffold as students gain confidence integrating and connecting evidence independently.

9-10.CW.5.3 Evaluate the relevance, credibility, accuracy, and authoritativeness of each source.

In grades 9-10, students build on their knowledge of source assessment by carefully comparing options for sources, making criteria-based judgments about source quality, and defending the use of chosen sources as part of the evaluation process. In addition to evaluating the relevance, credibility, and accuracy of each source, grades 9-10 students judge the authoritativeness of each source and understand that referencing authoritative authors, texts, and publications improves the overall quality of their research. Students evaluate individual sources to ensure their research products reflect conclusions grounded in existing expertise.

Students…
   * evaluate the relevance of each source
   * evaluate the credibility of each source
   * evaluate the accuracy of each source
   * evaluate the authoritativeness of each source

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	   * What makes this source relevant to your research question, and how do you know it adds new information or perspective?
   * What aspects of the author, publication, or website suggest this source is credible and trustworthy?
   * How can you check that the information in this source is accurate, and what evidence supports your judgment?
   * What indicates that this author or source is authoritative on this topic, and how does that influence how you use it in your research?
	   * [Source] is relevant to my research question because ___, and it adds new information by ___.
   * I consider [source] credible because the author ___, the publication ___, and the evidence is ___.
   * The information in [source] is accurate because ___, and I verified it by ___.
   * [Source] is authoritative because the author/publication ___, and this affects how I use it in my research by ___.

Classroom Considerations
   * Guide students to recognize authoritativeness as a concept that builds on relevance, credibility, and accuracy, helping them understand that the expertise or reputation of an author, text, or publication adds weight to the evidence provided. Ensure students understand that authoritativeness is dependent upon relevance, credibility, and accuracy but also the reputation of the author and publication. Support this with modeling and think-alouds that show how to check an author’s credentials, affiliations, or prior publications (e.g., walk through an author bio, show professional profiles or institutional pages).
   * Guide students to systematically evaluate sources using clear criteria and make informed judgments about the quality and usefulness of each source for their research. Support this through evaluation rubrics, checklists, or modeling, showing how to apply criteria such as accuracy, timeliness, expertise, objectivity, and depth (e.g., demonstrate scoring a source and explaining your reasoning aloud). Provide opportunities for students to justify evaluation decisions in writing or discussion, linking their judgment of a source to their research question and the role the source plays in supporting claims (e.g., have students annotate sources with notes about strengths, weaknesses, and potential contribution to their argument).
   * Introduce lateral reading as a strategy for verifying information by consulting multiple sources or sites, showing how cross-checking claims can strengthen understanding and ensure evidence is trustworthy. Support student use of lateral reading with modeling and think-alouds, demonstrating how to quickly check an author, organization, or claim across different sites (e.g., opening multiple tabs, comparing reporting, noting discrepancies) and provide structured practice for students to integrate lateral reading into their research workflow, showing how it informs decisions about which sources to use, how much weight to give their claims, and how sources connect to the research question. Encourage students to document findings in notes, tables, or graphic organizers to make comparisons explicit (e.g., a chart tracking what each source confirms or questions about a claim).
   * Introduce a common mnemonic to guide source evaluation in high school research contexts, reinforcing criteria that align with careful source evaluation:
   * CRAAP – Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose
   * RADAR – Relevance, Authority, Date, Accuracy, Reason for writing
   * TARES – Truthfulness, Authenticity, Respect, Equity, Social responsibility
   * SMELL – Source, Motivation, Evidence, Logic, Language
Consider choosing one mnemonic to teach students to increase their familiarity with and use of it, as well as reduce the cognitive load of having to remember multiple acronyms.

In grades 9-10, students develop an awareness that style, formatting, and citation guidelines are distinct based on both considerations for different disciplines and source types. Grades 9-10 students use the style guidelines appropriate to the discipline their research topic best fits. Students provide necessary in-text citations when quoting, paraphrasing, and summarizing ideas in existing sources to avoid plagiarism and provide formal, full citations on a works cited, reference page, or bibliography as appropriate to their research.

Students…
   * conform to style manual guidelines for their research discipline
   * conform to style manual guidelines for their source type
   * avoid plagiarism by providing necessary citations

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	   * Which citation style is most appropriate for your research topic? Why?
   * How does[citation] follow the rules of the style manual for this discipline (e.g., MLA, APA, Chicago), and what parts are most important to check for accuracy?
   * In what ways have you credited the ideas of others to avoid plagiarism, and how do these citations strengthen your argument?
   * How does your use of multiple sources and correct citation help your reader understand the research question and the evidence supporting your claims?
	   * The most appropriate citation style for my research topic is ___ because it fits the [expectations of discipline] and matches guidelines for [source type].
   * This citation follows [style manual] guidance because ___, and the parts I need to check carefully are ___.
   * I have credited the ideas of others by ___, which helps me avoid plagiarism and strengthens my argument because ___.
   * Using multiple sources and correct citation helps my reader understand ___ and shows how the evidence supports ___.

Classroom Considerations
   * Ensure students know that different academic disciplines follow distinct citation norms, and that the choice of citation style communicates disciplinary expectations (e.g., MLA for English/literature, APA for social sciences, Chicago/Turabian for history or fine arts). Guide students to choose the citation style that best aligns with their research question and emphasize that correct citation is not just about formatting but communicating clearly and professionally with readers, showing where ideas come from and supporting transparency in research. Support this through demonstrating how to construct an annotated bibliography, using citation generators responsibly, and conducting peer reviews to check for accuracy and consistency in citations.
   * Provide opportunities for students to observe subtle and distinct differences in common citations styles with side-by-side modeling of the same source cited in different styles to highlight key distinctions, such as author-date vs. notes/bibliography, placement of titles, italics, and page numbers (e.g., project a source on a screen and annotate the differences between MLA, APA, and Chicago). Also, provide opportunities for students to convert citations from one style to another, helping them notice stylistic differences and understand the reasoning behind formatting conventions. Provide these opportunities both with in-text citations and bibliographic information on works cited and reference pages.
   * Encourage reflection on the ethical and professional dimensions of citation, guiding students to connect proper attribution to academic honesty, intellectual respect, and the credibility of their own work. Strategies include class discussions, quick writes, or reflective journals where students explain why citations matter in research and writing.
   * Provide opportunities for students to practice identifying when citation is needed using hands-on exercises:
   * Highlight sentences in sample texts that require attribution,
   * Compare properly cited versus uncited paraphrases,
   * Work in pairs or small groups to annotate research notes with citation decisions.
Use think-alouds to show how to integrate sources into writing with signal phrases, parenthetical citations, or footnotes–as appropriate to the citation style and source type–demonstrating the difference between attribution and plagiarism in context.

Classroom Considerations
   * Emphasize that planning is intentional, meaning-making phases of composition rather than a preliminary exercise to be completed and set aside. Model each step for students with sample prompts/tasks and allow time for students to apply the strategy to classroom tasks:
   * Do: Underline the words and phrases that establish what the assignment/task overview is asking students to do.
   * What: Highlight information that explains what topic/idea/consideration the student should write/speak about.
   * How: Place an asterisk (*) beside specific instructions that reveal how the written/spoken product should be crafted.

Grades 9-10 students increasingly make purposeful choices about emphasis and focus, drafting in order to strengthen how well the product communicates what matters most for its purpose and audience.

Drafting should be seen as the crucial step of translating ideas/information from planning into formal language, experimenting with organization, structure and voice based on the specific purpose, task, and audience.  Use guided feedback, checklists, and conferencing to help students evaluate whether their draft(s) clearly fulfill the assignment, communicate effectively to the intended audience, and stay focused on its purpose. Explore ways to provide students with freedom and autonomy in how they draft, such as: choosing the order in which they work (e.g., starting with the conclusion, outlining first, freewriting, or drafting key sections), the tools they use (e.g. paper, digital documents, speech-to-text, graphic organizers), and the strategies that best support their thinking. Allowing multiple drafting pathways respects individual learning styles and encourages ownership of the product. Support this autonomy by offering a menu of drafting options and encouraging students to reflect on which methods work best for them.
   * As part of the drafting process, ensure students have access to supports for high-quality communication and writing. This could include mentor texts, samples of student work (student or teacher created), and rubrics and checklists that outline what drafts should include. These supports should be used to help students understand key expectations, while also providing high quality examples to inspire and promote creativity. These models should not be used to box students in or limit them to a specific approach, style, or answer. For guidance on utilizing mentor texts, see Recommendation 2 of The Writing Guidebook.
   * Embed structured pause points throughout drafting to prompt students to reflect on their progress and make informed decisions about next steps in the composition process. Support students in assessing whether their draft continues to align with their purpose, audience, and intended meaning, and whether additional planning is needed before moving forward. Instruction should pair these pause points with explicit metacognitive strategies, such as self-questioning and teacher think-alouds, that model how writers/speakers recognize uncertainty, misalignment, or stalled thinking as signals to return to pre-writing or planning in order to refine ideas and strengthen the overall composition.
   * Support students’ use of digital tools for drafting by explicitly modeling how different platforms can strengthen their work and streamline this part of the process. For documenting sources, introduce tools such as citation generators to help students organize references and practice ethical research. Word processing programs, presentation software, and audio/video tools can be taught as flexible drafting spaces where students revise text, visuals, and recordings over time, reinforcing drafting as a recursive process

9-10.CW.6.3 Revise, edit, and rewrite written and spoken products, using support and collaboration with peers and adults.

This is a continued emphasis from grade 8. In grades 9-10, students revise, edit, and rewrite strategically, using feedback from peers, teachers, and/or other adults to make meaningful improvements rather than surface-level changes to their products. Students may seek targeted feedback, evaluate suggestions, revise ideas and structure, refine language, and edit for conventions and clarity. Grades 9-10 students independently decide when and how to apply feedback or return to other stages of the composition process in order to strengthen the effectiveness and precision of their written or spoken products.

Students…
   * revise written and spoken products, using support and collaboration with peers and adults
   * edit written and spoken products, using support and collaboration with peers and adults
   * rewrite written and spoken products, using support and collaboration with peers and adults

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	   * Which piece(s) of feedback helped you reconsider your ideas, organization, or purpose?
   * What revision(s) did you make that goes beyond correcting errors to address task, audience, and purpose?
   * Where did you add, remove, or reorganize content based on feedback and how did that strengthen your product?
   * What specific feedback did you seek, and how did it help you improve this piece?
   * How did you decide which feedback to apply in order to strengthen your work?
	   * Based on feedback, I revised ___ by ___ to strengthen ___.
   * My revisions focused primarily on ___, while my editing required a focus on ___.
   * I asked for feedback on ___ because I wanted to improve ___.
   * I chose to/not to apply feedback regarding ___ because ___.

Classroom Considerations
   * Ensure students understand the distinction between revision and editing as related but different tasks in the composition process. Emphasize that revision focuses on meaning, structure, and effectiveness (i.e., reconsidering ideas, reorganizing sections, refining evidence, and clarifying relationships among claims) while editing attends to sentence-level correctness, style, and conventions once ideas are largely in place, including considerations for formatting in line with style manuals as appropriate. Help students recognize that revision and editing are recursive rather than linear: writers/speakers may return to revision after editing reveals gaps in logic, or pause revision to edit a key passage for clarity or tone. Explicitly model strategies to support revision and editing that demonstrates the conceptual differences between the two.
   * To support revision, consider teaching the RADaR strategy, a mnemonic that outlines key revision-aligned actions:
   * Reorder: Shift the sequence of sentences, paragraphs, sections, ideas, etc. as needed to clarify, emphasize, and/or __ the intended purpose and message;
   * Add: Include or extend additional evidence, commentary, analysis, and/or interpretations to further develop the intended purpose and message
   * Delete: Remove redundant, distracting, or otherwise unnecessary evidence, commentary, analysis, and/or interpretations in light of other revisions to the product; and
   * Replace: Exchange redundant, distracting, or otherwise unnecessary evidence, commentary, analysis, and/or interpretations with new or insightful material in light of other revisions to the product.
To support editing, consider teaching the CAP strategy, a mnemonic that outlines key editing-aligned actions at the text-, paragraph, and sentence-levels:
   * Conventions: Verify overall formatting and style guide adherence → fonts, headings, spacing, margins, citations/references, and consistent document features.
   * Accuracy: Ensure each paragraph is internally consistent and free of surface errors → grammar, agreement, capitalization, punctuation.
   * Precision: Confirm each sentence is grammatically sound, readable, and polished → spelling, word choice, punctuation, and clarity.
   * Support students in engaging with multiple levels of feedback–teacher, peer, and self–to strengthen revision and editing.
   * Provide support through targeted teacher feedback, such as individual or small-group conferences that focus on high-leverage strategies rather than correcting every error. Consider grouping students based on shared instructional needs, patterns of feedback, or stage in the writing process. When working with a small group during a conference, provide other students with clearly defined revision or editing tasks–such as targeted checklists, task cards, or exemplars–to ensure their work remains purposeful and focused.
   * Alternatively, have students engage in structured peer feedback or independent revision/editing tasks aligned to the same instructional goals. Structure peer feedback with clear expectations, using sentence frames, checklists, or focused prompts to guide attention to specific aspects of the draft and prevent feedback from becoming overly general or surface-level.
   * Teach students to engage in self-feedback by using strategies such as color-coding to analyze their own writing for patterns (e.g., claims, evidence, transitions, or recurring errors) and identify next steps independently. Ensure students understand that seeking feedback from teachers, peers, and themselves is a normal and essential part of how writers and speakers develop ideas and refine their work, and that sharing drafts at multiple stages supports growth rather than signaling weakness or failure.
For more guidance on feedback, see Recommendation 4 of the Writing Guidebook.
   * Support students in using digital tools strategically to strengthen both revision and editing as ongoing, recursive processes. Model how features such as revision history in digital documents allow writers to track changes over time, reflect on decision-making, and revisit earlier drafts when feedback or new insights warrant additional revision. Model and reinforce collaborative feedback practices by using and having students use comments, suggestions, and track-changes tools to signal whether feedback targets ideas and organization (revision) or sentence-level clarity and conventions (editing). Encourage students to use automated tools (e.g., grammar and usage checkers) as diagnostic supports rather than final arbiters, guiding them to evaluate suggested changes for accuracy, purpose, and voice. When appropriate, incorporate browser add-ons such that allow for voice-recorded feedback to clarify expectations, prioritize next steps, and humanize the feedback process.

Students may format text, integrate visuals or media, rehearse delivery, and revise based on the chosen platform or context, making intentional choices about how each element contributes to meaning. Grades 9-10 students independently select, apply, and refine digital tools and multimodal elements to enhance understanding and engagement for their audience.

Students...

   * How should I integrate images, charts, audio, etc. to clarify/emphasize ideas and/or improve comprehension/ engagement?

Classroom Considerations
   * NCDPI does not endorse specific digital tools or companies. This objective allows for the use of a wide variety of tools and digital resources. Educators are encouraged to find multiple ways for students to demonstrate learning, and implementation decisions should be based on student characteristics and public school unit policies. Teachers are encouraged to expose students to multiple types of digital tools for various purposes, using 1:1 devices, computer labs, mobile carts, or group instruction as needed. As students utilize tools, ensure their data and privacy is protected and that technology is being used responsibly. For additional guidance on digital tools and best practices, see the NC Digital Teaching & Learning Standards.
   * Support students with creating written and spoken products that intentionally incorporate multiple modes, such as visuals, imagery, audio, and video. These expectations should be clearly embedded in learning tasks and rubrics so students understand what high-quality multimodal work looks like. Model effective use of multimodal tools and provide guided support to build student independence and confidence (e.g. showing how to navigate or use a platform for the first time, exploring built in features, providing ongoing feedback on student usage, etc.). Use mentor texts to highlight strong examples, asking students to analyze how specific modes are used purposefully to enhance meaning and communication. For example, share a digital slideshow that effectively combines images, captions, and voice narration, guiding the class to discuss how each mode helps clarify the main idea and engages the audience. Similarly, consider presenting a video poem that blends spoken word, background music, and visuals, and lead students in analyzing how these elements work together to strengthen the poem’s emotional impact. Adapt these examples to specific tasks and student needs.
   * Support students in producing, publishing, and presenting their work by explicitly drawing attention to key aesthetic elements, such as contrast, repetition, alignment, proximity, color, effective use of images, etc. Through modeling and collaborative discussion, help students understand why design features matter and how they influence clarity, readability, and audience engagement with a text. Ensure students understand the difference between using elements creatively and using them intentionally to achieve a specific purpose or impact. Students should learn that design choices should enhance their message and communication rather than distract from or weaken it. For example, there is a difference between using multiple bright colors, animated text, and decorative images in a slideshow because they look “fun,” versus designing a webpage with bold headline font and a high-contrast background to draw attention to key information or callouts.
   * Encourage students to publish and present their work to authentic audiences–such as through blogs, wikis, websites, or school-approved social networks–and by sharing recorded or live presentations beyond the classroom. Doing so increases student motivation, accountability, and pride in their work by showing that their ideas have real purpose and reach. Collaborate with a media specialist to help identify appropriate platforms, address digital citizenship, and support safe and effective publishing. Providing structured opportunities for peers and outside audiences to view and respond to student work reinforces that learning is meant to be shared, not created solely for the teacher. For example, have students participate in peer presentation sessions or gallery walks where classmates leave specific feedback/responses on each other’s work. They could also publish student writing/speaking on a class blog where families and community members can be invited to view and comment. As these options are considered, ensure relevant policies on student data and privacy are followed.
   * In addition to live presentations, explore ways to have students also present asynchronously using platforms that allow them to record video or audio of themselves and their presentation. As a teacher, this could provide a flexible way to assess student understanding, revisit responses over time, and give more targeted feedback. An asynchronous approach would also be valuable to students because it lowers presentation anxiety, supports multiple learning styles, and gives them time to reflect, revise, and communicate their ideas more clearly to an authentic audience.

Glossary
	Argument - The position an author or speaker establishes in an attempt to persuade others to think or feel differently about an issue, to change how they act, or to resolve disagreements between themselves and other parties. Authors or speakers accomplish these ends by presenting claims supported with reasons, evidence, and appeals.

Call to Action - A concise prompt designed to spur an immediate response from an audience, guiding them toward a specific action and/or goal.

Claim - A specific point or assertion that authors or speakers want the audience to accept as true and act on. Claims are debatable and require supporting evidence.

Counterclaim - A challenge or objection to a point or set of points that identifies an issue, drawback, consequence, alternative interpretation, and/or unconsidered evidence.

Evidence - Facts or information (e.g., quotes, examples, anecdotes, data, etc.) that can be referred to in order to support an opinion, point, or claim.

Classroom Considerations
   * Support students in differentiating claims from specific claims by explicitly teaching them to notice key features of specific claims. Some features students can be made aware of include that specific claims avoid overly broad or absolute language, clarify the scope of the argument by using conditional or qualified language, and signal a clear relationship to evidence. Display claims and specific claims side-by-side (e.g. Social media is bad for teens. vs. Excessive social media use among teens can increase anxiety and reduce face-to-face communication skills when screen time replaces in-person interaction.) and point out differences between them to build student awareness of subtle distinctions. Provide opportunities for students to compare the features of claims and specific claims through partnered discussion tasks where students identify what makes the precise claim more focused, defensible, and evidence-linked. Additionally, allow students to apply understandings of crafting precise claims by providing time for students to revise general/non-specific claims so that they are more narrow by adding context, scope, and evidence (e.g., add population, timeframe, or specific circumstances) or by having students conduct peer reviews of each other’s persuasive texts. Sentence frames that capture the nuance of specific claims can scaffold student revision as they refine claims to make them more specific:
   * Implementing/introducing ___ in ___ leads to ___ because ___.
   * ___ can influence ___ by ___.
   * In ___, ___ has the potential to ___, particularly when ___.
   * ___ improves/reduces ___ by ___.
   * Implementing/introducing causes ___, particularly when ___.
   * Model how to develop claims and counterclaims using well-placed rhetorical techniques. Explicitly teach common rhetorical techniques and their generally understood impact on audiences so that students can effectively determine which techniques might best support the development of claims and counterclaims. Some techniques to consider include appeals to logic (logos), appeals to emotion (pathos), appeals to credibility (ethos), appeals timeliness (kairos), figurative language, repetition devices, sound devices, front-loading key claims, delaying key claims, delaying the strongest evidence, concession, refutation, qualification, and rhetorical questions.
   * Explicitly teach various organizational structures at the text-, paragraph-, and sentence-level to support students with crafting cohesive persuasive texts, and emphasize the rhetorical function or impact of those different structures, so that students can choose structures that best support cohesion of their argument. At the text-level in particular, students should understand that how sections of text are sequenced and developed will affect the perceived momentum and impact of the argument by establishing how quickly, how clearly, and how persuasively their audience is led through the presented reasoning. Encourage students to consider how they want their argument to feel to their audience by its conclusion and teach structures that produce different outcomes:
   * at the text-level, consider modeling these structures to students and discussing their relative strengths/weaknesses in the contexts of their arguments: problem-solution, cause-effect, compare-contrast, and layered/cumulative claims;
   * at the paragraph-level, consider modeling these structures to students and discussing their relative strengths/weaknesses in the contexts of their arguments: claim development cycle, logical flow signposting, and paragraph chaining; and
   * at the sentence-level, consider modeling these structures to students and discussing their relative strengths/weaknesses in the contexts of their arguments: cause-effect, contrast framing, and qualification framing.
Where applicable, draw students attention to how different organizational patterns can be used to structure sentences, paragraphs, sections, and entire texts.
   * Ensure students understand that calls to action range in explicitness from subtle to overt and that calls to action may not always strengthen or fit well within a given argument. Where they are appropriate, calls to action allow the audience to reflect on, consider, or respond to the argument. To help students understand how to frame calls to action effectively, consider teaching subtle approaches that imply reflection through consequences, moderate approaches that suggest ideas without commanding, and overt approaches that directly urge action. Provide guided rewriting exercises, signal phrase lists (e.g., therefore, as a result, it is essential that, we must), and sample conclusions at varying levels of explicitness to help students draft concluding sections that convey urgency, significance, and actionable next steps aligned with the argument.
   * When planning for instruction, connect student knowledge from reading, interpreting, and analysing persuasive texts with drafting their own original communications. Provide students with a range of sample mentor texts so they can explore various styles and apply similar techniques to their own writing. As students craft their own products, ensure they engage in a recursive writing process that allows them to plan, draft, receive feedback on, and refine their communications. For more ideas and resources on the use of mentor texts, the writing process, and students transferring techniques to their own writing, see CW.6, The Writing Guidebook, and the text choices and features resource.

In grades 9-10, students move beyond simply stating a topic and previewing information to strategically orienting an audience to how complex ideas and explanations will be developed across a text. Support students in articulating not only what the focus is, but how the text will unfold (e.g., explaining causes before effects, comparing perspectives before synthesizing conclusions, or moving from background information to analysis). Model how introductions can anticipate audience expectations by previewing relationships among ideas rather than only listing sections. Introduce and model the use of sentence frames that prioritize the relationships between ideas, forthcoming sections, etc. such as
   * By examining ___, we can see ___, which leads to ___.
   * ___ interacts with ___, revealing ___.
   * Through ___, the author shows ___, setting up ___.
Ensure students are aware of how introductory sentences can explicitly or subtly signal the organization of the body/main sections of their product and compare introductions that merely announce topics to those that establish a clear line of reasoning, explanatory, or analytical approach. Students should use their stated focus and organizational preview to guide structural decisions throughout the product, including paragraphing, sectioning, and the use of headings or subheadings when appropriate.
   * Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with developing and refining explanations and analyses of complex ideas using relevant, well-integrated supporting information. Grades 9-10 students should focus on depth, precision, and explanatory/analytical power, selecting information that not only supports a topic but clarifies relationships among ideas and concepts. Instruction should emphasize synthesizing information from multiple sources or categories (e.g., definitions, examples, data, quotations, historical or contextual information) to explain how and why ideas function or relate. Model how to strengthen explanations for specific purposes (e.g. clarifying relationships among ideas; layering evidence; synthesizing multiple sources; deepening explanations/analyses; establishing precision) and provide sentence frames that are applicable to each purpose.
   * [Overview of idea] connects to ___ because ___.
   * Understanding ___ helps explain why ___.
   * While the data indicate ___, the example of ___ clarifies how this occurs in practice.
   * [Summary of evidence] builds on the earlier evidence by showing ___.
   * When ___ is considered alongside ___, it becomes clear that ___.
   * Historical context helps explain why ___, which is evident in ___.
   * This pattern suggests that ___.
   * This distinction is important because ___.
   * This example complicates the idea that ___.
Support students in evaluating the relative importance of details and determining where information best fits within the overall structure of the text. Activities might include reorganizing supporting information to improve clarity, revising explanations to better connect evidence to ideas, or justifying why certain details belong in one section rather than another. Build in opportunities for students to discuss how and why pieces of information or evidence are best suited to a given section, paragraph, or sentence; support depth and analytical voice through brief writing tasks that prompt students to examine ideas from multiple analytical angles (e.g., cause-effect relationships, comparison or contrast among ideas, progression or development over time, problem-solution structures, part-to-whole reasoning, or claim-evidence relationships) and articulate how each angle influences how the presented information is perceived by the audience.
   * Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with clarifying relationships among explanations using purposeful transitions, precise language, and domain-specific vocabulary. In grades 9-10, transitions should do more than connect ideas smoothly; they should signal how explanations relate across sentences, paragraphs, and sections. Teach students to select transitions based on the relationship they want to communicate (e.g., building on, contrasting with, qualifying, limiting, synthesizing) and to vary both word choice and placement. Model revision of vague or general language into more precise academic phrasing, emphasizing verbs and nouns that reflect disciplinary ways of thinking (e.g., revising general verbs such as shows or talks about to discipline-specific verbs like demonstrates, examines, or reveals, and replacing vague nouns like things or ideas with precise terms such as concept, claim, or evidence). Support students in using domain-specific vocabulary accurately and intentionally, including defining or contextualizing terms for an audience when needed. Instruction should reinforce that cohesion is created not only through transitions, but through repeated key terms, consistent focus, and explicit references back to earlier explanations.
   * Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with crafting conclusions that synthesize and reinforce explanations across an entire text. Grades 9-10 students move beyond brief, single-paragraph conclusions toward multi-paragraph concluding sections when appropriate, especially in longer written or spoken products. Rather than simply restating information, students should use conclusions to synthesize major ideas, clarify relationships among explanations, and reinforce the overall understanding developed. Model how conclusions can revisit the focus from a broader perspective, connect key explanations across sections, and highlight the significance or implications of the information presented. Support students in distinguishing between repetition and synthesis by having them combine ideas, explain patterns, or articulate what becomes clear when ideas are considered together. Encourage students to determine which moves are best suited for their concluding sections by proving time to reflect on or discuss questions related to the development and body of their product such as
   * What has become clear/er now about my focal topic/text?
   * How do these explanations/analyses connect?
   * What pattern(s) runs across sections?
   * Why does this information matter beyond this product?
Emphasize that longer conclusions work when each paragraph has a distinct function (e.g., synthesis → pattern → significance), not when ideas are restated with different wording.
   * When planning for instruction, connect student knowledge from reading, interpreting, and analysing analytical expository texts with drafting their own original communications. Provide students with a range of sample mentor texts so they can explore various styles and apply similar techniques to their own writing. As students craft their own products, ensure they engage in a recursive writing process that allows them to plan, draft, receive feedback on, and refine their communications. For more ideas and resources on the use of mentor texts, the writing process, and students transferring techniques to their own writing, see CW.6, The Writing Guidebook, and the text choices and features resource.

Glossary
	Descriptive Text - A creative or artistic text that seeks to describe something—an object, person, place, experience, emotion, situation, etc.—to paint a vivid, sensory impression in the mind of the audience.

Figurative Language - A type of descriptive language used to convey meaning in a way that differs from its literal meaning and is often used to add style to written or spoken language or to simplify a complex idea.

Literary Elements - The core building blocks of a text (e.g., plot, setting, theme, characters, mood, allegory, conflict, etc.).

Literary Techniques - The stylistic choices the author makes to add depth, emotion, and meaning using literary elements and figurative language (e.g., foreshadowing, imagery, metaphor, etc.).

Narrative Text - A text that seeks to tell a story—real or imagined.

Construct spoken and written poetic, descriptive, and narrative texts to develop real or imagined experiences, events, or ideas and convey theme(s) using effective technique, well-chosen details, and well-structured sequences.

Classroom Considerations
   * Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with crafting introductions that engage the audience and orient them to the text’s context, literary elements, and thematic focus. Help students brainstorm multiple ways to immediately capture audience attention, such as beginning in the middle of action, opening with vivid dialogue, presenting a compelling scenario, or introducing a provocative or insightful observation. To build student capacity in making these choices, use short, collaborative writing and speaking activities in which the teacher provides the premise of a narrative, descriptive, or poetic work, and students work together to brainstorm and experiment with several opening options. Encourage students to select openings that are the most engaging, establish perspective, and support the intended theme(s). Guide students in determining how many perspectives are necessary to fully develop the intended theme(s) and in introducing those perspectives through character voice, narrator stance, or speaker point of view. Have students analyze sample introductions, discuss how different perspectives are established, and justify which approach best signals the intended theme(s). Teach students how to signal the thematic focus of their product by highlighting key conflicts, depicting scenes relevant to intended theme topics (e.g., redemption, justice, friendship, etc.), or establishing recurring motifs in the opening lines or through the choice of perspective. Encourage students to tweak, abandon, or embed new literary elements as needed to signal the thematic focus and emphasize intentional use of literary elements in introductions–including plot events, structure, characterization, perspective, setting, style, figurative language, symbolic patterns, and genre/form–to establish and advance perspectives while engaging the audience. Ensure students understand that layered, compelling introductions involve strategically combining context, perspective, and literary elements to create depth and invite curiosity through techniques such as vivid sensory imagery, suggestive dialogue, tension-filled action, or thought-provoking observations.
   * Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with developing experiences, events, and literary elements that reinforce the intended theme(s). Teach students to use telling and sensory details as purposeful choices rather than decorative description by selecting and expanding intentionally chosen concrete moments that reveal character motivation, emotional shifts, setting, or consequences. Guide students to ask which specific actions, gestures, thoughts, or sensory impressions best show what matters in a scene, and to replace general statements with details that allow the audience to infer meaning (e.g., what a character does, notices, avoids, or reacts to). Emphasize that effective sensory detail is selective and intentional, helping the audience understand why an experience or event is significant to the theme rather than simply what happens. Additionally, explicitly teach students how to use rhetorical techniques to shape meaning and influence audience interpretation. Ensure students understand that rhetorical techniques are not limited to argumentative or persuasive texts, but are also essential tools in literary writing for shaping meaning, emphasizing theme(s), revealing character and perspective, and guiding how the audience interprets experiences, events, and language choices. Model how repetition and parallelism can emphasize key ideas or emotional states, how juxtaposition can highlight contrasts or tensions within an experience, and how tone shifts, irony, or understatement can signal complexity or deepen thematic impact. Support students in making rhetorical choices based on purpose by asking them to consider what they want the audience to notice, feel, or question at key moments in the text. Encourage students to revise by examining whether rhetorical techniques clarify relationships among ideas, strengthen emphasis, or guide interpretation, and by removing or refining techniques that distract from or dilute the intended theme(s). Reinforce that strong writing integrates telling details and rhetorical techniques so that experiences and events not only unfold but also communicate meaning clearly and powerfully. Help students diagnose weaknesses in developing experiences, events, and literary elements by prompting them to examine their drafts with questions such as
   * Do the experiences/events connect to the theme?
   * Are stylistic choices intentional and varied?
   * Do sensory and telling details clarify meaning?
   * Do dialogue and actions reveal character or advance theme(s)?
Encourage alignment of events, experiences, and literary elements with the intended theme(s).
   * Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with arranging topics, experiences, events, or characters in sequences that clearly and meaningfully convey the intended theme(s). Help students understand that sequencing is a deliberate writing decision that shapes how the audience notices relationships, patterns, and significance–not just the order in which things happen. Teach multiple sequencing strategies, such as chronological development, strategic use of flashback, thematic grouping of moments, or other purposeful arrangements, and guide students to choose the structure that best highlights the theme(s) they are developing.  Support students in strengthening thematic clarity by showing how literary techniques–such as contrast between moments or characters, parallel experiences, repeated situations, or recurring motifs–can be used to draw attention to pivotal events and reinforce patterns across the text. Encourage students to sustain focus by elaborating on moments, details, and relationships that are essential to the theme, rather than introducing loosely connected scenes or ideas. Model how effective sequencing builds meaning cumulatively, with each section deepening the audience’s understanding of what matters most. Help students diagnose weaknesses in sequencing and development by prompting them to examine their drafts with questions such as
   * Does the order of events or experiences help the audience understand the theme more clearly?
   * Do transitions and connections between sections signal how ideas or moments relate?
   * Do repeated elements, contrasts, or turning points reinforce important relationships?
   * Are key moments developed with enough detail and emphasis to carry thematic weight?
   * Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students in crafting conclusions that synthesize and reinforce ideas, events, and perspectives developed across narrative, descriptive, or poetic texts. Help students understand that effective literary conclusions often return to something already established earlier in the text–such as a motif, image, situation, or perspective–but with added insight, change, or significance. Emphasize that conclusions should reveal what has been learned, changed, or clarified as a result of what occurred. Support students with specific methods for concluding literary texts, such as revisiting a recurring image or symbol and showing how its meaning has shifted; echoing an earlier scene, action, or line of dialogue while revealing new understanding; or reflecting on how a character’s perspective, attitude, or awareness has changed by the end of the text. Model how writers can accomplish this by reusing key language with purposeful variation, placing familiar imagery in a new context, or slowing down to focus on a final moment that captures the theme’s significance. Guide students in drawing attention to patterns across the text by helping them trace repeated moments, details, or ideas during drafting and then intentionally return to one of those elements in the conclusion. Encourage students to explain or imply why that element matters now, given everything the audience has seen. Help students diagnose weaknesses in their conclusions by asking
   * Does the ending return to an important idea, image, or experience from earlier in the text?
   * Does it show change, insight, or significance rather than simply stopping the story?
   * Does it clarify how the theme(s) developed across events, experiences, or perspectives?
   * Does the final moment feel earned based on what came before?
Encourage rereading openings alongside endings, experimenting with different focal moments or images, and refining language to ensure the conclusion reinforces the text’s overall meaning.
   * When planning for instruction, connect student knowledge from reading, interpreting, and analysing poetic, descriptive, and narrative texts with drafting their own original communications. Provide students with a range of sample mentor texts so they can explore various styles and apply similar techniques to their own writing. As students craft their own products, ensure they engage in a recursive writing process that allows them to plan, draft, receive feedback on, and refine their communications.    * Collective nouns (singular when acting as a unit, plural when acting as individuals)
   * Inverted sentences (identify the true subject first, e.g., There are many books)

	Students need to know the progressive aspect is formed with a past, present, or future form of “be” (i.e., state of being) + a progressive verb “-ing” to show ongoing actions (e.g., is running, were eating, will be fishing). Progressive verbs function to indicate actions in progress.
	Environmentalists are cleaning up the trash that is polluting our oceans.

	Use modal auxiliaries
	Students need to know modal verbs (e.g., can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would, ought to, need to, used to, have to) show ability, possibility, necessity, obligation, or permission. Modals are a specific type of auxiliary verb that add meaning to, and are always followed by, the base form of a main verb.
	People should clean up the trash that is polluting our oceans.
	4
	Form and use perfect aspect
	Students need to know the perfect aspect is formed with a past, present, or future form of “have” + a past participle to show completed actions related to another point in time (e.g., has walked, had eaten, will have fished). Perfect aspect functions to indicate actions that are finished relative to the present or past.
	Oceans currents have greatly affected our weather.
	5
	Form and use perfect progressive aspect
	Students need to know the perfect progressive aspect is formed with a past, present, or future tense of “have” + been + a progressive verb (-ing) to show an action that was ongoing for a period of time leading up to a specific point in time (e.g., has been running, had been eating, will have been fishing). This aspect emphasizes duration in relation to completion or a reference moment, highlighting both the ongoing nature of the action and its connection to another time.
	She has been texting her friend group about this evening's school dance since yesterday.
	6
	Recognize and correct inappropriate tense shifts
	Students maintain consistent verb tense within sentences or related sentences. Students need to know that verbs in a sentence or related sentences should maintain consistent tense unless the timeline changes.
	Last night he studied for the quiz, reviewed his notes, and completed the practice questions.
	6
	Distinguish between active and passive voice
	Students need to know that active voice emphasizes the subject performing the action, while passive voice emphasizes the subject receiving the action. They also need to know that passive constructions typically use a form of “be” + past participle.
	Active Voice:
The students designed the posters for Spirit Week.

Passive Voice:
The posters were designed by the students for Spirit Week.
	7
	Use active and passive voice appropriate to purpose, audience, task
	Students select active or passive voice to match their writing goal (e.g., active for directness, passive for emphasis on the action). Students need to know which form emphasizes the subject or action.
	Active Voice:
Our dedicated team won the championship game after months of practice.

Passive Voice:
The championship game was won by our dedicated team after months of practice.
	8
	Use indicative, imperative, and interrogative moods
	Students need to know that verb mood indicates the function or purpose of a sentence. In the indicative mood, verbs express statements or facts. In the imperative mood, verbs give commands or instructions. In the interrogative mood, verbs form questions. Students also need to recognize that verb forms, word order, and auxiliary verbs often signal the mood of a sentence.
	Indicative Mood:
We are taking the bus home from school tomorrow.

Imperative Mood:
Finish your meal before you can have dessert.

Interrogative Mood:
Did you upload your homework to the class website?
	8
	Use conditional and subjunctive moods
	Students need to know that the conditional mood expresses hypothetical situations and possible, but not guaranteed, outcomes by often using if/if-were clauses and modal auxiliaries. The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, demands, or possibilities that are not factual. Students need to recognize that the subjunctive often appears in clauses following verbs expressing desire, recommendation, or uncertainty and that the verb in the subjunctive is usually the base form (i.e., no -s, -ed, or -ing ending). The past subjunctive, especially with the verb to be (e.g., were), can sound unusual or “formal” because it does not follow normal past-tense patterns and is often replaced by was in casual speech; however, using were correctly signals a hypothetical or non-factual situation, which is essential for expressing wishes, contrary-to-fact statements, or imagined scenarios.
	Conditional Mood:
If the weather improves, we will go for a hike tomorrow.

If the data were more complete, the results would be more reliable.

Subjunctive Mood:
Members of the book club wished their next read were available for pick-up.

The committee recommended that the proposal be revised before approval.

Participles act as adjectives: present participles are formed with -ing , and past participles often end in -ed for regular verbs or take an irregular form. Infinitives are formed with to + base verb and can function as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs. Students also need to recognize that verbals allow sentences to vary in structure, provide additional detail, and express actions, qualities, or purposes in different grammatical roles.

Present Participle (as an adjective):

	Choose appropriate modifier based on what is being modified
	Students need to know modifiers must match the type of word they modify: adjectives with nouns and adverbs with verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Students need to know that placing the wrong type of modifier changes or confuses the meaning. They also need to understand basic placement rules: modifiers should be close to the word they describe.
	We saw a beautiful sunset.

The girl spoke softly.
	2
	Form and use comparative adjectives/ adverbs
	Students need to know comparatives are forms of adjectives or adverbs that show ranges of difference between two things (e.g., taller, more quickly). Students need to know that comparatives often use “-er” or “more” depending on word structure. They also need to recognize how to compare actions or qualities accurately using these forms.
	Comparative Adjective:
The area of the large rectangle is the sum of the two smaller rectangles.

Comparative Adverb:
The area of the large rectangle is found more easily by adding the two smaller rectangles.
	3
	Form and use superlative adjectives/
adverbs
	Students need to know superlatives are forms of adjectives or adverbs that show the highest or extreme degree among three or more things (e.g., tallest, most quickly). Students need to know that superlatives often use “-est” or “most” depending on word structure. They also need to recognize how to identify or form the “most extreme” quality in a set.
	Superlative Adjective:
Heat always moves from the hottest object to the cooler one.

Superlative Adverb:
Heat moves from a warmer object to a cooler one the fastest when they touch each other.
	3
	Use relative adverbs
	Students need to know that relative adverbs (where, when, why) introduce clauses that provide additional information about a noun or verb. They need to know that these clauses follow the word they describe and function like adjectives or adverbs, explaining location (where), time (when), or reason (why). Students also need to recognize that these relative adverb clauses cannot stand alone and always link their clause to a specific noun or verb in the main clause.
	I need to use a pictorial model when showing my work for the word problem.
	3
	Order adjectives according to conventional patterns
	Students need to know that when multiple adjectives modify a noun, they usually follow a specific sequence (e.g., quantity, opinion, size, age, color, origin, material). Students need to know the conventional order of adjectives to avoid confusion. They also need to recognize how each adjective type answers a different descriptive question.
	Opinion → Age → Material → Noun:
The beautiful old wooden box contained many valuables.
	4
	Recognize
and correct misplaced modifiers
	Students need to know that a misplaced modifier is a word or phrase that is too far from the word it describes, which can create confusion or an unintended meaning. They need to know that modifiers must be placed as close as possible to the word they describe. To identify misplaced modifiers, students should locate the word or phrase being described and check whether the modifier is positioned directly next to it. They also need to identify key words in the sentence to attach modifiers correctly.
	Misplaced Modifier:
I almost listened to music for three hours while doing homework.

Corrected Misplaced Modifier:
I listened to music for almost three hours while doing homework.
	7
	Recognize
and correct dangling modifiers
	Students need to know that a dangling modifier is a descriptive word or phrase that does not clearly modify any word in the sentence, often leaving the sentence confusing or illogical. They need to know that every modifier must have a clear word it describes. To identify dangling modifiers, students should determine the intended subject or main noun for the modifier; if the intended noun is missing or unclear, the modifier is dangling and the sentence needs revision.
	Dangling Modifier:
Walking to school, the bus passed by.

Dangling Modifier Corrected:
While I was walking to school, the bus passed by.
	7

Phrase, Clause, & Cohesion Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use coordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet) connect words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance. They also need to recognize that coordinating conjunctions join ideas without changing the grammatical function of the connected elements.
	She is small but strong!
	1
	Use subordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, if, when, since, while, etc.) connect a dependent clause to an independent clause. They also need to recognize that the dependent clause cannot stand alone and functions to provide time, cause, condition, contrast, or reason.
	I stayed inside because it was storming.

If it snows, we will build a snowman.
	2
	Use prepositional phrases
	Students need to know that a prepositional phrase consists of a preposition and its object (noun or pronoun) and functions as an adjective or adverb. They also need to recognize how prepositional phrases provide information about location, time, direction, cause, or manner.
	The price of toys went up because of the high demand in the store.
	3
	Use correlative conjunctions
	Students need to know that correlative conjunctions work in pairs (e.g., either–or, neither–nor, both–and, not only–but also) to connect balanced elements of equal grammatical importance. They also need to recognize that each part of the pair must attach to similar structures (e.g., noun to noun, clause to clause).
	Both the mountains and the rivers affect where people build cities and farms in the United States.
	5
	Form and use verbal phrases for variety, precision, and nuance
	Students need to know that verbal phrases are built around verbals—gerunds (-ing forms functioning as nouns), participles (present -ing or past -ed/irregular forms functioning as adjectives), and infinitives (to + verb forms functioning as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs). They need to recognize that verbal phrases combine these verbals with other words (modifiers or objects) to create noun, adjective, or adverb phrases and that verbal phrases can appear at the beginning, middle, and end of sentences. Students also need to know that using verbal phrases allows sentences to vary in structure, add descriptive detail, and convey subtle nuances of meaning, such as timing, cause, or purpose and that verbal phrases can be layered with each other for increased sophistication.
	Gerund Phrase (as a noun):
Analyzing primary sources requires questioning assumptions and evaluating context.

Present Participial Phrase (as an adjective):
Clutching the letter in her trembling hands, she realized the truth had arrived too late.

Past Participial Phrase (as an adjective):
Worn by decades of use, the book still held its authority.

Infinitive Phrase (as an adverb):
The city invested in more efficient public transportation to reduce energy consumption.

Infinitive Phrase (as a noun):
To forgive after such betrayal felt impossible.

Layered Verbal Phrases (participial + infinitive):
Hoping to persuade undecided voters, the candidate delivered a speech designed to highlight economic stability.
	9-12
	Use parallel structure to emphasize parity between ideas
	Students need to know that parallel structure means using the same grammatical form for words, phrases, or clauses that are joined or compared. They need to know that elements that can be made parallel include lists of words or phrases, paired ideas connected by coordinating or correlative conjunctions, and clauses within a sentence. Students also need to recognize that maintaining parallel forms ensures clarity, readability, and equal emphasis across ideas in a sentence and that when combined with extended repetition, parallelism lends itself to emphasis of ideas.
	Parallel Verbs in a List:
The researcher collected, analyzed, and interpreted the data.

Parallel Gerund Phrases in a List:
Effective study habits include reviewing notes regularly, completing practice problems, and seeking feedback.

Parallel Infinitive Phrases in a List:
She wanted to leave quietly, to avoid conflict, and to preserve her dignity.

Parallel Structure with Correlative Conjunctions:
The policy aims not only to reduce costs but also to improve accessibility.

Parallel Dependent Clauses:
When the evidence is examined carefully and when the context is considered honestly, the argument becomes more convincing.
	9-12

	K

	Students need to know that proper nouns naming specific people begin with capital letters.
	Catie is my friend.

I sit next to John.

	Capitalize dates
	Students need to know that the day and month in a date are capitalized.
	My birthday is on Monday, April 20.
	1
	Capitalize holidays
	Students need to know that names of holidays are capitalized.
	We wear red on Valentine’s Day.
	2
	Capitalize brand names
	Students need to know that specific brands or trademarks are capitalized.
	I drink Gatorade after every baseball game.
	2
	Capitalize names of places
	Students need to know that specific geographic locations are capitalized.
	We took a trip to the Greenville Zoo.

I used to live in Florida.
	2
	Capitalize titles of texts
	Students need to know that–generally–nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, and subordinating conjunctions in titles of books, articles, and other works are capitalized. Generally, articles, prepositions, and coordinating conjunctions are not unless they are the first or last word of a title.
	Charlotte is very caring and kind towards Wilbur in Charlotte’s Web.

In The One and Only Ivan, Ivan spends most of his life in a shopping mall.

	Students need to know that the first word of a speaker’s statement is capitalized, even within a sentence or paragraph.
	Continuous Dialogue: Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.

Interrupted Dialogue Response (Dialogue tag interrupts two sentences):  “Hard work pays off,” said Paul, “We’ll need to keep practicing every day!”

Interrupted Dialogue Response (Dialogue tag interrupts a single sentence):  “I knew you could do it,” said Marcus, “because you practice harder than anyone else.”
	3
	Capitalize abbreviations
	Students need to know that standard abbreviations for proper nouns or titles are capitalized (e.g., Doctor→Dr., Junior→Jr., United States of America →U.S.A, Street→St., frequently asked questions→FAQ, etc.).
	Dr. Jones says that all students need sufficient exercise.
	4
	Capitalize proper adjectives
	Students need to know that adjectives derived from proper nouns are capitalized.
	My love of Italian food comes from my childhood.
	4
	Capitalize formal titles
	Students need to know that official titles are capitalized when used with a person’s name.
	The bravery of Captain Adams saved the entire crew that day.
	4
	Use capitalization appropriately to indicate direct speech and text quotes
	Students need to know that the first word of a direct speech statement and the first word of a complete quoted sentence are capitalized. Quoted statements that cannot stand alone are not capitalized.
	Concluding With a Quote (quoting a complete sentence): The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting an incomplete sentence): The author explains that ocean currents are “powerful forces that shape Earth’s climate” and affect weather patterns around the world.

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting a complete sentence): The author states, “Ocean currents move warm and cold water around the Earth,” to explain how they affect climate.

Comma Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use commas in dates
	Students need to know that a comma separates the day and year in full dates.
	We moved on June 26, 2023.
	1
	Use commas to separate words in a series
	Students need to know that commas separate three or more items in a list.
	My favorite colors are pink, green, and purple.
	1
	Use commas before coordinating conjunctions in compound sentences
	Students need to know that a comma comes before coordinating conjunctions (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) when joining two independent clauses.
	I wanted to play outside, but it started to rain.
	1
	Use commas in addresses
	Students need to know that in addresses written in sentences, commas separate the street address, the city, and the state. When addresses are written on separate lines, a comma is generally only needed between the city and state.
	The birthday party is at 123 Happy Place, Cary, North Carolina.

123 Happy Place
Cary, North Carolina
	2
	Use commas in greetings and closings
	Students need to know that commas follow the greeting or closing in letters, emails, cards, etc.
	Example Greeting:
Dear Mom,

Example Closing:
Love, Mary
	2
	Use commas in dialogue
	Students need to know that commas separate the words of a speaker from dialogue tags.
	Continuous Dialogue: Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.

Interrupted Dialogue Response:  “I knew you could do it,” said Marcus, “because you practice harder than anyone else.”
	3
	Use commas to indicate direct address
	Students need to know that commas set off names or titles of people being spoken to. When direct addresses are at the beginning of a sentence, the comma comes after them. When direct addresses are at the end of a sentence, the comma comes before.
	John, I agree with what you said about the photograph helping readers understand deforestation better.

I agree with what you said about the photograph helping readers understand deforestation better, John.
	4
	Use commas to set off “yes” and “no”
	Students need to know that commas follow introductory yes or no in a sentence.
	Yes, I do believe that I included enough supporting evidence.
	4
	Use commas to indicate direct speech and quotations
	Students need to know that commas separate quoted material from the rest of the sentence unless the quoted material is integrated into the grammatical structure of the sentence. When quoted material concludes a sentence, a comma comes only before the opening quotation mark. When quoted material is introduced mid-sentence, a comma comes before the opening quotation mark and before the closing quotation mark.
	Concluding With a Quote (quoting a complete sentence): The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting a complete sentence): The author states, “Ocean currents move warm and cold water around the Earth,” to explain how they affect climate.

Integrating Quote into Grammatical Structure (quoting an incomplete sentence): The author explains that ocean currents are “powerful forces that shape Earth’s climate” and affect weather patterns around the world.
	5
	Use commas after introductory elements
	Students need to know that commas follow introductory words, phrases, or clauses.
	Single Word:
Finally, I believe that students should have longer recess because research shows that it leads to better mental health.

Prepositional Phrases as an Introductory Element:
In the second paragraph of the article, the author provides evidence that recycling helps reduce pollution.

Complex Sentence (When Dependent Clause is First):
Because of the growing list of grievances, the colonists decided to declare independence.
	5
	Use commas to set off tag questions
	Students need to know that commas separate a statement from a short question at the end of a sentence.
	The evidence for this is stated in the first paragraph, isn’t it?
	5
	Use commas in compound- complex sentences
	Students need to know that commas help separate clauses in sentences with multiple independent and dependent clauses for clarity.
	When I finished my homework, I texted my friends because I wanted to relax, and we planned our gaming session.
	6
	Use commas to separate coordinate adjectives
	Students need to know that commas separate two or more adjectives that equally modify a noun.
	She bought a trendy, oversized hoodie at the mall.
	7
	Use commas for nonrestrictive/parenthetical elements
	Students need to know that commas can set off extra information or details that are not essential to the core meaning of the sentence. This extra information often appears in recognizable structures, such as appositives, nonrestrictive relative clauses, and nonessential participial phrases.
	My best friend, who sits next to me in math class, helped me study for the test.
	7

Quotations & Dialogue Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use quotation marks in dialogue
	Students need to know that spoken words are enclosed in quotation marks.
	Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.
	3
	Use quotation marks in titles (when appropriate)
	Students need to know that titles of short works (poems, articles, short stories, chapters in larger texts) are enclosed in quotation marks.
	In “Chapter 4” of Charlotte’s Web, Wilbur experiences loneliness.
	3
	Use quotation marks  to indicate direct speech and text quotations
	Students need to know that a speaker’s exact words go inside quotation marks and that any words copied directly from a text must also be placed inside quotation marks. When quoting text that includes dialogue, students should use single quotation marks to signal the dialogue.
	Quoting Text:
The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Quoting Dialogue:
In Bud, Not Buddy, Bud explains, “I remember what Momma always told me, ‘If you have to tell a lie, make it simple and believable.’”

	Format block quotes appropriately
	Students need to know that long quotations (usually more than four lines) are formatted as block quotes. For quoting verse/poems specifically, students should maintain the original line breaks in the source text. Unlike other in-text citations, students should include the appropriate citation after the period of the final sentence.

Steinbeck consistently highlights the responsibility George feels towards Lennie, showing how George attempts to insulate Lennie from the consequences of his own problematic behavior. Early in the novel, Steinbeck demonstrates how George guides Lennie’s behavior and thinking:
"’Course you did. Well, look Lennie - if you jus’ happen to get in trouble like you always done before, I want you to come right here an’ hide in the brush."
"Hide in the brush," said Lennie slowly.
"Hide in the brush till I come for you. Can you remember that?"
"Sure I can, George. Hide in the brush till you come." (Steinbeck 15)
Even in the first chapter of the novella, it is apparent that there is a significant mental load for George in his friendship with Lennie.

	To get to the park, turn right at the light (right next to the grocery store) and take the next left into the parking lot.
	7
	Use hyphens with affixes and compound words
	Students need to know that hyphens connect prefixes, suffixes, or compound words.
	She is a well-known social media creator at our school.

	Students need to know that ellipses indicate omitted words within a quotation, especially when citing a source. They can also be used stylistically in writing to signal a pause or trailing thought.

I was going to hang out with my friends tonight… but Netflix won.

Her weekend plans will keep her busy each day: going to the library, meeting friends for coffee, swimming at the lake, and finishing her art project.
	9-10
	Use colons to introduce quotations
	Students need to know that colons follow a complete sentence and introduce quotations, either direct (from a speaker) or text-sourced (from an authored text). Direct quotations that follow colons are indicated with an opening and closing quotation mark, and the end punctuation (e.g., period, question mark, exclamation point) is contained within the closing quotation mark. Text-sourced quotations are also indicated with an opening and closing quotation mark, but the end punctuation follows an appropriately formatted in-text citation, as needed.
	Colon Introducing a Direct Quotation:
Coach Stevens reminded the team of their golden rule: “Always give your best effort.”

Colon Introducing a Text-Sourced Quotation:

	Students need to know that em dashes can set off extra information or clarifications that are not essential to the main sentence meaning or indicate a break in thought. When parenthetical/nonrestrictive elements occur in the middle of a sentence, the content is enclosed in em dashes. When  parenthetical/nonrestrictive elements occur at the end of a sentence, the content is set-off by one em dash.
	Em Dash with Mid-Sentence Parenthetical:
Shakespeare’s depiction of Hamlet–the prince torn between duty and desire–reveals the enduring tension between action and contemplation.

Em Dash with End-of-Sentence Clarification:
The novel critiques societal inequality–through both subtle imagery and stark dialogue–highlighting the pervasive effects of class.

Em Dash with Break in Thought/Interruption:
The argument seemed sound–but upon closer inspection, the evidence revealed several inconsistencies.

Semicolons & Colons Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use semicolons to separate items in a series when items include internal commas
	Students need to know that semicolons separate complex list items that already contain commas to preserve clarity.
	For our day at the park, I packed snacks, including chips and candy; Ava made the playlists, selecting music we each like; and Leo brought the games, hoping to keep us all entertained.

I have lived in Omaha, Nebraska; Litchfield, Connecticut; and Mason City, Iowa.
	8
	Use semicolons with conjunctive adverbs or transitional phrases to link independent clauses
	Students need to know that semicolons can join two independent clauses, providing an additional way to connect complete ideas. This is especially useful when the clauses already contain internal commas or when using conjunctive adverbs (e.g., however, therefore, nevertheless, nonetheless, consequently, furthermore, moreover) or transitional phrases (e.g.,as a result, for this reason, in light of this, in contrast, on the other hand). When a conjunctive adverb or transitional phrase is used, the semicolon comes before it, and a comma always follows it, helping to maintain clarity in complex sentences.
	Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs, No Internal Punctuation:
The novel explores themes of ambition and betrayal; however, the protagonist’s personal growth remains central to the story.

Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs, Internal Punctuation:
Researchers have studied the effects of social media on teenagers, examining its impact on mental health, including anxiety and self-esteem, its role in shaping peer relationships and social norms, and its influence on academic motivation and time management; therefore, they argue that schools and families should guide responsible use while encouraging balance and critical thinking.

Semicolons with Transitional Phrases, No Internal Punctuation:
She completed the research experiment successfully; in the meantime, her classmates continued testing alternative hypotheses.

Semicolons with Transitional Phrases, Internal Punctuation:
The study examined social media use among teenagers, including time spent on messaging apps, participation in online communities, and exposure to trending challenges; as a result, researchers found significant correlations between frequent use, sleep disruption, and lower academic focus.
	9-12
	Use semicolons with coordinating conjunctions when independent clauses contain internal punctuation
	Students need to know that sometimes a semicolon can be used with a coordinating conjunction to join two sentences together and preserve clarity when the sentences are long and contain additional internal punctuation. They also need to know that this is an uncommon construction and is most helpful when one or both sentences already include several commas (e.g., complex sentences, series of phrases, etc.). Students should recognize that in this construction, the semicolon comes before the coordinating conjunction, and no additional comma is needed immediately after it.
	Semicolons with Coordinating Conjunctions, Internal Punctuation:
Since he speaks French, Spanish, and Italian, he assumed he would have no trouble traveling in southern Europe, especially since many people in the region now speak English; but when he arrived in Sicily, his unfamiliarity with the Sicilian dialect made communication difficult.

The treaty established new boundaries, clarified trade agreements, and outlined military obligations; and although it was celebrated in the capitals, many local leaders opposed its terms.

Sentence Production & Expansion Skills

Sentence Type
	Description
	Examples
	Grade
	Simple Sentence
	Students need to know that a simple sentence is made up of one independent clause, which includes a subject and a predicate and expresses a complete thought. In most simple sentences, the subject comes before the predicate. To produce simple sentences, students should write an independent clause with a clear subject–predicate structure that expresses a complete thought.
	Simple Sentence
The dog ran.
	K
	Expand complete simple sentences
	Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional information to a simple sentence while keeping the sentence grammatically correct.  Students should flexibly use K grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.
	Expanded Simple Sentence
The big dog ran fast.
	K
	Produce complete simple and compound sentences
	In addition to producing simple sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a compound sentence consists of two or more independent clauses joined by coordinating conjunctions (e.g., for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) with commas. To produce compound sentences, students should join independent clauses using a coordinating conjunction and a comma.
	Simple Sentence
Plants need water.

Compound Sentence
Plants need water, so they can grow.
	1
	Expand complete simple and compound sentences
	Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional information to simple and compound sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., simple → simple; compound → compound), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound). Students should flexibly use K-1 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.
	Expanded Simple Sentence
Plants need fresh water every day.

Expanded Compound Sentence
Plants need fresh water every day, so they can grow big and strong.
	1
	Produce complete simple, compound, and complex sentences
	In addition to producing simple and compound sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a complex sentence includes one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses, which do not express a complete thought on their own and are often introduced by subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, when, if) or relative pronouns. When the dependent clause comes before the independent clause, it is attached with a comma. When it comes after, a comma is not needed. To produce complex sentences, students should appropriately attach a dependent clause to an independent clause.
	Simple Sentence
The student solves the problem.

Compound Sentence
The student solves the problem, and she checks her work.

Complex Sentence
The student solves the problem because she wants to be sure it is correct.
	2
	Expand complete simple, compound, and complex sentences
	Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional clauses to simple, compound, and complex sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-2 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.
	Expanded Simple Sentence
