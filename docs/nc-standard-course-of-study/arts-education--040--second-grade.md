# Arts Education - Second Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Theatre Arts Standards Unpacking Second Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for theatre arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with theatre build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 theatre standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed theatre educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Theatre Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 theatre programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

2.CN.1.1 Describe how American theatre reflects heritage, customs, and traditions of people in the United States, including various Indigenous and cultural groups.
Glossary
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Heritage - Something transmitted by or acquired from a predecessor; Tradition.14
* Customs - A usage or practice common to many or to a particular place or class or habitual with an individual; the whole body of usages, practices, or conventions that regulate social life.14
* Traditions - Pattern of practices and beliefs within a societal group.17
* Indigenous - Of or relating to the earliest known inhabitants of a place and especially of a place that was colonized by a now-dominant group.14
* Culture - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students compared theatre from a variety of cultures and now they are describing how American theatre reflects heritage, customs, and traditions of people in the United States, including various indigenous and cultural groups. In the next level they will describe theatre in the local community or region.
Enduring Understanding
A theatre artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of stories, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* A variety of American theatre examples
* What heritage, customs, and traditions are
* How theatre can reflect heritage, customs, and traditions of groups
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of how American Theatre reflects cultural celebrations, traditions, and artistic practices in the United States
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* American theatre is linked to American customs and traditions.
* Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts.
* This objective is very thick. It is imperative to cover every group in the objective to fully master the content: “heritage, customs, and traditions” as well as “various Indigenous and cultural groups.”
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare materials that show theatre in American culture and traditions.
* Prepare materials that show theatre in American heritage of cultural groups, including indigenous communities.
* Guide the creation of a classroom-wide chart, organizing examples into columns of heritage, culture, and tradition, citing the group.
* Provide opportunities for students to summarize the characteristics of how American Theatre reflects cultural celebrations, traditions, and artistic practices in the United States, giving guidance as appropriate.
* Monitor individual students’ ability to describe how American theatre reflects its people through formative and summative assessment, offering feedback as needed.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Appalachian Folktales
* Native American Folktales
* NC Outdoor Drama
* Melodramas of 19th century
* Vaudeville
* The Little Theatre movement and community theatre
* The Federal Theatre Project
* Musical Theatre such as; Rogers & Hammerstein

The roles, skills, and behaviors of a theatre artist are necessary for success in both artistic and other professions.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* What an actor does
* What a director does
* What designers do
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Identifying similarities and differences in the roles of actors, directors and designers
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Each role involved in a production is equally important but they bring different things to the production. They all have roles, responsibilities and need to collaborate to be successful!
* Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts.
* This objective calls for “actor, director, and designer” so it is imperative that all three are compared.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare a definition for each role: explanations of what actors, directors, and designers do in a production.
* Communicate the importance of each role by sharing how each role contributes to the overall success of a performance
* Guide students to share what they already know about actors, directors, and designers.
* Provide graphic organizers and/or sentence stems to aid in the comparison of roles. Model for students before asking them to use these tools.
* Monitor individual students’ ability to compare the roles of actors, directors, and designers using disciplinary vocabulary through formative and summative assessment.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Behind the scenes
* What are the similarities and differences between an actor, director, set or costume designer?
* Documentaries about theatre: The Lion King Experience
* Performing a Show

Now they are improvising a variety of characters in given scenarios using dramatic play. In the next level, they will improvise a variety of resolutions to given problems using dramatic play.
Enduring Understanding
Exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of theatre artists to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Instructions for dramatic play
* Components of characters
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Spontaneously inventing characters
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Dramatic play at this age encourages students to step into different perspectives.
* Students can begin to develop an understanding of basic narrative structure—how characters interact with each other, how they change over time, and how conflicts and resolutions unfold within a story.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

* Character swap

2.CR.1.2 Create an original scene using dialogue with support.
Glossary
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
* Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
* Dialogue - A conversation between two or more characters.17
* Support - Assistance provided temporarily to enable a student to perform a task that would be difficult to perform unaided, best implemented in a manner that helps develop that student’s capacity to eventually perform the task independently.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students created an original sequence of events by acting, drawing, or writing with support.  Now they are creating an original scene using dialogue with support. In the next level, they will create a cohesive scene using elements of playwriting with support.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are recorded by theatre artists and playwrights through both written and visual documentation.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Components of a scene with dialogue
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating a new scene with dialogue
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Teaching students how to use dialogue effectively can deepen their understanding of character development and interaction.
* When students role play using dialogue for communication during created scenes it helps them better communicate and create effective dialogue in real-world situations.
* Note that “create” does not require students to write down the dialogue to master this objective. This can be a useful step, but it is not required for mastery of this objective.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare examples of short scenes with dialogue, engaging prompts to spark creative scene creation (ie, Meeting a new friend, helping someone in need, solving a crime etc)
* Demonstrate how to create dialogue for a scene between two characters through role-play
* Guide students through theatre games prompting them to role play dialogue for two characters
* Provide support, including scaffolding through sentence starters, dialog templates, peer collaboration, and modeling
* Monitor each student’s ability to create an original scene using dialogue through formative and summative assessment, collecting individual evidence of mastery through observation and documentation.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Model Character Conversations: Ask students to imagine what two characters might say to each other in different situations (e.g., meeting for the first time, solving a problem, etc.) and help them build the dialogue step-by-step.
* Graphic Organizers: Use storyboards or graphic organizers where students can plan out their scenes with boxes for each part of the dialogue.
* Role-Playing: Have students act out their scenes to help them understand how dialogue is used in real conversations. This can help them visualize the flow of dialogue and how it builds the scene.
* Partner Work: Let students work in pairs to create a short scene with dialogue. Encourage them to act out the scene first, then write it down.

2.CR.1.3 Create simple costumes, props, or masks to enhance a dramatic presentation.
Glossary
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
* Costumes - Clothing worn by actors to portray specific characters.29
* Dramatic Presentation - Referring to the production of a play or dramatic work, or the specific acting/technical job of a production member in a play.29
* Props - Anything that the actor uses on stage that is not scenery (i.e., a coffee cup or a cell phone).29
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students created simple costumes and props to enhance dramatic play using available resources. Now they are creating simple costumes, props, or masks to enhance a dramatic presentation. In the next level, they will create simple set design, costume design, or prop design to support the story of a play.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are actualized by theatre designers and craftspersons intentionally to fulfill dramatic purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Components of simple costumes, props, and masks
* Instructions for dramatic play
* Considerations for enhancing dramatic play
* Props, costumes, and masks are used to help create a full picture for the audience to suspend belief and invest in the story being performed
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating new costumes, props, or masks
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Costumes, props, and masks help students connect with the characters they are portraying. By creating their own costumes, props, or masks, students learn how these items reflect personality, role, and story and enhance the dramatic presentation of each
* This objective will be most meaningful when supporting improvisations (CR.1.1), original creation (CR.1.2), or as part of presenting works (PR.1.2). This objective has students creating the technical elements used in PR.2.3
* Since this objective has the language “costumes, props, or masks” any may be created to master this objective
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare materials for costumes, props, or masks (e.g., fabric, cardboard, scarves, paint, markers, yarn). Share pictures or videos of simple costumes, props, or masks used in children's theater or well-known stories and discuss how these items help tell the story
* Determine whether your students will all create costumes, props, or masks, or if students will be assigned to differing tasks
* Demonstrate how to create a simple costume, prop, or mask, emphasizing freedom of creativity and imagination
* Guide students in creating their own costumes, props, or masks
* Provide temporary support as needed, encouraging students to think through what items are needed in their story (e.g., animal, hero, princess, or troll mask) to support their storytelling
* Monitor individual students’ ability to create costumes, props, or masks through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Safety
* Realistic or representative, simplistic or complicated
* Props and set decor
* Costume detail
* Mask designs in various cultures
* Costumes, Masks, and/or props for CR.1.1, CR.1.2, or CR.2.1
* Costumes, Masks, and/or props for PR.1.2, PR.2.1, or PR.2.3

2.CR.2.1 Adapt a familiar story for dramatic presentation with support.
Glossary
* Adapt - To change, or to change something, to suit different conditions or uses.4
* Dramatic Presentation - Referring to the production of a play or dramatic work, or the specific acting/technical job of a production member in a play.29
* Support - Assistance provided temporarily to enable a student to perform a task that would be difficult to perform unaided, best implemented in a manner that helps develop that student’s capacity to eventually perform the task independently.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students arranged the sequence of events from a story with support. Now they are adapting a familiar story for dramatic presentation with support. In the next level, they will derive dialogue from familiar stories distinguishing unique characters.
Enduring Understanding
Theatre artists balance inspiration and originality with ethics by creatively transforming or adapting other artistic ideas into new works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* A familiar story for adaptation
* Procedures for adapting stories into dramatic presentations
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Modifying a familiar story to be performed
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* The ability to adapt a story is foundational for understanding story structure
* Adapting is not just about retelling the story; it also involves envisioning how story elements can be presented visually through performance and technical elements
* Since this objective has the language “with support” students are able to master this objective even with temporary assistance
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare familiar stories and simple materials (e.g., props, costume pieces, puppets, character cards) that inspire creative choices and help students imagine how the story could be presented onstage
* Demonstrate how to adapt a story by selecting key moments, assigning roles, and deciding how characters, setting, and actions can be brought to life through performance
* Guide students through the adaptation and rehearsal process, offering support as they develop dialogue, movement, and staging ideas that stay true to the original story
* Provide visual prompts, role reminders, and cue cards to help students stay engaged and remember their parts during the dramatic presentation
* Monitor individual students’ ability to adapt a familiar story for dramatic presentation with support through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Second graders often study fables, folk and fairytales. These are perfect for adaptation.
* Choose a shortened or picture-book version which can be completed in 10 minutes or less.
* Invite students to choose the story they want to dramatize for buy-in

2.CR.2.2 Explain how to share theatre arts fairly.
Glossary
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Fairness - Complying with appropriate, ethical, and equitable rules and guidelines.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students explained how and when to give credit to others when creating derivative work and now explain how to share theatre artworks fairly. In the next level, students will identify theatre artworks that can be used by everyone fairly.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that respect for originality and ownership of intellectual property is shown through the ethical use of artistic works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How visual artwork is shared
* What “fairly” looks like in practice (getting permission or limiting use)
* How to credit artists for their work
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the process for sharing theatre artworks fairly
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students should be able to answer the question, “How do I ethically share someone else’s work?” to show mastery of this objective.
* Students should be able to explain that theatre artists choose how and when they want their works to be shared. Just as students want others to respect when and how others share their artwork, they need to respect when and how other theatre artists want their work to be shared
* An age-appropriate explanation of fair-use could include respecting ownership, giving credit, using publicly available resources, and making decisions about sharing their own original work
* Sharing theatre artworks fairly means giving credit to the person who made it and not taking, exhibiting, posting online, showing, or copying it without asking
* Many people casually break copyright when they give others a digital or physical copy of a song, script, or recorded performance. This might really surprise students and their parents
