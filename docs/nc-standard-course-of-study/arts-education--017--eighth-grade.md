# Arts Education - Eighth Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Eighth Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The 6-8 Dance standards and objectives are written for middle school students who are taught by a licensed Dance educator for at least 90 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

8.CN.1.1 Identify the influence of North Carolinian styles and dancers on the broader dance community.
Glossary
* Community - A unified body of individuals: such as a group of people with a common characteristic or interest living together within a larger society; a body of persons of common and especially professional interests scattered through a larger society.14
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
* Style - Specific movement characteristics, qualities, or principles that give it distinctive identity; style may also refer to the unique artistic choices of a particular [artist].17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students analyzed how individuals and groups of dancers have influenced various cultures and now identify the influence of North Carolinian styles and dancers on the broader dance community. Students with a K-8 continuum in Dance should progress to the intermediate level where they will explain how dance trends, styles, and dancers are impacted by societal, cultural, and historical events. Beginning level students will describe how the emergence of dance as an art form has influenced society.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Examples of North Carolinian dance styles and dancers
* The influence of North Carolinian dance styles on the broader dance community
* The influence North Carolinian dancers have had on the broader dance community
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing how North Carolinian dance styles and dancers have influenced the broader community
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Many popular dance styles and cultural trends have been influenced by individual dancers or dance styles from North Carolina
* Artists can have an impact on societal and cultural trends
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to identify the influence of North Carolinian styles AND dancers on the broader dance community. Both dancers and styles need to be considered in mastery of this objective
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Provide examples of how North Carolinian styles and dancers have influenced on the broader dance community
* Guide student investigation into how North Carolinian styles and dancers have influenced the broader dance community
* Design learning activities for students to identify the influence of North Carolinian styles and dancers on the broader dance community
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to identify the influence of North Carolinian styles and dancers on the broader dance community
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students use internet resources to identify “dancers from North Carolina” as a starting point
* History project of North Carolina styles and dancers past and present
* Mock role-play interview with dancer
* Create a montage of video clips that show North Carolinian styles and dancers who have influenced the broader dance community

Glossary
* Apply - To put into operation or effect.14
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4

Beginning level students will apply concepts from other content areas to support the creation and performance of dance.

* How to generate solutions for academic and life problems
Skills

* Describing the reasoning of how the application of dance skills and processes can be used to solve academic and daily life problems
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery

Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective asks students to generate ideas of how to use skills and processes to approach various academic and daily life problems, it is not requiring students to actually solve problems
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to explain how dance skills AND processes can be applied to solve a variety of academic AND daily life problems. Both skills and processes need to be considered in how they can be applied to solve problems in both academic and daily life contexts. Depending on the learning assignment, these can be addressed simultaneously or broken apart in multiple assignments over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Introduce transferable skills and concepts from dance that are applicable to daily life situations and experiences
* Provide examples of scenarios of academic study and daily life challenges which could be solved by applying dance skills and processes
* Brainstorm with students on possible academic and daily life problems and how dance skills and processes could be used to find creative solutions
* Design learning activities in which students can explain how dance skills and processes can be applied to solve a variety of academic and daily life problems
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to explain how dance skills and processes can be applied to solve a variety problems in academic and daily life
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Spatial awareness - map reading, geometry and navigation skills, moving through crowds
* Teamwork and collaboration - academic group projects, sports, communication, compromise, conflict and resolution
* Focus and discipline - meeting deadlines, working on projects, test-taking, time management
* Improvisation - used in speaking presentations, adapting quickly
* Creative design process - used in generating and prototyping ideas, using feedback, refining, and producing something
* Performance - confidence in presentations, public speaking
* Persistence - repeated practice, growth mindset, learning from mistakes, grit

8.CR.1.1 Improvise movement using multiple dance elements to fulfill a movement score.
Glossary
* Dance element - The key components of movement; movement of the body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement; see Elements of Dance Organizer by Perpich Center for Arts Education (used with permission).17
* Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14
* Movement Score - A written notation of a musical or dance composition, which allows the work to be performed at a later date or by another performer.16
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students improvised movement in response to cues from a partner or group and now use multiple dance elements in improvisation to fulfill a movement score. Students with a K-8 continuum in dance should progress to the intermediate level where they will improvise extended movement sequences using a variety of sources as inspiration. Beginning level students will improvise extended movement sequences using a movement score.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of dancers and choreographers to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The basic dance elements
* How to create spontaneous movement
* How to interpret a movement score
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating spontaneous movement through improvisation that explores multiple dance element concepts in fulfillment of a movement score
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* The elements of dance can be used to inspire and improvise movement
* Movement scores are "outlines" for the structure to be used in guiding the flow of improvisation. Basing improvisation off of movement scores keeps the student centered on exploring specific elements or concepts that exist in body movement. This helps to avoid the random strung together performance of pre-known "dance steps" or "tricks" but instead encourages a more organic exploration of movement possibilities
* The objective does not specify who created the movement score, therefore, it can be created by the teacher or in their collaboration with the class, student-generated, or peer-generated
* Scores can be individually interpreted based on personal preference to the prompt
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Review what it means to improv
* Teach and review how to use various cues and the elements of dance when improvising
* Create and share examples of movement scores with students (can also work with students on designing a movement score as a class or have students create their own scores)
* Demonstrate and call on student volunteers to show movement improvisation using multiple dance elements to fulfill a movement score
* Guide students through movement improvisation exercises using multiple dance elements to fulfill a movement score
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to improvise movement using multiple dance elements to fulfill a movement score
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Teacher-created scores that include multiple elements of dance. The scores can be a series of different instruction sets so that students have practice exploring the set scores while varying the focus on the elements
* Advanced students may be expected to create their own score varying the elements of dance and swapping with a partner to improvise movement
* Students may be put into small groups to individually perform the improvised score for group members and then have a discussion about how each individual differently interpreted the score based on personal preference

8.CR.1.2 Organize dance phrases into simple dances using a variety of choreographic forms.
Glossary
* Choreographic Form- The organization of choreography and movement to fulfill the artistic intent of a dance or dance study (for example, AB, ABA, or theme and variation); often referred to as choreographic form.2
* Dance Phrase - A sequence of movements that are ordered based upon a preconceived choreographic intent and contains a clear beginning, middle, and end that aligns with artistic intent. A movement phrase could be compared to a paragraph of movement and would contain two or more movement sentences.29
* Organize - To arrange by systematic planning and united effort.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students created dance phrases using selected sequences of abstract movement and simple choreographic forms and now organize dance phrases into simple dances using a variety of choreographic forms. Students with a K-8 continuum in dance should progress to the intermediate level where they will create dance phrases that use a variety of forms to fulfill choreographic intent. Beginning level students will generate original and abstracted movements that vary the use of dance elements.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are actualized into choreography by dancers and choreographers to intentionally fulfill specific purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to use an organizational structure in arranging dance phrases
* How to use a variety of choreographic forms in the creation of simple dances
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Putting dance phrases in order to craft simple dances that use of a variety of choreographic forms
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Choreography is created through purposeful choices by the artist
* Students must have time to either create or learn short dance phrases that are to be used in mastery of this objective of creating simple dances
* Dance phrases can be created by the teacher, student, or peers
* The objective does not specify if the choreographic form is given or self-determined, therefore it is up to the discretion of the teacher
* Note that “dances” and “forms” are both plural, implying more than one. Instruction of this objective will likely occur multiple times throughout the course of study so that students have experience using multiple choreographic forms in their work
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain (or review) how choreographic forms are used in the design of choreography
* Provide practice for all students to learn and perform short dances made of multiple dance phrases that use different choreographic forms
* Guide student development of dance phrases to be used in larger choreography
* Select a choreographic form for students to use (AB, ABA, Rondo, Natural Form, etc) or have students select a form to use in their choreography
* Design learning activities in which students can organize dance phrases into simple dances using a variety of choreographic forms
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to organize dance phrases into simple dances using a variety of choreographic forms
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Using choreographic forms such as AB, ABA, ABCD (natural form), ABACAD (Rondo) dances can be made to express
   * Technique based movements for different genres of dance: modern, jazz, ballet, tap, majorette, hiphop, etc.
   * Cross-curricular concepts
   * Other ideas, feelings, topics, or themes

8.CR.1.3 Reconstruct a given dance sequence documented in pictures, symbols, writing, or media technology.
Glossary
* Dance Sequence - A brief sequence of movements that are not necessarily ordered into a formal choreographic structure that communicates artistic intent but rather demonstrates a solution to a movement problem. A movement sequence could contain as few as two or movements.29
* Digital Tools and Media - Anything in electronic form including photos, images, video, audio files, or artwork created or presented through electronic means; a gallery of artwork viewed electronically through any device.17
* Given - Qualities or traits for assessing achievement level that are provided to students.17
* Reconstruct - To create an accurate reenactment or understanding of.14
* Symbol - An image, sign, or element, such as color, that is understood, by convention or context, to suggest some other meaning.22
* Technology - The mechanical aspects and contexts of arts production, including hardware, software, networks, code, etc.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students documented an original dance sequence using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology and now reconstruct a given dance sequence documented in pictures, symbols, writing, or media technology. Students with a K-8 continuum in dance should progress to the intermediate level where they will document an original dance phrase using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology. Beginning level students will plan a dance phrase using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are recorded by dancers and choreographers through notation and symbolic documentation.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to read and interpret choreography from dance pictures, symbols, writing, or media technology
* How to embody movements documented in pictures, symbols, writing, or media technology
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Accurately reenacting a documented dance sequence by reading pictures, symbols, writing, or using media technology
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* The “reconstruct” verb is used in this thread of objectives in two, very purposeful levels - Eighth Grade and Accomplished. It was the reasoning of the likelihood that students at these levels may be tasked with having to learn choreography for placement auditions into high school programs or into the most Advanced Honors Level and would likely be needing to learn choreography from pre-recorded materials. Teachers can use this objective to support those (and similar) opportunities
* This is an opportunity to connect to the CR.2.2 standards about the legal use of copying another artist's work
* Students can share notations of previous choreography with their peers from earlier in the semester or previous years in the program if saved
* The teacher can also use notation to record a given sequence of choreography
* With permissions, or using open access material, the student can also use media documentation of movement sequences created by others in reconstructing the choreography. Students likely do this all the time with dances they find on social media platforms
* Note the conjunction “or” used in this objective - the student must demonstrate mastery of their ability to reconstruct a given dance sequence documented in pictures, symbols, writing, OR media technology. It is up to the discretion of the teacher to decide which the student is to use or autonomy can be given to the student to decide
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Review the various methods used to record dance choreography
* Demonstrate how pictures, writing, symbols, and media technology can be used to reconstruct choreography
* Have students practice using pictures, symbols, writing, and media technology to record choreography they learn in class
* Prepare or have students identify a choreographic sequence to be reconstructed from documentation using pictures, symbols, writing, or media technology
* Provide time for students to reconstruct a dance sequence from notation or documentation
* Guide and support students in their understanding of how to interpret different forms of notation when reconstructing choreography
* Design learning activities that task students to reconstruct a given dance sequence documented in pictures, symbols, writing, or media technology

8.RE.1.1 Explain how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in various genres of dance.
Glossary
* Choreographic Forms - The organization of choreography and movement to fulfill the artistic intent of a dance or dance study (for example, AB, ABA, or theme and variation); often referred to as choreographic form.2
* Dance Elements - The key components of movement; movement of the body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement; see Elements of Dance Organizer by Perpich Center for Arts Education (used with permission).17
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Genre - Category of art or design identified by similarities in form, subject matter, content, or technique.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students explained how the elements of dance are used in various dance genres and now explain how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in various genres of dance. Students with a K-8 continuum in dance should proceed to the intermediate level where they will compare the elements of dance and choreographic forms used in various dance genres. Beginning level students will contrast the elements of dance and choreographic forms used in various dance genres.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that the techniques and elements in a dance work can be objectively identified and analyzed.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The elements of dance
* Choreographic forms
* How to analyze dance for the use of dance elements and choreographic forms
* How to recognize the elements of dance when observing or performing movement in various dance genres
* How to recognize choreographic forms when observing or performing movement in various dance genres
* How to linguistically discuss choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the reasoning of the use of elements and choreographic forms in various dance genres
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Having a video recording to observe will help facilitate conversation as it can be rewound and paused when discussing. A recording would also provide the opportunity for the student to watch it several times to help develop their analytical skills
* This objective shows how choreographic forms and the elements are commonly used in all dance styles and genres
* Note that genres is plural. The student should be analyzing how dance elements and choreographic forms are used in a variety of movement genres over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom instruction on the elements of dance and various choreographic forms
* Demonstrate explaining how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in a variety of genres
* Provide examples of dance in various genres for observation or physical embodiment (these can be generated by the teacher, student, or professionally created and performed)
* Guide students on how to observe dance for evidence of choreographic forms and the dance elements of body, space, time, and energy/effort
* Design activities for students to explain how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in various genres of dance
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to explain how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in various genres of dance
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Design a common checklist or graphic organizer for students to use when watching different dances from various genres over the course of study
* After observing a work of a specific genre, students can write a critique or record their analysis of their observations of the choreographic form and elements of dance used in the work. This assignment would be repeated, having students analyze different dance genres in each assignment
* Students can be shown or guided to research and find examples of dances performed in a variety of dance genres and write one critique or record themselves explaining how choreographic forms and elements of dance were commonly used in each of the examples

8.RE.1.2 Compare meanings interpreted from dance with the original artistic intent inferred from contextual information.
Glossary
* Artistic Intent - The purpose, main idea, and expressive or communicative goals(s) of a composition study, work, or performance.17
* Compare - To locate and/or list similarities and differences.14
* Context - Interrelated conditions surrounding the creation and experiencing of an artwork, including the artist, viewer/audiences, time, culture, presentation, and location of the artwork’s creation and reception.17
* Interpret - When Responding - To explain or tell the meaning of: present in understandable terms.14
* Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students described how personal experiences and perspectives influence interpretations of dance works and now compare meanings interpreted from dance with the original artistic intent inferred from contextual information. Students with a K-8 continuum in dance should progress to the intermediate level where they will interpret multiple meanings from choreographic works, given the historic and cultural context of the dance or style. Beginning level students will interpret meaning from choreographic works, given the historical and cultural context of the dance or style.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer’s interpretation of a work may align with or differ from the artist’s original intent.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* That there can be multiple interpretations of the same artistic work
* How meaning can be visually represented through dance movements or choreographic works
* How to analyze the artistic choices used in dance movements or choreographic work
* How to interpret meaning from artistic choices made in dance
* The historical and contextual information on the artist and the work being examined
* How to examine alignment and differences in interpretations of meaning with the artist's intent
* How to linguistically describe dance
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Identifying similarities and differences in interpretations of meaning and original artistic intent inferred from contextual information of a choreographic work
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective teaches students that interpretations of work are individualistic and may or may not align with the original artist’s intent. Interpretations of work can be better informed when there is contextual knowledge about the work including the original artist’s intent
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead discussion on how artistic choices and audience interpretations are influenced by personal experience, culture, and environment
* Give examples of how personal interpretations may, or may not align with the artistic intent of a work
* Provide examples of age-appropriate choreography that can be interpreted for meaning (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work)
* Create classroom activities to provide student practice in interpreting choreographed works
* Design activities for students to compare similarities and differences between the original artistic intent inferred from contextual information and other interpretations of meaning of a choreographed work
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to compare meanings interpreted from dance with the original artistic intent inferred from contextual information
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students watch a work they know little about and document their interpretation of the meaning of the work. The teacher then shares the context of the work and has the students watch the work a second time through that lens to infer meaning of the artistic intent. Students can then describe how their initial interpretations aligned with or differed from the artistic intent in a written or recorded assignment, or pair-share discussion
8.RE.2.1 Explain how personal preferences for dance are influenced by experience, culture, and media.
Glossary
* Cultural - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Media - The various commercial and informational channels and forums for media artworks, such as T.V., radio, internet, fine arts, non-profit, communications, etc.29
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students explained how personal preferences for dance change when considering multiple viewpoints and now explain how personal preferences for dance are influenced by experience, culture, and media. Students with a K-8 continuum in dance should progress to the intermediate level where they will compare how personal and cultural influences affect the way dance is viewed and critiqued. Beginning level students will describe how personal and cultural influences affect the way dance is viewed and critiqued.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with dance works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to explain personal preferences for dance
* How to identify the impact experience, culture, and media have in shaping personal preferences in dance
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the reasoning for the influences of experience, culture, and media on personal preferences for dance
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Personal experiences and both cultural and media influences can impact individual preferences for dance
* A dancer’s preference for dance is limited to what they are aware of. Increasing exposure to different dance styles, forms, and genres broadens their experience in how they view and physically embody a wide array of dance and helps to increase preferences. Students will also gain a better understanding and appreciation for a wider variety of dance even if they do not have a personal reference for it
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate their ability to explain how personal preferences for dance are influenced by experience, culture, AND media. Depending on the learning activity, the student can explain the influence of all three on personal preference or can explain them independently in multiple assignments over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how preferences for dance may develop or change with increased exposure influenced by experience, culture, and media
* Provide examples of dance that students have been exposed to by experience, culture, and media and discuss preferences
* Give examples of how societal or personal preferences for dance have changed over time with influence by experience, culture, and media
* Guide student discovery of how their preferences for dance over time may change with increased exposure influenced by experience, culture, and media
* Design activities for students to explain how personal preferences for dance are influenced by experience, culture, and media
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to explain how personal preferences for dance are influenced by experience, culture, and media
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* After a classroom discussion on how experience, culture, and media can impact personal preference, have students reflect on when their preference for something may have changed after having been influenced by media, culture, or personal experience then have them explain how this can apply to preferences for watching or performing dance of different styles, cultures, or genres
* Have students reflect on how their personal experiences, cultural backgrounds, and media exposure have influenced their dance preference. Have students compare their answers with peers in group discussions to gain a wider understanding of the phenomenon of influence and then write a response to the prompt “How can personal experiences, culture, and media influences impact personal preferences in dance?”

8.RE.2.2 Provide guidance for the improvement of a dance performance using established criteria.
Glossary
* Criteria - The rules or guidelines used for categorizing or judging; in arts assessment, the rules or guidelines used to judge the quality of a student’s performance.28
* Guidance - Assistance provided temporarily to enable a student to perform a task that would be difficult to perform unaided, best implemented in a manner that helps develop that student’s capacity to eventually perform the task independently.17
* Improve - To enhance in value or quality : make better.14
* Performance - Experience of engaging in the act of presenting [artistic skills] in a classroom or private or public venue.17
* Provide - To supply or make available.14
* Use - To put into action or service.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified strengths and areas for improvement based on student-generated criteria when viewing dance performance and choreography and now provide guidance for the improvement of a dance performance using established criteria. Students with a K-8 continuum in dance should progress to the intermediate level where they will provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography using established criteria, supporting evidence, and artistic intent. Beginning level students will provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography using established criteria and supporting evidence.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to use established criteria in the evaluation of areas for improvement in dance performance and choreography
* How to provide guidance on ways to improve performance and choreography
* How to linguistically discuss movement performance and choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Offering feedback with strategies for improvement of dance performance resulting from an evaluation using established criteria
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Providing suggestions or guidance when identifying areas for growth is an important aspect of constructive criticism. Students may need support in understanding the positive intention of constructive criticism
* The objective does not specify how the criteria is established. It can be given to students by the teacher or created in collaboration with students
* Because this objective is about providing guidance, it implies that feedback is being given to others
* This objective can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students Integrate self-assessment, instructor, and peer feedback in the process of refining dance performance
* This objective can also be paired with other CR and PR objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how relevant criteria used in evaluation of performance helps dancers to improve their skills
* Provide students with a rubric that has descriptive performance ratings for the criteria that is most essential to the work being observed
* Review the descriptors and provide exemplars whenever possible for each criteria rating
* Demonstrate using the rubric to provide guidance on areas in need of improvement with students
* Design activities for students to use established criteria to provide guidance for the improvement of a dance performance
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to provide guidance for the improvement of a dance performance using established criteria
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students use rubrics to evaluate dances performed by others to generate feedback and provide guidance of suggestions for improvement
* Use video to record dances so students can refer back to the video to evaluate the work of their peers
