# Arts Education - First Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking First Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 Dance standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed Visual Arts educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

1.CN.1.1 Explain how dance can reflect culture, values, and ideas.
Glossary
* Culture - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Ideas - An entity (such as a thought, concept, sensation, or image) actually or potentially present to consciousness.14
* Values - Relative worth, utility, or importance.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified dance in global communities. At the current level, students explain how dance can reflect culture, values, and ideas. In the next level, students describe how American dance reflects the heritage, customs, and traditions of people in the United States, including various indigenous and cultural groups.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The definition of culture and values
* That dance expression often has meaning or purpose
* That dance expression is a form of identity of an individual and/or society
* How different cultures use dance
* Characteristics of differing cultural dances
* How dance reflects values
* How dance reflects ideas
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the reasoning behind the ways through which dance can reflect culture, values, and ideas
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective is more focused on the explanation than the movement. Students should be able to explain how dances reflect in addition to (or in place of) learning the physical steps
* Students can explore how dance exists in the culture of their daily lives and how it is a form of expression that conveys meaning and ideas
* Connections can be made to learn about how dance exists in the rituals, celebrations, and traditions of global cultures or in cultures where there are restrictions on dance
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to explain how dance can reflect culture, values, AND ideas. Depending on the learning activity, culture, values, and ideas can be addressed simultaneously or independently explored in multiple learning activities over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare examples of dances that reflect culture, values, and ideas
* Model explaining how dance reflects culture, values, and ideas
* Give students accurate historical information about the customs and traditions of the culture in which the dance genre/style exists
* Guide students to examine how dance exists in their culture
* Guide students to examine how dance can communicate meaning and ideas
* Design learning activities for students to engage in discussion to explain their understanding of how dance reflects culture, values, and ideas
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to explain how dance can reflect culture, values, and ideas
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Chinese dances using large puppets
* Gumboot dances as a way of communication
* West African dances
* Greek wedding dances
* Dance at family gatherings
* Dance in commercial and social media

In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

* Have an understanding of concepts from Visual Arts, Music, and Theatre that are common with Dance
* Provide examples of dance connections with various arts disciplines
* Guide students in exploring the similarities of concepts between Dance and other arts disciplines
* Provide opportunity for students to identify how concepts in Dance and other arts disciplines are connected
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to identify cross-curricular connections between Dance and other arts disciplines
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* The creative design process used in all artistic disciplines
* Research and integrate other arts curricula with topics synonymous with dance concepts
* Create a dance score that uses types of lines learned in visual art and connect them with pathways learned in dance
* Use dance concepts to help represent music concepts: levels high, middle, and low to understand pitch, fast and slow movements to understand tempo, big and small movements to represent dynamics, following a steady beat
* Compare dramatic gestures and facial expressions with full body dance movement

1.CR.1.2 Create movement as a group for the beginning, middle, and end of a dance.
Glossary
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students built foundational knowledge of the overall structure of a dance having a beginning, middle, and end by creating a movement for each section. At the current level, students collaborate with a group to create movement for the beginning, middle, and end of a dance. In the next level, students will create short dance sequences with simple patterns.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are actualized into choreography by dancers and choreographers to intentionally fulfill specific purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* That dance has a beginning, middle, and end (sequence)
* How to sequence movements together
* How to collaborate with others in finding movement solutions to creative challenges
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Conceiving new movements through a collaborative effort that sequence movements in a specific order for the beginning, middle, and end of a dance
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Choreography is created through purposeful choices by the artist
* All dance compositions have a beginning, middle, and end
* Students connect dance sequences with the literacy concept of sequencing in stories
* Choreography focus is an understanding that movement is to be planned and reproduced exactly the same each time, just like a book is written and does not change each time it is read
* Collaboration may require guidance and support
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to create movement as a group for the beginning, middle AND end of a dance. All three must be considered
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Demonstrate how dances have a beginning, middle, and end
* Ask for volunteer ideas for movements to be used for beginning, middle, and end of classroom routines
* Provide practice for all students to contribute ideas for beginning, middle, and end movements for classroom routines
* Assign students to small groups with the task of creating movement for a dance beginning (entrance or form a beginning shape, etc), ending (exit or ending shape), and movement used for the middle content
* Facilitate discussion and use positive behavior support for expectations on how to share ideas and collaborate with others when making dances
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to create movement as a group for the beginning, middle and end of a dance
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Using a book as an example for beginning, middle, and end, front and back covers. Students are arranged in small groups and tasked to collaborate in creating a class dance sequence “book” with beginning, middle, and end movements. Students create, practice, memorize and perform their choreographed sequence for feedback (Use PR and RE objectives)

* Teach different types of shapes: curved, angled, straight, twisted, and create a class legend with symbols for each of the shapes. Have students perform a shape sequence and draw the symbols that represents the shapes that they are executing

1.CR.2.1 Adapt a dance sequence by changing the body shapes.
Glossary
* Adapt - To change, or to change something, to suit different conditions or uses.4
* Body Shapes - The spatial contour the body makes such as curved, angular, twisted, straight, symmetrical, or asymmetrical.18
* Dance Sequence - A brief sequence of movements that are not necessarily ordered into a formal choreographic structure that communicates artistic intent but rather demonstrates a solution to a movement problem. A movement sequence could contain as few as two or movements.29
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students adapted the size of movements in a pre-existing dance sequence and now adapt the dance sequence by changing the body shapes of the movement. In the next level, students will adapt the dance sequence by changing the level of the movements.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that dancers and choreographers balance inspiration and originality with ethics by creatively transforming or adapting other artistic ideas into new works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* An original dance sequence for manipulation
* How to alter the body shapes of movement
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Modifying body shapes used in original dance sequences to create new movement sequences
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students may need a clear demonstration or example of what "body shapes" may be in a dance sequence, especially in the sense that just changing the positioning of an arm will change the shape of the body
* Changing the shape of the body is one of the choreographic tools used to manipulate motifs to create new movement. Students can be challenged to change the body shape of any movement
* Students may find that as they change the body shapes the transition used in and out of shapes will also change. This may impact the timing of the movement so the tempo may also need to change so that they can appropriately perform their movements
* This objective can be paired with other CR, PR, and RE objectives as students create, perform, and respond to dance
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Create classroom activities that support student creation and manipulation of choreography
* Demonstrate how to adapt the body shapes used in movement motifs
* Ask for volunteer ideas for how a variety of motifs might be performed with different body shapes
* Provide practice for all students to contribute ideas for how to adapt the body shapes used in choreography
* Create original choreography for students to adapt or guide students in generating original material to be used
* Design a learning activity in which students can adapt the body shapes in a pre-existing dance sequence
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to adapt a dance sequence by changing the body shapes
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Show students how to adapt movements by changing the body shapes, for example making curved shapes into straight shapes or placing body parts in different positioning without changing much else. Play a movement game of “telephone” to teach students how to change shapes - the teacher or a volunteer student can begin with one shape which the next student must somehow alter one body part, and so on and so forth down the line until the end is reached
* Teach students a dance sequence and task them to perform specific movements of the sequence by changing the body position of a specific body part, for example, “when doing the jump, change the shape of your legs”, “when doing the turn, change the shape of your arms”
* Teach students a sequence with simple movements that can be adapted. Go through the phrase and talk about the body shapes that are made throughout. Ask students to recall the sequence and find their own ways to change the body shapes throughout the sequence

* This objective can be paired with CN1.1 as students are exposed to different styles, forms, or genres of dance in learning how dance reflects culture, values, and ideas
* Note the conjunction “or” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to describe preferences in observing various dance styles, forms, OR genres. It is up to teacher discretion as to which students are observing
* Note that this objective is plural - The student should be observing multiple styles, forms, or genres over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how preferences in dance can vary based on the individual
* Demonstrate describing preferences in observing dances from a variety of styles, forms, or genres (be mindful not to impart bias but allow students to have differing perspectives)
* Provide examples of dance performances in a wide array of styles, forms, or genres for observation (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work)
* Guide student discovery of preferences when observing works from different styles, forms, or genres
* Provide sentence stems to help students form descriptive sentences about their preferences  when they are observing various dance styles, forms, or genres
* Design activities for students to describe personal preference in observing various dance styles, forms, or genres
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to describe preferences in observing various dance styles, forms, or genres
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Design activities that ask students to describe what they like and dislike about each style, form, or genre
* Students can pair-share or contribute to a larger group discussion
* Four corners activity can have students grouping by level of preferences and then they can discuss in smaller groups
* Students can write a short reflection describing their preferences as an assignment or exit-ticket activity

1.RE.2.2 Provide positive feedback for self and others.
Glossary
* Feedback - A reaction or response to a particular design problem or design solution.29
* Provide - To supply or make available.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students provided positive feedback for self and others with guidance and now do the same task independently. In the next level, students will identify positive feedback and areas for improvement for self and others with guidance.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to identify the positive aspects of personal work
* How to identify the positive aspects of another's work
* How to linguistically discuss movement performance and choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Offering feedback that is complimentary in nature for personal and peer work
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students understand that they can use feedback to improve and also give feedback to help someone else improve. It is important to identify strengths when evaluating work so students build efficacy and can continue to build on their success.
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to provide positive feedback for self AND others. Both need to be considered
* This objective can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students improve dance skills by incorporating feedback from instructors and peers
* This objective can also be paired with other CR and PR objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how dancers grow from positive feedback
* Guide students on how to use a simple rubric to identify qualities of work
* Provide opportunities for students to compliment and identify areas of strength in their work and the work of their peers. This can be through demonstration of skills learned or in performance of student-generated work
* Provide examples of how to provide positive feedback to self or others
* Provide sentence stems to help students form postive feedback on aspects of dance creation or performance
* Design activities for students to provide positive feedback on dance performed or choreographed by self and others
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to provide positive feedback for self and others
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students perform something they have learned or choreographed. Film the students performing and then have students give positive feedback about their own performance and to each other by using a feedback form or sharing feedback verbally
