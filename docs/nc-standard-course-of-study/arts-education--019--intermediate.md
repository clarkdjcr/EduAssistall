# Arts Education - Intermediate

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Intermediate
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a students’ choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed Dance educator. Credit earned from middle and high school courses meeting these minimums satisfies the Arts Education Graduation requirement and applies toward the Arts Education Diploma Endorsement. Courses that do not meet these minimums are not designed to build proficiency and are often used to spark interest in more intensive proficiency-based study later in a student’s education.
To account for variations in skill progression, as well as to embed multiple entry points across the K-12 spectrum, the objectives in the high school NCSCOS for Dance are organized by proficiency level, rather than grade level. Minimum proficiency outcomes are provided at each level to show what a student should know and be able to do by the end of a course or program when they create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance. Intermediate High School Dance standards are designed for those students who have had a complete K-8 progression in Dance education or who have achieved beginning level high school standards and objectives.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

I.CN.1.1 Explain how dance trends, styles, and dancers are impacted by societal, cultural, and historical events.
Glossary
* Culture - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Societal - An enduring and cooperating social group whose members have developed organized patterns of relationships through interaction with one another; a community, nation, or broad grouping of people having common traditions, institutions, and collective activities and interests.14
* Styles - Specific movement characteristics, qualities, or principles that give it distinctive identity; style may also refer to the unique artistic choices of a particular [artist].17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students may be entering this level from either 8th grade where they identified the influence of North Carolinian styles and dancers on the broader dance community or beginning where they described how the emergence of dance as an art form has influenced society. At the current level, students now explain the impact of societal, cultural, and historical events on dance trends, styles, and dancers. In the next level, students analyze the development of dance throughout historical time periods.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* About important societal, cultural, and historical events
* Trends, styles of dance that reflect or were influenced by important societal, cultural, and historical events
* Dancers and choreographers whose work was impacted by societal, cultural, and historical events
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the reasoning of how dance trends, styles, and dancers are impacted by societal, cultural, and historical events
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Events may be selected so that they directly relate to students or the area in which they are taught. A specific area may have experienced a natural disaster, be home to a large group of immigrants from one area, or have roots in significant historic occurrences
* Note the conjunction “and” used twice in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to explain how dance trends, styles, AND dancers are impacted by societal, cultural, and historical events. Depending on the learning activity, the impact of all three: societal, cultural, and historical events on trends, styles, and dancers can be assessed in simultaneously or this objective can be broken apart and covered in multiple assessments over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Provide examples of dance trends, styles, and dancers that have been impacted by societal, cultural, and historical events
* Guide student investigation into how societal, cultural, and historical events have impacted dance trends, styles, and dancers
* Design learning activities for students to explain the impact of societal, cultural, and historical events on dance trends, styles, and dancers
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to explain how dance trends, styles, and dancers are impacted by societal, cultural, and historical events
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* The trend/popularity of different dance styles as influenced by popular tv shows and movies.
* Popularity of dance competitions as influenced by tv shows and movies.
* The role of social media in creating dance trends

Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.

In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to identify effective strategies for recognizing and preventing anatomical and psychological health issues that affect dancers
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Students research different awareness and injury prevention exercises that address anatomical challenges in dance and evaluate how well certain strategies work over an established period of time
* Students learn of strategies that help with recognizing and preventing psychological health concerns and make comparisons to evaluate how effective strategies could be in addressing the challenges dancers face
* Students could be provided with a list of various strategies for the recognition and prevention of both anatomical and psychological health issues common in dance and have to evaluate which ones would seem most logical and effective in addressing the issue

I.CR.1.3 Document an original dance phrase using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology.
Glossary
* Dance Phrase - A sequence of movements that are ordered based upon a preconceived choreographic intent and contains a clear beginning, middle, and end that aligns with artistic intent. A movement phrase could be compared to a paragraph of movement and would contain two or more movement sentences.29
* Digital Tools and Media - Anything in electronic form including photos, images, video, audio files, or artwork created or presented through electronic means; a gallery of artwork viewed electronically through any device.17
* Document - To create a record of (something) through writing, film, photography, etc.
* Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
* Symbol - An image, sign, or element, such as color, that is understood, by convention or context, to suggest some other meaning.22
* Technology - The mechanical aspects and contexts of arts production, including hardware, software, networks, code, etc.17

Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating a record of an original dance phrase by using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students will need to develop a method to document their choreography, this could be through written journal, video recording, pictures or symbols to annotate movement, facings, dynamics, etc. The student will need to be assessed on their ability to document their original choreography

* When using student-created images, students need to have the opportunity to explain why they drew the symbols they did so that it can be determined if their reasoning is sound
* Note the conjunction “or” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to document an original dance phrase using pictures OR symbols, writing, OR media technology. It is up to the discretion of the teacher to decide which the student is to use or autonomy can be given to the student to decide
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

I.RE.1.1 Compare the elements of dance and choreographic forms used in various dance genres.
Glossary
* Choreographic Forms - The organization of choreography and movement to fulfill the artistic intent of a dance or dance study (for example, AB, ABA, or theme and variation); often referred to as choreographic form.2
* Compare - To locate and/or list similarities and differences.14
* Dance Elements - The key components of movement; movement of the body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement; see Elements of Dance Organizer by Perpich Center for Arts Education (used with permission).17
* Genre - Category of art or design identified by similarities in form, subject matter, content, or technique.17
* Use - To put into action or service.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students may be entering this level from either 8th grade where they explained how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in various genres of dance or beginning where they contrasted how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in various dance genres. At the current level, students compare the elements of dance and choreographic forms used in various dance genres. In the next level, students will compare the use of elements of dance in different interpretations of the same choreographic work.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that the techniques and elements in a dance work can be objectively identified and analyzed.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The elements of dance
* Choreographic forms
* How to analyze dance for the use of dance elements and choreographic forms
* How to recognize the elements of dance when observing or performing movement in various dance genres
* How to recognize choreographic forms when observing or performing movement in various dance genres
* How to compare similarities and differences of the dance elements and choreographic forms used in performance of a variety of dance genres
* How to linguistically discuss choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Identifying similarities and differences in the dance elements and choreographic forms used in various dance genres
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* The elements of dance are common to all dance genres but comparing differences can be used in identifying styles and genres
* Having a video recording to observe will help facilitate conversation as it can be rewound and paused when discussing. A recording would also provide the opportunity for the student to watch it several times to help develop their analytical skills
* Note that genres is plural. The student should be analyzing how dance elements and choreographic forms are used in a variety of movement genres over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom instruction on the elements of dance and various choreographic forms
* Demonstrate comparing similarities and differences in how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in a variety of genres
* Provide examples of dance in various genres for observation or physical embodiment (these can be generated by the teacher, student, or professionally created and performed)
* Guide students on how to observe dance for evidence of choreographic forms and the dance elements of body, space, time, and energy/effort
* Design activities for students to compare similarities and differences in how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in a variety of dance genres
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to compare the elements of dance and choreographic forms used in various dance genres
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Design a common checklist or graphic organizer for students to use when watching different dances from various genres over the course of study
* After observing a work of a specific genre, students can write a critique or record their analysis of their observations of the choreographic form and elements of dance used in the work. This assignment would be repeated, having students analyze different dance genres in each assignment. After several works have been analyzed the student can make a comparison of the similarities and differences that were observed in a written or audio/visual assignment
* Students can be shown or guided to research and find examples of dances performed in a variety of dance genres and write one critique or record themselves explaining the similarities and differences in how choreographic forms and elements of dance were used in each of the examples

I.RE.1.2 Interpret multiple meanings from choreographic works, given the historic and cultural context of the dance or style.
Glossary
* Cultural Context - Ideas, beliefs, values, norms, customs, traits, practices, and characteristics shared by individuals within a group that form the circumstances surrounding the creation, presentation, preservation, and response to art.17
* Interpret - When Responding - To explain or tell the meaning of: present in understandable terms.14
* Style - Specific movement characteristics, qualities, or principles that give it distinctive identity; style may also refer to the unique artistic choices of a particular [artist].17
* Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students may be entering this level from either 8th grade where they compared meanings interpreted from dance with the original artistic intent inferred from contextual information or beginning where they interpreted meaning from choreographic works, given the historical and cultural context of the dance or style. At the current level, students interpret multiple meanings from choreographic works, given the historic and cultural context of the dance or style. In the next level, students will justify meaning interpreted from choreographic works based on personal, historical, and cultural contexts.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer’s interpretation of a work may align with or differ from the artist’s original intent.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* That there can be multiple interpretations of the same artistic work
* How meaning can be visually represented through dance movements or choreographic works
* How to analyze the artistic choices used in dance movements or choreographic work
* How to interpret meaning from artistic choices made in dance
* The historical and contextual information of the dance or style being examined
* How to use contextual information of a dance or style to inform multiple interpretations of meaning
* How to linguistically describe dance
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Explaining multiple ideas of the choreographic meaning of a work, given the historical and cultural context of the dance being observed
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective teaches students that having an understanding of the historical or cultural context of a work can shape how an individual interprets the meaning of the work
* This objective teaches students that there are often multiple interpretations of art and not just “one” correct answer. Encourage divergent thinking in viewing dance through multiple lenses
* Use works from various styles and genres
* Note the conjunction “and” and “or” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to interpret multiple meanings from choreographic works, given the historic AND cultural context of the dance OR style. Both the historic and cultural context need to be considered. Whether that information is about the specific dance or style is up to the discretion of the teacher or dependent upon what information is available to students
* Note that works is plural - The student should be interpreting multiple meanings from an array of works from different cultures and historical periods over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead discussion on how dance conveys meaning and that artistic choices are reflective of the historic and cultural context of the dance or style
* Give examples of how to use the historical and cultural contexts of a dance or style when forming multiple personal interpretations of meaning
* Provide examples of age-appropriate choreography for which historical and cultural contextual information of the dance or style is available
* Create classroom activities to provide student practice in using historical and cultural contextual information of works or styles to inform interpretations of choreographed works
* Design activities for students to use knowledge of the historical and cultural context of the dance or style to inform interpretations of multiple meanings of choreographed works
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to interpret multiple meanings from choreographic works, given the historic and cultural context of the dance or style
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* After learning about the history and cultural context of a work or style, the students watch a work through a contextual lens to interpret multiple meanings. Students can then describe interpretations of two or more meanings in a written or recorded assignment, pair-share discussion, or exit-ticket activity

I.RE.2.1 Compare how personal and cultural influences affect the way dance is viewed and critiqued.
Glossary
* Compare - To locate and/or list similarities and differences.14
* Critique(d) - Individual or collective reflective process by which artists or designers experience, analyze, and evaluate a work[s] of art or design.17
* Culture - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students may be entering this level from either 8th grade where they explained how personal preferences for dance are influenced by experience, culture, and media or beginning where they described how personal and cultural influences affect the way dance is viewed and critiqued. At the current level, students compare how personal and cultural influences affect the way dance is viewed and critiqued. In the next level, students will identify moments of kinesthetic empathy when viewing dance.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with dance works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How personal experiences influence bias in the observation and evaluation of dance
* How cultural backgrounds influence perception and bias in the observation and evaluation of dance
* How to make comparisons in how personal and cultural influences affect how dance is viewed and critiqued
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Identifying similarities and differences in how personal and cultural influences affect how dance is viewed and critiqued
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective teaches students that both personal and cultural influences shape preferences in dance. The student can compare how their own personal and cultural influences have played a role in shaping their personal aesthetic or compare how dance is viewed and critiqued among individuals of different personal and cultural backgrounds
* Note the conjunction “and” used twice in this objective - the student needs to demonstrate their ability to compare how personal AND cultural influences affect the way dance is viewed AND critiqued. Both personal and cultural influences need to be considered in the comparison for how dance is both viewed and critiqued
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how personal experiences and cultural influences shape personal preferences in dance and how preference can impact how dance is viewed and critiqued
* Provide examples of dances from a variety of dance styles, genres, and cultures for observation and physical embodiment
* Guide students in an investigation of the influence of personal experiences and cultural influences on how dance is viewed and critiqued
* Have students reflect on their own value and response to dance and on how their personal experiences and culture has played a role in developing their personal aesthetic
* Have students compare similarities and differences in how their personal aesthetic affects the way they view and critique different works
* Have students compare similarities and differences in how cultural influences affect how dance is viewed and critiqued
* Design activities for students to compare how personal and cultural influences affect the way dance is viewed and critiqued
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to compare how personal and cultural influences affect the way dance is viewed and critiqued
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students view and critique a dance through multiple cultural lenses examining how differences in culture impacts how the dance is viewed and critiqued
* Students can compare dance critiques of the same work from multiple sources and infer how personal and cultural backgrounds impacted the critique
* Students can compare how their own personal experiences and cultural backgrounds influence how they view and critique dance
* Students can compare their personal observations and evaluations of dance with peers and discuss how similarities and differences in their responses were influenced by personal and cultural influences

I.RE.2.2 Provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography using established criteria, supporting evidence, and artistic intent.
Glossary
* Artistic Intent - The purpose, main idea, and expressive or communicative goals(s) of a composition study, work, or performance.17
* Criteria - The rules or guidelines used for categorizing or judging; in arts assessment, the rules or guidelines used to judge the quality of a student’s performance.28
* Feedback - A reaction or response to a particular design problem or design solution.29
* Performance - Experience of engaging in the act of presenting [artistic skills] in a classroom or private or public venue.17
* Provide - To supply or make available.14
* Use - To put into action or service.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students may be entering this level from either 8th grade where they provided guidance for the improvement of a dance performance using established criteria or beginning where they provided detailed feedback for performance and choreography using established criteria and supporting evidence. At the current level, students provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography using established criteria, supporting evidence, and artistic intent. In the next level, students will provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography using student-generated criteria.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to observe dance using established criteria lens
* How to evaluate a work based on artistic intent
* How to discuss supporting evidence of personal observations in detail
* How to provide detailed objective feedback for performance and choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Offering detailed feedback for performance and choreography using established criteria, supporting evidence, and artistic intent
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students understand that they can use feedback to improve and also give feedback to help someone else improve. It is important to identify strengths when evaluating work so students build efficacy and can continue to build on their success. Identifying areas for growth is an important aspect of constructive criticism. Students may need support in understanding the positive intention of constructive criticism
* The objective does not specify how the criteria is established. It can be given to students by the teacher or created in collaboration with students
* This objective does not specify whose performance and choreography is being viewed therefore it is up to discretion of the teacher if the student is evaluating their own performance or work, or the performance or work of others
* Note the conjunction “and” used twice in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to provide detailed feedback for performance AND choreography using established criteria, supporting evidence, AND artistic intent. The evaluation must include established criteria, supporting evidence, and artistic intent, however, the evaluation can be made separately of performance and choreography when necessary
* This objective can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students apply self, peer, and instructor assessment to refine personal and group performance in dance
* This objective can also be paired with other CR and PR objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how relevant criteria used in evaluation of performance and choreography helps dancers to improve their skills
* Discuss how the choices made by artists should strengthen the intent of the work
* Provide given criteria or work with students to establish criteria and performance rating descriptors relevant for the performance and choreography being observed. Try to find exemplars of the descriptor ratings so students have a clear understanding of what each level of mastery looks like
* Demonstrate using the rubric to provide detailed feedback with supporting evidence of performance, choreography, and artistic intent with students
* Have students use criteria in their evaluation of work performed by self, peers, or even observations of professional works
* Create opportunity for student practice in providing detailed feedback when using the criterion ratings including offering supporting evidence of where in the observation of the performance or choreography each thing occurred and how artistic decisions align with artistic intent of the work
* Design activities for students to use established criteria, supporting evidence, and artistic intent to provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography using established criteria, supporting evidence, and artistic intent
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students use rubrics to generate detailed feedback considering the artistic intent of the work and including reference to specific areas of choreography when identifying strengths and areas for improvement for self and others in works that are created or performed
* Use video to record dances so students can watch themselves or refer back to the video to evaluate the work of their peers
