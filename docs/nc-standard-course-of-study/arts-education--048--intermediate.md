# Arts Education - Intermediate

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Theatre Arts Standards Unpacking Intermediate Proficiency Level
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for theatre arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with theatre build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Students at the high school level will have the option of studying an individual arts discipline as an area of interest, or specializing or completing a concentration in studies to prepare them for further education and/or a career in the arts. The Standards communicate what students should know and be able to do as a result of instruction at each proficiency level: beginning, intermediate, accomplished, and advanced (9-12). Intermediate High School Theatre Arts standards are designed for those students who have had a complete K-8 progression in Theatre Arts education or who have achieved beginning level high school standards and objectives. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed music educator. Credit earned from middle and high school courses meeting these minimums satisfies the Arts Education Graduation requirement and applies toward the Arts Education Diploma Endorsement. Courses that do not meet these minimums are not designed to build proficiency and are often used to spark interest in more intensive proficiency-based study later in a student’s education.
The North Carolina Theatre Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 theatre programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

I.CN.1.1 Analyze the development of theatre from the 1400s through the 1700s, identifying connections to history, society, and culture.
Glossary
* Analyze - Identifying and examining separate parts as they function independently and together in creative works and study of [the] arts.22
* Cultural - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
* Historical [Period] - The historical period focuses on when the dance was made. The relevant developments in that era may influence the dance.11
* Societal - An enduring and cooperating social group whose members have developed organized patterns of relationships through interaction with one another; a community, nation, or broad grouping of people having common traditions, institutions, and collective activities and interests.14
* Styles - Specific movement characteristics, qualities, or principles that give it distinctive identity; style may also refer to the unique artistic choices of a particular [artist].17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students described how the emergence of theatre as an art form has influenced society prior to 1400. Now they are analyzing the development of theatre from the 1400s through the 1700s, identifying connections to history, society, and culture. In the next level they will analyze the development of theatre from the 1800s through the 1900s, identifying connections to history, society, and culture.
Enduring Understanding
A theatre artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of stories, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How theatre developed from 1400s to 1700s
* Examples of historical events that developed theatre from 1400-1700
* Examples of societies that developed theatre from 1400-1700
* Examples of cultures that developed theatre from 1400-1700
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Drawing conclusions based on the identification and examination of the development of theatre between 1400-1700
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* As society has developed theatre has developed including reacting to and influencing changing dynamics of societies and cultures throughout history.
* Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts.
* Since this objective has the language “history, society, and culture” all three must be considered.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare and organize texts, videos, and timelines that highlight major theatrical movements and key cultural, historical, and societal developments from the Renaissance to the Enlightenment.
* Demonstrate analyzing a specific theatrical shift (e.g., the rise of Commedia dell’arte or Elizabethan theatre) and connect it to its societal and historical context.
* Guide student discussions or graphic organizers that compare theatrical developments across different regions and how they reflect or influence cultural norms.
* Provide analysis prompts, vocabulary supports, and annotated examples to deepen students’ understanding of theatre’s evolving role.
* Monitor student responses for evidence of analysis, accuracy of connections to history, society, and culture, and proper use of academic and theatrical terminology through formative and summative assessment, offering feedback as needed.

* Commedia dell’ arte
* The Four Humours (The Humors)
* Spanish plays/playwright (Lope de Vega / Calderon)

* This objective will be most meaningful when supporting improvisations (CR.1.1), original creation (CR.1.2), or as part of presenting works (PR.1.2).

Instruction should center on generating design concepts—costumes, props, lighting, sound, or sets—that reflect student ideas, creative choices, and artistic intent.
* Though implementation is often part of the design process, the performance strand (PR.2.3) is where students should focus on bringing designs to life—adapting, executing, and problem-solving within real-world constraints. These two throughlines are meant to work in tandem, but CR.1.3 is about the creation of technical ideas, while PR.2.3 is about the realization of those ideas onstage
* Since this objective has the language “facilities, technology, and tools” each of these should be considered when creating technical elements
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare materials and resources to support students in designing original set, lighting, sound, costume, prop, hair, or makeup elements. Begin by identifying and discussing what facilities, technology, and tools are available in your space and how those will inform the design process
* Determine whether students will focus on a shared technical element or explore different elements based on interest and project needs (e.g., set, lighting, sound, costumes, props, hair, or makeup)
* Demonstrate how to create an original technical design by showing how to research and plan for available resources and space limitations. Walk through a sample idea highlighting  how it’s adjusted based on access to tools or facility constraints
* Guide students in developing their own original designs, encouraging them to consider how elements such as setting, mood, character, and narrative shape their choices. Prompt them to sketch or outline how they will use available resources
* Provide supports like sample design templates, planning checklists, or tech specs to help students make realistic, creative decisions that align with the capabilities of the space and tools they’ll actually use
* Monitor students’ ability to design for the implementation of technical elements through formative and summative assessment

I.CR.2.1 Adapt unique personal experiences into theatrical works.
Glossary
* Adapt - To change, or to change something, to suit different conditions or uses.4
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
* Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students adapted theatrical works using personal experiences through a collaborative process. Now they are adapting unique personal experiences into theatrical works. In the next level, they will adapt a variety of source materials into theatrical works showing respect for differing cultural perspectives.
Enduring Understanding
Theatre artists balance inspiration and originality with ethics by creatively transforming or adapting other artistic ideas into new works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Procedures for adapting experiences into works
* Sources for material for adaptation (auto-biographical, biographical)
* How to select material for adaptation (reputable sources)
* How to adapt material featuring personal experiences from the original format into the format of a theatrical work (ex: letter to script, novel to script)
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Incorporating personal experiences originally expressed through another medium or work into a newly adapted theatrical work
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Theatrical works demonstrate the experiences of characters existing within a story.  Personal experiences may be recorded in our minds, our texts, and the various art forms
* When adapting a narrative, personal account, memory, or historical event into a staged production, theatre artists must work to recreate and bring to life the concept of the experience into a performable and observable art form
* When adapting personal experiences into theatrical works, the degree and range of adaptation may vary based on the artistic goals of the production
* Theatrical works that are adaptations should clearly reflect the source material and yet remain in keeping with the genre, style, and overall goals of the theatrical production
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare a supportive and respectful classroom environment by reinforcing rules and procedures that ensure safety, appropriateness, and creative freedom when working with personal material
* Demonstrate how unique personal experiences—whether directly lived or sourced with permission—can be transformed into compelling theatrical moments. Model how to adapt real-life moments into scenes, characters, and dialogue
* Guide students in identifying meaningful personal experiences or sourcing stories with care and integrity. Teach students how to evaluate the appropriateness of source material and ensure it is reputable and permissible for use
* Provide reflective tools, writing prompts, and story-mapping organizers to help students shape their experiences into theatrical structure. Offer scaffolds to help them focus on character development, conflict, and emotional impact
* Monitor individual students’ ability to adapt unique personal experiences into theatrical works through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* literary perspective (first, second, third)
* multiple perspectives (cultural, societal, historical, situational, reflective)
* individual as part of the whole (ensemble)
* origination vs. adaptation
* inspiration vs. appropriation
* context (time, location, situation)
* credit (individual, shared)
* literary diagram, story structure (key plot moments within the experience)
* literary components of the experience (character, setting, plot, mood, theme)
* theatrical components needed to present the experience (staging, technical elements, style, genre)
* degree of adapted vs. originated material included in theatrical works (auto-biographical vs. semi-auto-biographical, biographical vs. semi-biographical, historical vs. historical fiction, inspired by vs. adapted from)
   * Examples of theatrical adaptations of experiences: scenarios, monologue/dialogues scripts, improvised/staged performances, play/musical, comedy/tragedy/other
   * Examples of sources for personal experiences:  journals, letters, poems, songs, first-person narratives, recorded interviews, articles, photos, paintings, auto-biographical texts,  biographical texts

I.CR.2.2 Evaluate the ethics of making, producing, and distributing both original theatre and works inspired by other artists.
Glossary
* Derivative - Having parts that originate from another source.14
* Ethical - Moral guidelines and philosophical principles for determining appropriate behavior.17
* Evaluate - A judgment about the worth or quality of something. In education, data from tests, tasks, or performances are used to make judgments about the success of the student or program.28
* Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students may be entering this level from either 8th grade where they explained how to share works of art legally and ethically in both physical and digital spaces or beginning where they compared artistic adaptation, appropriation, fair use, copyright, public domain, open source, and creative commons in the theatre arts. At the current level, students evaluate the ethics of making, producing, and distributing both original and derivative art. In the next level, students will compare how parody, adaptation, and artificial intelligence relate to copyright and fair use of art.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that respect for originality and ownership of intellectual property is shown through the ethical use of artistic works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* What it means to make, produce, and distribute both original and derivative art ethically
* The value of the ethics in manufacturing, producing, and distributing original and derivative works
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Assessing the impact of making, producing, and distributing both original and derivative art ethically
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students should be able to answer the question, “Why does ethical behavior matter in the creation and distribution of art?” to show mastery of this objective
* This objective can be paired with CR.2.1 in which students apply knowledge of appropriation to create new ideas for personal artwork.
* There is “value” in being ethical and trustworthy both personally and to the art industry. Artists, venues, museums, and arts educators prefer to work with people they trust, and therefore acting ethically can be professionally beneficial
* Note the conjunction “and” used twice in this objective. Students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to evaluate the ethics of making, producing, AND distributing both original AND derivative art. Students can evaluate the ethics in all three aspects of making, producing and distributing both original AND derivative art in one learning activity or each can be assessed separately in multiple learning activities over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare examples of both original and derivative theatrical works alongside real-world ethical dilemmas (e.g., copyright infringement, attribution, payments or profit-sharing)
* Demonstrate how ethical decisions are made in the creation, production, and distribution of theatrical work by analyzing actual case studies or classroom scenarios
* Guide students in discussions that explore the ethical implications of borrowing, adapting, or distributing artistic ideas, helping them weigh intent, impact, and responsibility
* Provide opportunities for students to evaluate real or hypothetical theatre-making situations and justify whether they meet ethical standards in respect to originality, credit, and consent
* Monitor individual students’ ability to evaluate the ethics of making, producing, and distributing both original theatre and works inspired by other artists while correctly using theatrical vocabulary through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Scenarios
* Current Events
   * Stereophonic Lawsuit (2024) (Claim that Broadway play copied material from Fleetwood Mac memoir without permission)
   * Fela! Lawsuit (2011) (Biographer sued for uncredited use of his authorized memoir in the Broadway musical)
   * Urinetown Regional Productions Dispute (2006) (Creative team alleged that regional productions copied Broadway staging and choreography)
   * MTI vs. Unlicensed Hamilton Production (2019) (Community theater fined for producing Hamilton without a license)
   * Netflix vs. The Unofficial Bridgerton Musical (2022) (Creators sued for profiting from a live stage version without rights to the original IP)
   * etc.

Glossary

* Formal - Following or according with established form, custom, or rule.14
* Informal - Not following or according with established form, custom, or rule.14

* Techniques - Specific skills, pedagogies, theories, or methods of investigation used by an [artist].17

This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students communicated characters, situations, and scenarios using body and voice while adhering to staging conventions. Theatre artists communicate their artistic knowledge through demonstration of concepts and skills.

* Staging conventions
* How the formality level of a production changes choices
* Physical acting techniques (i.e. gesture, posture, movement, facial expressions)
* Vocal acting techniques (i.e. projection, articulation, tone, pitch, pacing)
* How staging and acting choices affect an audience's understanding
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Putting staging conventions and acting techniques into practice in both formal and informal presentations
Evidence of Mastery

Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Theatre is a performance art form in which characters, situations, and scenarios are presented physically and vocally as interpreted by actors
* Staging conventions—including locations, movements, and placement—are techniques used by performers and directors to relay visual and audible information to an audience
* The specific choices made by actors and directors may vary depending on the formality of the production
* While staging conventions and techniques are often standardized, they must be applied uniquely for each individual production
* Since this objective has the language “formal and informal theatrical productions” either type may be presented
* Since this objective has the language “vocal and physical” both acting techniques must be used
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

* Provide opportunities for rehearsal and reflection on the integration of staging and acting techniques. Scaffold instruction using the psychomotor taxonomy: begin with imitation, guide students toward accurate repetition from memory, and ultimately support confident, fluent performance
* Monitor individual students' ability to apply staging conventions to formal and informal theatrical productions by employing vocal and physical acting techniques through formative and summative assessment

I.PR.2.4 Compare the effect various theatrical venues have upon performance.
Glossary
   * Compare - To locate and/or list similarities and differences.14
   * Performance - Experience of engaging in the act of presenting [artistic skills] in a classroom or private or public venue.¹⁷
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified the responsibilities of a performer in a variety of theatrical venues. Now they are comparing the effect various theatrical venues have upon performance. In the next level, they will adapt blocking and stage directions for a variety of venues.
Enduring Understanding
Performance spaces influence how theatre artists adapt their work.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * Types of performance spaces (traditional and alternative)
   * Limitations of performance spaces (need to adapt blocking, technical theatre limits, audience space, etc)
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Identifying pros and cons of different performance spaces for performance types
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Theatrical works are presentations of stories communicated through the words and actions of performers.  The performer is responsible for communicating the thoughts and feelings of characters as they navigate environments and situations that exist within the story.    Venue type, size, and performance space will impact how the action is presented on stage.  Theatre artists must consider and evaluate the effectiveness of technical and artistic choices for each production, including how venue parameters affect production choices overall
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Prepare students by activating and discussing their prior knowledge about how performers may need to adjust their performance in different venues
   * Demonstrate how a performer adapts elements such as staging, blocking, or vocal techniques to suit a particular space, using video clips or other media. Highlight how delivery changes based on the venue’s characteristics
   * Guide students in comparing the modifications performers must make for various theatrical venues. Examples may include adjustments to staging, blocking, vocal projection, lighting awareness, costumes and makeup, audience proximity, and physical limitations of the performance space
   * Provide prompts, scaffolded questions, and diagrams or maps of different theatre spaces to support student understanding and analysis
   * Monitor individual students’ ability to compare the effect various theatrical venues have upon performance through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
   * venue options and limitations - architecture, size, scale, location, equipment, audience location
   * types of performance spaces available at various venues (proscenium, in the round, thrust, traverse, blackbox, flexible, arena)
   * impact of venue on communicating the story through vocal, physical,  and technical elements used by the performer
   * considerations of venue on performance regarding safety and accessibility for cast/crew, and audience
   * vocal elements: volume, diction, inflection, projection
   * movement elements: facial expression, body language,  blocking, body position, sight lines, remaining open
   * improvisations vs scripted performances
   * pantomime vs play vs musical
   * impact of size, scale, and location of venue
   * blocking terminology
   * directing terminology
   * examples of venues and related performance spaces
   * staging considerations regarding:
   * residency productions vs. touring productions
   * indoor vs outdoor
   * flexible space vs designated stage
   * expanding staging vs minimalizing staging
   * type and availability of technical equipment to support staging
   * size and location of the audience as it relates to the staging
   * venue examples may include a classroom, auditorium, community theatre, professional theatre, dinner theatre, 99-seat theatre, stadium, amphitheater, park, outdoor festival, parade, etc.

I.RE.1.1 Compare the structure, style, and genre of various written and presented theatrical works.
Glossary
      * Compare - To locate and/or list similarities and differences.14
      * Dramatic Structure - The organization of a script or story line that includes exposition, rising action, climax and falling action. 21
      * Genre - Category of art or design identified by similarities in form, subject matter, content, or technique. 17
      * Styles - Specific movement characteristics, qualities, or principles that give it distinctive identity. 17
      * Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students analyzed the dramatic structure of written and presented theatrical works. Now they are comparing the structure, style and genre of various written and presented theatrical works. In the next level, they will analyze the impact of historical and cultural context on written and presented theatrical works.
Enduring Understanding
The techniques and elements in a theatre work can be objectively identified and analyzed.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
      * Structure, style, and genre of various written works
      * Different genres and styles often follow specific structural patterns, but can also break or subvert them for effect
      * Presented works (i.e., performed productions) may emphasize or alter aspects of structure, style, or genre compared to written scripts
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
      * Identifying the similarities and differences in structure, style, and genre of various written and presented works
      * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
      * Theatrical works are stories, both written and performed, that range in structure, style, and genre.  Elements of dramatic structure are the building blocks for theatrical works and should be clearly identifiable in both written and performed works.  Theatre artists must be able to analyze the dramatic elements, including who, what, when, where, why, and how, in both written and presented theatrical works.
      * Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts
      * Since this objective has the language “written and presented” both types must be considered
      * Since this objective has the language “structure, style, and genre” each must be considered
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
      * Prepare examples of clearly defined works that have differing dramatic structures representing various styles and genres
      * Demonstrate how to analyze the dramatic structure of familiar theatrical works within the same genre by identifying the exposition, rising action, climax, and falling action and cataloguing these on a chart to make comparisons
      * Guide students in identifying dramatic structures and then comparing the structures between various styles and genres using stories from PR.1.2, PR.2.1, or CN.1.1
      * Provide opportunities for students to identify the dramatic structures of various styles and genres of presentations and supply worksheets or graphic organizers to help students compare them
      * Maintain established rules and procedures for appropriate, supportive, respectful, and reflective exploration of theatre development as it relates to society and history
      * Monitor individual students' ability to compare the structure style and genre of various written and presented theatrical works through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
      * Theatrical works with different structures, styles, and genres - key aspects, patterns, formats, and variations for each
      * Literary Terms: character, setting, plot, conflict, theme, mood, tone, situation, style, genre, protagonist, antagonist, goal
      * Script vs Scenario:  script structure, scenario components
      * Script - A/B format for dialogue and stage directions
      * Scenario - characters, conflict/situation, plot points
      * Story Guidelines: Freytag's Pyramid, Types of Conflict, Aristotle’s elements of drama and storytelling
      * Techniques to communicate effectively in action or writing, the dramatic elements and structure; dialogue, character goals, character behaviors, situations, setting, etc.

An observer’s interpretation of a work may align with or differ from the artist’s original intent.

I.RE.2.1 Explain how personal preferences impact the ability to view theatrical works objectively.
Glossary
      * Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it. 4
      * Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences. 17
      * Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students explained how personal preferences and previously experienced theatrical works can impact critiques. Now they are explaining how personal preferences impact the ability to view theatrical works objectively. In the next level, they will describe the influence of professional theatre and trends on personal preferences for theatrical works.
Enduring Understanding
An observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with theatre works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
      * Subjectivity is evaluation with bias, and is not objective analysis
      * Evaluators must first acknowledge their preferences in order to approach evaluation without bias
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
      * Describing the reasons personal preference can cloud objective analysis of theatrical works
      * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
      * Theatrical works come in many forms, including size, scale, genre, style, and budget
      * Theatre artists develop their own preference for certain styles, genres, and types of productions
      * Theatre artists need to reflect and evaluate how and why their personal preferences for various theatre elements and types of productions impact their ability to make observations objectively versus subjectively
      * Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
      * Prepare examples of performances that may elicit strong opinions or emotional responses and pair them with neutral analysis prompts
      * Demonstrate the difference between subjective and objective criticism and model how personal preferences can cloud or clarify critical judgment
      * Guide students in analyzing how their likes or dislikes affect how they evaluate a work and encourage balance between emotional response and objective criteria
      * Provide vocabulary charts, evaluation checklists, and reflection questions that help students develop awareness of bias and strive for balanced critique
      * Monitor individual students' ability to explain how personal preferences impact the ability to view theatrical works objectively through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
      * Define: perception, preference, critique
      * Define: objective vs. subjective observations
      * Previous theatrical experiences of students
      * Personal preferences of students regarding theatrical productions including style, genre, scale, staging choices, technical choices, etc.
      * Professional theatre vs amateur
      * Profit vs non-profit
      * Educational, community, regional, touring, residency
      * Categories of plays and musicals
      * Contemporary productions
      * Theatre districts, professional companies, and producers:  Broadway, Off-Broadway, West End, Disney, etc.
      * Current trends in theatre-structure, style,  genres, scale, technical elements
      * Size, scale, and logistics of productions
      * Budget, rights, royalties, admission price, production costs, merchandising
      * Audience, accessibility
      * Facilities required,  technical tools and equipment required
      * Historical trends in theatre
      * Pop culture and trends
      * Social media and marketing

I.RE.2.2 Generate objective critique of theatrical works using given criteria.
Glossary
      * Critique - Individual or collective reflective process by which artists or designers experience, analyze, and evaluate a work of art or design. 17
      * Criteria - The rules or guidelines used for categorizing or judging. 28
      * Generate - To create by means of a defined process : produce.14
      * Given - Qualities or traits for assessing achievement level that are provided to students.17
      * Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students generated verbal or written critique of theatrical works. Now they are generating objective critique of theatrical works using given criteria. In the next level, they will critique theatrical works citing specific areas of focus with effective suggestions for improvement.
Enduring Understanding
Providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
      * Given criteria
      * Objective critique has removed bias and preference
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
      * Producing objective evaluation of works using provided criteria
      * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
      * Theatre is an art form of continuous improvement in which reflections and critiques are used as part of the creative process.  Theatre artists should understand how to make objective critiques, both verbal and written, using specific criteria
      * Theatre artists must make observations that are focused on the presence and quality of choices made within theatrical works separately and as part of the overall production
      * Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
      * Prepare performance rubrics and shared vocabulary banks aligned to production goals
      * Demonstrate how to evaluate theatre using objective language and specific references to the criteria
      * Guide students through layered critique exercises focused on a single area (e.g. voice, gesture, emotion)
      * Provide feedback opportunities that allow students to revise based on received critique
      * Monitor individual students' ability to generate objective critique of theatrical works using given criteria through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
      * Define: critique, criteria
      * Specific requirements and qualities for successful scripts, performances, and technical elements
      * Objective vs. subjective observations
      * Communicating observations effectively through dialogue and  writing
      * Observations of script, performance, and technical elements
      * Criticism vs constructive criticism
      * Specific notes
      * Artist intention vs audience interpretation
      * Refinement, tightening, quality, scale, intensity, consistency, clarity
      * Intentional choices, bold choices
      * Action vs reaction
      * Authenticity and being "in the moment"
      * Intentional exaggeration vs intentional minimization
      * Tempo, cadence, rhythm, volume, diction, articulation
      * Imagery, placement, movement, timing, blocking, staging
      * Transitions, cues, in/out, enter/exit, early/late
      * Setting, plot, theme, tone, mood, style, genre
