# Healthful Living - High School

North Carolina Unpacking Document for Health Education

Health Education K-12

The Unpacking Documents for North Carolina K-12 Health Education were created in collaboration with teachers, NCDPI leadership, and members of the NCDPI Healthy Schools Team. These documents are intended to supplement the standard course of study and provide a comprehensive understanding for the teaching of the standards and objectives. The explanations and examples in this document are intended to be helpful in the planning of local curriculum and classroom instruction.

This document will provide:
* Strand: defined area of instruction
* Essential Standard: the State Board of Education approved standard(s) for a strand
* Clarifying Objective: the State Board of Education approved objectives for teaching and learning
* Verb: definition of the verb used to assess student understanding
* Students will know and be able to: information that the students should know and a description of how the student should be able to demonstrate mastery of the objective

The example topics provided with each objective are:
* Content examples for instruction that help to build student knowledge and understanding of the objective
* Sample activities to gauge learning that may be used to determine whether students are meeting the learning objective
* Recommendations, with the understanding that PSUs retain local control to determine curriculum

The example topics provided with each objective are:
* Not meant to be an exhaustive list
* Not meant to be content that must be taught all at once
* Not a checklist for basic recall or memorization
* Not a checklist for assessment for each objective

Note: These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a local public-school unit (PSU) or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-9 Health Education.

Essential Standard:
9.MEH.1 Promote the destigmatization of seeking mental health resources.
Clarifying Objective:
9.MEH.1.1 Identify causes, symptoms, and strategies for treating mental health challenges.
Verb Defined:
Establish or indicate who or what something is that is unfamiliar or unknown
Student will know and be able to:
Causes of Mental Health Challenges:
* Biological Factors:
   * Genetics:
      * Family history of mental illness can increase susceptibility.
   * Brain Chemistry:
      * Imbalances in neurotransmitters (chemicals that transmit signals between brain cells).
   * Hormonal Changes:
      * Hormonal fluctuations can affect mood and contribute to mental health issues.
* Psychological Factors:
   * Stressful Life Events:
      * Trauma, abuse, loss, major life changes, and chronic stress can trigger mental health problems.
   * Negative Thinking Patterns:
      * Cognitive distortions like all-or-nothing thinking and catastrophizing can contribute to anxiety and depression.
   * Low Self-Esteem:
      * Negative self-perception can impact mental well-being.
* Social Factors:
   * Social Isolation:
      * Lack of social connection and support.
   * Discrimination and Stigma:
      * Experiences of discrimination or social exclusion can have a significant impact on mental health.
   * Socioeconomic Factors:
      * Poverty, unemployment, and lack of access to resources can increase the risk of mental health problems.
* Environmental Factors:
   * Exposure to violence or trauma:
      * Witnessing or experiencing violence can have long-lasting psychological effects.
   * Substance abuse:
      * Use of alcohol and drugs can worsen existing mental health conditions or trigger new ones.
Symptoms of Mental Health Challenges:
* Mood Disorders:
   * Depression:
      * Persistent sadness, loss of interest, fatigue, changes in appetite or sleep.
   * Bipolar Disorder:
      * Extreme shifts in mood, energy, and activity levels.
* Anxiety Disorders:
   * Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD):
      * Excessive worry and anxiety.
   * Panic Disorder:
      * Recurrent panic attacks.
   * Social Anxiety Disorder:
      * Fear of social situations.
* Other Mental Health Conditions:
   * Eating Disorders:
      * Anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge-eating disorder.
   * Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD):
      * Re-experiencing trauma through flashbacks and nightmares.
   * Schizophrenia:
      * A severe mental disorder characterized by hallucinations, delusions, and disordered thinking.

Strategies for Treating Mental Health Challenges:
* Therapy:
   * Psychotherapy, such as cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) or interpersonal therapy, can help individuals identify and change negative thinking patterns and develop coping skills.
* Medication:
   * In some cases, medication may be necessary to address underlying biological factors, such as antidepressants for depression or anti-anxiety medications.
* Lifestyle Changes:
   * Regular exercise, a healthy diet, and sufficient sleep can significantly improve mental well-being.
* Support Groups:
   * Connecting with others who understand can provide valuable support and coping strategies.
* Mindfulness and Relaxation Techniques:
   * Practices like meditation, deep breathing, and yoga can help reduce stress and anxiety.

Student will know and be able to:
The at-risk populations for depression, suicide, and impaired mental health include:
* Adolescents and Young Adults:
   * This age group experiences significant hormonal and social changes, increasing vulnerability to mental health challenges.
* Seniors:
   * Isolation, chronic illnesses, and grief can increase the risk of depression and suicide in older adults.
* Individuals with Chronic Illnesses:
   * Conditions like diabetes, heart disease, and cancer can significantly impact mental health.
* People with Disabilities:
   * Physical and mental disabilities can increase the risk of social isolation, depression, and anxiety.
* LGBTQ+ Individuals:
   * Discrimination and social stigma can contribute to higher rates of mental health issues within the LGBTQ+ community.  (Note the statistics from the YRBS on high school mental health, depression, and risk of suicide for this group. Compare results to overall high school results of heterosexual youth.)
* Veterans:
   * Military service can expose individuals to trauma, leading to PTSD, depression, and increased suicide risk.
* First Responders:
   * Occupations like law enforcement and emergency services can expose individuals to high levels of stress and trauma.
Causes of Depression, Suicide, and Impaired Mental Health:
* Biological Factors:
   * Genetics
   * Brain chemistry imbalances
   * Hormonal changes
* Psychological Factors:
   * Stressful life events (e.g., loss, trauma, abuse)
   * Negative thinking patterns
   * Low self-esteem
* Social Factors:
   * Isolation and loneliness
   * Lack of social support
   * Discrimination and stigma
* Environmental Factors:
   * Exposure to violence or trauma
   * Socioeconomic disadvantage
   * Substance abuse
Symptoms of Depression:
* Persistent sadness
* Loss of interest or pleasure in activities
* Changes in appetite or sleep
* Fatigue
* Feelings of worthlessness or guilt
* Difficulty concentrating
* Thoughts of death or suicide
Symptoms of Suicide:
* Talking about suicide or death
* Giving away possessions
* Withdrawing from social activities
* Changes in eating or sleeping habits
* Changes in school performance; not going to school
* Increased alcohol or drug use
* Engaging in risky behaviors
Important Note:
* This is not an exhaustive list, and individual experiences can vary significantly.

Clarifying Objective:
9.MEH.1. 3 Develop a support plan for peers experiencing mental health challenges.
Verb Defined:
To create or produce especially by deliberate effort over time - to expand by a process of growth
Student will know and be able to:
Developing a support plan for high school peers experiencing mental health challenges is a valuable endeavor, but it's crucial to remember that you are not a professional therapist. Your role is to provide support and encourage them to seek professional help. Here's a framework for developing a support plan:
1. Recognize the Signs:

   * Understand common mental health challenges:
   * Depression, anxiety, eating disorders, self-harm, etc.
   * Be observant:
   * Notice changes in behavior, mood, academic performance, social interactions, and appearance.
   * Educate yourself:
   * Learn to recognize potential warning signs.
   2. Approach with Sensitivity and Empathy:

      * Choose the right time and place:
      * Find a private and comfortable setting for a conversation.
      * Express concern:
      * "I've noticed some changes in you lately, and I'm worried about you."
      * Listen actively and non-judgmentally:
      * Allow them to share their feelings without interruption or judgment.
      * Validate their feelings:
      * "That sounds really tough. It's okay to not be okay."
      3. Offer Support and Encourage Help-Seeking:

         * Suggest helpful resources:
         * School counselor
         * School psychologist
         * Therapist or psychiatrist
         * Crisis hotlines (e.g., 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
         * Support groups
         * Offer to help them find resources:
         * Assist with scheduling appointments or gathering contact information.
         * Be a supportive friend:
         * Offer to spend time together, engage in enjoyable activities, and provide a listening ear.
         4. Maintain Confidentiality:

            * Respect their privacy and maintain confidentiality unless there is an immediate threat to their safety or the safety of others.
            5. Take Care of Yourself:

               * Supporting a friend with mental health challenges can be emotionally draining.
               * Prioritize your own well-being and seek support from trusted adults or friends.
Important Considerations:
               * You are not a professional:
               * Understand the limitations of your role as a peer. Your primary goal is to encourage professional help.
               * Focus on listening and supporting:
               * Avoid giving unsolicited advice or trying to "fix" the problem.
               * Be patient and understanding:
               * Recovery takes time, and there may be setbacks.
               * Seek guidance from trusted adults:
               * If you're unsure how to best support your friend, consult with a school counselor, teacher, or other trusted adult.

Healthy behaviors to help with stress management
               * Relaxation Techniques:

               * Slow, deep breaths can calm the nervous system.
               * Mindfulness Meditation:
               * Focus on the present moment without judgment.
               * Progressive Muscle Relaxation:

Anxiety Management

               * Helps identify and change negative thinking patterns.
               * Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR):

               * Connecting with others who understand can provide valuable support and coping strategies.
Anger Management

               * Recognize situations, thoughts, or behaviors that tend to trigger anger.
               * Deep Breathing Exercises:
               * Calm down the body's stress response.
               * Assertiveness Training:
               * Learn to express feelings assertively and respectfully.
               * Problem-Solving Skills:
               * Develop effective strategies for resolving conflicts and addressing frustrations.
Managing Impulsive Behaviors
               * Impulse Control Techniques:
               * Mindful Pauses:
               * Take a moment to pause and think before acting on impulses.
               * Distraction Techniques:
               * Engage in activities that divert attention and allow emotions to subside.

               * Identify and challenge negative or irrational thoughts that may be contributing to impulsive behavior.
               * Goal Setting:
               * Set realistic goals and break them down into smaller, manageable steps.
               * Seek Professional Help:
               * If impulsive behaviors are interfering with life, consider seeking guidance from a therapist or counselor.

A person who lacks adequate sleep and rest can suffer from fatigue, lapses in concentration, weight gain or loss, and clumsiness. Getting adequate amounts of sleep and rest provides many benefits to one’s health and well-being.

Measures to improve sleep and rest:

               * limit napping to 30 minutes

Clarifying Objective:
9.PCH.1.6 Identify strategies for the prevention and early detection of skin cancer.
Verb Defined:
Establish or indicate who or what something is that is unfamiliar or unknown
Student will know and be able to:
Skin cancer often begins to develop early in life when there is overexposure to the sun. It is easier to detect than many other cancers because it can be seen. Self-examinations of the skin are important steps in preventing the spread of cancer. The ABCDE test for skin cancer should be used in a self-exam.
               * A stands for Asymmetry.
If a mole is asymmetrical, it should be seen by a dermatologist.

               * B stands for Border.
A mole that has a fuzzy or irregular border should also be seen by a dermatologist.

               * C stands for Color.
A mole with red, white, or blue colors in it is a mole that may be cancerous.

               * D stands for Diameter.
If the diameter of a mole is larger than the diameter of a pencil eraser, it should be seen by a doctor.

               * E stands for Evolving (changing).
Like other cancers, early detection of skin cancer is crucial for successful treatment. And like other cancers, prevention is the key.

Avoiding the use of tanning beds, avoiding extended time in the sun and wearing sunblock are important steps in preventing skin cancer.

Firearms

                     * Driving while fatigued or sleep-deprived.
                     * Vehicle Malfunction:
                     * Poorly maintained vehicles (e.g., faulty brakes, bald tires) can increase the risk of accidents.
Key Considerations:
                     * Responsible Ownership:
                     * Proper firearm storage, regular maintenance, and responsible handling are crucial for firearm safety.
                     * Defensive Driving:
                     * Practicing defensive driving techniques, such as maintaining a safe following distance, scanning the road ahead, and anticipating potential hazards.
                     * Education and Training:
                     * Firearms safety training courses and driver's education courses can provide valuable knowledge and skills.
                     * Laws and Regulations:
                     * Understanding and complying with all relevant laws and regulations related to firearms and motor vehicles.

Applicable State Laws Governing the Age of Sexual Consent in North Carolina (https://www.ncleg.net/enactedlegislation/statutes/html/bysection/chapter_14/gs_14-190.13.html)
In North Carolina, the age of sexual consent is 16 years old. This means that individuals who are 16 years of age or older can legally engage in consensual sexual activities. However, there are several important nuances and legal stipulations surrounding this law that must be understood to fully grasp its implications.
1. Legal Framework
The relevant statute governing the age of consent in North Carolina is found in the North Carolina General Statutes (N.C.G.S.) § 14-27.7. According to this law, it is illegal for an adult (defined as someone aged 18 or older) to engage in sexual activity with a minor under the age of 16. If such an act occurs, it may be classified as statutory rape or sexual offense, depending on the circumstances.
2. Statutory Rape and Sexual Offense
                     * Statutory Rape:
                     * Under N.C.G.S. § 14-27.7(a), if a person engages in sexual intercourse with a victim who is at least 13 but less than 16 years old, they can be charged with statutory rape if they are at least six years older than the victim.
                     * Sexual Offense:
                     * Similarly, N.C.G.S. § 14-27.4 outlines that engaging in sexual acts with a minor under these conditions can lead to charges of a sexual offense.
Both offenses are classified as felonies, which carry severe penalties.
3. Legal Consequences
Violating the age of consent laws can lead to serious legal consequences:
                     * Felony Charges:
                     * Both statutory rape and sexual offense are categorized as Class B1 felonies if the victim is under 15 years old, and the offender is more than six years older than them. Convictions can result in significant prison sentences—ranging from several years to life imprisonment depending on various factors, including prior criminal history.
                     * Sex Offender Registration:
                     * Individuals convicted of these offenses are also required to register as sex offenders, which has long-term implications for employment, housing, and personal relationships.
                     * Civil Penalties:
                     * In addition to criminal penalties, offenders may face civil lawsuits from victims seeking damages for emotional distress or other harms caused by the violation.
4. Defenses and Exceptions
While there are limited defenses available under North Carolina law regarding age of consent violations, one notable exception includes “Romeo and Juliet” laws that provide some leniency for individuals close in age (typically within four years). However, these exceptions do not apply universally and depend heavily on specific circumstances surrounding each case.
5. Importance of Understanding Consent Laws
It is crucial for individuals—especially those working with minors or involved in relationships where there may be an age difference—to understand these laws thoroughly to avoid unintentional violations that could lead to severe legal repercussions.
While the age of consent in North Carolina is set at 16 years old, violations involving minors can result in serious felony charges and lifelong consequences, including mandatory registration as a sex offender.

Sexually transmitted infections (STIs), including HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) and HPV (Human Papillomavirus), can significantly impact an individual's health. Understanding their risks, modes of transmission, symptoms, testing, and treatment is crucial for prevention and management.
Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)
                     * Risks
                     * HIV weakens the immune system, increasing vulnerability to opportunistic infections and certain cancers.
                     * If untreated, it progresses to AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome), which is life-threatening.

                     * Mode of Transmission
                     * Sexual contact:
                     * Vaginal, anal, or oral sex with an infected partner.
                     * Blood exposure:
                     * Sharing needles, syringes, or through contaminated blood transfusions.
                     * Mother-to-child:
                     * During pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding.
                     * Symptoms
                     * Early (acute) stage:
                     * Flu-like symptoms (fever, sore throat, swollen lymph nodes, rash).
                     * Chronic stage:
                     * May remain asymptomatic for years while the virus continues to damage the immune system.
                     * Advanced stage (AIDS):
                     * Severe infections, weight loss, and certain cancers.
                     * Testing
                     * Antibody/antigen tests (blood or saliva): Detect infection early.
                     * Nucleic acid tests (NAT): Identify the virus in the blood and confirm diagnosis.
                     * Treatment
                     * Antiretroviral therapy (ART):
                     * A lifelong combination of medications that suppress the virus, improve immune function, and reduce transmission risk.
                     * Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP)
                     * medicine people at risk for HIV take to prevent getting HIV from sex or injection drug use. PrEP can stop HIV from taking hold and spreading throughout your body.
                     * Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP)
                     * a treatment that can help prevent HIV infection after a possible exposure to the virus. It involves taking a combination of antiretroviral medications for 28 days after exposure.
Human Papillomavirus (HPV)
                     * Risks
                     * Most HPV infections resolve/clear on their own.
                     * Some strains can cause cervical, anal, penile, throat, and other cancers.
                     * Other strains may lead to genital warts.
                     * Mode of Transmission
                     * Sexual contact:
                     * Vaginal, anal, or oral sex.
                     * Skin-to-skin contact with an infected area.
                     * Symptoms
                     * Often asymptomatic, especially for high-risk strains.
                     * Genital warts:
                     * Small bumps or clusters in the genital or anal area.
                     * Persistent high-risk infections:
                     * May lead to abnormal cell changes detectable during cancer screening.
                     * Testing
                     * Pap smear:
                     * Detects abnormal cervical cells.
                     * HPV DNA test:
                     * Identifies high-risk HPV strains.
                     * Visual examination:
                     * For genital warts.

                     * Treatment
                     * No cure for the virus itself; management focuses on symptoms and complications:
                     * Vaccination:
                     * Prevents more than 90% of cancers caused by HPV. Helps prevent genital warts by protecting against the HPV strains that cause them.
                     * Genital warts:
                     * Medications or surgical removal.
                     * Precancerous changes:
                     * Procedures like cryotherapy, laser treatment, or excision.
General STI Considerations
                     * Prevention
                     * Abstinence
                     * Practice safe sex:
                     * Use condoms and dental dams consistently and correctly.
                     * Limit the number of sexual partners and maintain open communication about STI testing.
                     * Get vaccinated:
                     * HPV vaccines like Gardasil and Hepatitis B vaccines.
                     * Regular testing:
                     * Especially if sexually active with new or multiple partners.
Health Impacts of Untreated STIs
                     * Long-term health issues, including infertility, chronic pain, and cancer (e.g., from untreated/unvaccinated HPV).
                     * Increased risk of acquiring or transmitting other STIs, including HIV.
Timely testing, treatment, and preventive measures are essential to managing these infections and maintaining sexual and overall health.

                                          * carbohydrates
                                          * fats
                                          * proteins
                                          * water
                                          * vitamins
                                          * minerals

Carbohydrates, fat, and protein are considered macronutrients (macro = big) because large amounts of these nutrients are needed to function, and they provide our body with energy (measured in calories).

If a variety of foods are consumed from the different food groups (fruits, vegetables, grains, protein foods, and dairy) the 6 essential nutrients will be consumed.

By consuming the 6 essential nutrients in adequate amounts, the body performs its many functions at its best, reducing the risk for health concerns.

                                          * Some PEDs weaken the immune system, making athletes more susceptible to infections.
                                          6. Musculoskeletal Risks:

Long-Term Effects of Performance-Enhancing Drugs on Health

9.ANCOD.2.5 Discuss the complexity of addiction and its effects on individuals and society.

Student will know and be able to:
Addiction is a complex, multifaceted phenomenon that affects individuals and society in profound ways. It involves not only physical dependence but also psychological, social, and environmental factors. Understanding the complexity of addiction requires an exploration of its causes, effects, and consequences, both on the individual and at the societal level.
1. Causes of Addiction
        Addiction does not have a single cause but arises from a combination of factors:
                                          * Biological Factors:
                                          * Genetics play a significant role in addiction susceptibility. Certain individuals may have a genetic predisposition that makes them more likely to develop addiction when exposed to addictive substances or behaviors. Neurobiological processes, including changes in brain chemistry, are also implicated in the development of addiction. Drugs and alcohol, for instance, can alter neurotransmitter function, particularly dopamine, which is associated with pleasure and reward.
                                          * Psychological Factors:
                                          * Mental health disorders, such as anxiety, depression, and trauma, often co-occur with addiction. People may turn to substances or addictive behaviors as a coping mechanism to deal with emotional pain or distress. Psychological dependence can develop, where individuals feel that they cannot function without the substance or behavior.
                                          * Social and Environmental Factors:
                                          * Peer pressure, family dynamics, socioeconomic status, and cultural norms can contribute to the initiation and continuation of addiction. People who grow up in environments where drug use or addiction is normalized may be at higher risk. Additionally, stress from financial insecurity, unemployment, or relationship problems can trigger or exacerbate addiction.

2. Effects on Individuals
      Addiction has profound and wide-ranging effects on individuals, both physically and mentally:
                                          * Physical Health:
                                          * Chronic addiction can lead to numerous health problems, such as liver disease (from alcohol), lung cancer (from smoking), or heart problems (from stimulant use). Many addictive substances also weaken the immune system and increase susceptibility to infections.
                                          * Mental Health:
                                          * Addiction is often accompanied by mental health disorders like anxiety, depression, or psychosis. The cycle of addiction can exacerbate these conditions, leading to a vicious cycle where the individual uses the substance to cope with mental health issues, only to find their condition worsened over time.
                                          * Cognitive and Behavioral Effects:
                                          * Addiction can impair judgment, decision-making, and self-control. It may lead to compulsive behaviors, such as seeking substances or engaging in certain actions, regardless of the negative consequences. Over time, addiction may alter the brain's structure and function, leading to changes in how an individual thinks, behaves, and interacts with others.
                                          * Social Consequences:
                                          * Relationships with family, friends, and colleagues are often strained or destroyed by addiction. Individuals may isolate themselves, and their addictive behaviors can lead to conflicts, legal issues, or loss of employment. The stigma associated with addiction can also lead to feelings of shame and guilt, which may prevent individuals from seeking help.
3. Effects on Society
       Addiction does not only affect the individual but has broader social implications:
                                          * Economic Costs:
                                          * Addiction places a heavy burden on healthcare systems, law enforcement, and the economy. The cost of treating addiction-related health conditions, as well as the loss of productivity due to addiction-related absenteeism or job loss, can be substantial. The legal system also incurs costs related to criminal activities often associated with addiction, such as drug trafficking or theft.

                                          * Social Disruption:
                                          * Communities can be torn apart by addiction, as families and social networks suffer. The cycle of addiction may be passed down from one generation to the next, perpetuating a cycle of disadvantage and dysfunction. Areas with high addiction rates may also experience higher levels of crime, homelessness, and poverty, further exacerbating social inequalities.
                                          * Impact on Public Health:
                                          * Addiction can lead to a range of public health concerns, including the spread of infectious diseases (e.g., through needle sharing in the case of intravenous drug use), mental health crises, and increased rates of injury or death due to accidents, overdoses, or violence.
                                          * Stigmatization and Marginalization:
                                          * People with addiction often face stigma, leading to marginalization. This can make it more difficult for them to find help, recover, or reintegrate into society. Stigma can also discourage preventive measures or harm reduction programs from being fully embraced, further limiting societal efforts to address addiction.
Addiction is a multifaceted issue that involves biological, psychological, and social factors. Its effects on both individuals and society are profound, with addiction contributing to health issues, strained relationships, economic costs, and social instability. However, through prevention, treatment, harm reduction, and societal change, the cycle of addiction can be addressed, offering hope for individuals and communities alike.
