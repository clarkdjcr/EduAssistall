# English Language Development - Parent/Caregiver Guide

Parent/Caregiver Guide for English Language Development
Offering A Better Understanding of the K-12 Multilingual Learner Program

How Did We Get Here?
On March 4, 2021, the State Board of Education unanimously approved the 2020 Edition of the WIDA English Language Development (ELD) Standards as the North Carolina ELD Standard Course of Study (NC ELD SCOS) for implementation in the 2022-2023 school year. The NC ELD standards serve as the framework that guides each Public School Unit (PSU) in the development of the curriculum and instruction of their Multilingual Learners.

The North Carolina Department of Public Instruction Multilingual Learner (ML)/Title III (NCDPI ML/Title III) Team developed documents to support the implementation of the NC ELD SCOS. One of those support documents is the Parent/Caregiver Guide for ELD. This document is a guide to help parents/caregivers of Multilingual Learners (MLs) understand the meaning of the ML identification process, the ELD Standards, and access resources to support at-home learning.

It is important that you, as your child's parent/caregiver, reach out to your child's school to learn more about the services and opportunities offered in your school community based on this guidance.

Section 1: What does it mean that my child is a Multilingual Learner (ML)?
This section includes the Multilingual Learner (ML) identification process, the North Carolina ML identification and exit criteria, the annual English language proficiency assessment, your child's ML Status, and a sample of the language instruction services that your child might receive.

The NCDPI also uses the term Multilingual Learner (ML) to refer to MLs. Policy and legislation documents shared by your school may use the term English Learner or EL, and other documents may include ML. These terms are used interchangeably.

ML Identification Process Overview
The ML identification process moves your child through five stages: Enrollment, Screening, Services, Assessment, and Exit. Each stage is described below.

Stage 1: Enrollment
Every parent/caregiver completes a Home Language Survey (HLS) when they enroll their child, answering questions about the language(s) their family uses to communicate in their home.

A school representative may contact you to clarify some of the information you enter in the HLS. This conversation will help the school determine if your child needs to be screened for English language development services.

Stage 2: Screening
Your child completes a screening process to determine their English language proficiency (ELP) levels in listening, speaking, reading, and writing, and their ML status.

Stage 3: Services
Based on your child's English proficiency levels and status, they may qualify for ELD services:
* Instruction for English language development
* Academic supports in classes/courses
* Classroom Instructional Accommodations
* Testing accommodations on state assessments (only students who score below Level 5.0 Bridging on the reading domain of the ELP test)

Stage 4: Assessment
Your child participates in an annual statewide ELP assessment each spring until reaching the state's exit criteria. These scores help your child's school:
* Determine their progress in English
* Determine the type of continued ML program services if applicable

Stage 5: Exit
Your child meets the state's exit criteria when reaching 1.)  an overall score of 4.5 or above in the annual statewide ELP assessment or 2.) meets the Multiple Measure Tool Exit criteria.

If your child is instructed using the NC Extended Content Standards, your child meets the state's exit criteria when reaching an overall score of P2 or higher.

Your child is monitored for 4 years after they exit ML identification to ensure they continue to demonstrate English language growth.

Enrollment: ML Identification Process
All North Carolina Public Schools are required to identify potential Multilingual Learners (ML). When you enroll your child, you will complete a Home Language Survey (HLS). If a language other than English is identified on that survey, a school representative may contact you to clarify information on the HLS. This conversation will help the school determine if your child needs to be assessed. If your child is transferring from another school, previous language proficiency assessment results may be used to determine their ML status. You should be notified of your child's ML status within 30 days of your child's enrollment of their eligibility (SBE. Policy Test -011).

Screening: North Carolina ML Identification Criteria
The state of North Carolina is a member of the WIDA Consortium. This organization has developed a comprehensive system built on standards and assessments to support K-12 MLs. North Carolina uses the WIDA Screener scores in two or more of the language domains, listening, speaking, and reading and writing, to identify your child as a Multilingual Learner. Your child's school follows the criteria listed below to determine if your child requires ML services or not. You are informed about this process by the welcome center staff or the school's ML teacher.

Depending on which semester of the school year you enroll your child in the school, the ML identification criteria are as follows:
First Semester
* students in the first semester of kindergarten who score below a 4.5 on the Oral Language (listening and speaking) composite for the WIDA Screener for Kindergarten
* students in the second semester of kindergarten through grade 12 with an overall (listening, speaking, reading, and writing) composite score below 4.5 on the WIDA Screener assessment

Services: EL Services
Each school develops a Language Instruction Educational Program (LIEP) to determine which services your child receives. These services are developed and implemented by the ML teacher at your child's school. The ML teacher works in collaboration with classroom teachers and other staff to ensure your child is receiving the best possible instruction based on their language learning needs. The services are generally described on the child's annual ML Plan. Services may include the following:
ML Service
	Description
	Instruction for English Language Development
	Instructional programs that are designed to develop the social and academic English language proficiency of your child. Typical programs in North Carolina include English as a Second Language, Bilingual Programs, or Sheltered Instruction.
	Academic Supports
	Academic supports specific to Multilingual Learners that are used in classes/courses to support your child's English language development. Some examples of those supports include classroom modifications on assignments/projects, accommodations on assessments, use of first language resources, etc.
	Testing Accommodations
	State and federal laws require that all students participate in the Annual Testing Program. Multilingual Learners who score below Level 5.0 Bridging on the reading domain of the WIDA Screener/WIDA ACCESS are eligible to receive state-approved ML testing accommodations on all state tests. Accommodations such as the use of a bilingual word-to-word dictionary, reading assessments aloud, extended time, separate setting, ensure a more equitable opportunity for Multilingual Learners to participate (NCDPI. “Guidance for Testing Students Identified as English Learners”).
	Annual State English Language Proficiency Assessment
	WIDA ACCESS and Alternate ACCESS are annual English language proficiency assessments currently used in North Carolina. All current MLs are required to be assessed annually. NC has developed annual growth targets for each ML student based on their initial ACCESS score with a goal to exit the student from ML identification within five years. The assessment is usually administered in the January through March  time frame. Parents/Caregivers of current ML students receive notification of testing prior to the start of the testing window each year.

At the beginning of the academic school year, you are notified of your child's English language proficiency, ML Status, and the ML services provided by the school.  It is important to know that at any time, as a parent/guardian, you may inquire about, request, or decline any ML services based on the needs of your child. However, declining services does not change your child's identification as an ML and they should continue to be assessed annually on the state-adopted English language proficiency assessment until they meet the state-identified exit criteria.
Assessment: English Language Proficiency (ELP) Assessment
The English language proficiency assessment currently used by North Carolina is the Assessing Comprehension and Communication in English State-to-State for English Language Learner (WIDA ACCESS). If your child is instructed using the NC Extended Content Standards, your child is eligible to participate in the Alternate WIDA ACCESS test in place of the WIDA ACCESS. This alternate assessment for the WIDA ACCESS test is for students in grades 1-12 who are classified as MLs and have significant cognitive disabilities that prevent their meaningful participation in the regular WIDA ACCESS test. Unique features of this test include simplified language, repetition of questions, increased graphics, and larger testing materials (NCDPI. “Guidance for Testing Students Identified as English Learners”).

Current scores on the annual state ELP assessment are used to determine progress towards English proficiency (NCDPI, “Calculating EL Progress”), appropriate ELD services, specific language proficiency needs, your child's exit status, etc.
Individual Student Report
Every academic year, the school sends home your child's Individual Student Report. This report shows your child's scores in eight different categories. Four of these categories are the language domains of Listening, Speaking, Reading, and Writing. These four language domain scores are combined to create four additional score types:
* Oral language category is a combination of your child's Listening and Speaking scores.
* Literacy category is a combination of your child's Reading and Writing scores.
* Comprehension category is a combination of your child's Listening and Reading scores. It depends more on the Reading score than on the Listening score.
* Overall category is a combination of all four language domain scores. It depends more on the Reading and Writing scores than on the Listening and Speaking scores.

Two Scores for Each Category
For each of the eight categories on the Individual Student Report, there are two scores:
* Proficiency level scores place your child's current skills within the six WIDA English Language Proficiency Levels. Proficiency levels are not related to a student's grade level. The bottom of the Individual Student Report describes the language skills typical of your child's proficiency level.
* Scale scores are precise measures of how your child did on the test. They take into account your child's grade level and the difficulty of the test items your child completed. Teachers use these scores to understand how much your child's English language skills have grown since the last time your child took the Annual ELP Assessment (WIDA, “WIDA ACCESS”; “Alternate WIDA ACCESS”).

Exit: Exiting EL Status
Your child must meet the criteria set by the state in order to be moved out of the ML program. The state requires a student identified as English learners to reach an overall composite score of 4.5 or above on the WIDA ACCESS assessment, Exit through the Multiple Measure Tool (MMT), or score an overall composite of PL 2 or higher on the Alternate ACCESS assessment (SBE, “Policy TEST-011”).

If your child exits the ELD program, they will no longer be required to take the annual ELP test and will no longer qualify for ML services or testing accommodations on state assessments. After exiting the program, your child’s academic progress will be monitored for four years to ensure they continue to succeed in school.

In some cases, a student who has met the exit criteria may become eligible to receive ELD services again. This process involves a careful review of the student’s academic performance, along with discussions between parents and teachers, to determine the most appropriate support plan for the student.

Section 2: How does my child learn English at School?
This section explains the English Language Development standards that NC uses as your child learns social (verbal and body language used to communicate with others daily) and academic English (language used at school to talk about specific subjects). You will learn about the progression of English language skills and the developmental expectations as your child moves through their different grade levels and eventually exits their ML status, achieving English proficiency.

As mentioned in the previous section, each school uses a Language Instruction Educational Program (LIEP) to decide which services your child receives. These services are developed and implemented by the ML teacher at your child's school. The ML teacher works in collaboration with classroom teachers and other staff to ensure your child is receiving the best possible instruction based on their language learning needs. The services are generally described on the child's annual ML Plan.

Services may include the following:
* Instruction for English language development
* Academic supports in classes/courses: classroom modifications, testing accommodations, and/or others
* Testing accommodations on state assessments (NCDPI, “Guidance for Testing Students Identified as English Learners”)
Common ML program services may include pull-out sessions during the school day, where students receive direct instruction from an ML teacher, push-in support, where the ML teacher works with students in their regular classroom setting, and ESL classes in secondary school settings. Other Language Instruction Educational Program (LIEP) options may include newcomer centers, co-teaching, sheltered instruction, and extended learning opportunities such as after-school or summer programs.
Your child’s school will inform you about the services available to your child and explain how these supports were selected. Your child’s ML teacher can also provide information to help you better understand the curriculum used at the school.

A Multilingual Learner (ML) Plan is developed by your child’s ML teacher and classroom teachers. The plan outlines the instructional services, classroom supports, and state testing accommodations available to support your child’s learning throughout the school year.
If you have questions about the Language Instruction Educational Program (LIEP) or your child’s ML Plan, please contact your child’s school.
English Language Development (ELD) Standards
The NC ELD Standard Course of Study reflects that Multilingual Learners are best served when they learn content and language together in a learning environment that utilizes home language and cultural knowledge to engage and facilitate learning.
The NC ELD standards serve as the framework that will guide your child's school in developing the curriculum and instruction of their Multilingual Learners. These standards should be complemented by a content-rich curriculum and effective pedagogical approaches within an equitable educational program for your child.

ELD Standards Framework: Levels of the Framework
The ELD Standards framework contains four nested levels. The outermost level is ELD Standards Statements. Within that is Key Language Uses, within that is Language Expectations, and at the innermost level is Proficiency Level Descriptors.
ELD Standards Statements ELD Standards Framework Levels: Nested boxes showing the four levels of the ELD Standards framework, from outermost to innermost: ELD Standards Statements, Key Language Uses, Language Expectations, and Proficiency Level Descriptors.
The ELD standards statements show expectations of language use for your child in the service of learning—in other words, expectations for the English your child will learn to use for thinking and doing across all content areas for social and instructional purposes. ELD Standard 1: Language for Social and Instructional Purposes applies language across the entire range of educational settings and works both independently from and in conjunction with ELD Standards Statements 2-5 that are content-specific (WIDA 2020).

The Five ELD Standards
Standard 1 (Language for Social and Instructional Purposes) underlies Standards 2 through 5, which are each content-specific.

* Standard 1: Language for Social and Instructional Purposes
* Standard 2: Language for Language Arts
* Standard 3: Language for Mathematics
* Standard 4: Language for Science
* Standard 5: Language for Social Studies
Key Language Uses Key Language Uses Venn: Four-circle Venn diagram showing the overlapping Key Language Uses: Narrate, Inform, Explain, and Argue.
The ELD Standards employ Key Language Uses that describe how language is used in school across all disciplines and content areas. The Key Language Uses will help your child's teachers provide language focus and coherence in the lessons they create when integrating content and language. The Key Language Uses share some common aspects across disciplines and content areas to strengthen your child's English language proficiency in each.

Narrate
Highlights language to convey real or imaginary experiences through stories and histories. Your child's stories will serve many purposes, including to instruct, entertain, teach, or support their arguments.

Inform
Highlights language to provide factual information. As your child conveys information, they define, describe, compare, contrast, organize, categorize, or classify concepts, ideas, or events.

Explain
Highlights language to give an account for how things work or why things happen. As your child explains, they are able to express the inner workings of natural, man-made, and social experiences.

Argue
Highlights language to justify claims using evidence and reasoning. Arguments can be used to advance or defend your child's ideas or solutions, change their audience's point of view, bring about action, or accept a position or evaluation of an issue.
Language Expectations
The ELD Standards incorporate Language Expectations that set goals for your child's content-driven language learning. They support the ELD Standards Statements and Key Language Uses and make visible the language associated with the content areas (WIDA 2020).

The Language Expectations combine the four individual language domains (listening, speaking, reading, and writing) into two more inclusive modes of communication: interpretive and expressive.

Modes of Communication
Interpretive
	Expressive
	The interpretive communication mode includes listening, reading, and viewing.
	The expressive communication mode includes speaking, writing, and representing.
	Proficiency Level Descriptors
The ELD Standards also use Proficiency Level Descriptors (PLDs) to describe language development progress for activities that allow your child's teachers to target Language Expectations for your child.

The Proficiency Descriptors also explain how your child makes progress and develops language across the six levels of English language proficiency.

The PLDs are described in detail per grade level to help your child's teachers plan their instruction. Reach out to your child's school to receive specific information about services available for your child to develop their language skills and opportunities for you to engage in the process.

Section 3: How do I help my child Succeed at School?
This section offers a set of strategies and online free resources in English and other languages to help you advocate for your child and help your child develop English language skills at home.

Activities to Do at Home
Reading Strategies
* Read about an event you and your child attended, or talk about an event you shared.
* Read to your child or have your child read to you every day.
* Provide a time and space without distractions for your child to read every day.
* Listen to audiobooks and talk about the stories. Public libraries have collections of audiobooks students may check out online or at a physical location.
* Encourage your child to re-read favorite books and tell you about the story they are reading.
* Ask your child questions about the story. Say something like, “Why do you think ___ about ___?”, “Who are the main characters?”, “What do you think will happen next?”
* Encourage reading as a free-time activity.
* Help your child pick books that are at the right level, but not too difficult. The aim is to give your child lots of successful reading experiences.
* Talk about what you are doing (going to the doctor, getting some groceries, cooking together).

Writing Strategies
* Keep notebooks or paper available as well as pencils and pens.
* Ask your child to tell you stories as they make drawings or write about them.
* Encourage your child to practice writing, starting with their name, words, sentences, and paragraphs as they progress in their language learning.
* Play puzzles and other games that help increase vocabulary.
* Celebrate your child's drawings and writing.
* Ask your child to write down notes and lists for you.
* Tell your child your family's story and encourage them to draw and write it down.
* Work with your child to research information related to a topic of their interest. Ask your child to create a brochure, flyer, or “How To” manual or guide describing/explaining the topic, or how to do something.
* Under your supervision, help your child use a computer to research a topic or communicate with friends and family. Your child can also use the computer to write their own pieces or pieces you write together.
