# Arts Education - Sixth Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Theatre Arts Standards Unpacking Sixth Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for theatre arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with theatre build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The grade 6-8 standards and objectives are written for middle school students who are taught by a licensed theatre arts educator for at least 90 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Theatre Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 theatre programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

6.CN.1.1 Describe the connections between the emergence of theatre and the development of ancient civilizations around the world prior to 1400.
Glossary
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Civilizations - The culture characteristic of a particular time or place.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students described how theatre and major historical events in the United States influenced each other and now they are describing the connections between the emergence of theatre and the development of ancient civilizations around the world prior to 1400. In the next level they will analyze how theatre influenced various developing cultures around the world from the 1400s to the present.
Enduring Understanding
A theatre artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of stories, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How Theatre developed from ancient civilizations before 1400
* Greek innovationations, adaptations by Rome. rejection then exemptions by the Church.
* Comparison to development in non-western cultures
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of theatre development before 1400
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Theatre is an important part of society throughout many different cultures. Perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of stories, styles and genres
* Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare resources showing how theatre developed in ancient civilizations such as Greece, Rome, Egypt, India, and China before 1400.
* Demonstrate how to describe the influence of an ancient civilization (e.g., Greek democracy on theatre festivals) using academic vocabulary like chorus, tragedy, or ritual performance.
* Guide students through a timeline or comparison activity to explore similarities and differences in early theatrical forms across various ancient cultures.
* Provide structured supports like vocabulary charts, graphic organizers, and sentence frames to help students describe key cultural-theatre connections clearly.
* Monitor student discussions and written work for accurate use of terms and clear summaries of how theatre reflected or influenced early civilizations through formative and summative assessment, offering feedback as needed.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Greek theatre
* Roman Theatre
* Religious Theatre and Masques of Middle ages
* Japanese Theater- Noh, Kyogen  EM
* Sanskrit Drama

Exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of theatre artists to develop their creative aesthetic.

6.CR.1.2 Create original works using theatrical formatting and elements.
Glossary
   * Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
   * Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
   * Theatrical Elements:
   * Production Elements: Technical elements selected for use in a specific production, including sets, sound, costumes, lights, music, props, and makeup, as well as elements specific to the production such as puppets, masks, special effects, or other story telling devices/ concepts.29
   * Technical Elements: Include the set, lighting, sound, costumes, properties, and hair and makeup.29
   * Story Elements: Are the characters, setting, plot, conflict, and the resolution. Dramatic structure.29
   * Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students created original works using elements of playwriting and performance skills. Now they are creating original works using theatrical formatting and elements. In the next level, they will create original works using elements of dramatic structure.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are recorded by theatre artists and playwrights through both written and visual documentation.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * Theatrical formatting such as the construction of dialogue and stage directions into a script as opposed to the paragraphing of narrative formatting
   * Theatrical elements such as production, technical, and story elements
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Putting theatrical formatting and elements into practice when creating new theatrical works
Evidence of Mastery
   * Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Theatre involves actors presenting stories for an audience and, though it has all of the elements of narrative structure, it is formatted for external presentation rather than internal reading.
   * Since this objective has the language “theatrical formatting and elements” both must be used. Students are required to use both to master this objective.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Prepare prompts to incite artistic choices as well as examples of theatrical formatting and elements.
   * Build familiarity with identifying and naming formatting and elements (RE.1.1), especially how story elements are presented differently in dramatic structure vs. narrative structure.
   * Demonstrate creating a script modeling a specific theatrical format and addressing predetermined elements.
   * Guide students to plan an original work, choosing their formating as well as how they will use theatrical elements.
   * Provide templates, engaging prompts, and planning guides to assist drafting works. Provide feedback and support as appropriate.
   * Monitor each student’s ability to put theatrical formatting and elements into practice when conceiving new theatrical works through formative and summative assessment, collecting individual evidence of mastery through observation and documentation.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
   * Protagonist, Antagonist
   * Types of conflict
   * Fully developed scenes
   * Plot development leading to a climax and conclusion
   * Elements of dramatic structure (i.e. plot, theme, characters, setting, mood, dialogue and spectacle)
   * Dramatic format vs Literary format

Now they are creating simple costumes or masks for dramatic presentations. In the next level, they will create a variety of simple props and set pieces to support and enhance dramatic presentations.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are actualized by theatre designers and craftspersons intentionally to fulfill dramatic purposes.
Knowledge

   * Components of simple costumes
   * Components of simple masks
   * Costumes and masks are used to help create a full picture for the audience to suspend belief and invest in the story being performed

   * Creating new simple costumes or masks

Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Costumes, props, and masks help students connect with the characters they are portraying. By creating their own costumes, props, or masks, students learn how these items reflect the time period for the audience to suspend belief and invest in the story being performed.

In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

   * Guide students in creating their own costumes or masks
   * Provide key aspects of the play that inform design choices such as setting, time period, mood, theme, character traits, and plot details

Theatre artists balance inspiration and originality with ethics by creatively transforming or adapting other artistic ideas into new works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * Familiar stories with unique perspectives
   * Procedures for adapting stories

In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

   * Alternate ending shows such as Clue and versions of Spiderman.

6.CR.2.2 Describe how fair use, copyright, public domain, open source, and creative commons affect the ability to produce theatre.
Glossary
   * Artists - A person who creates art (such as painting, sculpture, music, or writing) using conscious skill and creative imagination; a person skilled in any of the arts.14
   * Copyright - Form of protection grounded in the U.S. Constitution and granted by law for original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium of expression, covering both published and unpublished works.17
   * Creative Commons - Copyright license templates that provide a simple, standardized way to give the public permission to share and use creative work on conditions of the maker’s choice.6
   * Derivative - Having parts that originate from another source.14
   * Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
   * Fair Use - Limitation in copyright law which sets out factors to be considered in determining whether or not a particular use of one’s work is “fair,” such as the purpose and character of the use, the amount of the work used, and whether the use will affect the market for the work.17
   * Open Source - Computer software for which the copyright holder freely provides the right to use, study, change, and distribute the software to anyone for any purpose.26
   * Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
   * Public Domain - A work of authorship is in the “public domain” if it is no longer under copyright protection or if it failed to meet the requirements for copyright protection. Works in the public domain may be used freely without the permission of the former copyright owner.32
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students cited artists correctly when utilizing or adapting their original artwork and now describe how fair use, copyright, public domain, open source, and creative commons affect the ability of artists to create original or derivative theatre. In the next level, students will identify the legal and ethical issues for creating derivative theatre.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that respect for originality and ownership of intellectual property is shown through the ethical use of artistic works.

To master this objective, students need to KNOW:

   * What is art/drama?  What is the function of art/drama in society?  How can art/drama influence our personal perceptions?

6.RE.2.1 Identify appealing qualities and effective elements of theatrical works.
Glossary
   * Elements -
   * Production Elements -  Technical elements selected for use in a specific production, including sets, sound, costumes, lights, music, props, and makeup, as well as elements specific to the production such as puppets, masks, special effects, or other story telling devices/ concepts.29
   * Technical Elements -  Include the set, lighting, sound, costumes, properties, and hair and makeup.29
   * Story Elements -  Characters, setting, plot, conflict, and the resolution. Dramatic structure.29
   * Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are. 27
   * Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students defended personal preferences for theatrical works. Now they are identifying appealing qualities and effective elements of theatrical works. In the next level, they will compare personal preferences in theatrical works as it relates to social or cultural background.
Enduring Understanding
An observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with theatre works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * Dramatic elements of theme, plot, character, setting, dialogue, spectacle and mood.
   * Supportive technical elements used to enhance performance
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Recognizing and naming the effective elements of a theatrical work and how the impact the appeal of the work
   * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Aesthetics is a branch of philosophy that studies the nature of beauty, art, and art criticism. In Theatre, aesthetics is found in the literature and spectacle
   * Identifying the differences of personal preferences in an analytical way helps students begin to shape their own artistic visions, justify their observations, and develop as theatre artists
   * Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts
   * Since this objective has the language “appealing qualities and effective elements” both must be considered
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Prepare examples with strong theatrical elements including theme, plot, character, setting, dialogue, spectacle, and mood
   * Demonstrate how to identify and describe effective artistic and technical choices using aesthetic criteria, such as “The lighting helped show the mood because it was dim and shadowy when the villain appeared”
   * Guide students in identifying specific elements that appealed to them or worked well and encourage them to articulate why those choices were effective
   * Provide aesthetic vocabulary charts, analysis stems, and group discussions to support students in articulating what made a theatrical moment strong or successful
   * Monitor individual students' ability to identify appealing qualities and effective elements of theatrical works through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
   * Definition of aesthetics
   * Objective vs Subjective
   * Dramatic elements of theme, plot, character, setting, dialogue, spectacle, and mood
   * What makes art/drama appealing?
   * What makes art/drama effective?

6.RE.2.2 Define constructive criticism for theatrical works.
Glossary
   * Critique - Individual or collective reflective process by which artists or designers experience, analyze, and evaluate a work[s] of art or design.17
   * Define - To make distinct, clear, or detailed.14
   * Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students constructed respectful critique of theatrical work created by self and peers. Now they are defining constructive criticism for theatrical works. In the next level, they will evaluate choices made in devised or scripted performance, based on objective criteria.
Enduring Understanding
Providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * What constructive criticism sounds like
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Detailing the components of constructive criticism
   * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Artists can be proud of their work and still want to improve it
   * Artistic choice is about building confidence. "The only wrong choice is no choice"
   * Though this objective uses the word “define,” building out a comprehensive definition goes beyond one sentence to include examples, models, exemplars, and guidelines which can be co-constructed as a class
   * Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Prepare definitions and examples that distinguish constructive criticism from unhelpful comments
   * Demonstrate how to frame feedback in a way that is actionable and kind
   * Guide students in using rubrics and guiding questions to evaluate peer work
   * Provide time for reflection and revision based on peer and self-feedback
   * Monitor individual students' ability to define constructive criticism for theatrical works through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
   * Definitions of Subjective versus Objective.
   * Definition of refinement, tightening, and bold choices
   * The difference between the performer's choice and the director's vision.
   * Constructive criticism, highlighting successes and areas needing improvement, leads to growth in performance
