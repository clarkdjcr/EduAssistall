# Arts Education - Fifth Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Theatre Arts Standards Unpacking Fifth Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for theatre arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with theatre build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 theatre standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed theatre educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Theatre Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 theatre programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

5.CN.1.1 Describe how theatre and major historical events in the United States influenced each other.
Glossary
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students described diverse North Carolina theatrical groups by geographic region and now they are describing how theatre and major historical events in the United States influenced each other. In the next level they will describe the connections between the emergence of theatre and the development of ancient civilizations around the world prior to 1400.
Enduring Understanding
A theatre artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of stories, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Examples of theatre being influenced by major US events
* Examples of US events that have been influenced by theatre
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the ways US events and theatre have impacted one another
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* History influences Theatre
* US events motivate Theatre
* Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare age-appropriate examples of U.S. historical events and corresponding theatrical works that reflect or were influenced by those events.
* Demonstrate how to describe the relationship between a specific historical event (e.g., the American Revolution) and a theatre piece (e.g., Hamilton), using academic vocabulary.
* Guide a class discussion or partner work to help students explore and articulate how theatre both shapes and responds to major U.S. events.
* Provide sentence frames, vocabulary supports, and structured tools (like comparison charts) to help students express their understanding clearly in spoken or written form.
* Monitor and assess students' use of disciplinary language and their ability to summarize the mutual influence between theatre and U.S. history through formative and summative assessment, offering feedback as needed.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Federal Theatre Project
* President Lincoln’s Assassination
* Origins of theatre groups, facilities, and works in response to laws, regulations, or cultural/societal movements.
* Theatre that changed in style or genre in response to pop culture and social movements, such as Hairspray or Rent.
* Plays and musicals inspired by historical events such as: 1776, Hamilton, The Crucible

5.CR.1.1 Improvise characters within comprehensive stories with support or coaching.
Glossary
* Characters - A person portrayed in a drama, novel, or other artistic piece.21
* Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14
* Support - Assistance provided temporarily to enable a student to perform a task that would be difficult to perform unaided, best implemented in a manner that helps develop that student’s capacity to eventually perform the task independently.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students improvised comprehensive stories with support or coaching. Now they are improvising characters within comprehensive stories with support or coaching. In the next level, they will improvise dialogue and movement for original scripts or devised theatre.
Enduring Understanding
Exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of theatre artists to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Components of comprehensive story characters
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Spontaneously inventing cohesive characters with guidance
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Improvisation encourages the use of expressive language and introduces new vocabulary, which can help develop vital communication skills.
* Improvisation can build empathy and a better understanding of different perspectives.
* Improvisation enhances creativity and imagination as they create stories and narratives.
* Improvisation provides opportunities to explore scenarios and find solutions to a variety of real-world problems.
* Since this objective has the language “support or coaching” either one may be provided. Students are not required to experience both to master this objective.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

* Monitor each students’ ability to spontaneously invent cohesive characters with guidance through formative and summative assessment, collecting individual evidence of mastery through observation and documentation.

May include, but are not limited to:

* Performance etiquette for improvisation.

* Putting the elements of playwriting and performance skills into practice when creating a new theatrical work

* Monitor each student’s ability to put the elements of playwriting into practice when conceiving a new monologue through formative and summative assessment, collecting individual evidence of mastery through observation and documentation.

* Scene Work
* Dialogue Creation
* Character Building
* Script structure
* Storytelling through writing and performance

Glossary
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
* Dramatic Presentations - Referring to the production of a play or dramatic work, or the specific acting/technical job of a production member in a play.29
* Technical Elements - Include the set, lighting, sound, costumes, properties, and hair and makeup.29
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students created simple lighting or sound options to support theatrical work. Now they are creating simple technical designs to support dramatic presentations. In the next level, they will create simple costumes or masks for dramatic presentations.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are actualized by theatre designers and craftspersons intentionally to fulfill dramatic purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Components of simple technical designs
* The basics of creating the physical environment of the play because scenic design helps establish the production's setting, time period, and atmosphere
* The basic lighting techniques because light creates mood, focus, and atmosphere, which enhances their ability to focus, retain information, and perform better academically.
* Light interacts with the environment and impacts perception allows students to optimize their learning spaces and improve their overall learning experience
* Sound, including sound effects and music, can enhance the dramatic presentation and contribute to the story and mood, helping to bring a narrative to life, adding layers of meaning and depth that might not be conveyed through visuals or dialogue alone
* Costumes convey character, era, and social status is important for students because it enhances their ability to analyze and interpret narratives, understand historical context, and develop critical thinking skills
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating new technical designs
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students should understand the basics of creating the physical environment of the play because scenic design helps establish the production's setting, time period, and atmosphere.
* Students should learn the basic lighting techniques because light creates mood, focus, and atmosphere, which enhances their ability to focus, retain information, and perform better academically. Understanding how light interacts with the environment and impacts perception allows students to optimize their learning spaces and improve their overall learning experience
* Understanding how sound, including sound effects and music, can enhance the dramatic presentation and contribute to the story and mood. Sound helps bring a narrative to life, adding layers of meaning and depth that might not be conveyed through visuals or dialogue alone.
* Understanding how costumes convey character, era, and social status is important for students because it enhances their ability to analyze and interpret narratives, understand historical context, and develop critical thinking skills
* Students do not need to execute their designs to master this objective, but only to create designs
* The technical design can be for any technical element
* This objective will be most meaningful when supporting improvisations (CR.1.1), original creation (CR.1.2), or as part of presenting works (PR.1.2). This objective has students creating the technical elements used in PR.2.3
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare materials for technical design (e.g., fabric, cardboard, scarves, paint, markers, yarn) to support costume, prop, set, lighting, or sound creation. While students are not required to physically create the items they design, doing so can support the implementation of PR.2.3. Having materials available can also spark creativity. Share pictures or videos of simple technical elements used in children's theater or well-known stories and discuss how they help tell the story
* Determine whether your students will all design the same technical element or if students will be assigned different tasks such as costumes, props, sets, lighting, or sound
* Demonstrate how to consider personality, role, social status, story, and production needs when designing a technical element. Emphasize creativity and imagination while explaining how design choices support the narrative, setting, and emotional tone
* Guide students in designing their own technical elements, considering key aspects of the play that inform design choices such as setting, period, mood, themes, characters, and plot
* Provide supports such as character cards, design prompts, or parameters to help students connect their technical designs to the needs of the specific theatrical work
* Monitor students’ ability to create technical designs for works through formative and summative assessment

5.CR.2.1 Adapt a variety of literary genres into theatrical performances.
Glossary
* Adapt - To change, or to change something, to suit different conditions or uses.4
* Genre - Category of art or design identified by similarities in form, subject matter, content, or technique.17
* Performances - Experience of engaging in the act of presenting [artistic skills] in a classroom or private or public venue.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students adapted various pieces of literature into theatrical works. Now they are adapting a variety of literary genres into theatrical performances. In the next level, they will adapt familiar stories with unique perspectives for theatrical performance.
Enduring Understanding
Theatre artists balance inspiration and originality with ethics by creatively transforming or adapting other artistic ideas into new works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* A variety of literary genres
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Modifying literary genres into theatre
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Adapting literature requires students to analyze and deconstruct the original work to identify core themes, characters, and plot points
* Adapting various genres allows students to experiment with different theatrical styles and techniques that are appropriate for conveying the essence of the source material
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare a variety of literary genres commonly explored in fifth grade, such as historical fiction, poetry, folktales, biographies, and science fiction. Choose examples that are accessible and engaging, possibly drawing from classroom texts, read-alouds, or familiar public domain stories
* Demonstrate how to adapt different genres by modeling how key elements of a story—such as theme, characters, plot, and tone—translate into theatrical form. Highlight how adaptation choices shift based on genre (e.g., a biography may focus on key life events; a poem may rely on mood, movement, or repetition). Use visuals, simplified vocabulary, and clear examples to support comprehension
* Guide students through selecting and adapting a literary genre of their choice with support. Encourage small-group or partner work, and use scaffolds such as graphic organizers, anchor charts, or whole-group brainstorms to help students map scenes, characters, and dialogue
* Provide targeted supports like templates, visual references, scene outlines, or genre-specific prompts to help students successfully adapt and perform pieces from diverse genres in ways that reflect their unique qualities and storytelling styles
* Monitor individual students’ ability to adapt a variety of literary genres into theatrical performances through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Define genre
* Converting narrative stories into scripts
* Poetry into performance
* Literary analysis

5.CR.2.2 Cite artists correctly when utilizing or adapting their original artwork.
Glossary
* Adapt - To change, or to change something, to suit different conditions or uses.4
* Artist - A person who creates art (such as painting, sculpture, music, or writing) using conscious skill and creative imagination; a person skilled in any of the arts.14
* Cite - To quote by way of example, authority, or proof.14
* Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students identified how to responsibly use resources for online access of theatre artworks and now cite artists correctly when utilizing or adapting their original artwork. In the next level, students will describe how fair use, copyright, public domain, open source, and creative commons affect the ability of theatre artists to create original or derivative theatre.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that respect for originality and ownership of intellectual property is shown through the ethical use of artistic works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Procedures for correctly citing the work of artists from whom students borrow material
* Citations should include the artist, title, date, medium, and source
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Referencing the original visual artist when using their work
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery

5.RE.2.2 Construct respectful critique of theatrical work created by self and peers.
Glossary
* Construct - To make or form by combining or arranging parts or elements, build.14
* Critique - Individual or collective reflective process by which artists or designers experience, analyze, and evaluate a work[s] of art or design.17
* Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students critiqued peer works using theatrical vocabulary and nonverbal cues. Now they are constructing respectful critique of theatrical work created by self and peers. In the next level, they will define constructive criticism for theatrical works.
Enduring Understanding
Providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to identify specific elements, offer constructive feedback, and engage in respectful dialogue
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating a respectful evaluation of works created by self and others
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students can understand how to construct respectful evaluations of works created by themselves and others by being mindful of the impact their feedback has on their peers, focusing on constructive and specific suggestions, and communicating with kindness and empathy
* Understanding how to construct evaluations for self and other students will help them learn to think critically, communicate effectively, collaborate with others, and take ownership for their growth and development
* Explain how to use positive language when addressing peers
* Provide positive critique language: positive language examples include: "I liked how you...", "A strong part of your work was...", "You did a great job explaining...", "This detail stood out to me...", "I can see you put a lot of effort into...", "One thing I especially enjoyed was...", "Your creativity shines through in...", and "I like how you used..."
* Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts
* Since this objective has the language “self and peers” feedback must be given to both
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare prompts and rubrics that emphasize constructive, specific, and respectful feedback
* Demonstrate how to give balanced critique by identifying successes and growth areas
* Guide students in using performance criteria to reflect on their own and others’ work
* Provide sentence stems and think-pair-share routines for structured critique
* Monitor individual students' ability to construct respectful critique of theatrical work created by self and peers through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Critiques
* Self-critiques
* Respectful critiques
* Constructive feedback techniques
* Peer evaluations
* Receiving feedback
* Theatrical analysis
