# Arts Education - Intermediate

Parent Guide for Intermediate Dance

The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a student’s choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed dance educator.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students explore how historical, societal, and cultural events have impacted dance. They also solve creative problems using skills and knowledge from other disciplines or personal experiences and identify healthy habits for physical and mental wellness in dance.
CN.2 Explore - Students compare the pros and cons of dance as a vocational, educational, and professional choice and describe innovative resources and tools used in dance.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students use a variety of sources of inspiration to prompt spontaneous movement invention of longer dance sequences, and learn to use a variety of choreographic structures to create dance phrases. Students also use pictures, writing, symbols, or media technology to document a dance phrase.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to create a dance phrase by adapting a key element used in a significant work and evaluate the ethics involved in making, producing, and distributing both original works and adaptations, including musical accompaniment.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Perform -  Students apply feedback from instructors, peers, and self-assessment to improve personal and group performance and perform movement skills in extended dance phrases with physical awareness. Students also execute each step in a dance clearly and completely with focus and full-body energy.
PR.2 Develop - Students develop a theme or topic, including selection of music for a solo, duet, or group performance, and self-evaluate the use safe and respectful behaviors for a variety of dance roles, settings, and cultural contexts. Students also explore production element designs that would strengthen the meaning of a dance.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students examine similarities and differences of elements and choreographic structures used in different dance techniques and use the historical and cultural background of a dance or style to make multiple interpretations of a work.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students compare how personal and cultural influences shape the way dance is viewed and evaluated, and provide feedback on dances by using clear guidelines, examples from what they observe, and an understanding of the choreographer’s artistic purpose.

How to Support Your Dance Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your dance student:
* Clear a little space so your child can move freely and safely.
* Discuss how dance styles or trends have been impacted by societal or cultural events.
* Talk about management strategies such as stretching, posture exercises, mindfulness, nutrition, hydration, and rest to protect their physical and mental health.
* Compare the pros and cons of dance as recreation versus as a career, including related careers like dance therapy, arts management, or teaching.
* Explore how apps, editing software, or online platforms are used to record, share, and innovate in dance.
* Challenge your child to improvise movements to a variety of sources of inspiration.
* Encourage your child to create a dance based on a meaningful idea or topic, then have them sketch movements, write notes, or use a phone to record their original choreography.
* Encourage practice of routines to improve skills.
* Watch two different dance styles and talk about similarities/differences in movement and form.
* Watch dance together, then discuss several possible interpretations of the work.
