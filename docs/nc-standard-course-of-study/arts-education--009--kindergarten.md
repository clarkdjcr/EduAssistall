# Arts Education - Kindergarten

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Kindergarten
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 Dance standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed Visual Arts educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

K.CN.1.1 Identify dance in global communities.
Glossary
* Community - A unified body of individuals: such as a group of people with a common characteristic or interest living together within a larger society; a body of persons of common and especially professional interests scattered through a larger society.14
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline by establishing foundational knowledge in how dance exists in global communities and cultures. At the current level, students identify dance in global communities. In the next level, students will explain how dance reflects culture, values, and ideas.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* What is meant by Global Communities
* How dance exists as a common artform across all cultures
* Examples of how dance exists in artistic, cultural, and social dance forms in global communities
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming examples of how dance exists in cultural celebrations, traditions, and artistic practices in various cultures
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Dance is global, and has meaning and purpose in its existence and representation of all cultures in global communities
* A variety of dance styles should be included and represented
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Expose students to dance from a variety of global cultures
* Guide student investigation into the existence and purposes of dance in diverse global communities
* Design learning activities for students to identify dance in global communities
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to identify dance in global communities
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Show examples of dance from around the world using photo and video footage
* Social dance
* Ballroom dance
* Common concert dance including ballet, tap, jazz, modern, etc.
* Percussive dance from various locations: step, tap, clog, gumboot, Haka, Bharatanatyam, Native American traditional dances

K.CN.1.3 Identify anatomical challenges encountered by dancers.
Glossary
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27

In the next level, students learn to identify psychological challenges that this throughline of objectives also teaches students how to effectively manage and address to improve performance.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that cultivating healthy physical and psychological habits is imperative to be successful and fulfilled in dance.

To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Anatomical challenges that impact dance performance
* Definition of “anatomical”
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming anatomical challenges encountered by dancers
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form

Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Recognize that dance is for all body types and all body types move differently
* Because of the physical demands of dance, bodies may encounter challenges related to overuse, joint and muscle injuries, fatigue, etc.
* Because bodies are unique, each dancer will find their own challenges in flexibility, stamina, strength, and ability
* Ensure that students know that there are no “good” or “bad” dancers, as dance offers opportunities for a variety of skill levels and abilities
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Teach and review basic anatomical vocabulary (body parts, spine, hips, knee, etc)
* Facilitate classroom discussion about how bodies of dancers are different but that there are common bodily challenges dancers face

* Guide student investigation of different anatomical challenges common to dancers
* Design learning activities for students to identify anatomical challenges encountered by dancers
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to identify anatomical challenges encountered by dancers
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students identify body parts and explore their movement, noting any challenges they might have
* Have students compare how body movements may be changed or affected by an individual’s level of flexibility, mobility, or strength
* Have students observe performances of companies for dancers with differently abled bodies: Candoco, Dance Wheels, AXIS, REVolutions, etc.

K.CR.1.1 Generate spontaneous movement in response to verbal cues.
Glossary
* Cue - A signal (such as a word, phrase, or bit of stage business) to a performer to begin a specific speech or action.14
* Generate - To create by means of a defined process : produce.14
* Improvisation - Movement that is created spontaneously, occurring within free or highly structured environments, but always with an element of chance. Provides the dancer with the opportunity to bring together elements quickly, and requires focus and concentration.18
* Spontaneous Movement - See “Improvisation”
* Verbal Cue - See “Cue”
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because it builds foundational knowledge of how improvisation happens in dance. At the current level, students generate spontaneous movement in response to verbal cues. At the next level, students use visual cues to generate spontaneous movement.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of dancers and choreographers to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to use verbal prompts as inspiration for spontaneous movement ideas
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating spontaneous movement through improvisation that expresses the student's personal response to verbal cues
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students learn to respond to verbal cues for movement exploration
* Provide repetition of routine verbal cues that will be used throughout classroom instruction, for example "ready and here you go" to begin movement together, "hold your freeze" to stop moving together, and other vocabulary words that are used in the class which will illicit a movement response
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain what it means to improv
* Demonstrate and call on student volunteers to show movement ideas of how to generate spontaneous movement to verbal cues
* Create a list of vocabulary cues for students to physically react to through movement improvisation
* Guide students through movement improvisation exercises using verbal cues
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to generate spontaneous movement in response to verbal cues
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Calling out locomotor and axial/non-locomotor movements such as skip, twist, jump, etc.
* Using imagery and action words such as burst, wiggle, zoom, twirl, pop, melt, etc.
* Calling out various body parts such as “use only your head”, “your shoulders”, “your arms”, etc.
* Using terms such as “freeze”, “go”, “fast”, “slow”, “high”, “low”, etc.
* When teaching students about the Energy element of dance, using verbal cues for each of the types of energy in order to help students visualize the movement they will make. For example, "smooth like spreading peanut butter" and "sharp like stop-animation stop and go movements". "Sudden like lightning and sustained like thunder". "Heavy like you're made of rocks and light like you are a feather." "Strong like you are powerful and weak like you are tired." "Bound like you are moving through jello and free like you are water moving anywhere and constantly changing."
* Verbal cues can include rhythmic structure for the Time element, including syllables for note value: titi for eighth notes, ta for quarter notes, ta-a for half notes, etc.

K.CR.1.2 Create a movement for the beginning, middle, and end of a dance.
Glossary
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline by building foundational knowledge in how to choreograph dance. At the current level, students learn the overall structure of a dance having a beginning, middle, and end and need to create a movement for each section. In the next level, students will collaborate with a group to create movement for the beginning, middle, and end of a dance.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are actualized into choreography by dancers and choreographers to intentionally fulfill specific purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* That a dance sequence has a beginning, middle, and end
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Conceiving new movements that are used to logically sequence the beginning, middle, and end of a dance
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Choreography is created through purposeful choices by the artist
* While dance movements can be done individually, they can also be sequenced together to make a basic work of art
* Compare dances to stories using vocabulary for beginning/first, middle/next, and end/last as taught in non-arts curriculum
* Choreography focus is an understanding that movement is to be planned and reproduced exactly the same each time, just like a book is written and does not change each time it is read
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to create movement for the beginning, middle AND end of a dance. All three must be considered
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Demonstrate how dances have a beginning, middle, and end
* Ask for volunteer ideas for movements to be used for beginning, middle, and end of classroom routines
* Provide practice for all students to contribute ideas for beginning, middle, and end movements for classroom routines
* Design a learning activity in which students can create their own movements for how a dance begins (entrance or form a beginning shape, etc) and ends (exit or ending shape) and movement used for the middle content
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to create movement for the beginning, middle and end of a dance
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Using a handout to create movements sequences with specific prompts for movements including locomotor and axial movements (and can include levels, pathways, etc). Students perform the sequence they have created. If recorded, students can watch their sequence from start to finish, identifying the beginning, middle, and end movements
* In smaller classes, the teacher can solicit movement ideas from students over the course of study for dance phrases to be taught during instruction permitting every student to add their idea for beginning, middle, and end movements of classroom routines. Use a checklist to ensure all students are successful in completing this objective for beginning, middle, and ending movement ideas

K.CR.2.1 Adapt a dance sequence by changing the size of the movements.
Glossary
* Adapt - To change, or to change something, to suit different conditions or uses.4
* Dance Sequence - A brief sequence of movements that are not necessarily ordered into a formal choreographic structure that communicates artistic intent but rather demonstrates a solution to a movement problem. A movement sequence could contain as few as two or movements.29
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline by building the foundational knowledge on how to adapt pre-existing material to create new movement. At this level, students adapt the size of movements in a pre-existing dance sequence. In the next level, students will adapt the body shapes of movement used in a dance sequence.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that dancers and choreographers balance inspiration and originality with ethics by creatively transforming or adapting other artistic ideas into new works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* A choreographic sequence for manipulation
* How to modify the size of movements
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Modifying the size of movements in a pre-existing choreographic sequence
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Changing the size of motifs is one of the choreographic tools used to manipulate motifs to create new movement. Students can be challenged to make any movement larger or smaller
* Students may find that as the size changes the tempo may also need to change so that they can appropriately perform their newly sized movements
* This objective can be paired with other CR, PR, and RE objectives as students create, perform, and respond to dance
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Create classroom activities that support student creation and manipulation of choreography
* Demonstrate how to adapt the size of movement motifs
* Ask for volunteer ideas for how a variety of motifs might be performed larger or smaller
* Provide practice for all students to contribute ideas for how to adapt the size of movements in choreography
* Create original movement for students to adapt or guide students in generating original material to be used
* Design a learning activity in which students can adapt the size of movements in a pre-existing dance sequence
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to adapt a dance sequence by changing the size of the movements
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Teach students a dance sequence and task them to perform all the motifs as largely as they can and then again, as small as they can
* Teach students a sequence with simple movements that can be adapted. Ask students to recall the sequence and change the movement sizes throughout, giving them the choice in which motifs to perform more larger or smaller
* Using a story with size differences like Goldilocks and the Three Bears, or everyday concepts in groups of three like small, medium, and large french fries or drinks may help students understand changing movement size

Glossary

* Work - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14

This objective develops the EQ-throughline by establishing foundational knowledge on how to share artistic ideas and works. At the current level, students explain the difference between original and copied dance ideas or works. In the next level, students will explain how and when to credit others when using their ideas in dance.

Knowledge

* Copied movement ideas or works are created by doing the same thing as something or someone else
* How to articulate the difference between original and copied ideas or works
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the process of copying dance ideas or works versus creating new movement ideas or works
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form

Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to identify the positive aspects of personal work
* How to identify the positive aspects of another's work
* How to linguistically discuss movement performance and choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Offering feedback that is complimentary in nature for personal and peer work with instructional support
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students understand that they can use feedback to improve and also give feedback to help someone else improve. It is important to identify strengths when evaluating work so students build efficacy and can continue to build on their success
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to provide positive feedback for self AND others with guidance. Both need to be considered
* This objective can be paired with other CR and PR objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how dancers grow from positive feedback
* Guide students on how to use a simple rubric to identify qualities of work
* Provide opportunities for students to compliment and identify areas of strength in their work and the work of their peers. This can be through demonstration of skills learned or in performance of student-generated work
* Provide examples of how to provide positive feedback to self or others
* Provide sentence stems to help students form positive feedback on aspects of dance creation or performance
* Design activities for students to provide positive feedback with instructional support on dance performed or choreographed by self and others
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to provide positive feedback for self and others with guidance
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Use positive behavior support to provide expectations on how to give positive feedback when watching performances of self and others
* Have students perform something they have learned or choreographed. Film the students performing and then have students give positive feedback about their own performance and to each other by using a feedback form or sharing feedback verbally
* Provide sentence stems or wordbanks for support
* Direct line of questioning can also be used to offer support to students in prompting their feedback responses
