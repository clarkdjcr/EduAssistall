# Arts Education - Second Grade

Parent Guide for Second Grade Dance

The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K-5 standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed dance educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students learn how American dance can reflect the heritage, customs, and traditions of people in the United States, including various Indigenous and cultural groups, and examine the cross-curricular relationships between dance and other subjects. Students also learn of strategies to address common physical challenges that are common to dance.
CN.2 Explore - Students make comparisons between the responsibilities of individuals who are responsible for creating and producing dance, and examine why new technologies are used in dance.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students explore using musical cues as prompts for spontaneous movement invention and how to use repetitive patterns when making short dance sequences. Students also learn how to represent movements on the three spatial levels (low, middle, high)  in dance by drawing pictures or using symbols.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to adapt movements by changing the level of movements used in a short dance sequence and how to share both physical and digital dance works fairly.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Perform - Students learn to use feedback from instructors, peers, and self-evaluation to improve their dance skills and to demonstrate an awareness of how their body moves when performing complex motor skills. Students also use instructional support to assist them in performing with focus when dancing for and with others.
PR.2 Develop - Students learn how to generate and compare topics or themes for improvisation or an informal dance presentation and to demonstrate safe and respectful behaviors when performing and observing formal and informal performances. Students also explore how dancing with props can enhance a performance.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students use instructional support to describe movement sequences that demonstrate how the elements of dance (body, space, time, effort quality) are used in dance, and also examine possible meanings of a variety of dance movements or works.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students examine personal preferences when performing dances from a variety of styles, techniques, and cultures, and use instructional support to provide constructive criticism in self and peer-evaluation of work.

How to Support Your Dance Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your dance student:
* Clear a little space so your child can move freely and safely.
* Watch short videos of American dances (tap, hip-hop, shag dancing, square dance, Native American powwow).
* Help your child to see patterns of dance steps, or connections to reading/writing in how a dance is created.
* Encourage your child to practice safe movement habits such as stretching before activity, resting when tired, drinking water.
* Watch examples of dance performances and point out how lighting, music, or video projections make a performance different.
* Play different types of music and ask your child to invent moves that match the sounds.
* Work together to create a short movement sequence using high, middle, and low movements, then have your child change the levels of some of the movements. Have your child draw arrows, shapes, or pictures to show the new movement levels in the dance.
* Try a dance move standing up, sitting, or crouching. Talk about how it looks different.
* Practice skipping, galloping, or spinning with balance and control, then have your child identify something they want to improve.
* Watch dance together and describe what the dancers are doing and discuss the “story” or idea of the dance.
* Discuss preferences for doing different styles of dance.
