# Arts Education - Fifth Grade

Parent Guide for Fifth Grade Dance

The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K-5 standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed dance educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students explore how customs, traditions, and history of indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States have influenced and been influenced by dance styles and dancers. They also make connections between dance and other art forms and practice healthy ways to handle physical and mental challenges common in dance.
CN.2 Explore - Students explore how characteristics and behaviors learned in dance connect to college, career, and post-secondary skills, and learn of artists or works of art innovative for their time.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students explore spontaneous movement invention using dance elements and a movement score and create short dances about abstract ideas. Students also learn how to show the movement qualities used in dance by drawing pictures, writing, or using symbols.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to adapt dance sequences by changing the qualities of movements and learn to correctly cite artists when using or adapting their original ideas or musical works in dance.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Perform - Students learn to combine personal goals and feedback from instructors and peers to improve performance and to demonstrate movement skills with strength, flexibility, stamina, and coordination. Students also strengthen performing with focus when performing longer dance phrases.
PR.2 Develop - Students compare themes, topics, or music choices for a whole class performance and demonstrate safe and respectful behavioral expectations when performing and observing performances in various cultures and settings. Students also choose production elements that help strengthen the artistic intent and overall experience of the audience.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students examine how multiple elements of dance (body, space, time, effort quality) are used in dance and also explore multiple interpretations of a choreographed work.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students examine their personal preferences for different aspects of a dance and create criteria to provide feedback in self and peer-evaluation of work.

How to Support Your Dance Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your dance student:
* Clear a little space so your child can move freely and safely.
* Watch dances from Native American, African American, or immigrant communities in the U.S. Talk about how these dances show traditions, customs, or identity.
* Encourage stretches, warm-ups, and rest. Remind your child that positive thinking helps with nerves before performing.
* Point out how dancers practice teamwork, discipline, and time management—skills also useful in school and future careers.
* Explore artists like Isador Duncan, Alvin Ailey, Martha Graham, or Misty Copeland and discuss how they brought new ideas to dance.
* Play music and have your child perform a movement “score” that gives a sequence of movements, such as a quick jump followed by a slow, low-level movement, and ending with a wide stretch.
* Ask your child to create a short dance that shows a feeling or an idea, then have them sketch or write about the quality of some of their movements.
* Encourage them to redo a dance sequence using different movement qualities.
* Remind them that if they borrow ideas from a choreographer or music online, they should say who created it.
* Support activities that increase flexibility and can help build muscles and endurance.
* Watch dance together and have your child identify how they see the elements of dance, then discuss several possible interpretations of the work.
