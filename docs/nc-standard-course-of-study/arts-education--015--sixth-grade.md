# Arts Education - Sixth Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Sixth Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The 6-8 Dance standards and objectives are written for middle school students who are taught by a licensed Dance educator for at least 90 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

6.CN.1.1 Explain how dance expresses and reflects the values of civilizations around the world.
Glossary
* Civilizations - The culture characteristic of a particular time or place.14
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Values - Relative worth, utility, or importance.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students described dance styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in the United States. At the current level, students explain how dance expresses and reflects values of civilizations on a global scale. In the next level, students analyze how individuals and groups of dancers have influenced various cultures.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Varying values of world civilizations
* How dance can express and reflect cultural values
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the reasoning of how dance can express and reflect values of civilizations around the world
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to explain how dance expresses AND reflects values of civilizations around the world. Both how dance expresses and reflects must be considered
* Collaborate with the Social Studies teacher to learn of units the students are studying
* Collaborate with other Arts teachers who may be teaching this same, or similar objective in their discipline
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Provide examples of how dance expresses and reflects values of civilizations around the world
* Model explaining how dance expresses and reflects values of civilizations around the world
* Guide student exploration in discovering how values of civilizations around the world are expressed and reflected through dance
* Design learning activities for students to explain how dance expresses and reflects values of civilizations around the world
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to explain how dance expresses and reflects values of civilizations around the world
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Start with a sharing of knowledge of cultural background and traditions of dance in the cultures of the students in the classroom
* Create a world map identifying several global civilizations to focus on
* Use important ceremonial celebrations, festivals, or rituals
* Explore how dance can be used for social justice or to bring awareness about global and societal issues

Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Collaborate with teachers of other subject disciplines to learn of skills and concepts in their subject areas. Use the NCDPI Standards of other disciplines for ideas
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to explain how skills AND concepts from other content areas inform the development of dance ideas
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

* Lead classroom discussion on how skills and concepts from other content areas can be used to inform ideas in dance
* Guide students through the investigative process to determine why or how the development of dance ideas were inspired by skills and concepts from other content disciplines
* Design learning activities for students to explain how the development of dance ideas can be informed by skills and concepts from other content areas
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to explain how skills and concepts from other content areas inform the development of ideas in dance
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Math: Ratios, geometry, expressions and equations, order of operations, fractions (for more concepts, see the NCDPI 6th grade Math Standards)
* Social Studies: Dance from historical and cultural contexts of Africa, Asia, Europe, and the Americas, government and structure, migration, cultural influences on society (for more concepts, see the NCDPI 6th grade Social Studies Standards)
* Science: the structure, states, and physical properties of matter, thermal and electrical energy transfer and interactions of matter and energy, the properties of waves and the wavelike property of energy in seismic, electromagnetic (including visible light), and sound waves (for more concepts, see the NCDPI 6th grade Science Standards)
* Literacy: Students can create dances based on themes of stories, writing, research, key ideas and evidence, collaboration and communication, idea integration and analysis (for more concepts see the NCDPI 6th grade ELA Standards)

Glossary

Knowledge

* Anatomical concerns that impact dance performance
* Psychological stressors that impact dancers
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Listing and defining anatomical alignment concerns and psychological stressors encountered by dancers
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective builds on knowledge of anatomical and psychological challenges learned in grades K-5. Given the likelihood that students will be entering middle school dance programs without a K-5 continuum, it is suggested that teachers familiarize themselves with the unpacking documents of this objective in earlier grades
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to name anatomical alignment concerns and psychological stressors encountered by dancers
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
Anatomical
* Teach and review anatomical vocabulary (large muscle groups, bones, joints)
* Facilitate classroom discussion about how improper anatomical alignment increases the risk for injury
* Provide examples of anatomical alignment concerns common in dance
* Guide student investigation of anatomical alignment issues common to dance injuries
Psychological
* Facilitate classroom discussion and provide examples of psychological stressors dancers may face when creating and/or performing dance
* Guide student investigation of different psychological stressors experienced by dancers when creating and/or performing
* Give the students the opportunity to identify their own psychological stressors with the support that stressors can be common and appropriate strategies can aid in helping to handle psychological stressors
Both
* Design learning activities for students to name anatomical alignment concerns and psychological stressors encountered by dancers
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to name anatomical alignment concerns and psychological stressors encountered by dancers
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Students can create a chart or audio/visual project that names concerns and stressors
* Have students contribute to a word cloud of concerns and stressors
* Interview dancers or ask questions of dancers via social media

6.CN.2.1 Identify the dance skills, characteristics, and behaviors that contribute to success in dance-related careers.
Glossary
* Career -  Profession for which one trains and which is undertaken as a permanent calling.14
* Characteristics - Attribute, feature, property, or essential quality (of form/structure). Terms drawn from traditional, modern, and contemporary sources that identify the range of attributes that can be used to describe works of art and design to aid students in experiencing and perceiving the qualities of artworks, enabling them to create their own work and to appreciate and interpret the work of others.17
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified the characteristics and behaviors of dancers that connected to college, career, and postsecondary skills. At the current level, students identify the dance skills, characteristics, and behaviors that contribute specifically to success in dance-related careers and will identify dance skills, characteristics, and behaviors that contribute to success in post-secondary education and career readiness in the next level.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that the roles, skills, and behaviors of a dance artist are necessary for success in both artistic and other professions.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The durable skills, characteristics, and behaviors necessary for success in dance-related careers
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming the characteristics and behaviors the skills that contribute to success in dance-related careers
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective teaches students of the value and importance of the transferable skills they learn in the arts
* Arts education and experiences teach individuals more than just artistic technique. Behaviors, attitudes, character traits, cognitive processes, and creative design and presentation skills are equally developed
* Skills are inclusive of the psychomotor, affective, and cognitive skills developed in the making, presenting, and responding to art
* Use Portrait of a Graduate Durable Skills and other durable skills such as creativity, patience, determination, self-regulation, etc.
* Make sure students understand dance careers extend beyond the roles of performers and choreographers
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective. Students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to identify the dance skills, characteristics, AND behaviors that contribute to success in dance-related careers. Skills, characteristics, and behaviors needed for dance-related careers can be identified simultaneously in one assessment or assessed separately in multiple assignments over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussions about the skills, characteristics, and behaviors developed in dance education
* Activate prior student knowledge and brainstorm with students on various dance careers and the skills, characteristics, and behaviors necessary for success in each career
* Guide student speculation of the characteristics and behaviors honed in dance that will support success in meeting dance career demands
* Design learning activities in which students can identify the dance skills, characteristics, and behaviors that contribute specifically to success in dance-related careers
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to identify the dance skills, characteristics, and behaviors that contribute to success in dance-related careers
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Mock auditions and interviews
* Appropriate etiquette in dance classes and rehearsal
* Ensure experience in a variety of dance techniques
* Choreography and performance skills
* Students can identify the Portrait of a Graduate durable skills: Learner’s Mindset, Empathy, Collaboration, Critical Thinking, Personal Responsibility, Adaptability, and Communication and how these characteristics and behaviors contribute to success in dance-related careers
* Students can create journal entries or audio recordings of reflections of how different characteristics and behaviors such as persistence, discipline, determination, curiosity, creativity, grit, patience, teamwork, etc. contribute to success in dance-related careers
* Bring in guest-artists or have students use social media to ask questions of professional dancers about the skills, characteristics, and behaviors that contributed to their success

6.CR.1.1 Improvise movement using multiple dance elements in response to musical or verbal cues.
Glossary
* Cue - A signal (such as a word, phrase, or bit of stage business) to a performer to begin a specific speech or action.14
* Dance element - The key components of movement; movement of the body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement; see Elements of Dance Organizer by Perpich Center for Arts Education (used with permission).17
* Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14
* Musical Cue - See “Cue”
* Verbal Cue - See “Cue”
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students improvised movement to fulfill a simple movement score using one or more dance elements and now improvise movement using multiple dance elements in their response to musical or verbal cues. At the next level, students will improvise movement in response to cues from a partner or group.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of dancers and choreographers to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The basic dance elements
* How to create spontaneous movement
* How to abstract musical or verbal cue interpretations into movement
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating spontaneous movement through improvisation that explores multiple dance element concepts in response to musical or verbal cues
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* The elements of dance can be used to inspire and improvise movement
* This may be a student’s first time experiencing movement improvisation in a structured dance activity. Remind students of expectations for safety and validate that there are endless possibilities for movement invention when improvising
* Students new to movement may fear judgement in improvisation exercises. Depending on the movement prompt, build confidence by having students first improv in stationary positions with their eyes closed or encourage demonstrations of familiar “social” movements to music students enjoy before tasking students with more complex improvisation prompts
* Note the conjunction “or” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to improvise movement using multiple dance elements in their response to musical OR verbal cues. It is up to the discretion of the teacher whether the student will use musical or verbal cues. Depending on the activity, autonomy can also be given to the student to decide
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain what it means to improv
* Teach (review if students have K-5 experience) how to use musical and verbal cues and the elements of dance when improvising
* Prepare a playlist of musical cues or have instruments such as drums, keyboard, etc. on hand to play music for students to physically react to through movement improvisation if using musical cues OR create a list of vocabulary cues for students to physically react to through movement improvisation
* Demonstrate and call on student volunteers to show movement improvisation using multiple dance elements in response to musical or verbal cues
* Guide students through movement improvisation exercises using multiple dance elements in response to musical or verbal cues
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to improvise movement using multiple dance elements in their response to musical or verbal cues
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Calling out locomotor and non-locomotor movements such as skip, twist, jump, etc.
* Using imagery and action words such as burst, wiggle, zoom, twirl, pop, melt, etc.
* Calling out various body parts such as “use only your head”, “your shoulders”, “your arms”, etc.
* Using terms such as “freeze”, “go”, “fast”, “slow”, “high”, “low”, etc.
* Musical cues such as songs of a variety of genres, instrumentation, tempos, and rhythms or live instruments to be used in improvisation activities
* Using the elements of dance such as spatial concepts, time concepts, Effort qualities, or ways in which the body moves (see 3rd grade unpacking for a more detailed description)

6.CR.1.2 Create a short dance or dance sequence using abstracted movement that has a beginning, middle, and end.
Glossary
* Abstraction - To remove movement from a particular or representative context and, by manipulating it through the elements of space, time, and energy, to create a new sequence or dance that retains the essence of the original.18
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
* Dance Sequence - A brief sequence of movements that are not necessarily ordered into a formal choreographic structure that communicates artistic intent but rather demonstrates a solution to a movement problem. A movement sequence could contain as few as two or movements.29
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students created short dances using basic choreographic forms that communicate abstract ideas and now create a short dance or dance sequence using abstracted movement that has a beginning, middle, and end. In the next level, students will create dance phrases using selected sequences of abstract movement and simple choreographic forms.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are actualized into choreography by dancers and choreographers to intentionally fulfill specific purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to abstract ideas into movement
* How to create a short dance or dance sequence with a beginning, middle, and end
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Conceiving a new short dance or sequence with abstract movement that has a beginning, middle, and end
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Choreography is created through purposeful choices by the artist
* This may be the student’s first experience in creating structured choreography that communicates an idea
* Abstraction can include altering the time, space, dynamics, and/or body shapes of actions of people, animals, or things that move. Abstraction of ideas can also come from images, objects, feelings, or other concepts
* Students can identify their own ideas to be communicated through abstraction or the ideas can be assigned to them
* Use interdisciplinary approaches to strengthen learning of concepts from other subject areas
* Note the conjunction “and” and “or” used in this objective - the student needs to demonstrate mastery of their ability to create a short dance OR dance sequence using abstracted movement that has a beginning, middle, AND end. It is up to the discretion of the teacher if the student is to use abstract movement to create a short dance sequence or longer choreography of a short dance however, both must have a clear beginning, middle, and end
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain the concept of abstraction and demonstrate how various concepts can be abstracted in choreography
* Explain (or review) how dances have a beginning, middle, and end
* Provide practice for all students to learn and perform short dances or movement sequences that use abstract movement and have a clear beginning, middle, and end
* Design learning activities in which students can use abstract movement to create short dance sequences or dances with a clear beginning, middle, and end
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to create a short dance or dance sequence using abstracted movement that has a beginning, middle, and end
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students create short sequences or dances about things they are familiar with or learning about in other subjects. Ideas could include science concepts or everyday situations such as a dance about getting ready for school
* Turn this into a game by identifying (or working as a class to identify) multiple ideas on index cards and then have students randomly draw from the piles. Give students time to create and practice then, in charades-like fashion the class must guess the idea performed by the student
6.CR.1.3 Plan an original dance sequence using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology.
Glossary

This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students depicted the movement qualities in a dance phrase by drawing a picture, writing, or using symbols and now plan an original dance sequence using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology. In the next level, students will document an original dance sequence using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology.

Knowledge

* How pictures, symbols, writing, or media technology can be used in the planning of choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Designing an original dance sequence by using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This thread of objectives is designed for students to record choreography they create, not observe. Pair with CR.1.2
* Teachers can use the Language of Dance Movement Symbols or have students create their own symbolic representation of movements
* When using student-created images, students need to have the opportunity to explain why they drew the symbols they did so that it can be determined if their reasoning is sound
* Note the conjunction “or” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to plan an original dance sequence using pictures OR symbols, writing, OR media technology. It is up to the discretion of the teacher to decide which the student is to use or autonomy can be given to the student to decide
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Teach (or review) how to use the elements of dance in choreography
* Teach (or review) the various methods for recording choreographic ideas through pictures, symbols, or writing
* Discuss how media technology can be used to support the creative process of designing choreography
* Demonstrate how pictures, writing, or symbols can be used to plan choreography
* Design learning activities that task students to use pictures, symbols, writing, or media technology in the planning of an original dance sequence
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to plan an original dance sequence using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology

6.RE.1.1 Identify the elements of dance observed in various genres of dance.
Glossary
* Element of Dance - The key components of movement; movement of the body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement; see Elements of Dance Organizer by Perpich Center for Arts Education (used with permission).17
* Genre - Category of art or design identified by similarities in form, subject matter, content, or technique.17
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified the elements of dance observed in a choreographic work and now identify the elements of dance observed in various genres of dance. In the next level, students will explain how the elements of dance are used in various dance genres.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that the techniques and elements in a dance work can be objectively identified and analyzed.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The elements of dance
* How to analyze dance for the use of dance elements
* How to identify the elements of dance in choreography of various genres
* How to linguistically discuss choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming the elements of dance observed in various dance genres
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Having a video recording to observe will help facilitate conversation as it can be rewound and paused when discussing. A recording would also provide the opportunity for the student to watch it several times to help develop their analytical skills
* This objective shows how the elements are commonly used in choreographed works across all dance genres
* Note this objective specified “observation”  - whereas in earlier grades students might analyze movement phrases they are also learning and performing, it now specifically mentions students’ observation of movement. The choreographic works can still be informal performances by peers in classroom activity but it is encouraged students be exposed to other professional works for analysis
* Note that “various genres of dances” implies that the student will need to observe two or more dances of different genres. The student should be analyzing how dance elements are used in a variety of dances from different genres over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom instruction on the elements of dance
* Demonstrate identifying elements of dance in observation of dance in a variety of genres
* Provide examples of dance in various genres for observation or physical embodiment (these can be generated by the teacher, student, or professionally created and performed)
* Guide students on how to observe dance for evidence of the dance elements of body, space, time, and energy/effort
* Design activities for students to identify the elements of dance observed in various genres of dance
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to identify the elements of dance observed in various genres of dance
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Design a common checklist or graphic organizer for students to use when watching different dances from various genres over the course of study
* Students can observe dances from different genres performed by other students or artists. The students then identify where they observed dance elements in the choreography. This can be done verbally or through a written critique. Students can also be provided with sentence stems such as “I saw use of pathways when…..” or “Examples of spatial relationships included…..”. The students can also be provided wordbanks to support their responses

6.RE.1.2 Identify multiple meanings for choreographic works, citing evidence.
Glossary
* Cite - To quote by way of example, authority, or proof.14
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
* Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students described alternative meanings that could be interpreted from the same choreographic work and now identify multiple meanings for choreographic works, citing evidence. In the next level, students will describe how personal experiences and perspectives influence interpretations of dance works.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer’s interpretation of a work may align with or differ from the artist’s original intent.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* That there can be multiple interpretations of the same artistic work
* How meaning can be visually represented through dance movements or choreographic works
* How to analyze the artistic choices used in dance movements or choreographic work
* How to interpret meaning from artistic choices made in dance
* How to reference evidence of meaning in choreography
* How to linguistically describe dance
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming multiple meanings for choreographic works using supporting evidence
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective teaches students that there are often multiple interpretations of art and not just “one” correct answer. Encourage divergent thinking in viewing dance through multiple lenses
* Use works from various styles and genres
* The student needs to provide evidence from their observations to support their interpretations
* As this may be the first time a student is in a dance class, provide scaffolding to help students understand how to interpret meaning from dance. Provide sentence stems or vocabulary word banks to support development of their responses
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how dance can communicate meaning
* Demonstrate how to identify multiple meanings and cite evidence from choreography that is either performed or observed (be mindful not to impart bias but allow students to have differing perspectives)
* Provide examples of age-appropriate choreography for observation or physical embodiment that can be interpreted for meaning (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work)
* Guide students on how to interpret meaning from dance
* Provide sentence stems to help students cite evidence in support of their interpretation of multiple meanings in dance observations or performance
* Design activities for students to identify multiple meanings for choreographic works, citing evidence from observations or performance of dance
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to identify multiple meanings for choreographic works, citing evidence
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Students could be paired with others who have differing interpretations of works and have to work together to prepare a brief presentation in which describe the multiple interpretations of the work citing evidence in support of the multiple interpretations
* Have students give their personal interpretation of a work, citing evidence, then challenge them to think differently when watching it again to make a different interpretation
* Have students watch a work they know little about and document their interpretation and evidence. The teacher then shares the artist’s intent and context of the work and has the students watch the work a second time through that lens. Students can then describe their multiple interpretations in a written or recorded assignment, or pair-share discussion being sure to use descriptive evidence from the choreography that supports their reasoning
6.RE.2.1 Explain how personal preferences for dance develop with increased exposure.
Glossary
* Develop - To (cause something to) grow or change into a more advanced, larger, or stronger form; to invent something or bring something into existence.4
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Exposure - The fact of experiencing something or being affected by it because of being in a particular situation or place.4
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified aspects of a dance that align and contrast with personal preference and now explain how personal preferences for dance develop with increased exposure. In the next level, students will explain how personal preferences for dance change when considering multiple viewpoints.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with dance works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to explain personal preferences for dance
* How dance participation can impact personal preference
* How learning about and/or viewing dance can impact personal preference
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the reasoning for the development of personal preferences resulting from increased dance participation, learning, or viewing experiences
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* A dancer’s preference for dance is limited to what they are aware of. Increasing exposure to different dance styles, forms, and genres broadens their experience in how they view and physically embody a wide array of dance and helps to increase preferences. Students will also gain a better understanding and appreciation for a wider variety of dance even if they do not have a personal reference for it
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how preferences in dance can develop with increased exposure to different artists, styles, forms, or genres
* Provide examples of dance in a wide array of artists, styles, forms, or genres for observation and physical embodiment
* Guide student discovery of how their preferences for dance over time may change with increased exposure of observing and performing dance from a wide array of styles, forms, and genres
* Design activities for students to explain how personal preferences for dance develop with increased exposure to dance performance and observation
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to explain how personal preferences for dance develop with increased exposure
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Before and After. This is a great opportunity for students new to dance in middle school programs to document their preferences at the beginning of the year and then again at the end of the course. Students can compare how their preferences evolved after increased exposure through instructional activities and dance experiences in a variety of styles, forms, and genres
* “I used to think…..now I think…” can be used as students are exposed to new styles, forms, and genres of dance. Have students explain how their personal preference developed after being exposed to something new

6.RE.2.2 Identify strengths and areas for improvement based on established criteria when viewing dance performance and choreography.
Glossary
* Criteria - The rules or guidelines used for categorizing or judging; in arts assessment, the rules or guidelines used to judge the quality of a student’s performance.28
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
* Improve - To enhance in value or quality : make better.14
* Performance - Experience of engaging in the act of presenting [artistic skills] in a classroom or private or public venue.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students generated criteria to provide feedback for self and others and now identify strengths and areas for improvement based on established criteria when viewing dance performance and choreography. In the next level, students will use student-generated criteria to identify strengths and areas for improvement when viewing dance performance and choreography.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to use established criteria in the evaluation of strengths and areas for improvement in dance performance and choreography
* How to linguistically discuss movement performance and choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming strengths and areas for improvement in dance performance and choreography based on feedback from established criteria
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students understand that they can use feedback to improve and also give feedback to help someone else improve. It is important to identify strengths when evaluating work so students build efficacy and can continue to build on their success. Identifying areas for growth is an important aspect of constructive criticism. Students may need support in understanding the positive intention of constructive criticism
* The objective does not specify how the criteria is established. It can be given to students by the teacher or created in collaboration with students. In the next level, students generate their own criteria to be used in evaluation
* This objective does not specify whose performance and choreography is being viewed therefore it is up to discretion of the teacher if the student is evaluating their own performance or work, or the performance or work of others
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to identify strengths AND areas for improvement based on established criteria when viewing dance performance AND choreography. Both areas of strength and areas of improvement need to be identified however the evaluation can be made separately of performance and choreography when necessary
* This objective can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students analyze how self-assessment, instructor feedback, and peer feedback is used to refine dance performance
* This objective can also be paired with other CR and PR objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how relevant criteria used in evaluation of choreography and performance helps dancers to improve their skills
* Provide students with a rubric that has descriptive performance ratings for the criteria that is most essential to the work being observed
* Review the descriptors and provide exemplars whenever possible for each criteria rating
* Demonstrate using the rubric to provide feedback on strengths and areas in need of improvement with students
* Design activities for students to use established criteria to identify strengths and areas for improvement when viewing dance performance and choreography
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to identify strengths and areas for improvement based on established criteria when viewing dance performance and choreography
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students use rubrics to evaluate dances they create and/or perform to generate feedback on strengths and areas for improvement for self and others
* Use video to record dances so students can watch themselves or refer back to the video to evaluate the work of their peers
