# English Language Arts - 2026 ELA Standard Course of Study

K-12

North Carolina Standard Course of Study English Language Arts
Grades K-2

The North Carolina Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) defines the appropriate content standards for each grade or proficiency level and each high school course to provide a uniform set of learning standards for every public school in North Carolina. These standards define what students are expected to know and be able to do by the end of each school year or course so that they are prepared for postsecondary opportunities, be it college, career, or military. The NC ELA Standard Course of Study serves as the framework that guides each public school unit (PSU) in the development of the curriculum and instruction of English Language Arts. K–1 educators should offer prompting and support, when appropriate, to guide students toward independent mastery of grade-level expectations.

Fundamental Principles

The NC ELA SCOS is grounded in three fundamental principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling; and 3) Variety of Texts.
Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center Text complexity triangle with three equal measurements of quantitative qualitative and reader and task

To truly master grade-level standards, students must be able to apply them to grade-appropriate, complex texts. Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task. Students are expected to read and comprehend increasingly complex texts as they work up the staircase of complexity within their grade band, as appropriate for each grade.

Quantitative measures are computer-generated readability measures that measure word length, word frequency, sentence length, and text cohesion. In North Carolina, Lexile is the most commonly used quantitative measure. The Lexile bands for each grade within the K-2 grade-band are below.

**Beginning Reader (BR) is a code given to texts that are below 0L on the Lexile scale. The lower the number following the BR code, the more advanced the text is. The higher the number, the less complex the text.

Qualitative measures are used to locate a text within a specific grade band and are used to determine whether a text is appropriate for the grade in relation to text structure, language conventions, knowledge demands, and layers of meaning within the text.

Reader and task considerations involve the use of professional judgment by the teacher when determining if the text is appropriate for the instructional purpose with a particular set of students. All three measures should be taken into consideration when selecting texts for classroom use that meet the demands of grade-level standards.

Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation.  These texts are the vehicle for students to access the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) and build their academic skill sets. In K–2, much of this explicit instruction occurs through interactive read-alouds that allow students to hear, comprehend, and discuss increasingly complex texts while their word-reading skills are still developing. During interactive read-alouds, students should be exposed to complex texts that are two to three grade levels above their own, providing access to fluent modeling, rich content, academic vocabulary, and complex language structures.
Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling

The ELA NCSCOS includes the core components of Scarborough’s Reading Rope and illustrates how four language domains of reading, writing, speaking, and listening are interrelated and are mastered in integration with one another. Writing, speaking, and representing are used to support comprehension of the text being read, listened to, or viewed. Reading, listening, and viewing texts support students’ understanding of text structures, features, style, grammar and conventions, and presentation which they can emulate in their own writing, speaking, and representing.  This integration supports students’ mastery in applying skills across language domains in various contexts and from a range of texts.

The standards in the ELA NCSCOS are designed to ensure that students encounter and engage with the same standards throughout the academic year or course. Each time these standards are revisited, they are presented with increasing complexity, reinforcing previously acquired knowledge and skills. This spiraling provides continuous reinforcement and support towards standards mastery and emphasizes that literacy is not developed through an isolated skills-based approach.
Variety of Texts

The ELA NCSCOS allows for flexibility in the complex texts used for instruction. Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. They draw on a wide array of modalities, sometimes combining modalities into multimodal forms. They may be printed on paper or published in digital forms (NAEP 2025). Educators should ensure that students engage with a diverse range of genres, structures, media, and formats, including narratives, poetry, articles, primary documents, videos, music, and art.

Decodable texts serve an important role in advancing students’ word reading during explicit phonics instruction. Decodable texts are an onramp to independently reading complex texts. The development of oral reading fluency is the instructional transition point to shift from reading decodable texts to independently reading complex texts. Because children develop these skills at different rates, they may demonstrate readiness to independently read complex texts at different points within the K-2 grade band.

Complex texts play an essential role in helping students access grade-level standards. In K-2, complex texts are used during interactive read-alouds to build students’ background knowledge, vocabulary, language comprehension, and familiarity with rich text structures. These read-aloud experiences ensure all students—regardless of their current decoding abilities—engage with the rigorous content required by the standards. In addition, students encounter complex texts during independent reading as they develop the foundational skills necessary for reading increasingly complex texts on their own. Because students progress at different rates, their readiness to independently read and comprehend complex texts will emerge at different points within the K–2 grade band.

NC ELA Standard Course of Study Framework

The NC ELA Standard Course of Study consists of three strands: Complex Texts, Comprehension Development, and Communication & Writing. These three strands work together to illustrate an integrated model of literacy.

The Complex Texts Strand should be integrated across the other two strands: Comprehension Development and Communication & Writing. This integration places a focus on text-centered instruction, emphasizing that--in order to master all standards within the NC ELA SCOS--students must be able to apply these skills to grade-appropriate complex texts.  Effectively applying these skills to a variety of grade-appropriate complex texts demonstrates that students can independently construct meaning across language domains from a range of texts.

 Three strands 1 comprehension development that includes foundational skills 2 communication and writing and 3 complex texts which wraps around the other two strands

The Comprehension Development Strand includes a Foundational Skills Substrand outlining the progression of reading skills that underpin students’ ability to independently access complex text. Targeting foundational skills and comprehension development, as portrayed in Scarborough’s Reading Rope, (2001), in concert serve to grow students’ ability to discern more from and make fuller use of texts. Students should demonstrate their proficiency in these standards both through interpretive and expressive language domains: reading, listening, viewing, speaking, writing, and representing.

The Communication & Writing Strand addresses communicating clearly and coherently through expressive language. Please note that CW.6 emphasizes that composing written and spoken products is not a linear process. This standard should be explicitly taught as well as integrated with CW.7-CW.9 to ensure students learn how to apply writing and speaking strategies when composing different text types for different audiences and purposes.

To further assist educators in understanding the North Carolina SCOS for English Language Arts, view the K-12 ELA SCOS Glossary, which provides definitions of key terms used throughout the standards and objectives.

Notes:

Local Control. These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a local public-school unit (PSU) or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for English Language Arts.

  North Carolina Standard Course of Study English Language Arts
Grades 3-5

The North Carolina Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) defines the appropriate content standards for each grade or proficiency level and each high school course to provide a uniform set of learning standards for every public school in North Carolina. These standards define what students are expected to know and be able to do by the end of each school year or course so that they are prepared for postsecondary opportunities, be it college, career, or military. The NC ELA Standard Course of Study serves as the framework that guides each public school unit (PSU) in the development of the curriculum and instruction of English Language Arts.

Fundamental Principles

The NC ELA SCOS is grounded in three fundamental principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling; and 3) Variety of Texts.
Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center Text complexity triangle with three equal measurements of quantitative qualitative and reader and task

To truly master grade-level standards, students must be able to apply them to grade-appropriate, complex texts. Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task. Students are expected to read and comprehend increasingly complex texts as they work up the staircase of complexity within their grade band, as appropriate for each grade.

Quantitative measures are computer-generated readability measures that measure word length, word frequency, sentence length, and text cohesion. In North Carolina, Lexile is the most commonly used quantitative measure. The Lexile bands for each grade within the 3-5 grade-band are below.

Qualitative measures are used to locate a text within a specific grade band and are used to determine whether a text is appropriate for the grade in relation to text structure, language conventions, knowledge demands, and layers of meaning within the text.

Reader and task considerations involve the use of professional judgment by the teacher when determining if the text is appropriate for the instructional purpose with a particular set of students. All three measures should be taken into consideration when selecting texts for classroom use that meet the demands of grade-level standards.

Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation.  These texts are the vehicle for students to access the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) and build their academic skill sets. When engaging in interactive read alouds, students should be exposed to complex texts that are two-to-three grade levels above their own, enabling them to hear fluent reading and engage with rich content.
Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling

The ELA NCSCOS includes the core components of Scarborough’s Reading Rope and illustrates how four language domains of reading, writing, speaking, and listening are interrelated and are mastered in integration with one another. Writing, speaking, and representing are used to support comprehension of the text being read, listened to, or viewed. Reading, listening, and viewing texts support students’ understanding of text structures, features, style, grammar and conventions, and presentation which they can emulate in their own writing, speaking, and representing.  This integration supports students’ mastery in applying skills across language domains in various contexts and from a range of texts.

The standards in the ELA NCSCOS are designed to ensure that students encounter and engage with the same standards throughout the academic year or course. Each time these standards are revisited, they are presented with increasing complexity, reinforcing previously acquired knowledge and skills. This spiraling provides continuous reinforcement and support towards standards mastery and emphasizes that literacy is not developed through an isolated skills-based approach.
Variety of Texts

The ELA NCSCOS allows for flexibility in the complex texts used for instruction. Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. They draw on a wide array of modalities, sometimes combining modalities into multimodal forms. They may be printed on paper or published in digital forms (NAEP 2025). Educators should ensure that students engage with a diverse range of genres, structures, media, and formats, including narratives, poetry, articles, primary documents, videos, music, and art.

NC ELA Standard Course of Study Framework

The NC ELA Standard Course of Study consists of three strands: Complex Texts, Comprehension Development, and Communication & Writing. These three strands work together to illustrate an integrated model of literacy.

The Complex Texts Strand should be integrated across the other two strands: Comprehension Development and Communication & Writing. This integration places a focus on text-centered instruction, emphasizing that-- in order to master all standards within the NC ELA SCOS--students must be able to apply these skills to grade-appropriate complex texts.  Effectively applying these skills to a variety of grade-appropriate complex texts demonstrates that students can independently construct meaning across language domains from a range of texts.
 Strands visual

The Comprehension Development Strand includes a Foundational Skills Substrand that emphasizes the use of morphology and combined use of all previously learned phonics-based skills. This strand outlines the progression of reading skills that underpin students’ ability to independently access complex text. Targeting foundational skills and comprehension development, as portrayed in  Scarborough’s Reading Rope, (2001), in concert serve to grow students’ ability to discern more from and make fuller use of texts. Students should demonstrate their proficiency in these standards both through interpretive and expressive language domains: reading, listening, viewing, speaking, writing, and representing.

The Communication & Writing Strand addresses communicating clearly and coherently through expressive language. Please note that CW.6 emphasizes that composing written and spoken products is not a linear process. This standard should be explicitly taught as well as integrated with CW.7-CW.9 to ensure students learn how to apply writing and speaking strategies when composing different text types for different audiences and purposes.

To further assist educators in understanding the North Carolina SCOS for English Language Arts, view the K-12 ELA SCOS Glossary, which provides definitions of key terms used throughout the standards and objectives.

North Carolina Standard Course of Study English Language Arts
Grades 6-8

The North Carolina Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) defines the appropriate content standards for each grade or proficiency level and each high school course to provide a uniform set of learning standards for every public school in North Carolina. These standards define what students are expected to know and be able to do by the end of each school year or course so that they are prepared for postsecondary opportunities, be it college, career, or military. The NC ELA Standard Course of Study serves as the framework that guides each public school unit (PSU) in the development of the curriculum and instruction of English Language Arts.

Fundamental Principles

The NC ELA SCOS is grounded in three fundamental principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling; and 3) Variety of Texts.
Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center Text complexity triangle with three equal measurements of quantitative qualitative and reader and task

To truly master grade-level standards, students must be able to apply them to grade-appropriate, complex texts. Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task. Students are expected to read and comprehend increasingly complex texts as they work up the staircase of complexity within their grade band, as appropriate for each grade.  When engaging in interactive read alouds, students should be exposed to complex texts that are two-to-three grade levels above their own, enabling them to hear fluent reading and engage with rich content.

Quantitative measures are computer-generated readability measures that measure word length, word frequency, sentence length, and text cohesion. In North Carolina, Lexile is the most commonly used quantitative measure. The Lexile bands for each grade within the 6-8 grade-band are below.

Qualitative measures are used to locate a text within a specific grade band and are used to determine whether a text is appropriate for the grade in relation to text structure, language conventions, knowledge demands, and layers of meaning within the text.

Reader and task considerations involve the use of professional judgment by the teacher when determining if the text is appropriate for the instructional purpose with a particular set of students. All three measures should be taken into consideration when selecting texts for classroom use that meet the demands of grade-level standards.

Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation.  These texts are the vehicle for students to access the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) and build their academic skill sets.
Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling

The ELA NCSCOS includes the core components of Scarborough’s Reading Rope and illustrates how four language domains of reading, writing, speaking, and listening are interrelated and are mastered in integration with one another. Writing, speaking, and representing are used to support comprehension of the text being read, listened to, or viewed. Reading, listening, and viewing texts support students’ understanding of text structures, features, style, grammar and conventions, and presentation which they can emulate in their own writing, speaking, and representing.  This integration supports students’ mastery in applying skills across language domains in various contexts and from a range of texts.

The standards in the ELA NCSCOS are designed to ensure that students encounter and engage with the same standards throughout the academic year or course. Each time these standards are revisited, they are presented with increasing complexity, reinforcing previously acquired knowledge and skills. This spiraling provides continuous reinforcement and support towards standards mastery and emphasizes that literacy is not developed through an isolated skills-based approach.
Variety of Texts

The ELA NCSCOS allows for flexibility in the complex texts used for instruction. Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. They draw on a wide array of modalities, sometimes combining modalities into multimodal forms. They may be printed on paper or published in digital forms (NAEP 2025). Educators should ensure that students engage with a diverse range of genres, structures, media, and formats, including narratives, poetry, articles, primary documents, videos, music, and art.

NC ELA Standard Course of Study Framework

The NC ELA Standard Course of Study consists of three strands: Complex Texts, Comprehension Development, and Communication & Writing. These three strands work together to illustrate an integrated model of literacy.

The Complex Texts Strand should be integrated across the other two strands: Comprehension Development and Communication & Writing. This integration places a focus on text-centered instruction, emphasizing that-- in order to master all standards within the NC ELA SCOS--students must be able to apply these skills to grade-appropriate complex texts.  Effectively applying these skills to a variety of grade-appropriate complex texts demonstrates that students can independently construct meaning across language domains from a range of texts.
 strand visual

The Comprehension Development Strand includes a Foundational Skills Substrand that emphasizes instruction in oral reading fluency, word recognition, and word knowledge skills. This strand outlines the progression of reading skills that underpin students’ ability to independently access complex text. Targeting foundational skills and comprehension development, as portrayed in Scarborough’s Reading Rope, (2001), in concert serve to grow students’ ability to discern more from and make fuller use of texts. Students should demonstrate their proficiency in these standards both through interpretive and expressive language domains: reading, listening, viewing, speaking, writing, and representing.

The Communication & Writing Strand addresses communicating clearly and coherently through expressive language. Please note that CW.6 emphasizes that composing written and spoken products is not a linear process. This standard should be explicitly taught as well as integrated with CW.7-CW.9 to ensure students learn how to apply writing and speaking strategies when composing different text types for different audiences and purposes.

To further assist educators in understanding the North Carolina SCOS for English Language Arts, view the K-12 ELA SCOS Glossary, which provides definitions of key terms used throughout the standards and objectives.

  North Carolina Standard Course of Study English Language Arts
Grades 9-12

The North Carolina Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) defines the appropriate content standards for each grade or proficiency level and each high school course to provide a uniform set of learning standards for every public school in North Carolina. These standards define what students are expected to know and be able to do by the end of each school year or course so that they are prepared for postsecondary opportunities, be it college, career, or military. The NC ELA Standard Course of Study serves as the framework that guides each public school unit (PSU) in the development of the curriculum and instruction of English Language Arts.

Fundamental Principles

The NC ELA SCOS is grounded in three fundamental principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling; and 3) Variety of Texts.
Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center Text complexity triangle with three equal measurements of quantitative qualitative and reader and task

To truly master grade-level standards, students must be able to apply them to grade-appropriate, complex texts. Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task. Students are expected to read and comprehend increasingly complex texts as they work up the staircase of complexity within their grade band, as appropriate for each grade.  When engaging in interactive read alouds, students should be exposed to complex texts that are two-to-three grade levels above their own, enabling them to hear fluent reading and engage with rich content.

Quantitative measures are computer-generated readability measures that measure word length, word frequency, sentence length, and text cohesion. In North Carolina, Lexile is the most commonly used quantitative measure. The Lexile bands for each grade within the 9-12 grade-band are below.
Grade

	Grade 9
	1050L - 1260L
	Grade 10
	1080L - 1335L
	Grades 11 - 12
	1185L - 1385L

Qualitative measures are used to locate a text within a specific grade band and are used to determine whether a text is appropriate for the grade in relation to text structure, language conventions, knowledge demands, and layers of meaning within the text.

Reader and task considerations involve the use of professional judgment by the teacher when determining if the text is appropriate for the instructional purpose with a particular set of students. All three measures should be taken into consideration when selecting texts for classroom use that meet the demands of grade-level standards.

Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation. These texts are the vehicle for students to access the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) and build their academic skill sets.
Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling

The ELA NCSCOS includes the core components of Scarborough’s Reading Rope and illustrates how four language domains of reading, writing, speaking, and listening are interrelated and are mastered in integration with one another. Writing, speaking, and representing are used to support comprehension of the text being read, listened to, or viewed. Reading, listening, and viewing texts support students’ understanding of text structures, features, style, grammar and conventions, and presentation which they can emulate in their own writing, speaking, and representing. This integration supports students’ mastery in applying skills across language domains in various contexts and from a range of texts.

The standards in the ELA NCSCOS are designed to ensure that students encounter and engage with the same standards throughout the academic year or course. Each time these standards are revisited, they are presented with increasing complexity, reinforcing previously acquired knowledge and skills. This spiraling provides continuous reinforcement and support towards standards mastery and emphasizes that literacy is not developed through an isolated skills-based approach.
Variety of Texts

The ELA NCSCOS allows for flexibility in the complex texts used for instruction. Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. They draw on a wide array of modalities, sometimes combining modalities into multimodal forms. They may be printed on paper or published in digital forms (NAEP 2025). Educators should ensure that students engage with a diverse range of genres, structures, media, and formats, including narratives, poetry, articles, primary documents, videos, music, and art.

NC ELA Standard Course of Study Framework

The NC ELA Standard Course of Study consists of three strands: Complex Texts, Comprehension Development, and Communication & Writing. These three strands work together to illustrate an integrated model of literacy.

The Complex Texts Strand should be integrated across the other two strands: Comprehension Development and Communication & Writing. This integration places a focus on text-centered instruction, emphasizing that-- in order to master all standards within the NC ELA SCOS--students must be able to apply these skills to grade-appropriate complex texts.  Effectively applying these skills to a variety of grade-appropriate complex texts demonstrates that students can independently construct meaning across language domains from a range of texts.

 strand visual

The Comprehension Development Strand includes a Foundational Skills Substrand that emphasizes instruction in oral reading fluency, word recognition, and word knowledge skills. This strand outlines the progression of reading skills that underpin students’ ability to independently access complex text. Targeting foundational skills and comprehension development, as portrayed in Scarborough’s Reading Rope, (2001), in concert serve to grow students’ ability to discern more from and make fuller use of texts. Students should demonstrate their proficiency in these standards both through interpretive and expressive language domains: reading, listening, viewing, speaking, writing, and representing.

The Communication & Writing Strand addresses communicating clearly and coherently through expressive language. Please note that CW.6 emphasizes that composing written and spoken products is not a linear process. This standard should be explicitly taught as well as integrated with CW.7-CW.9 to ensure students learn how to apply writing and speaking strategies when composing different text types for different audiences and purposes.

During interactive read-alouds, students should be exposed to complex texts that are two to three grade levels above their own, providing access to fluent modeling, rich content, academic vocabulary, and complex language structures.
Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling

The ELA NCSCOS includes the core components of Scarborough’s Reading Rope and illustrates how four language domains of reading, writing, speaking, and listening are interrelated and are mastered in integration with one another. Writing, speaking, and representing are used to support comprehension of the text being read, listened to, or viewed. Reading, listening, and viewing texts support students’ understanding of text structures, features, style, grammar and conventions, and presentation which they can emulate in their own writing, speaking, and representing.  This integration supports students’ mastery in applying skills across language domains in various contexts and from a range of texts.

The standards in the ELA NCSCOS are designed to ensure that students encounter and engage with the same standards throughout the academic year or course. Each time these standards are revisited, they are presented with increasing complexity, reinforcing previously acquired knowledge and skills. This spiraling provides continuous reinforcement and support towards standards mastery and emphasizes that literacy is not developed through an isolated skills-based approach.
Variety of Texts

The ELA NCSCOS allows for flexibility in the complex texts used for instruction. Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. They draw on a wide array of modalities, sometimes combining modalities into multimodal forms. They may be printed on paper or published in digital forms (NAEP 2025). Educators should ensure that students engage with a diverse range of genres, structures, media, and formats, including narratives, poetry, articles, primary documents, videos, music, and art.

Decodable texts serve an important role in advancing students’ word reading during explicit phonics instruction. Decodable texts are an onramp to independently reading complex texts. The development of oral reading fluency is the instructional transition point to shift from reading decodable texts to independently reading complex texts. Because children develop these skills at different rates, they may demonstrate readiness to independently read complex texts at different points within the K-2 grade band.

Complex texts play an essential role in helping students access grade-level standards. In K-2, complex texts are used during interactive read-alouds to build students’ background knowledge, vocabulary, language comprehension, and familiarity with rich text structures. These read-aloud experiences ensure all students—regardless of their current decoding abilities—engage with the rigorous content required by the standards. In addition, students encounter complex texts during independent reading as they develop the foundational skills necessary for reading increasingly complex texts on their own. Because students progress at different rates, their readiness to independently read and comprehend complex texts will emerge at different points within the K–2 grade band.

The North Carolina Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) defines the appropriate content standards for each grade or proficiency level and each high school course to provide a uniform set of learning standards for every public school in North Carolina. These standards define what students are expected to know and be able to do by the end of each school year or course so that they are prepared for postsecondary opportunities, be it college, career, or military. The NC ELA Standard Course of Study serves as the framework that guides each public school unit (PSU) in the development of the curriculum and instruction of English Language Arts.

Fundamental Principles

The NC ELA SCOS is grounded in three fundamental principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling; and 3) Variety of Texts.
Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center Text complexity triangle with three equal measurements of quantitative qualitative and reader and task

To truly master grade-level standards, students must be able to apply them to grade-appropriate, complex texts. Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task. Students are expected to read and comprehend increasingly complex texts as they work up the staircase of complexity within their grade band, as appropriate for each grade.  When engaging in interactive read alouds, students should be exposed to complex texts that are two-to-three grade levels above their own, enabling them to hear fluent reading and engage with rich content.

Quantitative measures are computer-generated readability measures that measure word length, word frequency, sentence length, and text cohesion. In North Carolina, Lexile is the most commonly used quantitative measure. The Lexile bands for each grade within the 6-8 grade-band are below.

Qualitative measures are used to locate a text within a specific grade band and are used to determine whether a text is appropriate for the grade in relation to text structure, language conventions, knowledge demands, and layers of meaning within the text.

Reader and task considerations involve the use of professional judgment by the teacher when determining if the text is appropriate for the instructional purpose with a particular set of students. All three measures should be taken into consideration when selecting texts for classroom use that meet the demands of grade-level standards.

These texts are the vehicle for students to access the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) and build their academic skill sets.
Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling

The ELA NCSCOS includes the core components of Scarborough’s Reading Rope and illustrates how four language domains of reading, writing, speaking, and listening are interrelated and are mastered in integration with one another. Writing, speaking, and representing are used to support comprehension of the text being read, listened to, or viewed. Reading, listening, and viewing texts support students’ understanding of text structures, features, style, grammar and conventions, and presentation which they can emulate in their own writing, speaking, and representing.  This integration supports students’ mastery in applying skills across language domains in various contexts and from a range of texts.

The standards in the ELA NCSCOS are designed to ensure that students encounter and engage with the same standards throughout the academic year or course. Each time these standards are revisited, they are presented with increasing complexity, reinforcing previously acquired knowledge and skills. This spiraling provides continuous reinforcement and support towards standards mastery and emphasizes that literacy is not developed through an isolated skills-based approach.
Variety of Texts

The ELA NCSCOS allows for flexibility in the complex texts used for instruction. Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. They draw on a wide array of modalities, sometimes combining modalities into multimodal forms. They may be printed on paper or published in digital forms (NAEP 2025). Educators should ensure that students engage with a diverse range of genres, structures, media, and formats, including narratives, poetry, articles, primary documents, videos, music, and art.

NC ELA Standard Course of Study Framework

The NC ELA Standard Course of Study consists of three strands: Complex Texts, Comprehension Development, and Communication & Writing. These three strands work together to illustrate an integrated model of literacy.

The Complex Texts Strand should be integrated across the other two strands: Comprehension Development and Communication & Writing. This integration places a focus on text-centered instruction, emphasizing that-- in order to master all standards within the NC ELA SCOS--students must be able to apply these skills to grade-appropriate complex texts.  Effectively applying these skills to a variety of grade-appropriate complex texts demonstrates that students can independently construct meaning across language domains from a range of texts.

Students should demonstrate their proficiency in these standards both through interpretive and expressive language domains: reading, listening, viewing, speaking, writing, and representing.

Grades 9-10

The North Carolina Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) defines the appropriate content standards for each grade or proficiency level and each high school course to provide a uniform set of learning standards for every public school in North Carolina. These standards define what students are expected to know and be able to do by the end of each school year or course so that they are prepared for postsecondary opportunities, be it college, career, or military. The NC ELA Standard Course of Study serves as the framework that guides each public school unit (PSU) in the development of the curriculum and instruction of English Language Arts.

Fundamental Principles

The NC ELA SCOS is grounded in three fundamental principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling; and 3) Variety of Texts.
Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center Text complexity triangle with three equal measurements of quantitative qualitative and reader and task

To truly master grade-level standards, students must be able to apply them to grade-appropriate, complex texts. Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task. Students are expected to read and comprehend increasingly complex texts as they work up the staircase of complexity within their grade band, as appropriate for each grade.  When engaging in interactive read alouds, students should be exposed to complex texts that are two-to-three grade levels above their own, enabling them to hear fluent reading and engage with rich content.

Quantitative measures are computer-generated readability measures that measure word length, word frequency, sentence length, and text cohesion. In North Carolina, Lexile is the most commonly used quantitative measure. The Lexile bands for each grade within the 9-12 grade-band are below.
Grade
	Quantitative Measure Only
	Grade 9
	1050L - 1260L
	Grade 10
	1080L - 1335L
	Grades 11 - 12
	1185L - 1385L

Qualitative measures are used to locate a text within a specific grade band and are used to determine whether a text is appropriate for the grade in relation to text structure, language conventions, knowledge demands, and layers of meaning within the text.

Reader and task considerations involve the use of professional judgment by the teacher when determining if the text is appropriate for the instructional purpose with a particular set of students. All three measures should be taken into consideration when selecting texts for classroom use that meet the demands of grade-level standards.

Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation. These texts are the vehicle for students to access the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) and build their academic skill sets.
Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling

The ELA NCSCOS includes the core components of Scarborough’s Reading Rope and illustrates how four language domains of reading, writing, speaking, and listening are interrelated and are mastered in integration with one another. Writing, speaking, and representing are used to support comprehension of the text being read, listened to, or viewed. Reading, listening, and viewing texts support students’ understanding of text structures, features, style, grammar and conventions, and presentation which they can emulate in their own writing, speaking, and representing. This integration supports students’ mastery in applying skills across language domains in various contexts and from a range of texts.

The standards in the ELA NCSCOS are designed to ensure that students encounter and engage with the same standards throughout the academic year or course. Each time these standards are revisited, they are presented with increasing complexity, reinforcing previously acquired knowledge and skills. This spiraling provides continuous reinforcement and support towards standards mastery and emphasizes that literacy is not developed through an isolated skills-based approach.
Variety of Texts

The ELA NCSCOS allows for flexibility in the complex texts used for instruction. Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. They draw on a wide array of modalities, sometimes combining modalities into multimodal forms. They may be printed on paper or published in digital forms (NAEP 2025). Educators should ensure that students engage with a diverse range of genres, structures, media, and formats, including narratives, poetry, articles, primary documents, videos, music, and art.

NC ELA Standard Course of Study Framework

The NC ELA Standard Course of Study consists of three strands: Complex Texts, Comprehension Development, and Communication & Writing. These three strands work together to illustrate an integrated model of literacy.

The Complex Texts Strand should be integrated across the other two strands: Comprehension Development and Communication & Writing. This integration places a focus on text-centered instruction, emphasizing that-- in order to master all standards within the NC ELA SCOS--students must be able to apply these skills to grade-appropriate complex texts.  Effectively applying these skills to a variety of grade-appropriate complex texts demonstrates that students can independently construct meaning across language domains from a range of texts.

 strand visual

The Comprehension Development Strand includes a Foundational Skills Substrand that emphasizes instruction in oral reading fluency, word recognition, and word knowledge skills. This strand outlines the progression of reading skills that underpin students’ ability to independently access complex text. Targeting foundational skills and comprehension development, as portrayed in Scarborough’s Reading Rope, (2001), in concert serve to grow students’ ability to discern more from and make fuller use of texts. Students should demonstrate their proficiency in these standards both through interpretive and expressive language domains: reading, listening, viewing, speaking, writing, and representing.

The Communication & Writing Strand addresses communicating clearly and coherently through expressive language. Please note that CW.6 emphasizes that composing written and spoken products is not a linear process. This standard should be explicitly taught as well as integrated with CW.7-CW.9 to ensure students learn how to apply writing and speaking strategies when composing different text types for different audiences and purposes.

Communication & Writing Strand

Standard
	Objectives
	11-12.CW.1 Initiate and propel collaborative discussions about texts, building on others’ perspectives and expressing their own in a compelling manner.
	* 11-12.CW.1.1 Work with peers to promote civil, collegial discussions and decision-making, establishing individual roles as needed.
* 11-12.CW.1.2 Refer to evidence from texts and other research on the topic or issue to clarify, verify, or challenge ideas and conclusions.
* 11-12.CW.1.3 Probe reasoning and evidence to promote divergent and creative perspectives regarding broader themes, larger ideas, prior knowledge, and real-world contexts.
* 11-12.CW.1.4 Synthesize comments, claims, and evidence made on all sides of an issue to resolve contradictions in pursuit of consensus.
	11-12.CW.2 Demonstrate strategic command of grades K-12 grammar and usage when writing or speaking to create a unique style and voice.
	* 11-12.CW.2.1 Use verbals and verbal phrases to increase variety, precision, and nuance based on their function.
* 11-12.CW.2.2 Use verb tenses and aspects to convey conditional and subjunctive moods in order to suggest possibilities or express hypothetical situations.
* 11-12.CW.2.3 Use parallel structure to emphasize parity between ideas.
	11-12.CW.3 Demonstrate strategic command of grades K-12 conventions when writing to create a unique style and voice.
	* 11-12.CW.3.1 Use em dashes for nonrestrictive/parenthetical elements.
* 11-12.CW.3.2 Use a semicolon with conjunctive adverbs or transitional phrases to link independent clauses, as well as with coordinating conjunctions when independent clauses contain internal punctuation.
* 11-12.CW.3.3 Apply conventions for formatting block quotes.
	11-12.CW.4 Produce sentences appropriate for grades 11-12, with increasing fluency and complexity.
	* 11-12.CW.4.1 Create texts that demonstrate command of transcription skills.
* 11-12.CW.4.2 Use a wide variety of sentence structures to achieve particular effects.
* 11-12.CW.4.3 Use non-standard sentence structures to achieve particular literary or rhetorical effects.
	11-12.CW.5 Construct thorough research projects to answer a question or to make informed decisions related to a topic, concept, or theme aligned to grades 11-12 content.
	* 11-12.CW.5.1 Narrow or broaden the scope of inquiry when appropriate to answer a question, using advanced searches effectively.
* 11-12.CW.5.2 Synthesize relevant information from multiple authoritative sources to maintain a clear line of reasoning.
* 11-12.CW.5.3 Evaluate the validity and authoritativeness of each source.
* 11-12.CW.5.4 Conform to the guidelines in a style manual appropriate for the discipline and source type, avoiding plagiarism and overreliance on any one source.
	11-12.CW.6 Use a recursive process to refine spoken and written products.
	* 11-12.CW.6.1 Plan written and spoken products by organizing information and ideas around a topic.
* 11-12.CW.6.2 Draft written and spoken products to address what is most significant for a specific purpose, anticipating the knowledge level, concerns, values, and possible biases of the audience.
* 11-12.CW.6.3 Revise, edit, and rewrite written and spoken products, using support and collaboration with peers and adults.
* 11-12.CW.6.4 Publish, present, and produce written and spoken products, making strategic use of digital tools and multimodalities to enhance understanding and add interest.
	11-12.CW.7 Construct spoken and written persuasive texts to support claims derived from an analysis of substantive topics or texts, using valid reasoning and relevant and sufficient evidence.
	* 11-12.CW.7.1 Introduce and contextualize precise, knowledgeable claim(s), establish the relevance of the claim(s), and distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims.
* 11-12.CW.7.2 Develop claim(s) and counterclaims fairly and thoroughly, using the most relevant evidence and rhetorical techniques.
* 11-12.CW.7.3 Connect the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships between claim(s) and reasons, reasons and evidence, and claim(s) and counterclaims, using words, phrases, and clauses.
* 11-12.CW.7.4 Provide a concluding section that supports or emphasizes the argument presented, including conveying the urgency of the argument or a call to action, where appropriate.
	11-12.CW.8 Construct spoken and written analytical expository texts to convey complex ideas, concepts, and information clearly and accurately through the effective selection, organization, and examination of content.
	* 11-12.CW.8.1 Introduce the topic and focus clearly, and strategically structure complex ideas, concepts, and information.
* 11-12.CW.8.2 Develop the topic thoroughly by selecting the most significant and relevant facts, extended definitions, concrete details, quotations, and other information or examples related to the topic.
* 11-12.CW.8.3 Create cohesion, link major sections of the text, and clarify the relationships between explanations of complex ideas and concepts, using varied transitions and domain-specific vocabulary.
* 11-12.CW.8.4 Provide a concluding section that follows and supports the information or explanation presented.
	11-12.CW.9 Construct spoken and written poetic, descriptive, and narrative texts to develop real or imagined experiences, events, or ideas that center around a consistent theme(s) using effective technique, well-chosen details, and well-structured sequences.
	* 11-12.CW.9.1 Engage and orient the audience by setting out a problem, situation, or observation and its significance, establishing one or multiple perspectives, and introducing literary elements.
* 11-12.CW.9.2 Convey experiences and other literary elements that are complementary to the intended theme(s), using precise words and phrases, figurative language, rhetorical techniques, and telling and sensory details.
* 11-12.CW.9.3 Establish a sequence of well-developed topics, experiences, events, or characters to convey intended theme(s) using literary techniques.
*  11-12.CW.9.4 Conclude the text in a manner that emphasizes the theme(s) developed over the course of the text.
