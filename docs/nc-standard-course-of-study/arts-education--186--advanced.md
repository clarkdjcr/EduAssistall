# Arts Education - Advanced

Parent Guide for Advanced Visual Arts
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for visual arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with visual arts builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a student’s choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed visual arts educator.

Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students explore the impact of history, society, culture, and personal experiences on visual art and examine how the skills learned when creating and presenting art are relevant across different professions.
CN.2 Explore - Students explore the training, skills, and action plans necessary for a successful career and examine how opportunities in business, careers, and life are expanding as new tools, ideas, innovations, and technologies are developed.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students investigate and experiment with a variety of ideas, materials, and meanings to create several works of art with a common concept or theme.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to respectfully take inspiration from other artists and cultures and use new tools and media to express those ideas. Students also explore how a visual artist gets permission to use another artist’s work.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Present - Students use feedback to improve artwork for a collection or portfolio and also create a statement about a personal work of art that communicates their philosophy and artistic voice.
PR.2 Develop - Students select personal work that shows artistic choices and growth over time for an exhibit or portfolio. They also consider how to design strategies to encourage appropriate audience behaviors in formal or informal art exhibit settings.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students compare differences in a variety of art movements and how an artist’s intent and purpose of a work aligns with or differs from audience interpretation and knowledge of the history or cultural context of a work.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students give reasons for what they like and dislike in art and acknowledge how history, society, culture, and commercial art may influence their preferences. Students also use criteria and personal artistic vision to analyze and evaluate their own work.

How to Support Your Visual Arts Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your visual arts student:
* Visit a museum, watch a movie, or see public art, and discuss how art is impacted by historical, societal, cultural, or personal events.
* Discuss how art skills and concepts used in creation and presentation can be applied to other career fields.
* Examine the skills and training necessary for a specific career in visual arts.
* Discuss how technology is changing how art is made and shared.
* Provide a variety of materials for exploration and experimentation in art making.
* Encourage your child to create multiple works of art based on a central concept, theme, or idea.
* Have your child create an artist statement that describes their process, artistic choices, and personal philosophy as an artist.
* Create a digital or physical portfolio where your child can track progress, growth, and personal style.
* Discuss how to encourage audience participation and engagement with different types of exhibits.
* Expose your child to different works of art and have them compare distinguishing characteristics.
* Look at art together and compare personal interpretations with the artist’s intent and purpose.
* Discuss aesthetic responses to art and how society and culture may have influenced preferences.
