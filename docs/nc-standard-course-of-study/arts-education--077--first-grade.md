# Arts Education - First Grade

Parent Guide for First Grade Dance

The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K-5 standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed dance educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students learn how dance can reflect culture, values, and ideas along with cross-curricular connections of visual arts to other arts disciplines. Students also learn of mental challenges that are common to dance.
CN.2 Explore - Students learn of various roles of individuals who are responsible for dance creation and production, and how technology, innovations, and digital media are used in the creation and presentation of dance.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students explore using visual cues as prompts for spontaneous movement invention and collaborate with peers to create movements for the beginning, middle, and end of dance routines. Students also learn how to represent body shapes in dance by drawing pictures or using symbols.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to adapt movements by changing the shape or position of the body and examine how to give credit to other artists when borrowing their existing ideas.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Perform - Students learn to use feedback from instructors and peers to improve their dance skills and to demonstrate an awareness of how their body moves when performing multiple basic motor skills in sequence with one another. Students also strengthen their ability to perform with focus when dancing with others.
PR.2 Develop - Students learn how to use instructional support to generate and compare topics or themes for improvisation or an informal dance presentation and to demonstrate safe and respectful behaviors when performing and observing informal performances. Students also compare how dancing with props from a list of options can enhance a dance.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students describe movements that demonstrate how the elements of dance (body, space, time, effort quality) are used in dance, and also describe how various choreographed dances make them feel.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students examine personal preferences when watching dances from a variety of styles, techniques, and cultures, and identify positive feedback in self and peer-evaluation of work.

How to Support Your Dance Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your dance student:
* Clear a little space so your child can move freely and safely.
* Watch short clips of dances from different cultures.
* Talk about how creativity is part of all arts disciplines. Ask if there are any other similarities in the things they are learning and doing in their music, visual arts, or theatre classes to what they learn and do in dance?
* Talk about how it is natural for dancers to feel nervous sometimes or that they might forget the moves if they don’t practice. Discuss how dancers need to be confident and focused when performing.
* Talk about the many roles when watching dance—choreographers, dancers, costume designers, lighting technicians.
* Help your child to record a dance with your phone or other technology.
* Hold up a picture and ask your child to create a dance move inspired by it.
* Have your child create a short dance with a beginning, middle, and end, then let your child draw shapes or symbols to represent their favorite move.
* Practice motor skills such as jumping or skipping while others watch respectfully.
* Ask your child to tell you about their favorite style of dance (ballet, hip-hop, folk)
* Use scarves or ribbons to explore how props change movement.
* Watch dance together and describe what the dancers are doing and the feelings or emotions that are evoked.
* Practice giving compliments when watching your child dance.
