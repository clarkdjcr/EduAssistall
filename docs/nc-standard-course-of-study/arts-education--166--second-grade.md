# Arts Education - Second Grade

Parent Guide for Second Grade Visual Arts
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for visual arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with visual arts builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K-5 standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed visual arts educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students learn how American Visual Arts exist in the diverse heritage, customs, and traditions of various cultures in the United States and examine the cross-curricular relationships between visual arts and other subjects.
CN.2 Explore - Students learn about art careers in the local community and why technology, innovations, and digital media tools are used to make art.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students experiment with creative solutions to challenges to create art and learn to create original art that expresses ideas about people, neighborhoods, communities,
and imaginary sources of inspiration.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to adapt materials, style, or ideas from artists or artworks and
how to share both physical and digital works of art fairly.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Present - Students learn how to use constructive feedback from instructors, peers, and self to improve personal artwork and how to describe the personal choices and processes used when making art.
PR.2 Develop - Students learn how to display personal artwork both formally and informally and about the various ways audiences can interact with works of art.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students learn how to sort art into categories such as landscapes and portraits, and how to interpret the “story” communicated in a work of art.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students learn about their personal preferences for how the elements of art are used in works of art and how to provide constructive feedback on the work of others.

How to Support Your Visual Arts Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your visual arts student:
* Share how art appears in your family’s traditions or in community festivals, murals, and celebrations.
* Talk about how art connects to reading, history, math, or science.
* Point out local artists, designers, or photographers.
* Examine how innovation, technology, and digital media are used in artmaking.
* Celebrate art about real places, people, and imaginary worlds.
* Have your child explain the steps they took or choices they made in their artwork.
* Display your child’s artwork at home, create a digital slideshow, or let your child organize a “mini-gallery” for family and friends.
* Look at art together and sort them by theme, style, or other similarities.
* Look at different works of art and discuss interpretations of the story or meaning of each work.
* Encourage your child to explain what they like in their own work and in others’—colors, shapes, details—and practice giving kind, constructive feedback.
