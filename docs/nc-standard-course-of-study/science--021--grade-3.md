# Science - Grade 3

Grade 3 Science: 2023 Support Document

Third Grade

Purpose:
The 2023 North Carolina K-12 Science Standards were officially approved by the State Board of Education on July 6, 2023. The Standard Course of Study is designed to help students continually grow their science knowledge and abilities. The standards are intended to foster conceptual understanding, develop scientifically literate students, and provide opportunities to build knowledge and practices within each grade band or course.

The K-12 Science Support Documents serve as resources for teachers, administrators, science specialists, instructional coaches, parents, and other stakeholders in understanding the standards and planning instruction. They do not serve as curricula nor as a means to limit instruction in the classroom, but rather are intended to serve as a guide for discerning and describing features of students and their work necessary to meet grade level proficiency.

Science Teaching and Learning:
A coherent and consistent approach throughout grades K-12 is key to realizing the vision for science education embodied in A Framework for K-12 Science Education.  Students should actively engage in science and engineering practices and apply crosscutting concepts in every grade level to deepen their understanding of each field’s disciplinary core ideas.

Science Safety:
It is the responsibility of teachers and school administrators to use appropriate legal standards and best professional practices under duty of care to make the science laboratory safe.  Follow the Public School Unit’s guidelines regarding safety in the classroom. For more information, review the National Science Teaching Association’s position statement on safety and school science instruction.

The 2023 Science Standards are grouped in three domains: Physical Science, Life Science, and Earth & Space Science. The standards are organized in K-12 strands which increase in sophistication through the grade bands. The eleven strands include:

* Earth’s Place in the Universe

	Dimension 1: Science and Engineering Practice (SEP)
[Science and Engineering Practice included in Objective]
Practices refer to the things that scientists and engineers do and how they actively engage in their work. This section provides further clarification for what students should be doing to engage in this practice. There are various ways that each practice can be used, as articulated in A Framework for K-12 Science Education (NRC, 2012) and the NSTA’s SEP Matrix.

The Framework identifies a small number of disciplinary core ideas that all students should learn with increasing depth and sophistication, from Kindergarten through grade twelve. Key to the vision expressed in the Framework is for students to learn these disciplinary core ideas in the context of science and engineering practices. The importance of combining science and engineering practices and disciplinary core ideas is stated in the Framework as follows: Standards and performance expectations that are aligned to the framework must take into account that students cannot fully understand scientific and engineering ideas without engaging in the practices of inquiry and the discourses by which such ideas are developed and refined. At the same time, they cannot learn or show competence in practices except in the context of specific content (NRC Framework, 2012, p. 218).
The eight practices of science and engineering that the Framework identifies as essential for all students to learn and describes in detail are listed below:
1. Asking questions (for science) and defining problems (for engineering)
2. Developing and using models
3. Planning and carrying out investigations
4. Analyzing and interpreting data
5. Using mathematics and computational thinking
6. Constructing explanations (for science) and designing solutions (for engineering)
7. Engaging in argument from evidence
8. Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information
	Dimension 2: Crosscutting Concepts (CCC)
Crosscutting concepts are conceptual tools used along with the SEP and DCI to help students explain and predict science phenomena. They support students in making connections between disciplines and provide a context for sense-making. Teachers should encourage students to frame their thinking around the terminology of the CCC through questions and classroom discussions.
The most relevant Crosscutting Concepts may vary in light of the specific learning objectives and how the teacher structures the topic. Teachers should choose the one(s) that best support(s) their instructional goals and help(s) students make connections across different aspects of the content.
Suggested Crosscutting Concept(s): [Crosscutting Concept Name(s)] A Framework for K-12 Science Education identifies seven crosscutting concepts that bridge disciplinary boundaries and unite core ideas. Their purpose is to help students deepen their understanding of the disciplinary core ideas and develop a coherent and scientifically based view of the world (p. 83).
The seven crosscutting concepts of the Framework are:
1. Patterns
2. Cause and effect: Mechanism and explanation
3. Scale, proportion, and quantity
4. Systems and system models
5. Energy and matter: Flows, cycles, and conservation.
6. Structure and function
7. Stability and change

	What does it look like to demonstrate proficiency?

Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Infer - Drawing a logical conclusion from presented information

* Students make observations and develop the argument that air takes up space and has mass.
* Students provide evidence of air having mass.
* Students provide evidence of air taking up space.
* Students infer that air takes up space from presented information (e.g., balloon demonstrations, upside down cups in water tanks, inflating sports equipment, bike tires).
* Students infer that air has mass from presented information such as weighing filled and unfilled items (e.g.balloons, kickball, etc.).
* Plan and Carry out Investigations- Plan and conduct an investigation collaboratively to produce data to serve as evidence, using fair tests in which variables are controlled and the number of trials considered.

	Academic Language
Questions/Sentence stems that utilize academic language:
* What is the difference in the weight of the filled and unfilled balloon? Why is there a difference?  (A filled balloon has more weight as it contains air which is matter.)
* How could we test whether air has weight? (e.g., weigh a filled and unfilled ball or balloon, hang a ruler from a string and put a filled balloon on one side and an unfilled balloon on the other side)
* How could we test if air takes up space?  (e.g., filling tires/balloons/basketball with air, trying to crush an empty water bottle with the cap on)
* What evidence presented in the scenario supports the claim that air takes up space? (e.g. the balloon increases in size.)
* What evidence presented in the scenario supports that air has mass? (e.g. the balloon weighs more when blown up.)

Words to support student discourse: observation, matter, mass, gas, states of matter, evidence, weight

Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Classify - Determining that something belongs to a category based on relevant features or patterns

* Students identify three states of matter: solids, liquids and gases.
* Students know that all matter exhibits the following properties:
   * Solids- have mass, have a definite shape, and volume
   * Liquids-have mass, a definite volume but not a definite shape
   * Gases- have mass, do not have a definite shape nor a definite volume
* Students know that gases, liquids and solids are all made up of particles, but the spacing of these particles differ in the three states (gas, liquid, solid).
* Plan and Carry Out Investigations-
   * Make observations and/or measurements to produce data to serve as the basis for evidence for an explanation of the properties of solids, liquids and gases.
   * Classify states of matter using simple investigations such as if it does or does not take the shape of container, changes volume, etc.
	Academic Language
Questions/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:
* Does _________change shape when we change the container? (e.g., Does this ice cube change shape when we change the container? Use a solid, liquid and gas in different containers to answer this question)
* Does ________ change volume or mass when we change the container? (e.g., Does this ice cube change size or mass when we change the container? Use a solid, liquid and gas in different containers to answer this question)
* How did you classify the ____________ (solid, liquid, or gas)? (e.g., classified as a ________ based on properties)
* How are the particles spaced in a solid, liquid, and gas?
* What characteristics define each one? (solid - has mass, definite shape and volume, liquid - has mass, definite volume, but shape changes, gas - has mass, shape and volume change depending on the container)
* What are some other (solids/liquids/gases) you can think of? (answers will vary depending on the presented examples)

Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Explain - Constructing a cause-and-effect model of a system

* Students know that heating or cooling matter can result in changes in state.
* Students give examples of heating and cooling changes (e.g., melting ice, boiling water, melting chocolate, warming up soup, melting ice cream, freezing water, cooling boiling water, burning paper, cooking pancakes).
* Students know when heating matter:
   * some changes are reversible (e.g., melting ice)
   * some changes are not reversible  (e.g., pancake batter into pancakes, cooking an egg)
* Students compare changes in size and shape that do not alter the identity of the substance (cutting paper, reshaping clay, freezing water) with changes that do alter the identity of the substance (burning paper, baking a cake)
* Engage in Argument from Evidence-
   * Use data (observational) to evaluate claims about how heating or cooling causes changes in matter (e.g., boiling water changing to steam; water on a sidewalk freezing)
   * Students make observations to describe materials before and after changes.
   * Students make claims about the nature of the change (reversible or not reversible).
   * Students use evidence to conclude that heating or cooling matter can change its state.
	Academic Language
Questions/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:
* What evidence do you have that the matter has changed states? (e.g., changes shape or changes volume)
* What characteristics help you classify the change? (e.g., shape, volume)
* Can the matter go back to its original form? If so, how? (e.g., yes, the ice cube could melt and turn back into liquid water)
* What would happen when we cool this…?
* What would happen if we add heat…?
* How do changes in temperature affect the state of matter? (e.g. Liquid water can be heated to make water vapor/steam (gas). Water can be cooled and changed to ice (solid).
