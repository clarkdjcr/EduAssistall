# Arts Education - Fourth Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Theatre Arts Standards Unpacking Fourth Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for theatre arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with theatre build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 theatre standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed theatre educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Theatre Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 theatre programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

4.CN.1.1 Describe diverse North Carolina theatrical groups by geographic region.
Glossary
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Diverse - Including many different types of people or things.4
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students described theatre in the local community or region. Now, they are describing diverse North Carolina theatrical groups by geographic region. In the next level, they will describe how theatre and major historical events in the United States influenced each other.
Enduring Understanding
A theatre artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of stories, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* NC geographic regions
* A diverse representative set of North Carolinian theatrical groups
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of NC theatrical groups
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Exposing students to cultural diversity in NC through theatre can help students feel connected to the world around them
* Students can engage in theatre by recognizing successful theatre artists in North Carolina regions as resources and examples.
* Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Show students the different geographic regions on a map
* Present and share websites on outdoor theatres and their region
* Provide an opportunity for students to practice describing theatrical groups
* Provide an opportunity for students to practice categorizing theatrical groups by region
* Monitor students’ ability to describe NC theatre by region through formative and summative assessment, offering feedback as needed.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Community Theatre
* Children’s Theatre
* Regional Outdoor Drama
* Cultural Celebrations
* American Indians
* Unto These Hills Outdoor Theatre (Cherokee)
* Slavery
* Educational theatre groups (HBCUs, UNCP, groups in NC) College/Universities)
* Artists/Performers represented by the NC Arts Council / Artist in Residency

* Lighting - The illumination of the performance dictated by the given circumstances of the play, including the source of light, the time of day, the mood of the play, as well as the specific requirements of the scenic design (for example, the areas to be lit).29

This objective develops the EQ-throughline because, in the previous level, students created simple set designs, costume designs, or prop designs to support the story of a play. Now, they are creating simple lighting or sound options to support theatrical work. In the next level, they will create simple technical designs to support dramatic presentations.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are actualized by theatre designers and craftspersons intentionally to fulfill dramatic purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Components of simple lighting designs
* Components of simple sound designs
* The basic lighting techniques because light creates mood, focus, and atmosphere, which enhances their ability to focus, retain information, and perform better academically.
* Light interacts with the environment and impacts perception allows students to optimize their learning spaces and improve their overall learning experience
* Sound, including sound effects and music, can enhance the dramatic presentation and contribute to the story and mood, helping to bring a narrative to life, adding layers of meaning and depth that might not be conveyed through visuals or dialogue alone
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating new lighting or sound options
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students do not need to execute their designs to master this objective, but only to create designs for lighting and/or sound
* This objective will be most meaningful when supporting improvisations (CR.1.1), original creation (CR.1.2), or as part of presenting works (PR.1.2). This objective has students creating the technical elements used in PR.2.3
* Since this objective has the language “lighting or sound” either design type may be created to master this objective
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare materials for lighting and sound design. While students are not required to physically create the items they design, doing so can support the implementation of PR.2.3. Share pictures or videos of simple lighting and sound options used in children's theater or well-known stories and discuss how these help tell the story. As many students may be unfamiliar with the equipment, show illustrations of basic lighting and sound. If available, demonstrate how basic lighting and sound equipment can support theatrical work
* Determine whether your students will all design simple lighting or sound options, or if students will be assigned to differing tasks
* Demonstrate how to create simple lighting and/or sound options for a specific theatrical work
* Guide students in designing their own simple lighting and/or sound options, considering key aspects of the play that inform design choices such as setting, period, mood, themes, characters, and plot
* Provide parameters for students to create lighting and/or sound options for a specific work
* Monitor students’ ability to create lighting and/or sound options for works through formative and summative assessment

May include, but are not limited to:
* The definition of (lighting)backlight, blackout, color, dimmers, dimming up, dimming down, floodlight, and spotlight
* Location of lighting
* Lighting equipment
* Special lighting effects
* Basic principles of lighting

4.CR.2.1 Adapt various pieces of literature into theatrical works.
Glossary
* Adapt - To change, or to change something, to suit different conditions or uses.4
* Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students derived dialogue from familiar stories, distinguishing unique characters. Now, they are adapting various pieces of literature into theatrical works. In the next level, they will adapt a variety of literary genres into theatrical performances
Enduring Understanding
Theatre artists balance inspiration and originality with ethics by creatively transforming or adapting other artistic ideas into new works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Procedures for adapting literature
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Modifying literature into theatre
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Adaptation reinforces an understanding of the text by requiring students to actively analyze characters, plot, themes, and settings. It also promotes critical thinking, creativity, collaborative work, and deeper engagement with reading. Through this process, students learn to make interpretive decisions that honor the source material while transforming it into a performance medium
* Prioritize characters when beginning adaptation work, as they serve as entry points into the story. Exploring character motivations, relationships, and arcs can guide students in deciding what moments are most important to include, cut, or reimagine for the stage. This character-first approach helps students make meaningful choices that maintain the heart of the original story
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare a piece of familiar literature to serve as a model for adaptation. This could come from classroom read-alouds, texts used in ELA, or well-known public domain stories like The Three Little Pigs, Cinderella, or The Tortoise and the Hare that students already know well
* Demonstrate how to adapt the literature by identifying key story elements (theme, characters, plot, setting). Use visuals to support understanding, simplify unfamiliar vocabulary, and show how to turn narration into stage action or dialogue. Model how to condense or omit less important scenes while keeping the heart of the story intact
* Guide students through adapting their own piece of literature with support — this might include brainstorming as a class, working in small groups, using storyboards or graphic organizers, and giving students choices about what scenes or characters to adapt
* Provide tools like sentence starters, visual aids, simplified scripts, character cards, and scene outlines to help students build their theatrical adaptations with confidence and creativity
* Monitor individual students’ ability to adapt various pieces of literature through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Definitions - literature, fiction, nonfiction, and drama
* Fairytales
* Classical stories
* Poetry
* News stories
* Singing songs in style
* Songs as Plays
* Reading List (4th grade).

4.CR.2.2 Identify how to responsibly use resources for online access of theatre works.
Glossary
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
* Use - To put into action or service.14

This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students identified theatre artworks that can be used by everyone fairly and now identify how to responsibly use resources for online access of theatre artworks. In the next level, students will learn to cite theatre artists correctly when utilizing or adapting their original artwork.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that respect for originality and ownership of intellectual property is shown through the ethical use of artistic works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Appropriate procedures for accessing theatre online
* How to use resources responsibly
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming the process for responsibly accessing resources for theatre online
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students should be able to answer the question, “How do I access theatre online?” to show mastery of this objective
* “Resources” include tools, websites, databases, apps, and digital platforms such as search engines, video-sharing sites, and social media. These are the spaces where students gain access to theatre, whether it’s in the public domain, hosted online or part of a subscription-based service
* Students may have false assumptions about how to responsibly access resources for theatre
* This objective can be paired with CR.2.1 in which students adapt literature written by others

   * Peer critiques empower students to take responsibility for and manage their learning.
   * Students develop lifelong skills in assessing and providing feedback to others.
   * Students will self-assess which will improve their work.
   * Critiques foster empathy, critical thinking, and positive social interactions.
   * Continue to encourage positive critique language. Positive language examples include: "I liked how you...", "A strong part of your work was...", "You did a great job explaining...", "This detail stood out to me...", "I can see you put a lot of effort into...", "One thing I especially enjoyed was...", "Your creativity shines through in...", and "I like how you used..."
   * Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts
   * Since this objective has the language “vocabulary and nonverbal cues” both must be given
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Prepare key theatre vocabulary and examples of supportive nonverbal communication
   * Demonstrate how to combine words and body language to deliver respectful, specific feedback
   * Guide students in identifying strong moments in peer work using theatre terms
   * Provide practice scenarios to role-play delivering and receiving feedback with appropriate cues
   * Monitor individual students' ability to critique peer works using theatrical vocabulary and nonverbal cues through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
   * Reasons for the director's input and notes.
   * Use guidelines for the peer feedback process.
   * Provide examples of positive feedback.
   * Discuss positive feedback versus negative criticism.
   * Role-playing activities.
