NCDPI Arts Education Course Manual
About This Document
The Arts Education Team at the North Carolina Department of Public Instruction has compiled all relevant information pertaining to courses in K-12 Dance, Music, Theatre Arts, and Visual Arts into this document to provide clarity around the unique proficiency-based Standard Course of Study in the Arts. Though broken down into discrete sections, the information contained in each section is often repeated and cross-referenced with information from an additional section; i.e. in order to properly understand the course coding structure, an understanding of proficiency levels and the suggested course progression is also necessary.
The content is presented in five sections:
1. Course Codes - This section provides an explanation of the coding structure including the unique honors structure of Arts Education courses, Specialization Courses, and how proficiency levels are used. Additional NCDPI Course Coding resources include links to introductory course coding webinars.
2. Arts Education Licensure - This section provides the NC School Board of Education Policy and testing information for teacher licenses in the areas of Dance, Music, Theatre Arts and Visual Arts. Further information on in-field licensure can also be found.
3. Suggested Course Progression - An in-depth look at how students enter and progress through Arts Ed courses is provided. Guidance on the implementation of VIM proficiency levels (VIMplementation) is provided. Discussion of placement examinations, skipping levels, as well as recommendations for student profiles, concentrations in the arts, and example schedules are also provided.
4. Arts Education Graduation Requirement - An overview of the requirement along with a timeline are provided.
5. Arts Education Diploma Endorsement - A restatement of the quick guide for easy access.
In addition to the foundational information in each section, FAQs are provided from archived questions from the field.

Course Codes
For the most up-to-date resources on Course Codes, visit the NCDPI SIS webpage.
Overview
Course codes are ten digits long.  The first seven digits are designated by NCDPI and the last 3 digits are available for individual LEA/school use. Important information about courses and grade level subjects is contained within the seven-digit course code.
The first four digits represent the course. The first digit of the four digits represents the academic area as follows:
        0 = non specific subject
        1 = English/Language Arts; World Languages; Public Speaking
        2 = Mathematics
        3 = Science
        4 = Social Studies
        5 = Arts
        6 = Health/PE
9 = Occupational Course of Study; ROTC; Approved Online Vendor Courses; Special Interest Topics; Teacher Cadet; SAT prep; ACT prep; Pre-K
Alpha = Career and Technical Education courses
The course codes for the most recent school year are available at the NCEd Connect Page. However, the information below is provided for quick reference:
* Dance - 50; 51
* Music - 52; 56
* Theatre Arts - 53
* Visual Arts - 54; 55
* Other Local Arts Elective - 57

Honors FAQs
Q: Can students receive weighted (honors) credit for courses in arts education?
A: Yes, students in NC have been able to receive weighted credit for courses in arts education since 1994. Accomplished and Advanced courses are inherently honors
Q: What is the purpose of honors courses?
A: The purpose of honors courses in arts education is to provide the opportunity for advanced work and to promote rigorous academic study and practical application of knowledge and skills.  These courses are designed for students who have demonstrated an advanced level of achievement in a given arts discipline.
Coursework at the Accomplished and Advanced levels in the 2024 NC Arts Education Standards aligns with SBE policy SCOS-016 on academic rigor as well as the highest identified levels of achievement in the National Standards for Arts Education.
Q: What is the current honors policy for arts education courses in NC?
A: State Board of Education Policy GRAD-009
Section 3.1.2. states “3.1.2. Honors courses - Course content, pace and academic rigor place high expectations on the student, demanding greater independence and responsibility. Such courses are more challenging than standard level courses and are distinguished by a difference in the depth and scope of work required to address the NCSCoS. These courses provide credit toward a high school diploma and require the end-of-course test for those courses identified as such in the NC accountability program. An honors review process shall be followed, as outlined in the latest edition of the North Carolina Honors Course Implementation Guide. The state course weighting system awards the equivalent of one (1) quality point to the grade earned in Honors courses. Effective with the freshman class of 2015-16, the weighting for Honors courses shall be one-half (.5) of a quality point.”

Section 4.3 addresses courses eligible for weights:
“4.3. Arts education courses meeting the standards for proficient and advanced dance, music, theatre arts, and visual arts are assigned Honors-level weighting in accordance with section 3.1.2 above.”

Section 6 has additional guidance for LEAs:
6.         In accordance with GS 116-11 (10a), each student’s official class rank shall be listed on the standardized transcript.
6.1. The official class rank shall be calculated using the weighted grade point average in which quality points are provided for passing grades in standard, Honors, AP/IB/CIE, and college-level courses according to the weighting system defined in sections 3 and 4 above.
6.2. Local education agencies may re-calculate class rank for local purposes such as determination of valedictorian, salutatorian, and other graduation honors. Such re-calculations may be used for local purposes only, and the official class rank provided on the standardized transcript shall not be altered.
This policy aligns with the 2024 NC Arts Education Standards and NC Course Coding Structure.
Q: How do the proficiency levels inform students’ studies in the arts?
The 2024 NC Arts Education Standards reflect four levels of proficiency for high school courses for credit. The Beginning and Intermediate levels build the foundation for student success in continuing coursework at the Accomplished and Advanced levels.  Students who take coursework at the Accomplished or Advanced levels are applying their skills and understandings in increasingly sophisticated, complex, and challenging ways. Note: VIM courses have five levels for credit.
Q: Can students receive weighted credit for more than one course at each proficiency level?
A: Students are not restricted by policy to only one honors credit at the accomplished and/or advanced levels. Courses should be developed and aligned with these inherently advanced standards and students must achieve mastery of the objectives in order to progress to the next level. It is important for school systems to have in place criteria for placement of students at the appropriate level (e.g., student profile, checklists, pre-requisites and other criteria).
Q: What about the Honors Implementation Guide and Rubric?
A: According to the North Carolina Honors Level Course Rubric Framework, approved by the North Carolina State Board of Education in July 2012, “Inherently Advanced/Inherently Honors Courses” are exempt from the state-level honors review process. “Inherently advanced” courses include courses such as Pre-calculus, World Languages Levels III and above, Advanced Placement (AP)/International Baccalaureate (IB), higher proficiency levels for Arts Education and Career and Technical Education Courses and Non-AP/IB Calculus.
Q: What course codes should we be using for honors courses in the arts?
A: Course numbers will be those courses using the accomplished and advanced standards that are coded as accomplished and advanced within each arts discipline (see chart above for codes and titles).
Q: Can students repeat honors courses for new credit?
A: Yes, students may take courses multiple times and receive new credit at any of the arts education proficiency levels.  Students have been able to do this since before 2011. It is important for school systems to have in place criteria for placement of students at the appropriate level (e.g., student profile, checklists, etc.).
Q: If a student takes the same course more than once, will each instance count as a separate credit toward graduation?
A: Yes. When a student repeats the same art education course, each enrollment counts as a separate elective credit toward graduation. For example, if a student takes Band Accomplished four times, all four instances count toward the elective credit requirement within the minimum 22 credits needed for graduation.
Q: What is an “Other Arts Elective”?
A: Locally created interdisciplinary arts (i.e. film and sound editing) courses or a “wheel” of 9-week courses in dance, music, theatre, and visual arts are examples of “Other Arts Electives.” Courses that are focused on the arts but do not fit into the other categories are appropriate for these codes.

Specialization Courses
Specialized courses may be offered at each proficiency level in each arts education discipline.  Specializations are aligned to the proficiency levels and take the place of courses that were not previously aligned.
School systems may specify content in their short titles or they may pull down the course and create a new iteration, for example: “Music Specialization (Accomplished): Piano” or “Theatre Arts (Advanced): Players.”
Multiple courses can be offered and aligned with the same state course code.  There may be several specialized studies available within a given discipline and proficiency level, however, they MUST teach the entirety of the standard course of study in order to be coded as a specialization course. Courses which do not teach the entirety of the Standard Course of Study should be coded as a local elective. (See the chart that follows for  examples):
Specialized Course Examples
In the past, specialization courses have been an option for LEAs to use to offer courses which didn’t neatly fit into the state provided courses. However, with the 2024 update to the standards and the course codes, the NCDPI Arts Education team recommends discontinuing the use of specialization courses for those that are simply variations of the main course, and to instead use the three alpha-numeric codes at the end of the state provided code to differentiate the course. For example, the use of the Accomplished Visual Arts code can be differentiated for painting, 3D media, and ceramics by adding PNT, 3DM, and CER to the accomplished Visual Arts state code, respectively.
Specialization course codes should be reserved for innovative courses that do not fit at all into the state course code structure, but do follow the state SCOS for that discipline. Any course that does not teach the entirety of a SCOS must be coded as a local elective

Proficiency Levels
International Use of Proficiency Based Programs
* Mastery-based programs occur in nations throughout the world and at all levels of education. When compared to traditionally taught classes, students in mastery learning classes consistently have been shown to learn better, reach higher levels of achievement, and develop greater confidence in their ability to learn and in themselves as learners (Guskey, 1997, 2001).
* Extensive research from Asia (Kim et al., 1969, 1970; Wu, 1994), Australia (Chan, 1981), Europe (Dyke, 1988; Langeheine, 1992; Mevarech, 1985, 1986; Postlethwaite & Haggarty, 1998; Reezigt & Weide, 1990, 1992; Yildiran, 2006), South America (Cabezon, 1984), and the United States (Anderson, 1994; Block, Efthim, & Burns, 1989; Guskey & Pigott, 1988; Walberg, 1984, 1988), shows the careful and systematic application of mastery learning principles can lead to significant improvements in student learning.
National Use of Proficiency Based Programs
* The National Standards for Arts Education (1994) describe the cumulative knowledge and skills expected of students, defined through proficient and advanced achievement levels for grades 9-12, in each arts discipline.  Standards provide rigorous, sequential guidelines for quality arts education for all students with students expected to achieve the proficient level in at least one arts discipline by the time they graduate.
* Many states, such as Alabama, Arizona, California, Delaware, Ohio, Rhode Island, Tennessee, and North Carolina include proficiency levels as part of their state arts standards.
* The Council of Chief State School Officers includes Performance Based Learning (Proficiency Based Teaching and Learning), as one of the six attributes of Next Generation Learning. (CCSSO, 2011)
* Many of the state-wide movements towards proficiency are in response to the business community and to College and Career Readiness initiatives. PBTL addresses business community concerns by focusing classroom instruction on mastery of clearly defined outcomes as opposed to reflexively equating seat time with competency.
North Carolina’s Use of Proficiency-Based Programs
* The beginning high school proficiency level addresses the needs of multiple entry points for students who have had limited or no K-8 education in the arts. The proficiency-based standards continue through intermediate, accomplished, and advanced levels, which are aligned with the National Standards. Students receive weighted, honors credit for inherently advanced coursework at the accomplished and advanced levels (State Board of Education policy GRAD-010).
* The North Carolina high school proficiency standards provide multiple options for students to study the arts as an area of interest or to specialize in studies to prepare them for further education and/or a career in the arts. As reflected in other states, such as Delaware, the spiraling nature of the North Carolina standards allow students to develop proficiency from beginning through advanced levels.
* Proficiency Based Teaching and Learning exists in North Carolina’s Public School Units (PSUs) in World Languages and Arts Education. North Carolina’s arts education proficiency levels represent a hierarchy of objectives that describe artistic and cognitive student learning expectations which scaffolds artistic behaviors from the simplest to the most complex.
* Development and implementation of the proficiency-based Arts Education Essential Standards has involved input from stakeholders, including LEAs, IHE members, professional associations, and others.  In creating the Arts Education Essential Standards (2010), over 100 academic and research-based sources were investigated. Proficiency levels were then implemented in the 2014 National Arts Education Standards as well as the 2024 NC Arts Education Standard Course of Study.

Proficiency Levels FAQs
Q: Can students repeat courses for new credit?
A: Yes, students may take courses multiple times and receive new credit at any of the arts education proficiency levels.  Students have been able to do this since before 2011. It is important for school systems to have in place criteria for placement of students at the appropriate level (e.g., student profile, checklists, etc.).
Q: If a student takes the same course more than once, will each instance count as a separate credit toward graduation?
A: Yes. When a student repeats the same art education course, each enrollment counts as a separate elective credit toward graduation. For example, if a student takes Band Accomplished four times, all four instances count toward the elective credit requirement within the minimum 22 credits needed for graduation.
Q: What are the proficiency levels?
A: The NC Arts Education Essential Standards reflect four levels of proficiency for high school courses for credit. The Beginning and Intermediate levels build the foundation for student success in continuing coursework at the Accomplished and Advanced levels. Note: VIM courses have five levels for credit including Novice and Developing in place of Beginning.
Q: When do students move from one proficiency level to the next?
A: Mastery of the objectives is the criteria for advancement.  Thus, students may take courses multiple times and receive new credit within a given proficiency level before moving to the next level, based on their mastery of the standards.
Q: How does my school system determine student placement?
A: Use the standards as your guide.  Courses using beginning standards are for those students who have not received a complete K-8 education within a particular arts education discipline.  Intermediate standards are for those students who have achieved the beginning Standards (or their equivalent through K-8 instruction); accomplished standards are for those students who have achieved the Intermediate standards, and advanced standards are for those students who have achieved the accomplished standards.  It is highly likely that students may complete multiple courses at a given level before moving to the next, based on their mastery of the standards.
Q: Will the state provide placement exams or other tools for appropriate placement of students to determine whether they should use beginning or progress immediately to intermediate standards at the high school level?
A: There are no state placement tools.  The standards require that any student who has not received a complete K-8 education or who has not met beginning level standards should begin coursework at the Beginning (or Novice) level.  Intermediate standards are for those students who have received a complete K-8 education or who can provide evidence of having met beginning level standards.  A student profile/portfolio could be used to document evidence of student learning.
Q: Do students need to wait until the end of the course to progress if they meet standards mid-course?
A: Teachers must differentiate the standards as appropriate to the individual student.  If there is evidence that students have achieved all of the standards within a given proficiency level, it is up to the teacher to ensure that the student has opportunities to either extend the standards or work toward the next level of proficiency. Course titles and levels will only change on a student’s transcript with each new course enrollment.

In-Field Licensure
Policy EVAL-034
According to SBOE Policy EVAL-034, All professional assignments in the area of an individual’s non-provisional license are considered “in-field”.  Any teaching assignment, other than remedial, that is outside an individual’s non-provisional license area is considered “out-of-field.” Non-provisional license includes CPL, IPL, Lateral Entry, and/or Residency licenses. The LEA may assign any licensed teacher who is the best qualified to teach remedial courses, regardless of licensure area.

Additionally, at the beginning of each school year, LEAs must notify the parents/guardians of each student attending a Title I school that they may request the following qualifications of their child’s teacher:
   * Whether the teacher has met NC licensing requirements
   * Whether the teacher has had any licensure requirements waived
   * What the teacher’s bachelor degree major(s) is/are, other degrees held and teaching license area(s) held
   * Whether the teacher is or is not teaching within their certified field

Suggested Course Progression
The North Carolina Arts Education Standards communicate what students should know and be able to do as a result of instruction at each high school proficiency level: beginning; intermediate; accomplished (shown below as proficient, updated in 2024); and advanced. Note: Vocal and Instrumental Music have a different, five-proficiency pathway.
In Dance, General Music, Theatre Arts, Technical Theatre, and Visual Arts:
   * Beginning: Standards are for students with no or limited K-8 progression in the arts education discipline (dance, music, theatre arts, or visual arts).
   * Note: Vocal & Instrumental Music uses two proficiency levels in place of the one Beginning level. The base level, novice, can be started in middle school or at any point in a student’s musical career.
   * Novice: Standards are for students with no or limited K-8 progression in vocal or instrumental music (band, choir, orchestra, percussion, modern band, guitar, etc). Students with elementary band exploratory programs would still begin in Novice.
   * Developing: Standards are for students who have achieved novice level standards in vocal or instrumental music (band, choir, orchestra, percussion, modern band, guitar, etc).

VIMplementation
The Vocal and Instrumental Music (VIM) Implementation Guide gives Public School Units, administrators, scheduling counselors, and VIM teachers examples for implementing the five VIM proficiency levels within and across the typical grade spans where VIM instruction typically occurs. The examples are for illustrative purposes only and are not intended to be prescriptive. These standards will be implemented in all North Carolina schools beginning in the 2025-26 school year. For more information on the VIM Standard Course of Study, the Arts Education Course Manual, or more unpacking documents, see the Arts Education Hub at go.ncdpi.gov/AE-HUB. The shortlink to the document is go.ncdpi.gov/VIMplementation-Guide. An abbreviated version of the information follows:

The Vocal and Instrumental Music (VIM) SCOS consists of five levels aligned to the world language standard proficiency levels. These were created in response to the call from the music field to create standards that were proficiency-based and allowed students to begin vocal or instrumental classes with absolutely no prior experience in the high school levels.
* Denotes courses that are individual instrument courses instead of ensemble courses.

The General Music SCOS is intended for use for ALL K-5 students before differentiating into either a Vocal and Instrumental Music (VIM) pathway or a General Music pathway.

K-5 General Music ➠ Novice VIM ➠ Developing ➠ Intermediate ➠ Accomplished ➠ Advanced

Placement Examination FAQs
Q: What should be offered at the middle school level to assure students are prepared for their Arts Education courses at the high school?
A: Providing students with the opportunity to receive instruction in each of the arts disciplines using the NC Arts Education Standards in grades K-8 will help to prepare them for more advanced studies in high school; however, the beginning level high school standards are designed for those students who have not benefited from a complete K-8 education within a particular arts discipline, thereby allowing for multiple entry points based on students’ prior education in the arts.
Q: Do the achieved standards have to be met in the public school system K-8?  An example might be a student who has had private dance lessons for 8 years prior to starting high school.
A: Students’ achievement of the standards could be influenced by in and/or out-of-school educational experiences in any of the arts disciplines.  School systems should have criteria in place to help document students’ achievement of the standards.  While a student who received eight years of private dance lessons may excel in the technique of a particular dance form (such as ballet), he or she may not have achieved other standards such as connecting dance with other disciplines, analyzing or evaluating dance, or creating dances using specific criteria, for example.
Q: What evidence of proficiency can be provided for students who have outside/private training?
A: A student profile, which documents this outside/private training and experience, as well as other criteria, such as rubrics, checklists, auditions, or other tools, may be used to help document evidence of proficiency. Student proficiency should be evaluated for Connect, Respond, Create and Present objectives.
Q: Even if a student is not "evaluated" to be ready for intermediate or advanced, are they still going to be able to CHOOSE to enroll in the honors level against recommendation?
A: While it is not advisable for a student to choose to enroll in a course at a higher proficiency level than they are ready for, this situation will need to be addressed through local policy and practice.
Q: If a student transfers to us in the intermediate level and our teacher recommends the beginning level.  Who decides the academic level?
A: Student placement should be tied directly to achievement of the standards. This is an issue that could be addressed through discussions and collaboration at the local level.
Q: Can a student skip a level?  For example, jump from beginner to accomplished.
A: Yes, if a student has achieved the previous level standards, they could, for example, go directly into a more-advanced set of standards.  It is highly likely that students may complete multiple courses at a given level before moving to the next, based on their mastery of the standards. Skipping an entire proficiency level (especially beyond the beginning level) would be rare, and not typical for most students.
Q: How does my school system determine student placement?
A: Use the standards as your guide. Courses using Beginning standards are for those students who have not received a complete K-8 education within a particular arts education discipline. Intermediate standards are for those students who have achieved the Beginning standards; Accomplished standards are for those students who have achieved the Intermediate standards, and Advanced standards are for those students who have achieved the Accomplished standards. It is highly likely that students may complete multiple courses at a given level before moving to the next, based on their mastery of the standards.
Q: Will the state provide placement exams or other tools for appropriate placement of students to determine whether they should use beginning or progress immediately to intermediate standards at the high school level?
A: There are no state placement tools. The standards require that any student who has not received a complete K-8 education or who has not met beginning level standards should begin coursework at the beginning level. Intermediate standards are for those students who have received a complete K-8 education or who can provide evidence of having met beginning level standards. A student profile/portfolio could be used to document evidence of student learning.
Credit by Demonstrated Mastery (CDM)
Credit by Demonstrated Mastery (CDM) is the process by which a student may earn credit for a high school course by demonstrating a deep understanding of the content; without course enrollment or seat time. CDM was designed to respond to the needs of students, families, AIG community, school personnel and leadership.
There is no current CDM policy for Arts Education.
Locally Developed Student Profile
Upon entry to high school level study, it is suggested that each student be assessed via a student profile developed at the local level. The profile documents previous arts education and student academic performance in any of the four arts education disciplines at the K-8 levels, as well as any arts studies that the student has completed outside of the school (private lessons, internships, studio classes, etc.).
This profile should be maintained throughout the high school career, so that each student has a history of his/her education, academic performance, and experiences in the arts.
Teachers and school counselors may use the student profile to help determine the appropriate placement of students at the high school level. Those students who have had limited or NO instruction in the arts discipline prior to the high school level will enter at a beginning level of study in any high school coursework. Students may participate in a variety of electives from beginning through advanced levels in each of the arts disciplines.
Concentrations in the Arts
It is recommended that concentrations in the arts reflect a sequence of courses in an arts discipline to include at least one advanced level course. Courses beyond the intermediate level are considered advanced.
Standards at the accomplished and advanced levels are aligned with the highest achievement standards at the national level and are intended to provide in-depth, rigorous, and challenging study for advanced level students. These would include honors, AP, or IB courses and specialization in any area of study within an arts discipline.
The completion of the concentration should lead to a culminating project or capstone experience which allows the student to demonstrate advanced skills in the  arts discipline and which may also be used as part of a professional portfolio for entrance into institutions of higher education or a career in the arts. Examples of culminating projects include: producing a student-written play; choreographing a dance for a public performance; publishing and conducting a student-written musical composition; or producing a student exhibition of original art.

FAQs
Why is There a New Requirement?
S681 passed in June of 2020 and became Session Law SL2020-78 in July. This law directed the State Board of Education (SBE) to modify graduation requirements, which previously included an option to include an arts education credit, to require one credit of arts education to be completed at any time during grades six through twelve. The new requirement begins with students entering Grade 6 in 2022-23 and, subsequently, in Grade 9 in 2025-26.
Where Can I Find the Updated Policy?
The updated language for NC SBE Policy GRAD-004 is located in the SBE Policy Manual. Please note: The total number of credits required to graduate is not changing. The new requirement is embedded as part of the 6 elective credits of the 22 required credits for graduation.
How Will the Requirement be Tracked?
Completion of the requirement will be tracked via course code in Infinite Campus and will be shown on a student’s transcript. This means that it is imperative to code courses correctly.
What Courses Count Toward the Requirement?
All Arts Education courses in grades 6-8 that follow the SCOS for their assigned grade level will satisfy the graduation requirement. All high school courses which follow the NC SCOS for Arts Education, Advanced Placement (AP), Cambridge International Education (CIE), and International Baccalaureate (IB) courses will satisfy the graduation requirement. Only those courses with officially recognized course codes will be automatically tracked in Infinite Campus. DO NOT use course codes beginning with “9”. To learn more about the NCDPI Arts Education Course Coding structure, please visit the information in previous sections.
Should the High School Standard Course of Study be taught to Middle Schoolers?
No! Middle schoolers should receive the Standard Course of Study appropriate to the grade level they are in. Though students will not receive high school credit for middle school arts education courses, the arts education graduation requirement can be satisfied when a middle schooler takes a middle school level arts education course.
NCVPS Arts Courses: A Flexible Option for Meeting Graduation Requirements
For schools that may not currently offer in-person arts education, the North Carolina Virtual Public School (NCVPS) provides high-quality, fully SCOS-aligned arts education courses. These online courses are an excellent option for ensuring students meet the state’s arts education graduation requirement, especially when hiring a full-time arts educator is not feasible.
NCVPS currently offers both middle and high school visual arts courses, with additional options in Digital Music Production and Screendance in development. We strongly encourage districts and schools to consider these rigorous, standards-aligned courses to support equitable access to arts education for all students.

	What Courses DO NOT Count Toward the Requirement?
Any course that does not deliver the entirety of the Standard Course of Study of the arts education discipline should use a local elective code. Examples could include courses students take for nine weeks in a “wheel” rotation or any locally developed course which does not utilize the SBE-approved SCOS. Media Arts courses with a locally developed curriculum should use the “57” codes.
Is There An Option for Early College Students?
In order to receive a well-rounded arts education (Connect, Respond, Create, Present) and fulfill the arts content requirement for high school graduation through dual enrollment, NCDPI has approved a two-course community college combination in the same discipline. Students may take both the Appreciation (Connect/Respond) and Practicum (Create/Present) courses to satisfy the arts requirement. These new options are responsive to requests from NC’s Cooperative Innovation High Schools.
These three options for students to satisfy the arts graduation requirement through CCP are:
      * ART-111 (Art Appreciation) and ART-131 (Drawing)
      * DRA-111 (Theatre Appreciation) and DRA-130 (Acting I)
      * MUS-110 (Music Appreciation) and MUS-111 (Music Fundamentals)
Are There Exceptions?
Students moving from another state to North Carolina in the ninth grade or later may be exempt from the arts education graduation requirement if requiring them to do so would keep them from graduating with the cohort to which they are assigned.

Arts Proficiency Endorsement Overview
As of 2024, the Arts Proficiency Endorsement is a part of State Board of Education policy, Grad 007.
      * Students in North Carolina public schools and public charter schools may earn one or more endorsements on their high school diploma. These endorsements indicate that students have completed specific course concentrations preparing them to be ready for careers and/or college.
      * Students may earn more than one diploma endorsement. Students are not required to earn an endorsement in order to receive a diploma.
      * Based on State Board of Education policy (GRAD-007), the implementation of the diploma endorsements is required for all Local Education Agency high schools. This policy is optional for charter school boards of directors.
ENDORSEMENT POLICY:
      1. The student shall complete a minimum of four arts courses.
      2. The student shall earn an unweighted GPA of 3.0 or higher in each arts credit.
      3. The student shall complete a minimum of 40 hours of arts-related extracurricular activities.
      1. The student shall participate in an arts-related extracurricular activity approved by the local board of education.
      2. The student shall complete all of the required hours outside of instructional hours.
      3. The student shall not receive any course credit for participation in the activity.
      4. In accordance with State Board guidance, the student shall document the hours on the form provided to local boards of education.

NOTE:  The NC-SIS will not be computing the Arts Endorsement this year.  LEAs and others may use local processes to award the Arts Endorsement. It will not appear on the transcript this year.

ARTS PROFICIENCY ENDORSEMENT COURSE GUIDANCE
In an effort to promote equity and celebrate the creative commitment of our student artists, the list of courses that satisfy the Arts Education Diploma Endorsement is being expanded. Effective beginning in the 2025–26 school year, the following courses will now count toward fulfilling the four-course endorsement requirement:
      * All high school course codes beginning with 50, 51, 52, 53, 54, 55, 56, and 57
      * All Advanced Placement (5A), International Baccalaureate (5I), and Cambridge (5V) arts courses
The main change for 2025–26 is the inclusion of local electives, especially Technical Theatre and General Music courses at the Intermediate or Advanced levels, as well as AP Art History.

      1. Evidence of Participation: Students must provide convincing and verifiable documentation demonstrating their attendance or active participation in the activity.
      2. Non-Curricular Activity: The activity must satisfy all other requirements regarding eligibility put forth in the policy.
