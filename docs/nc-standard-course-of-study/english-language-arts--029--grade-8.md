# English Language Arts - Grade 8

Introduction
Unpacking the ELA Standard Course of Study
Grade 8

On January 8, 2026, the State Board of Education unanimously approved the 2026 North Carolina English Language Arts (ELA) Standard Course of Study (SCOS) for implementation in the 2027-2028 school year. To support successful implementation of these standards, the NCDPI ELA Team has created Unpacking Documents to deepen the understanding of the NC ELA SCOS and its Fundamental Principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center, 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling, and 3) Variety of Texts.

The Unpacking Documents include the ELA Standards and Objectives as well as a glossary, clarifications, standards-based question stems and sentence frames, classroom considerations, and supplemental resources.  Please note that these unpacking documents are not meant to be exhaustive.

Because North Carolina is a local-control state, all curricular decisions (e.g., texts, tasks, activities, etc.) are approved at the local level. Prior to implementing any of the materials in this resource, teachers should follow appropriate policies and procedures to have their curriculum vetted and approved by their school and district leaders.

Acknowledgements: The NCDPI ELA Team consulted with educators in the field as well as the Offices of Early Learning and Exceptional Children during the creation of this resource.

Questions? Contact the NCDPI ELA Team.

CT.1
Overview and Purpose

The Complex Text Strand (CT) ensures students are developing into proficient, independent readers and listeners, outlining essential skills that students should be practicing every day, in every lesson, and with every text. This strand deepens students’ understanding of how to become skilled, confident readers who can sustain attention and build stamina with increasingly challenging texts. The CT standards and objectives are designed to speak to the complex nature of reading comprehension and provide all educators with a deeper understanding of skills students should engage in routinely to access and comprehend grade-level complex texts.

In order to place text at the center of ELA teaching and learning, CT standards and objectives should be integrated across the other two strands of the NC ELA SCOS— Comprehension Development (CD) and Communication and Writing (CW)— when designing standards-aligned instruction.  This integration ensures that students practice skills within the context of grade-appropriate complex texts.  As students apply these skills across a variety of such texts, they demonstrate the ability to independently construct meaning across language domains.

Through the CT Strand, students build knowledge and strengthen comprehension by reading and listening to a range of worthy, content-rich texts. This includes students
* applying a range of strategies to monitor, support, and deepen comprehension
* drawing on a wide range of reading and listening experiences to build knowledge and vocabulary
* transferring techniques from the texts to their own evolving forms of communication and writing
* Independently and proficiently reading/listening to grade-level complex texts within the 6-8 text complexity band

These CT standards and objectives reflect critical, research-based components of reading comprehension and reinforce rigorous grade-level expectations across all strands of the NC ELA SCOS. The Complex Texts strand serves as a foundation for standards-based, text-centered instruction in every ELA classroom.
Complex Text Strand and the Fundamental Principles

The Complex Text Strand standards and objectives ensure the fundamental principles of 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy; and 3) a Variety of Texts are living out in the ELA classroom. Designed to be recursive in nature, CT standards and objectives make explicit and implicit references to these fundamental principles to ensure students are engaging with them routinely throughout instruction.
* Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center | Grade-level complex text is the foundation of the CT.1 standard and the primary means through which students engage with the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) to build essential academic competencies. Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation.
* Integrated Model of Literacy | CT.1 objectives support students’ ability to apply and integrate skills across language domains in various contexts and across a range of texts. For example, CT.1.1 requires students to return back to the texts they are reading to ground discussion and writing in textual evidence. CT.1.2 emphasizes repeated readings, writing in response to texts, and collaborative discussion as key drivers of comprehension. CT.1.4 promotes the reading-writing connection, ensuring students engage with language receptively through reading, and transfer that knowledge generatively to their own communication.
* Variety of Texts | Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. CT.1.3 encourages students to read widely to build knowledge and vocabulary across a range of topics, themes, and concepts.

A note on text selection and local control

North Carolina is a local-control state. Because of this, the North Carolina Department of Public Instruction (NCDPI) does not provide a recommended text list, as curricular decisions are made at the PSU (public school unit) level. NCDPI cannot endorse specific curricula or instructional materials for local use. The standards do not restrict or preclude the teaching of specific modes, types, genres, mediums, and formats of text.

PSUs should carefully analyze potential  texts for each grade by examining the qualitative, quantitative, and reader and task measures to ensure students engage with  increasingly complex texts. Through text complexity analysis, educators can determine when a particular mode, type, genre, medium, format of text best supports the instructional purpose. Multiple approaches are appropriate and should be used in alignment with the standards to ensure students experience a range of texts and text types that prepare them for postsecondary opportunities.

Classroom Considerations
* Carefully select high-quality texts by ensuring the text is appropriately complex for the grade-level. Determine overall text complexity using a variety of measures including qualitative, quantitative, and reader & task considerations. Educators should examine and weigh all three measures equally; one is not more important than another, and all three combined provide the most accurate level of complexity.
* Plan daily extensive reading instruction using increasing complex texts at the high end of the 6-8 text complexity band, strategically integrating intentional entry points that support student access while preserving the full complexity of the text. Ensure students are exposed to a variety of text modes, types, genres, structures, and formats.
* Use think-alouds as a primary strategy to model how proficient readers make meaning from grade-level complex texts, explicitly demonstrating how to navigate challenging vocabulary, structures, and ideas. Follow this modeling with guided practice that allows students to apply these processes with support, gradually releasing responsibility as they demonstrate increasing independence.
* Equip students with tools that build their capacity to independently navigate grade-level, complex texts by teaching them to recognize when meaning breaks down, ask and answer questions as they read/listen to monitor their comprehension, and reflect on what they have read/heard. Provide regular opportunities for students to practice these behaviors so they become self-directed, strategic readers/listeners. Support students’ metacognition by engaging them before, during, and after reading to reflect on what they are learning and how they are making meaning from the text.
* Adolescents must learn from, comprehend, and analyze texts which, compared to texts in earlier grades, are significantly longer and more complex at the word, sentence, and structural levels. As this complexity increases grade-to-grade, these texts present greater conceptual challenges and demand a much higher level of information synthesis. As students move into secondary grades, research-based adolescent literacy strategies should be used to teach the general content literacy skills students need to access grade-level complex texts and engage with the discipline-specific reading, writing, and discussing skills in English Language Arts. For more guidance, research, and supports on adolescent literacy, see Keys to Literacy’s Adolescent Literacy: A Guide.

8.CT.1.1 Cite the most relevant textual evidence to support analysis of what a text states as well as inferences drawn from a text.

This is a continued emphasis from grade 7, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 8. Grade 8 students continue to closely examine the text to locate and use specific details and examples to support interpretations and analysis of what the author/speaker says directly and what can be reasonably concluded or indirectly implied from the text. Students refer to multiple details or pieces of evidence in a text to support their analysis. In grade 8, students build on prior citation skills by ensuring they are selecting the strongest and best evidence to support their analysis.

Students…
* analyze what a text says explicitly/directly
* analyze what a text says implicitly/indirectly
* cite multiple pieces of relevant textual evidence to support analysis

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	* What information is clearly given in this section related to the main idea/theme of ___?
* What does this detail suggest about ___? What can you figure out even though it is not directly stated?
* Where in the text did you find multiple pieces of evidence for your idea? What quotes or details best support your thinking?
* Which evidence most strongly supports the idea/theme of ___?
	* In the text, the author/speaker clearly states that ___. This supports the analysis that ___.
* The author/speaker implies that ___ by showing/saying/explaining ___. This supports the analysis that ___.
* The text says ___. Another example from the text is ___. These details support my idea that ___.
* The evidence that most strongly connects and supports the idea/theme of ___ is ___.

Classroom Considerations
* Teach and support students with text annotations for evidence tracking. Show students how to: mark key ideas, highlight places in the text, use margin notes, etc. to track important details as they read for specific purposes. For secondary students, encourage annotation of common interpretive cues in literary and informational texts to support not only initial comprehension but also analytical annotation.
* Plan instruction for close reading that allows students to respond to text-dependent questions that ask them both what the text states directly (what a text states) and what it says indirectly (inferences drawn from text)
* Through modeling and explicit instruction, show students that an inference comes from combining text clues with what they already know, not guessing. Think aloud to show students how to notice clues in the text and turn them into inferences.
* Support students in citing multiple pieces of textual evidence by modeling how to select strong examples from the text, including both direct quotations and brief summaries of key events or ideas. Students should be encouraged to return to the text to locate their evidence and include page numbers so others can easily find the same information. In grade 8, students should be analyzing text for deeper meaning and craft. Help students clearly connect their evidence to their ideas about text by building in regular time for writing and discussion where students must point to the text and explain how their evidence supports their ideas. Focus on citing evidence for analytical purposes by creating frequent opportunities for students to practice explaining why a piece of evidence matters and can be unpacked for a deeper meaning, not just identifying where it appears in the text.
* Grade 8 students are expected to gather multiple relevant pieces of evidence to support analysis. When analyzing a text, students should understand that they cannot rely on just one example or detail to support their ideas. Instead, they should learn to gather multiple pieces of evidence from the text, including several quotations, details, and examples that work together to strengthen their analysis. Using a variety of evidence helps confirm interpretations, demonstrates close reading, and adds depth and clarity to students’ thinking. Teachers should set clear expectations for how much evidence students must include in their work and guide them in selecting and connecting strong examples from across the text. To support students with relevant evidence, teach students to select the most meaningful and appropriate details from the text to make their points. Model how to choose the strongest evidence– not just the first example students find– by comparing weak and strong examples to clearly support a point or interpretation.

8.CT.1.2 Use self-monitoring, repeated reading, objective summarizing, writing in response to texts, and discussions with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension.

This is a continued emphasis on these comprehension supports from grade 7, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 8. Students utilize various supports when reading a text to strengthen comprehension. These include:
* using self-monitoring strategies to gauge and repair where their comprehension may break down
* re-reading texts multiple times for various purposes (including understanding) and levels of meaning
* summarizing the main points of what has been read, and
* engaging in writing and discussion about texts to communicate understanding and collectively reinforce learning.
Each of these approaches ensure students are accessing and comprehending grade-level complex texts with increased independence.

Students…
* use self-monitoring to strengthen comprehension
* use repeated reading to strengthen comprehension
* use summarizing to strengthen comprehension
* use writing in response to texts to strengthen comprehension
* use discussions with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension

Classroom Considerations
* Foster students’ active engagement with self monitoring their comprehension, explicitly teaching and supporting students to flexibly use a repertoire of comprehension strategies.
* Follow the principle of gradual release to ensure students use strategies independently.
* Provide explicit instruction and opportunities for extended discussion on text meaning and interpretation, including opportunities for whole-class interactive read-alouds and performing close reading of complex text.
* Support students in writing in response to reading for a variety of purposes, including to summarize, analyze, interpret, and apply information from texts they have read.

8.CT.1.3 Draw on a wide reading of texts, including texts on the Holocaust and genocide, to build knowledge and vocabulary.

This is a continued emphasis on wide reading to build knowledge and vocabulary from grade 7, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 8. In grade 8, students read/listen to a variety of texts on a given subject or topic–both teacher determined or student selected–in order to expand their knowledge, understanding, and vocabulary. Students draw on this information to expand their comprehension of content being studied in class and to better access grade-level complex texts. At a given point in grade 8, students read/listen to and engage with texts on the Holocaust and genocide to continue to build their understanding of these topics.

Students…
* draw on a wide range of texts to build knowledge generally
* draw on a wide range of texts to build vocabulary generally
* draw on a wide range of texts about genocide and the Holocaust to build knowledge and vocabulary

Classroom Considerations
* Provide text sets connected by topic/theme and ensure they include a variety of text types, genres, modes, formats, mediums, and/or lengths. Texts in the set should reflect a range of complexity levels so students can build the knowledge and vocabulary needed to access the grade-level text being studied. Consider potential models for pairing and sequencing texts in a set depending on the instructional purpose and task.
* To increase the impact of wide reading, regularly activate, connect, build, and extend students’ prior knowledge to help them bridge new learning with what they already understand and have studied. Doing so improves comprehension, supports vocabulary development, and increases engagement by making content more accessible. Writing and discussion strategies can be incorporated into instruction to help uncover what students know before introducing new material or text (e.g. anticipation guides, cloze, concept sorts, ABC brainstorming, quick writes, etc).
* Build and maintain a robust classroom library that offers students a wide range of texts–diverse authors, high-interest topics, both informational and literary works. etc. Consider organizing and promoting student selection of texts from a topic-focused collections to help students reinforce and extend learning from class through independent reading.
* Provide regular, protected time for wide reading to allow students to explore multiple texts on the same topic. When applicable, use this time to encourage student choice within a topic to boost motivation while simultaneously supporting knowledge and vocabulary growth. Model habits of wide reading for students by doing brief book talks, highlighting how reading multiple texts deepens understanding of a topic, and allowing time for students to talk, write, or reflect about what they are learning from their reading. Explicitly draw students’ attention to how their existing banks of knowledge are expanded through additional reading about a given topic and linking newly learned information or realizations to previously established knowledge.
* Collaborate with a librarian or media specialist to help identify digital resources, databases, and multimedia that broaden the range of texts students can draw on. Involve these specialists in helping students learn how to locate, evaluate, and select texts that support their knowledge building. Consider how research or independent reading tasks can be used to get students to read more widely on a topic being studied.
* Support student vocabulary development as they read by following a comprehensive vocabulary framework that promotes word learning in a multifaceted way: teach high-impact Tier II and Tier III words related to a specific topic/text, equip students with strategies for figuring out unfamiliar words when they encounter them, foster curiosity about language by studying the way words work and relate, and provide rich and varied written and spoken language experiences so students encounter new vocabulary in meaningful contexts. Together, these components help students build stronger, more flexible word knowledge as they read.
* Provide students with a carefully chosen range of literary and informational texts that focus on the Holocaust and other genocides, ensuring content is age-appropriate. Include personal narratives, historical accounts, and literary depictions to help students build knowledge of key events, historical context, and the experiences of individuals. Texts should also introduce relevant vocabulary and concepts, support critical thinking, and foster empathy while developing students’ ability to draw connections across multiple sources. Learn more about the NC Holocaust Education Act and explore sample curriculum and supporting resources by visiting the NCDPI webpage.

8.CT.1.4 Recognize the choices and features used by authors and transfer those techniques to communication and writing.

This is a continued emphasis on recognizing and transferring techniques from grade 7, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 8. In grade 8, students notice and learn from how authors write and craft texts, using those same techniques to improve their own communication and writing. Students determine how an author’s choices impact a text and its effects on readers, looking at elements and features such as: literary and rhetorical elements, literary and rhetorical techniques, word choice, figurative language, text features, etc. Students apply similar techniques to their own writing or speaking, developing the style, complexity, and individual voice of their own communication based on their specific purpose and task.

Students…
* recognize the choices and features used by authors
* transfer those techniques to communication and writing

Classroom Considerations
* Support students with identifying and reflecting on the impact of choices authors make in texts to enhance their craft and style. These text choices and features should include elements such as literary and rhetorical elements, literary and rhetorical techniques, word choice, figurative language, text features, etc.
* Plan instruction that integrates skills across language domains– reading, writing, speaking/listening, and language– so students can notice techniques in texts they are reading/listening/viewing and practice applying them in their writing and speaking in various contexts.
* Use mentor texts and supports for quality writing to help students analyze and emulate in their own communication. Select high-quality texts that clearly demonstrate different techniques across genres and styles so students develop a broader understanding of what communication can look like catered to a variety of purposes and audiences.
* Ensure instruction includes both interpretive (analysis) and expressive (creation) opportunities for students, so they can both understand and examine texts (to support reading/listening comprehension), as well as transfer techniques when producing original texts as authors/speakers with specific intentions of their own. Interpretive tasks focus on how students understand, analyze, and interpret information they receive through reading, listening, or viewing. Expressive tasks focus on how students communicate their own thoughts, ideas, and feelings through speaking, writing, or representing. Explore The Writing Guidebook and The Discourse Playbook for additional guidance and ideas for pairing reading with writing, speaking, and listening.

8.CD.F.3
Glossary
	Morphology - The study of word structure and how words are formed by looking at their meaningful units (morphemes) — prefixes, roots, and suffixes (e.g., mis-spell-ing).

Syllabication - The act of breaking words into syllables.

Text(s) - Texts are generated by authors to communicate to readers. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres or modalities (e.g., visual, auditory, aural, linguistic, gestural, spatial, etc.). Texts may be printed on paper or published in digital forms.

Word Knowledge - The ability to recognize, understand, and use words effectively, aiding in making meaning from text (comprehension). Word knowledge includes vocabulary and morphological awareness.

Word Recognition - The ability to automatically recognize a previously-learned word.

Apply K-8 word recognition and word knowledge skills to comprehend texts at the high end of the 6-8 text complexity band.
* 8.CD.F.3.1 Apply knowledge of syllabication to support word recognition.
* 8.CD.F.3.2 Apply knowledge of morphology to support word knowledge.

Clarification

This is a continued emphasis from grade 7, but with texts that are appropriately complex for grade 8. In grade 8, students recognize, pronounce, understand, and use their knowledge of words to help comprehend grade 6-8 complex text. Grade 8 students build on prior phonics skills learned in grades K-7 to recognize and comprehend longer, multisyllabic words in increasingly complex texts. To support automatic word recognition, students break down words into smaller, manageable, familiar syllables rather than sound out each letter. To support their word knowledge, students use their understanding of word parts (i.e., roots, prefixes, suffixes) to comprehend or make meaning of words they may or may not have previously learned.

Adolescent Literacy
	As students progress through the secondary grades, instruction should place less emphasis on learning how to read words and more continued emphasis on efficiently recognizing familiar words and using word knowledge (morphology, syllable patterns, and meaning) to tackle increasingly complex texts and their academic vocabulary. Automatic word recognition frees cognitive space for comprehension, allowing students to focus on meaning rather than decoding. Strong word knowledge and recognition continue to directly support understanding of increasingly complex texts across disciplines and are an essential part of ensuring students continue to grow these skills throughout their secondary education. For more guidance, research, and supports on adolescent literacy, see Keys to Literacy’s Adolescent Literacy: A Guide.

Classroom Considerations
8.CD.F.3.1 Apply knowledge of syllabication to support word recognition.

Considerations
	Examples
	Ensure students understand that a focus on syllabication in the upper grades builds on prior learning and is intended to support flexible, strategic word recognition of increasingly complex, uncommon, or specialized multisyllabic words as text complexity increases. Define word recognition for students and explain that strong word recognition supports fluent reading by freeing cognitive resources for making meaning, which in turn strengthens comprehension of complex texts. Reinforce that syllables are organized around vowel sounds and that skilled readers may try multiple divisions, drawing on familiar syllable patterns, morphemes, and context to arrive at accurate pronunciation.

Planning for Instruction: Prior to instruction, identify a few multisyllabic words in grade-level complex texts that are key to comprehension that students may struggle understanding. Regularly utilize a routine such as Flexible Word Chunking to practice syllabication and morphological analysis. Provide students with multiple opportunities to practice this routine in the context of reading grade-level texts to integrate syllable instruction with the study of morphemes. Monitor student progress with reading multisyllabic words in order to identify students who would benefit from additional intervention.
	Flexible Word Chunking: This strategy helps students use syllabication and morphological analysis flexibly. Word chunking supports stronger reading comprehension, especially when encountering academic and content-area texts. Once modeled, students can begin to use this as a strategy when independently reading. As students become familiar with this routine, syllables, and morphemes over time, ask them for ideas on how to break apart a word to correctly read it.

8.CD.F.3.2 Apply knowledge of morphology to support word knowledge.

Considerations
	Examples
	Ensure students understand that a focus on morphology in the upper grades is meant to support their word knowledge— noticing not only how words are spelled and pronounced, but what they mean, how they’re related, and how they’re built of smaller parts. Define morphology/morphemes for students and explain their role in supporting fluent reading and, ultimately, comprehension of complex texts. Support students with strategies to utilize morphology to increase their comprehension when it breaks down while reading.
	Example 1:
Inside, Outside, Beyond Strategy: This strategy helps students analyze the meaning of unknown or unfamiliar words when reading. Note that the first step, “inside the word,” relies on looking at a word’s morphemes. Model and practice the routine for students to scaffold them toward independence.
	Support students in understanding when they encounter an unknown or unfamiliar word, they can use knowledge of morphology and individual word parts (i.e., roots, prefixes, suffixes) to determine the larger meaning of a term. This is helpful for students reading complex or difficult texts because they use an increased number of multisyllabic and discipline-specific words, a large majority of which are derived from Old-English, Latin, and Greek.
	Example 2:
Word Origins Anchor Chart: This sample anchor chart lists origin languages of modern English words and provides examples. Co-create a similar resource with students to use when introducing, practicing, or reviewing word origins. This will help students understand how morphemes lead to common meanings.
	Planning for Instruction:  Identify a few morphologically rich words in grade-level complex texts that are key to comprehension or the topic/concept being studied. Explicitly teach and review the most common or relevant prefixes, Greek and Latin roots, and suffixes as part of regular vocabulary instruction, but also point out word parts to students as they naturally appear in the context of reading grade level texts. Provide explicit instruction on what a prefix, root, and suffix are and ensure students are able to isolate them in multisyllabic words.
	Example 3:
Sample Morphological Analysis Routine: This routine helps students use meaningful word parts to figure out the meaning of unfamiliar or complex words. By analyzing morphemes, students build a flexible understanding of vocabulary rather than relying on rote memorization of words. This supports stronger reading comprehension, especially when encountering academic and content-area texts. Once modeled, students can begin to use this as a strategy when independently reading.
	Provide students with multiple opportunities to practice morphological analysis in the context of reading grade-level texts. For example, practice deconstructing words into meaningful word parts, and using that knowledge to determine meaning. Note to students this strategy can help provide the gist but not always an exact definition of a word. Students can also practice building words, exploring different combinations of morphemes. Finally, students can use morphemes to build out word families, introducing a word that appears in a classroom text and brainstorming related words to support increased understanding. Monitor student progress with morphology practice in order to identify students who would benefit from additional intervention.
	Example 4:
Word Matrix Boxes: This routine helps students practice building words using morphemes. Students create real words by selecting one prefix, the root, and one suffix, then read the word, check its meaning, and use it in a sentence. This routine strengthens students’ ability to decode, encode, and understand multisyllabic Greek and Latin–derived words by highlighting how meaningful parts combine to form new words.

Adolescent Literacy
	As students move into secondary grades, instruction should place less emphasis on decoding with accuracy and automaticity and more continued emphasis on adjusting rate, phrasing, and expression based on genre, discipline, and task to support deep comprehension and analysis. Secondary students should continue to practice and refine their oral reading fluency, with particular attention to navigating increasingly complex syntax as text complexity rises across the grades. Strong oral reading fluency continues to directly support understanding of increasingly complex texts across disciplines and is an essential part of ensuring students continue to grow their skills throughout their secondary education. For more guidance, research, and supports on adolescent literacy, see Keys to Literacy’s Adolescent Literacy: A Guide.

Classroom Considerations
8.CD.F.4.1 Read texts for a variety of purposes.

Considerations
	Examples
	Ensure students understand the reasons and benefits of reading the same text multiple times for a variety of purposes. Rereading a piece of text with a different reason or lens each time will often lead to increased understanding. Multiple reads of an excerpt or passage also provide a useful structure for pairing fluency practice with reading comprehension. Reading the same passage several times can build fluency, but if not structured well, it can be perceived as a dull and discouraging task, especially for older students. Students use knowledge, strategies, and processes related to grammar and sentence structure– looking specifically at the arrangement of words, phrases, and clauses– to comprehend what a sentence is saying. Grade 8 students can summarize and paraphrase the main ideas of complex or grammatically convoluted sentences found in grade-level texts.    In grades K-5, syntactical awareness  is taught as part language comprehension because students are still learning how sentences work—how words are ordered, how simple sentences are formed, and how punctuation guides meaning—so they can decode and read smoothly. As students move into secondary grades, texts use longer, more complex sentences with multiple clauses, embedded phrases, and varied structures, so syntax shifts into a key comprehension skill: students must analyze how a sentence is built in order to interpret the author’s meaning, follow complex ideas, and make sense of academic or literary writing. Because of this, CD.4.5 begins in grade 6 and remains through grade 12 as part of the Comprehension Development (CD) strand.

Students...
* interpret the syntax of phrases to comprehend texts within the 6-8 text complexity band
* interpret the syntax of clauses to comprehend texts within the 6-8 text complexity band
* interpret the syntax of sentences to comprehend texts within the 6-8 text complexity band

Classroom Considerations
* Build students’ knowledge and academic vocabulary around syntax and related concepts. Ensure students can define and understand what syntax is and its relationship to sentences, phrases and clauses.
   * Emphasize that phrases and clauses are units of communication separately and that they are combined in sentences to communicate meaning as a whole. Ensure students know that phrases are groups of words that function as a single part of speech in a sentence without a subject-verb pair and that they cannot stand alone. Common phrase types to draw students’ attention to include:
      * noun phrases, which include a noun and its modifiers, typically structured with the modifiers preceding or following the noun;
      * verb phrases, which include a main verb and any auxiliaries, typically structured with auxiliaries before the main verb;
      * verbal phrases, which include verbs functioning as other parts of speech—infinitives, participles, or gerunds—and are typically structured with the verb form plus any objects or modifiers;
      * prepositional phrases, which include a preposition and its object, typically structured with the preposition first and the object noun or pronoun last, with any modifiers in between;
      * adjective phrases, which include an adjective and its modifiers, typically structured with modifiers before or after the adjective;
      * adverbial phrases, which include an adverb and its modifiers, typically structured with modifiers surrounding or preceding the adverb, often describing verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs;
      * absolute phrases, which include a noun and a participle with any modifiers, typically structured as a detached phrase that adds detail or context to the main clause.
   * Similarly, ensure that students know that clauses are groups of words that include a subject and a predicate and function as units in sentences and that they can stand alone or work within larger sentences. Common clause types to draw students’ attention to include:
      * independent clauses, which include a subject and a predicate, typically structured to express a complete thought that can stand alone;
      * dependent/subordinate clauses, which include a subject and a predicate, typically structured with a subordinating conjunction that makes it unable to stand alone;
      * relative adjective clauses, which include a subject and predicate and functions as an adjective, typically structured with a relative pronoun introducing the clause;
      * relative adverb clauses, which include a subject and predicate and functions as an adjective, typically introduced by a relative adverb like where, when, or why;
      * adverbial clauses, which include a subject and predicate and functions as an adverb, typically structured with a subordinating conjunction showing time, reason, condition, or contrast;
      * noun clauses, which include a subject and predicate and functions as a noun, typically structured with words like that, what, who, or whether introducing the clause.
   * Support students in understanding that sentence types are defined by how independent and dependent clauses are combined, and analyzing these patterns helps students interpret emphasis, hierarchy of ideas, and relationships such as cause, contrast, and condition within a text. Sentence types to draw students’ attention to include:
      * Simple sentences, which consist of a single independent clause. Although they may include multiple phrases or compound subjects or verbs, they express one complete idea. Drawing attention to simple sentences supports students in identifying core ideas and recognizing moments of emphasis or clarity in a text.
      * Compound sentences, which consist of two or more independent clauses joined by coordinating conjunctions or appropriate punctuation. Analyzing compound sentences helps students notice how writers link related ideas of equal importance and track shifts or continuations in meaning across clauses.
      * Complex sentences, which consist of one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses. Examining complex sentences supports comprehension by helping students recognize how writers signal relationships such as cause and effect, time, condition, or contrast, and how supporting information is subordinated to a main idea.
      * Compound-complex sentences, which consist of two or more independent clauses and at least one dependent clause. Analyzing these sentences builds students’ capacity to unpack layered syntax, trace multiple ideas across a sentence, and understand nuanced relationships that contribute to meaning in more complex texts.
* Engage students regularly in syntax analysis practice to develop their awareness of how sentence structure contributes to both understanding and communicating meaning. Utilize a common, structured routine, such as the Juicy Sentences Protocol, to build student familiarity with analysis. Identify rich sentences from grade level texts for teacher-guided or modeled practice. These could include sentences that are complex, contain a large number of ideas, may have unusual word order, or contain stylistic aspects unique to a specific subject, time period, etc.
* Explicitly teach, model, and practice strategies students can use when reading a grade level text and their comprehension breaks down at the sentence, clause, or phrase level. For example, create an anchor chart for students they can reference when a sentence feels confusing. Possible tips may include: find the main idea of the sentence (look for the main subject and the main verb, ignoring extra words that interrupt them), remove extra dependent clauses (“mini-sentences” inside the big sentence that add details), ignore extra add-ons (such as extra adjectives, adverbs, side comments, etc.), try to divide the sentence into smaller sentences (replacing semicolons, conjunctions, and other clause joiners with periods).

Classroom Considerations
* Ensure students can define and understand the importance of remaining civil and collegial in their discourse. Civil discourse is a conversation in which there is a mutual airing of views without hostility or hate. Civil discourse is not a contest, rather its intended goal is to promote greater understanding. Collegial discourse creates an environment where students are respectful and polite to others and are safe to say what they honestly think without fear of  facing ridicule, hostility, or harsh/negative judgment. By being open to other perspectives and remaining professional, students can have deeper conversations about the subjects they’re studying. Utilize anchor charts, discussion norms, or brief mini-lessons to help students see what these behaviors look and sound like in practice. One way to start is by having students watch other people debate contentious topics in a peaceful fashion. This is an effective way of modeling the kind of discourse you are trying to promote. * To support effective collaborative discussions, involve students in co-creating norms or expectations. This process builds buy-in, strengthens student agency, and increases accountability.  Support students with this process by having them consider larger ideas that directly impact the qualities of effective collaborative discussion (e.g., respecting the opinions of others, listening to understand, participation, quoting accurately when explaining what the text states, coming to discussions prepared, etc.)
* Scaffold students toward greater independence and autonomy in their collaboration and discussion. In prior grades, students may have been assigned specific roles (e.g., facilitator, time keeper, recorder, summarizer, clarifier, connector, etc.). Provide greater ownership by having students come up with their own discussion roles relevant to specific tasks and contexts. Successful discourse with peers should also increasingly include effective group decision making. Use resources like a collaboration rubric, group agreements, Start-Stop-Continue reflections/check-ins, etc. to provide students structures to own their engagement with classmates.
* Provide explicit instruction and a range of opportunities for students to have extended discussion on text meaning and interpretation by using whole-class, teacher-led group discussions, as well as small-group, student-led group discussions. Classroom Considerations
* Grade 8 students are expected to incorporate additional research into their collaborative discussions. When planning instruction, provide students with a discussion topic/focus and question and give them time to explore and locate information to  inform their discussion. Example tasks could include role-based research discussion where students research the topic from an assigned role (e.g., scientist, historian, community member) and during discussion speak from that perspective. Students could also complete Jigsaw research discussions, where a topic/focus is divided into subtopics or perspectives. Dialectical journals support close reading by pairing specific quotes from the text with analysis or questions, giving students clear, text-based evidence they can draw on during discussion. These tasks also reinforce key skills for students, including the ability to cite evidence, quote, paraphrase, and summarize.
* To support students with evidence-based discussions, plan for instruction that includes interpretive questions that allow students to deeply probe or analyze ideas in the text. Interpretive questions require students to look at multiple lines and passages of reading and give rise to a number of related questions about an author’s meaning. A successful interpretive question should allow students to reference the text multiple times in various places and lead to sustained discussion. A set of interpretive questions require students to collaboratively build a comprehensive understanding of text meaning by integrating answers and understandings across the course of a discussion. For qualities and an example of a set of probing interpretive questions, see the Supports for Evidence-Based Discussion Questions resource.

* To support students with actively processing a discussion, model and have students practice tracking key points made during discussions using a note-taking method.  Consider methods like graphic organizers, thinking maps, or Cornell Notes. This skill will allow students to more effectively acknowledge what their peers are sharing, as well as offer opportunities for reflection after a discussion. Ensure students also practice strong paraphrasing and summarizing skills. See Recommendation 2 of The Academic Discourse Playbook for additional note-taking and summary writing methods that will also support students during discussion.
* When planning instruction, invest instructional time in having students not only prepare and engage in discussions, but metacognitively reflect on how discourse with peers changed, grew, or reinforced their understanding. Build in short, guided reflection routines into learning, such as Turn and Talk, or use exit tickets, discussion debriefs, learning logs, etc., to ask students to paraphrase a peer’s point and explain how it influenced their thinking after more formal discussions. Allowing students to see their thinking shift over time helps them recognize discourse as a learning process and builds metacognitive skills that strengthen future discussions.
* Grade 8 students are expected to justify opinions shared during discussions, and qualify the limits of their thinking when appropriate. Ensure students can define these two qualities and understand why they are essential for productive discourse, both allowing conversations to remain evidence-based, reasoned, and flexible.    * To justify an opinion, students can refer back to a text or other research to support their views. When contributing to a discussion, students should always be prepared and proactive in offering evidence.
   * To qualify an opinion, students should be able to explain the conditions, limits, or exceptions of what they believe instead of presenting their view as always or completely true. Students should be able to acknowledge that their views may not apply in every situation, recognize other perspectives or evidence offered by peers, and show openness to revising their thinking.
   * Support students with qualifying by asking them to argue or articulate opposing views from their own or placing their perspectives in additional contexts. Scaffold this skill by providing students with language that signals qualifying, such as: in some cases, often/sometimes /usually, may/might/can, tends to, under certain conditions, depending on, to some extent, etc.
* Provide explicit instruction and opportunities for students to have extended discussion on text meaning and interpretation by using whole-class, teacher-led group discussions, as well as small-group, student-led group discussions. Vary the formality, structure, format, and purpose of these opportunities to provide students with multiple means to practice applying their communication skills. Example protocols/tasks that allow students to acknowledge perspectives of peers and respond include Fishbowls, Get Off the Fence, Four Corners, concentric circles, etc. See Recommendation 1 of The Academic Discourse Playbook for additional examples.

Classroom Considerations
* Focus grammar instruction on a functional and rhetorical level, instead of solely focusing on memorizing the grammatical terms/concepts themselves. Consider a more student-centered approach to grammar instruction by teaching it in context, considering how it can be taught synchronously with the texts being read and produced by students. To teach grammar and usage in context, consider the following, in order:
   * Student-Level Context: Examine student writing to determine the grammar/usage concept that will benefit students.
   * Text-Level Context: Identify a mentor text (sentence, paragraph, or larger excerpt) that shows the grammar/usage concept in use. Study and discuss how the grammar/usage concept is used in the text to achieve a particular goal or effect.
   * Writing/Speaking-Level Context: Have students practice applying the grammar/usage concept to their own communication via a prompt, shared writing, scenario and task, etc. Ask and expect students to transfer their learning of the grammar/usage concept to their larger writing or speaking tasks. For more guidance on teaching grammar in context, see Recommendation 3 of The Writing Guidebook.
* Teach students the various grammar/usage concepts appropriate to whatever type of communication or writing they are doing, studying, or reading, providing a rich and varied array of examples of how these concepts are used in real-world texts and by authentic writers and speakers. Provide students mentor texts and models of quality writing that illustrate how certain constructions, grammar conventions, and usage rules are applied in practice.
* Utilize the following practices when designing explicit instruction on grammar and usage:
   * Help students build knowledge on a specific concept by outlining what it is, how it works, and why they should know and use it.
   * Create opportunities for students to practice a specific concept to gauge and refine student understanding (e.g., through mini lessons, etc.).
   * Have students apply knowledge and use a specific concept in an actual piece of writing or communication so they can demonstrate their understanding and receive feedback.
   * Continue to provide feedback and additional opportunities for students to apply their learning of the concept over time in more independent and sophisticated ways. Concepts should not be considered a one-and-done, but something that continues to be applied over time.
* As students master grammar/usage concepts, consider how they can experiment with them to create a unique style and voice. Find ways to intentionally move students from “using grammar correctly” to “using grammar purposefully.” Examples of this could include:
   * helping students see that grammar choices affect tone/mood/voice (e.g., showing how punctuation can change the voice of sentence)
   * having students imitate an author’s style using correct grammar
   * teaching students to adapt their grammar based on what they are trying to do (e.g., persuasive writing using strong/direct sentences or poetic/narrative writing using dashes or parentheticals)
   * providing feedback on effective grammar choices not just mistakes (e.g., “Your use of short sentences here creates tension.”)
   * having students explain their grammar/usage choices via quick reflection prompts to build metacognition (e.g., “Which sentence are you most proud of and why?” or “What grammar move did you try and how did it affect your voice?”).
* Intentionally activate and reinforce grammar and usage concepts from prior grades when introducing new grade-level expectations (e.g., revisiting past, present, and future verb tenses as part of teaching the perfect-progressive verb aspect). This will help students build knowledge cumulatively and connect past learning to more complex concepts. Maintain an ongoing anchor chart or reference list of taught concepts—with clear definitions and student-generated examples— to support students in applying conventions independently and help in providing specific, standards-aligned feedback during writing and speaking tasks, whether that feedback be to peers or themselves as part of the editing process.

To teach conventions in context, consider the following, in order:
   * Student-Level Context: Examine student writing to determine the conventions concept that will benefit students.
   * Text-Level Context: Identify a mentor text (sentence, paragraph, or larger excerpt) that shows the conventions concept in use. Study and discuss how the conventions concept is used in the text to achieve a particular goal or effect.
   * Writing/Speaking-Level Context: Have students practice applying the conventions concept to their own communication via a prompt, shared writing, scenario and task, etc. Ask and expect students to transfer their learning of the conventions concept to their larger writing tasks. For more guidance on teaching grammar in context, see Recommendation 3 of The Writing Guidebook.
* Teach students the various conventions appropriate to whatever type of communication or writing they are doing, studying, or reading, providing a rich and varied array of examples of how these conventions are used in real-world texts and by authentic writers and speakers. Provide students mentor texts and models of quality writing that illustrate how certain constructions, conventions, and usage rules are applied in practice.
* Utilize the following practices when designing explicit instruction on conventions:
   * Help students build knowledge on a specific concept by outlining what it is, how it works, and why they should know and use it.
   * Create opportunities for students to practice a specific concept to gauge and refine student understanding (e.g., through mini lessons, etc.).
   * Have students apply knowledge and use a specific concept in an actual piece of writing or communication so they can demonstrate their understanding and receive feedback.
   * Continue to provide feedback and additional opportunities for students to apply their learning of the concept over time in more independent and sophisticated ways. Concepts should not be considered a one-and-done, but something that continues to be applied over time.
* As students master specific conventions, consider how they can experiment with them to create a unique style and voice. Find ways to intentionally move students from “using grammar correctly” to “using grammar purposefully.” Examples of this could include:
   * helping students see that conventions choices affect tone/mood/voice (e.g., showing how punctuation can change the voice of sentence)
   * having students imitate an author’s style using a specific convention
   * teaching students to adapt their conventions based on what they are trying to do (e.g., persuasive writing using strong/direct sentences or poetic/narrative writing using dashes or parentheticals)
   * providing feedback on effective conventions choices not just mistakes (e.g., “Your use of ellipsis here creates tension.”
   * having students explain their conventions choices via quick reflection prompts to build metacognition (e.g., “Which sentence are you most proud of and why?” or “What conventions did you try and how did it affect your writing?”).
* Intentionally activate and reinforce conventions concepts from prior grades when introducing new grade-level expectations (e.g., revisiting quotation marks and periods when teaching students how to use ellipses for various purposes). This will help students build knowledge cumulatively and connect past learning to more complex concepts. Maintain an ongoing anchor chart or reference list of taught concepts—with clear definitions and student-generated examples— to support students in applying conventions independently and help in providing specific, standards-aligned feedback during writing tasks, whether that feedback be to peers or themselves as part of the editing process.

Concepts and Example Sentences

Sentence Production & Expansion Skills

Produce complete simple, compound, complex, and compound- complex sentence structures

In addition to producing simple, compound, and complex sentences based on prior learning, students need to know that compound sentences can also be formed using a semicolon. Students also need to know that a compound-complex sentence combines multiple independent clauses with at least one dependent clause. To produce compound-complex sentences, students should coordinate independent clauses and attach at least one dependent clause within a single sentence.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Simple Sentence
	My dog waits by the door every afternoon.
	Compound Sentence
	I wanted to go fishing at the lake, but the weather suddenly changed.

	Complex Sentence
	When the sun set behind the mountains, the campers started a fire.
	Compound-Complex Sentence
	After we packed our bags for the road trip, we stopped for snacks, and everyone picked their favorite treats.

Expand complete simple, compound, complex, and compound- complex sentence structures

Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional independent or dependent clauses to simple, compound, and complex sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; compound-complex → compound-complex), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., compound → compound-complex; simple → compound-complex). Students should flexibly use K-8 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Expanded Simple Sentence
	My energetic dog waits patiently by the front door every afternoon.
	Expanded Compound Sentence
	I really wanted to go fishing at the quiet lake, but the weather suddenly changed and became stormy.
	Expanded Complex Sentence
	When the bright orange sun set behind the distant mountains, the tired campers started a warm fire.
	Expanded Compound-Complex Sentence
	After we carefully packed our bags for the long road trip, we stopped at a small gas station for snacks, and everyone picked their favorite sweet and salty treats.

Rearrange complete simple, compound, complex, and compound- complex sentence structures

Students need to know how to move modifiers, phrases, and clauses within simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex sentences to improve flow, coherence, or emphasis and without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that rearranging parts of sentences can improve flow, coherence, and emphasis without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by requiring adjustment to clause structure (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-8 grammar and convention knowledge to rearrange sentences appropriate to the grade-level.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Rearranged Simple Sentence
	Every afternoon, my energetic dog waits patiently by the front door.
	Rearranged Compound Sentence
	The weather suddenly changed and became stormy, but I still really wanted to go fishing at the quiet lake.
	Rearranged Complex Sentence
	As the tired campers started a warm fire, the bright orange sun set behind the distant mountains.
	Rearranged Compound-Complex Sentence
	During the long roadtrip, we stopped at a small gas station for snacks, then everyone put their sweet and salty treats in their carefully packed bags.

Sentence Accuracy & Error Correction Skills

Correct sentence fragments

Students need to know that a fragment is an incomplete thought lacking a subject, predicate, or both. Students need to know that the simple predicate is the main verb or verb phrase that shows the action or state of being and that the complete predicate includes the simple predicate and all the words that modify or complete it, such as objects, complements, and modifiers. Students need to know how to add missing subjects, predicates, or clauses to incomplete sentences to form grammatically complete statements.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Fragment Missing a Subject

	Rushed through the crowded hallway and down the stairs to the science lab.
	Corrected with Added Subject
	The student rushed through the crowded hallway and down the stairs to the science lab.
	Fragment Missing a Complete Predicate
	The exhausted students in the normally noisy cafeteria.
	Corrected with Added Verb/Complete Predicate
	The exhausted students in the normally noisy cafeteria collapsed quietly into their seats after a long day of testing.
	Dependent Clause/Incomplete Thought
	Because we studied all night for the difficult math test and reviewed our notes twice.
	Corrected with Added Independent Clause
	Because we studied all night for the difficult math test and reviewed our notes twice, we felt confident the next morning.

Correct run-on sentences

Students need to know that a run-on occurs when two or more independent clauses are joined improperly. Students need to know that fused sentences are a type of run-on where two or more independent clauses are joined with no punctuation. Students also need to know that comma splices are a type of run-on where two or more independent clauses are joined only with a comma. Students need to know how to separate  independent clauses correctly using end punctuation or join them using conjunctions and commas or semicolons appropriately to form grammatically correct sentences.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Comma Splice Sentence Run-on
	I forgot my homework at home, I tried to explain it to my teacher.
	Corrected by Separating with a Period
	I forgot my homework at home. I tried to explain it to my teacher.
	Fused Sentence Run-on
	We practiced after school every day we still lost the game.
	Corrected by Joining with a Semicolon
	We practiced after school every day; we still lost the game.
	Fused Sentence Run-on
	She stayed up late studying for the test she was exhausted the next morning.
	Corrected by Joining with a Coordinating Conjunction and Comma
	She stayed up late studying for the test, so she was exhausted the next morning.

Classroom Considerations
* Ensure students understand that transcription skills–such as accurate handwriting or typing, spelling, and punctuation–support the fluent production and overall clarity of sentences in written and spoken products. As appropriate (i.e., when transcription patterns in student work interfere with readability or sentence control), provide targeted feedback on the legibility of handwritten and typed products, and support students in using word processing tools efficiently, including increasing awareness of shortcuts, features, and capabilities. Build opportunities for students to practice both handwriting and typing within grade-level tasks, such as sustained drafting or note-taking and extended writing tasks that require students to maintain accuracy and efficiency over time so that transcription becomes increasingly automatic and does not interfere with sentence construction. Attention to transcription should focus on maintaining accuracy, efficiency, and readability, enabling students to concentrate on developing ideas and composing sentences with precision and sophistication.
* Students should first learn to understand and accurately identify the four types of sentence structures– simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex– so they can recognize how sentences function in effective communication. Grade 6 students should be explicitly taught compound-complex sentences, receiving direct instruction on, models of, and practice with this sentence type. Provide opportunities for students to practice producing the four sentence types within the context of their speaking and writing, applying them purposefully to enhance clarity and meaning. Reinforce  learning and skill with sentence structures by explicitly establishing expectations for sentence variety in task rubrics and providing targeted feedback on student drafts and final products. To build students’ knowledge and confidence, craft focused mini-lessons and provide regular opportunities for guided and independent practice so students gain experience with different sentence structures. Instruction should include utilizing strategies  to support students in developing more elaborate and sophisticated sentences, such as: sentence combining (manipulating and rearranging multiple sentences into one), sentence deconstruction (breaking longer sentences down into smaller parts), or generating/expanding an original sentence on a topic using the “W” questions (who, what, when, where, why, which, how).
* Support students with not only recognizing but correcting sentence fragments in their writing and speaking. Scaffolds such as sentence frames and anchor charts can be used to promote students' thinking, writing, and discussing in complete thoughts. Have students complete a sentence checklist or use a routine like the two word sentence strategy to have students take their sentences and drill them down to essential elements: e.g., What is the subject? What is the verb? Has a complete thought been expressed? Point out to students that many fragments come from dependent clauses and teach these words as red flags (e.g., because, when, although, if, since, while, after, before, etc.). Use an anchor chart or rule to help students look for these words in their work and ensure they’re used correctly to create complete sentences.
* Support students with not only recognizing but correcting run-on sentences in their writing and speaking. Help students recognize run-on sentences by using the “two complete sentences” test, in which students ask themselves whether the sentence can be split into two sentences that make sense on their own– encourage students to draw a slash where the sentence could divide and read each part aloud to check for a subject, verb, and complete thought. Instead of simply instructing students to fix their run-ons, provide them with clear options and explicit instruction to address them. Examples include utilizing a period to form one sentence into two, utilizing a semicolon, or using a comma + conjunction (e.g., using the mnemonic FANBOYS) to join ideas. Draft an anchor chart students can reference so they can choose their approach when run-ons are found.

For direct references, explain that when the author’s name/source title is included in the sentence, only the page number (or no page number for many digital sources) is needed afterward. Explain that when the author/source is not named in the sentence, that information is included in parentheses. Support students with modeling and practice in drafting in text citations, including when and where to include them after a source is paraphrased or summarized, versus when a source is directly quoted. Reinforce this expectation through repeated practice across subjects, not just ELA.

Students identify the topic, focus, requirements, and expectations of their product as outlined in an assignment or task. Students select, group, and sequence ideas and information in a way that clearly develops and supports a specific topic. Students revisit and refine their topic, focus, ideas, and plans throughout the composing process as they gain new insights, feedback, and understanding.

Students…
* plan written and spoken products

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	* What does the task ask you to do?
* Who is the audience for this task?
* What information and ideas about your topic do you need to cover? What facts, examples, or details support your topic?
* How can you arrange your ideas/information clearly? How would you outline what comes first, next, and last?
	* The goal of my product is to ___.

Classroom Considerations
* Emphasize to students that planning is an intentional, meaning-making phase of composition rather than a preliminary exercise to be completed and set aside. Students should understand that effective writers/speakers engage in planning to generate ideas, clarify purpose, test thinking, and make decisions about direction, and that these decisions continue to evolve as drafting progresses. When planning assignments and tasks, ensure that instructions and assignment overviews contain specific guidance that encourages students to explicitly consider task, purpose, and audience and consider aligning those considerations to rubrics/scoring methods, as appropriate. Use mnemonics to ensure assignment descriptions account for task, audience, and purpose, e.g.:
   * MMAPS: Mode, Media, Audience, Purpose, and Situation; and
   * RAFT: Role, Audience, Form, Topic.
As students plan, reinforce that writers/speakers deliberately return to planning to address emerging challenges in the composition process–such as refining a focus, reconsidering evidence, or reshaping structure– to strengthen their work.
* Model a range of planning approaches and explicitly connect each strategy to the demands of the task, the writer’s purpose, and the intended audience. To support students in initially recognizing what a given written/spoken task requires, consider introducing the Do/What/How strategy. Model each step for students with sample prompts/tasks and allow time for students to apply the strategy to classroom tasks:
   * Do: Underline the words and phrases that establish what the assignment/task overview is asking students to do.
   * What: Highlight information that explains what topic/idea/consideration the student should write/speak about.
   * How: Place an asterisk (*) beside specific instructions that reveal how the written/spoken product should be crafted.
To support students in initially organizing ideas in preparation for drafting, consider introducing the PLAN strategy. Model each step for students with sample prompts/tasks and allow time for students to apply the strategy to classroom tasks:
* P: Pay attention to the prompt (can include referencing Do/What/How or another approach).
* L: List the central point or concept that needs communicating.
* A: Add supporting ideas from knowledge/text(s).
* N: Number ideas to sequence them effectively.
Instruction in using the Do/What/How and PLAN strategies should make visible how different genres and communicative goals call for different kinds of planning–for example, how a persuasive composition task may benefit from outlining claims and evidence, while a narrative or multimodal task may call for storyboarding or visual mapping. Support students in analyzing the task at hand and selecting planning strategies that help them organize ideas, anticipate audience needs, and maintain focus throughout drafting. Encourage students to develop autonomy in choosing the approaches that best support their individual composition processes rather than requiring a single, uniform method. For guidance on different planning strategies, see Recommendation 3 of The Writing Guidebook.
* Encourage student use of digital organizational tools to support planning, emphasizing how different tools lend themselves to various purposes, including sharing information during collaborative work. Consider introducing students to
   * collating sources, text, materials, etc. via Web browser bookmark folders, digital visual collaboration boards, and digital note-taking, document archiving, and task-management platforms;
   * organizing sources, text, materials, and ideas via digital mind- and concept-mapping platforms.
Model how to select, pare down, and commit to a single comprehensive tool–or a few specialized tools–to streamline planning. As familiarity with various tools and their functionalities increase, provide students autonomy in which digital tools they use to support their planning.

8.CW.6.2 Draft written and spoken products to sufficiently address purpose, task, and audience.

This is a continued emphasis from grade 7. Grade 8 students continue to draft, formulate, and create as part of the cycle of producing and refining written or spoken products. Students create multiple iterations of their work, as needed, to clearly and fully  meet the purpose/goal of the task (e.g., to inform, to explain, to entertain, etc.), follow the requirements and expectations set out in the task/prompt, and appropriately address the intended audience. Students make intentional choices in content, organization, language, and tone to fully meet the goal, assignment, and intended reader/viewer/listener.

Students…
* draft a written or spoken product to sufficiently address a specific purpose
* draft a written or spoken product to sufficiently address a specific task
* draft a written or spoken product to sufficiently address a specific audience

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	* What is your main purpose in this product? What main takeaways or messages do you want your audience to understand?
* What does the task require you to do? Have you followed all parts of the directions– did you address the required format, length, and components?
* Who will read or listen to your product? What may they already know and what will they need to know? How can you adjust your writing/speaking to make this more engaging for your audience?
	* The purpose of my product is to ___. My main ideas/takeaways include ___.
* The task asks me to ___. I have addressed each part of the task by fulfilling the following requirements ___.
* My audience is ___. I have drafted my writing/speaking specifically for this audience in the following ways ___.

Classroom Considerations
* Help students understand drafting as a recursive, flexible, and purposeful process rather than a one-time event. Drafting should be seen as the crucial step of translating ideas/information from planning into formal language, experimenting with organization, structure and voice based on the specific purpose, task, and audience.  Use guided feedback, checklists, and conferencing to help students evaluate whether their draft(s) clearly fulfill the assignment, communicate effectively to the intended audience, and stay focused on its purpose. Explore ways to provide students with freedom and autonomy in how they draft, such as: choosing the order in which they work (e.g., starting with the conclusion, outlining first, freewriting, or drafting key sections), the tools they use (e.g., paper, digital documents, speech-to-text, graphic organizers), and the strategies that best support their thinking. Allowing multiple drafting pathways respects individual learning styles and encourages ownership of the product. Support this autonomy by offering a menu of drafting options and encouraging students to reflect on which methods work best for them.
* As part of the drafting process, ensure students have access to supports for high-quality communication and writing. This could include mentor texts, samples of student work (student or teacher created), and rubrics and checklists that outline what drafts should include. These supports should be used to help students understand key expectations, while also providing high quality examples to inspire and promote creativity. These models should not be used to box students in or limit them to a specific approach, style, or answer. For guidance on utilizing mentor texts, see Recommendation 2 of The Writing Guidebook.
* Embed structured pause points throughout drafting to prompt students to reflect on their progress and make informed decisions about next steps in the composition process. Support students in assessing whether their draft continues to align with their purpose, audience, and intended meaning, and whether additional planning is needed before moving forward. Instruction should pair these pause points with explicit metacognitive strategies, such as self-questioning and teacher think-alouds, that model how writers/speakers recognize uncertainty, misalignment, or stalled thinking as signals to return to pre-writing or planning in order to refine ideas and strengthen the overall composition.
* Support students’ use of digital tools for drafting by explicitly modeling how different platforms can strengthen their work and streamline this part of the process. For documenting sources, introduce tools such as citation generators to help students organize references and practice ethical research. Word processing programs, presentation software, and audio/video tools can be taught as flexible drafting spaces where students revise text, visuals, and recordings over time, reinforcing drafting as a recursive process.

Classroom Considerations
* Ensure students understand the distinction between revision and editing as related but different tasks in the composition process. Emphasize that revision focuses on meaning, structure, and effectiveness (i.e., reconsidering ideas, reorganizing sections, refining evidence, and clarifying relationships among claims), while editing attends to sentence-level correctness, style, and conventions once ideas are largely in place, including considerations for formatting. Help students recognize that revision and editing are recursive rather than linear: writers/speakers may return to revision after editing reveals gaps in logic, or pause revision to edit a key passage for clarity or tone.
* Explicitly model strategies to support revision and editing that demonstrates the conceptual differences between the two. To support revision, consider teaching the RADaR strategy, a mnemonic that outlines key revision-aligned actions:
   * Reorder: Shift the sequence of sentences, paragraphs, sections, ideas, etc. as needed to clarify, emphasize, and/or __ the intended purpose and message;
   * Add: Include or extend additional evidence, commentary, analysis, and/or interpretations to further develop the intended purpose and message;
   * Delete: Remove redundant, distracting, or otherwise unnecessary evidence, commentary, analysis, and/or interpretations in light of other revisions to the product; and
   * Replace: Exchange redundant, distracting, or otherwise unnecessary evidence, commentary, analysis, and/or interpretations with new or insightful material in light of other revisions to the product.
To support editing, consider teaching the CAP strategy, a mnemonic that outlines key editing-aligned actions at the text-, paragraph, and sentence-levels:
* Conventions: Verify overall formatting and style guide adherence → fonts, headings, spacing, margins, citations/references, and consistent document features.
* Accuracy: Ensure each paragraph is internally consistent and free of surface errors → grammar, agreement, capitalization, punctuation.
* Precision: Confirm each sentence is grammatically sound, readable, and polished → spelling, word choice, punctuation, and clarity.
* Support students in engaging with multiple levels of feedback–teacher, peer, and self–to strengthen revision and editing.
   * Provide support through targeted teacher feedback, such as individual or small-group conferences that focus on high-leverage strategies rather than correcting every error. Consider grouping students based on shared instructional needs, patterns of feedback, or stage in the writing process. When working with a small group during a conference, provide other students with clearly defined revision or editing tasks–such as targeted checklists, task cards, or comparative exemplars–to ensure their work remains purposeful and focused.
   * Alternatively, have students engage in structured peer feedback or independent revision/editing tasks aligned to the same instructional goals. Structure peer feedback with clear expectations, using sentence frames, checklists, or focused prompts to guide attention to specific aspects of the draft and prevent feedback from becoming overly general or surface-level.
   * Teach students to engage in self-feedback by using strategies such as color-coding to analyze their own writing for patterns (e.g., claims, evidence, transitions, or recurring errors) and identify next steps independently.
   * Ensure students understand that seeking feedback from teachers, peers, and themselves is a normal and essential part of how writers and speakers develop ideas and refine their work, and that sharing drafts at multiple stages supports growth rather than signaling weakness or failure. For more guidance on feedback, see Recommendation 4 of The Writing Guidebook.
* Support students in using digital tools strategically to strengthen both revision and editing as ongoing, recursive processes. Model how features such as revision history in digital documents allow writers to track changes over time, reflect on decision-making, and revisit earlier drafts when feedback or new insights warrant additional revision. Model and reinforce collaborative feedback practices by using and having students use comments, suggestions, and track-changes tools to signal whether feedback targets ideas and organization (revision) or sentence-level clarity and conventions (editing). Encourage students to use automated tools (e.g., grammar and usage checkers) as diagnostic supports rather than final arbiters, guiding them to evaluate suggested changes for accuracy, purpose, and voice. When appropriate, incorporate add-ons such as voice-recorded feedback to clarify expectations, prioritize next steps, and humanize the feedback process.

Classroom Considerations
* Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with crafting strong introductions that clearly outline their claims and elude to how they will be supported. Support students with understanding the relationship between the elements inherent to a persuasive text: argument, claims, reasons, and evidence.
   * An author’s argument is the central viewpoint or conclusion they want the audience to accept—it answers the question, “What am I trying to convince my audience of overall?”
   * Claims are the individual points or assertions that break that argument into parts, explaining why the author/speaker believes the argument is valid and providing reasons that can be supported with evidence.
   * If claims are the main points that support a position, reasons explain why each claim is valid, and evidence provides the facts, examples, or data that prove those reasons are true.
   * Beginning in grade 8, students should also start addressing counterclaims in their writing/speaking. Help students understand counterclaims can take many forms– direct disagreement, alternative reasons, partial agreement, practical concerns, or value-based beliefs, etc– and understanding the nature of another viewpoint helps respond to it.
A quality introduction makes the argument and claims clear to the audience and begins to address potential counterclaims.
* Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with developing the supporting reasons and evidence for their claims utilizing a range of sources.  Ensure students are selecting and using credible, accurate sources that are trustworthy, reliable, truthful, and correct. Brainstorm qualities that can prove credibility/accuracy and practice finding them in sources
   * For credibility: e.g., the author or organization is named, the information matches or can be confirmed by other sources, the source is recent, the writing or speech is factual, not opinion-based, the source/website ends in trusted domains, etc.
   * For accuracy: e.g., the facts can be verified across multiple sources, data and claims are supported by evidence, information is up to date, the text clearly distinguishes between facts and opinions, and details are consistent and free from errors or contradictions, etc.
* In addition to supporting claims through reliably sourced reasons and evidence, beginning in grade 8 students should also start to incorporate rhetorical techniques into their persuasive writing/speaking. Ensure students understand the common rhetorical appeals, including ethos (how the author shows they are trustworthy, credible, or knowledgeable) logos (how the author uses logic, reasoning, and evidence to support a point), and pathos (how the author uses feelings, values, or emotional language to connect with the audience). Model and practice with students how to identify the appeals in a text and locate specific examples for each in their own writing/speaking, such as: expert opinions, references to qualifications, trustworthy tone, fairness to both sides (ethos); statistics, examples, comparisons, cause/effect, logical explanations (logos); and emotionally charged words, stories/anecdotes, appeals to values, imagery (pathos).
* Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with the organization and flow of their writing and speaking by using language that clarifies the relationships between the information being shared. Show students how language works as a bridge between claims, reasons, evidence, and counterclaims and brainstorm examples they can use in their writing/speaking. For example, teach students signal words, phrases, and clauses that fit their intended relational function:
   * To connect claims to reasons: e.g., because, since, due to the fact that, one reason is, this is important because, etc.
   * To introduce evidence: e.g., for example, for instance, according to, the data shows, the study/author states, etc.
   * To explain how evidence supports a claim: e.g., this shows that, which proves, as a result, therefore, etc.
   * To introduce/acknowledge a counterclaim: e.g., some people argue that, others believe that, while it may be true that, although some believe, etc.
   * To respond to or refute a counterclaim: however, on the other hand, this view overlooks, nevertheless, despite this concern, etc.

Strategies to help students increase cohesion could include: repeating or restating key terms and ideas, incorporating pronouns and nouns to refer back to key information (e.g., this reason, such evidence, these results, etc.), or ending paragraphs by connecting forward (e.g., “This reason shows how uniforms reduce distractions. Another important benefit is their effect on school safety.”).

Example ways to support this could include: provide a mini-lesson on the difference between restating and repeating, showing students how to reword ideas using synonyms and sentence variety (e.g., original claim: School start times should be later. Restated claim: Delaying the start of the school day is a necessary change.). Students then combine this brainstorm into a concluding paragraph. Grade 8 students should also expand their conclusions to include stylistic choices that not only restate their argument but bring emphasis to it. Teach students specific techniques that show emphasis and practice identifying and drafting some examples, such as:
   * using strong language by incorporating assertive verbs and striking a decisive tone
   * reiterating why their argument matters by connecting the topic to a real-world impact or broader significance
   * leave the audience with a clear takeaway that summarizes the core message in a memorable, focused statement
   * end with a memorable line that leaves a lasting impression by creating a vivid image, using repetition, or incorporating another type of figurative language
* When planning for instruction, connect student knowledge from reading, interpreting, and analysing persuasive texts with drafting their own original persuasive communications. Glossary
	Expository Text - A text that seeks to inform, define, demonstrate, review, instruct, and/or explain a topic in detail. Expository texts require an investigation of the topic and evaluates evidence.

Structure - Texts can be organized in different ways depending on their purpose (e.g., narrative, descriptive, poetic, opinion/persuasion, explanatory, expository, informational). Within these broader types, authors use common text structures such as compare and contrast, problem and solution, cause and effect, sequence/chronology, and description. These structures may appear across an entire text or within sections, paragraphs, and individual sentences.

Text(s) - Texts are generated by authors to communicate to readers. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres or modalities (e.g., visual, auditory, aural, linguistic, gestural, spatial, etc.). Texts may be printed on paper or published in digital forms.

Construct spoken and written expository texts to examine a topic and clearly convey ideas, concepts, and information through the selection, organization, and examination of relevant content.

Classroom Considerations
* Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with crafting strong introductions that clearly provide the topic, state the specific focus/sub-topic/category being covered, and outline the information that will be covered in more depth. Beginning in grade 6, students create expository texts that have more depth and focus instead of broadly or generically covering a topic or concept. Encourage more specific focus in expository writing and speaking by helping students narrow topics, clarify purpose, and write with a clear “lens”, rather than trying to explain everything about a subject. For example, help students move from broad to focused by modeling how to shrink a topic step by step using a specific example (e.g., broad: animals, narrowed: endangered animals, focused: why sea turtles are endangered). Explicitly model what focus looks like by comparing two paragraphs: one that briefly lists many facts about a topic versus one that explains fewer facts but with detail, examples, and explanation. Activities such as this will help students see that they should be going deeper, not wider in their topic selection– explaining the “how” and “why,” not just the “what.” Students should use their topic and focus to organize and structure their final product. Organization should not only involve students planning out their sections, paragraphs, heading/subheadings, but also using organizational structures such as chronological order, cause and effect, comparison and contrast, and problem and solution, etc.
* Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with developing the supporting information that explains their topic and focus. Help students expand and explain a topic by adding meaningful, accurate information that directly supports it, rather than making general or unsupported statements in their writing. Brainstorm with students types of information they should be using, including defining and providing examples of: facts, definitions, details, quotations, statistics, anecdotes, comparisons/analogies, etc. Grade 8 students should focus on increasing the relevance of their supporting information, ensuring their details are on-topic, connected, and clarifying. Sample activities could include modeling how to expand one sentence using multiple types of information or color-code facts, definitions, and examples in mentor texts or their own drafts. Grade 8 students should continue to hone this skill by selecting the most relevant information for their writing/speaking. One way to support this would be to have students organize their research notes into categories: e.g., essential (most relevant), helpful but not essential, and interesting but unnecessary. This can be done with sticky notes, a graphic organizer, color coding, etc. before any drafting is started.
* Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with the organization and flow of their writing and speaking by focusing on strong transitions, precise language, and domain-specific vocabulary. Students often understand what they want to say in expository writing but need structured support to express ideas clearly and academically.
   * Teach grade 8 students transitions based on the intended function (e.g., cause/effect, compare/contrast, sequence, example, conclusion, etc.) and consider creating anchor charts with examples for each (e.g., adding: for example…, in addition…; cause/effect: because…, therefore…, as a result…; contrast: however…, on the other hand…, etc.). Grade 8 students should not only utilize clear transitions, but make sure they are varied. Brainstorm with students multiple ways to phrase the same transition and challenge students to diversify their usage in their writing/speaking.
   * For supporting precise language, consider using modeling and think-alouds to show students how to revise vague language (e.g., changing “things” to “factors” or “elements,” or “ a lot” to “numerous,” etc.).
   * Point out to students that often in expository writing/speaking, covering a specific topic involves correctly using subject-specific terms that need to be defined and explained for audiences. Support students with taking a domain-specific word/concept related to their topic and practice writing an audience-friendly definition and example. Students should also understand that based on their topic, they should adjust their communication style and language to fit with the norms of a specific domain. For example, “groups” may become “factions” or “parties” when writing/speaking about social studies/history-related topics. Or “numbers” may become “values” or “data” when writing/speaking about math-related topics.
Beginning in grade 7, teachers should support students with ensuring their writing/speaking is cohesive and that all ideas and concepts work together intentionally, and transition smoothly from one to the next. Strategies to help students increase cohesion could include: repeating or restating key terms and ideas, incorporating pronouns and nouns to refer back to key information (e.g., this reason, such evidence, these results, etc.), or ending paragraphs by connecting forward (e.g., “This reason shows how uniforms reduce distractions. Another important benefit is their effect on school safety.”).
* Through explicit instruction, modeling, practice, and feedback, support students with crafting strong conclusions that restate the topic, and summarize/ reiterate the key supporting information and explanations provided. Grade 8 students should focus on ensuring their conclusions support the rest of their writing/speaking, not only wrapping up their topic but strengthening their explanation in the closing moments with the audience. Help students understand that a conclusion supports when it: returns to the main ideas covered, synthesizes key information (not simply repeats it), and explains why the topic/information matters. An example activity could require students to identify 2–3 key ideas from the body of their text, combine them into one sentence, and explain the overall significance. Sentence frames could be provided to support this, such as: “The most important ideas were ___, ___, and ___. Together, they show ___.” or “Taken together, these examples demonstrate ___.” or “Understanding ___ helps explain ___.”
* When planning for instruction, connect student knowledge from reading, interpreting, and analysing expository texts with drafting their own original expository communications. Provide students with a range of sample mentor texts so they can explore various styles and apply similar techniques to their own writing. As students craft their own products, ensure they engage in a recursive writing process that allows them to plan, draft, receive feedback on, and refine their communications. For more ideas and resources on the use of mentor texts, the writing process, and students transferring techniques to their own writing, see CW.6, The Writing Guidebook, and  our resource on Text Choices and Features.

CW.9
8.CW.9
Glossary
	Descriptive Text - A creative or artistic text that seeks to describe something — an object, person, place, experience, emotion, situation, etc. — to paint a vivid, sensory impression in the mind of the audience.

Figurative Language - A type of descriptive language used to convey meaning in a way that differs from its literal meaning and is often used to add style to written or spoken language or to simplify a complex idea.

Literary Elements - The core building blocks of a text (e.g., plot, setting, theme, characters, mood, allegory, conflict, etc.).

Literary Techniques - The stylistic choices the author makes to add depth, emotion, and meaning using literary elements and figurative language (e.g., foreshadowing, imagery, metaphor, etc.).

Narrative Text - A text that seeks to tell a story—real or imagined.

Construct spoken and written poetic, descriptive, and narrative texts to develop real or imagined experiences, events, or ideas using effective technique, relevant descriptive details, and well-structured sequences.

	Form and use action verbs with -ing, -ed, -s

	Students need to know that regular verbs form the past with -ed, while irregular verbs have unique past forms..

	Use regular and irregular verbs in simple tenses
	Students need to know that regular verbs form their past tense by adding -ed (e.g., walk → walked), while irregular verbs have unique past tense forms that do not follow a predictable pattern (e.g., go → went, eat → ate, am/is/are → was/were). They also need to recognize that simple tenses–past, present, and future–express actions or states at a single point in time.
	Past: We went outside to observe how much the plants had grown.

Present: She goes outside every morning to observe how much the plants have grown.

Future: We will go outside tomorrow to measure the growth of the plants.
	3
	Ensure subject-verb agreement
	Students need to know that singular subjects take singular verbs and plural subjects take plural verbs, even when intervening words or phrases appear between the subject and verb. They also need to know how agreement works with:
* Compound subjects (“and” usually = plural; “or/nor” = verb agrees with nearest subject)
* Indefinite pronouns (singular: everyone, each; plural: few, many; some can be singular or plural depending on meaning)
* Collective nouns (singular when acting as a unit, plural when acting as individuals)
* Inverted sentences (identify the true subject first, e.g., There are many books)
* Titles, sums, or amounts (usually singular, e.g., The United States is large)
	Singular Subject:
The teacher needs to know where you found evidence of this in the text.

Plural Subject:
They need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.

Indefinite Singular:

Indefinite Plural:

Compound Subject:

Singular Collective Noun:

Plural Collective Noun:

Inverted Sentence:

Amount:

	3
	Form and use progressive aspect
	Students need to know the progressive aspect is formed with a past, present, or future form of “be” (i.e., state of being) + a progressive verb “-ing” to show ongoing actions (e.g., is running, were eating, will be fishing). Progressive verbs function to indicate actions in progress.
	Environmentalists are cleaning up the trash that is polluting our oceans.
	4
	Use modal auxiliaries
	Students need to know modal verbs (e.g., can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would, ought to, need to, used to, have to) show ability, possibility, necessity, obligation, or permission. Modals are a specific type of auxiliary verb that add meaning to, and are always followed by, the base form of a main verb.
	People should clean up the trash that is polluting our oceans.
	4
	Form and use perfect aspect
	Students need to know the perfect aspect is formed with a past, present, or future form of “have” + a past participle to show completed actions related to another point in time (e.g., has walked, had eaten, will have fished). Perfect aspect functions to indicate actions that are finished relative to the present or past.
	Oceans currents have greatly affected our weather.
	5
	Form and use perfect progressive aspect
	Students need to know the perfect progressive aspect is formed with a past, present, or future tense of “have” + been + a progressive verb (-ing) to show an action that was ongoing for a period of time leading up to a specific point in time (e.g., has been running, had been eating, will have been fishing). This aspect emphasizes duration in relation to completion or a reference moment, highlighting both the ongoing nature of the action and its connection to another time.
	She has been texting her friend group about this evening's school dance since yesterday.
	6
	Recognize and correct inappropriate tense shifts
	Students maintain consistent verb tense within sentences or related sentences. Students need to know that verbs in a sentence or related sentences should maintain consistent tense unless the timeline changes.
	Last night he studied for the quiz, reviewed his notes, and completed the practice questions.
	6
	Distinguish between active and passive voice
	Students need to know that active voice emphasizes the subject performing the action, while passive voice emphasizes the subject receiving the action. They also need to know that passive constructions typically use a form of “be” + past participle.
	Active Voice:
The students designed the posters for Spirit Week.

Passive Voice:
The posters were designed by the students for Spirit Week.
	7
	Use active and passive voice appropriate to purpose, audience, task
	Students select active or passive voice to match their writing goal (e.g., active for directness, passive for emphasis on the action). Students need to know which form emphasizes the subject or action.
	Active Voice:
Our dedicated team won the championship game after months of practice.

Passive Voice:
The championship game was won by our dedicated team after months of practice.
	8
	Use indicative, imperative, and interrogative moods
	Students need to know that verb mood indicates the function or purpose of a sentence. In the indicative mood, verbs express statements or facts. In the imperative mood, verbs give commands or instructions. In the interrogative mood, verbs form questions. Students also need to recognize that verb forms, word order, and auxiliary verbs often signal the mood of a sentence.
	Indicative Mood:
We are taking the bus home from school tomorrow.

Imperative Mood:
Finish your meal before you can have dessert.

Interrogative Mood:
Did you upload your homework to the class website?
	8
	Use conditional and subjunctive moods
	Students need to know that the conditional mood expresses hypothetical situations and possible, but not guaranteed, outcomes by often using if/if-were clauses and modal auxiliaries. The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, demands, or possibilities that are not factual. Students need to recognize that the subjunctive often appears in clauses following verbs expressing desire, recommendation, or uncertainty and that the verb in the subjunctive is usually the base form (i.e., no -s, -ed, or -ing ending). The past subjunctive, especially with the verb to be (e.g., were), can sound unusual or “formal” because it does not follow normal past-tense patterns and is often replaced by was in casual speech; however, using were correctly signals a hypothetical or non-factual situation, which is essential for expressing wishes, contrary-to-fact statements, or imagined scenarios.
	Conditional Mood:
If the weather improves, we will go for a hike tomorrow.

If the data were more complete, the results would be more reliable.

Subjunctive Mood:
Members of the book club wished their next read were available for pick-up.

The committee recommended that the proposal be revised before approval.

	9-12
	Use verbals (gerunds, participles, infinitives)
	Students need to know that verbals are verb forms that function in ways other than as the main verb of a clause. Gerunds are formed by adding -ing to a verb and act as nouns. Participles act as adjectives: present participles are formed with -ing , and past participles often end in -ed for regular verbs or take an irregular form. Infinitives are formed with to + base verb and can function as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs. Students also need to recognize that verbals allow sentences to vary in structure, provide additional detail, and express actions, qualities, or purposes in different grammatical roles.
	Gerund (as a noun):
Writing improves with consistent practice.
She values listening in difficult conversations.

Past Participle (as an adjective):
The exhausted runner collapsed at the finish line.

Present Participle (as an adjective):
The emerging theory challenged earlier assumptions.

Infinitive (as a noun):
To learn requires patience and persistence.

Infinitive (as an adverb of purpose):
She stayed late to revise.

Infinitive (as an adjective):
The committee proposed a plan to implement.
	9-12

Adjective & Adverb Use Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use adjectives
	Students need to know that adjectives change or clarify the meaning of a noun. They also need to recognize that adjectives usually come directly before the noun or after certain verbs like be or seem.
	I saw black clouds in the sky.
	K
	Use adjectives and adverbs
	Students need to know adjectives modify nouns, while adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Students need to know the distinction between the two types of modifiers. They also need to recognize the questions each answers: adjectives answer which one, what kind, how many; adverbs answer how, when, where, or to what extent.
	The girl drew three boxes on her paper.

She drew them quickly.
	2
	Choose appropriate modifier based on what is being modified
	Students need to know modifiers must match the type of word they modify: adjectives with nouns and adverbs with verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Students need to know that placing the wrong type of modifier changes or confuses the meaning. They also need to understand basic placement rules: modifiers should be close to the word they describe.
	We saw a beautiful sunset.

The girl spoke softly.
	2
	Form and use comparative adjectives/ adverbs
	Students need to know comparatives are forms of adjectives or adverbs that show ranges of difference between two things (e.g., taller, more quickly). Students need to know that comparatives often use “-er” or “more” depending on word structure. They also need to recognize how to compare actions or qualities accurately using these forms.
	Comparative Adjective:
The area of the large rectangle is the sum of the two smaller rectangles.

Comparative Adverb:
The area of the large rectangle is found more easily by adding the two smaller rectangles.
	3
	Form and use superlative adjectives/
adverbs
	Students need to know superlatives are forms of adjectives or adverbs that show the highest or extreme degree among three or more things (e.g., tallest, most quickly). Students need to know that superlatives often use “-est” or “most” depending on word structure. They also need to recognize how to identify or form the “most extreme” quality in a set.
	Superlative Adjective:
Heat always moves from the hottest object to the cooler one.

Superlative Adverb:
Heat moves from a warmer object to a cooler one the fastest when they touch each other.
	3
	Use relative adverbs
	Students need to know that relative adverbs (where, when, why) introduce clauses that provide additional information about a noun or verb. They need to know that these clauses follow the word they describe and function like adjectives or adverbs, explaining location (where), time (when), or reason (why). Students also need to recognize that these relative adverb clauses cannot stand alone and always link their clause to a specific noun or verb in the main clause.
	I need to use a pictorial model when showing my work for the word problem.
	3
	Order adjectives according to conventional patterns
	Students need to know that when multiple adjectives modify a noun, they usually follow a specific sequence (e.g., quantity, opinion, size, age, color, origin, material). Students need to know the conventional order of adjectives to avoid confusion. They also need to recognize how each adjective type answers a different descriptive question.
	Opinion → Age → Material → Noun:
The beautiful old wooden box contained many valuables.
	4
	Recognize
and correct misplaced modifiers
	Students need to know that a misplaced modifier is a word or phrase that is too far from the word it describes, which can create confusion or an unintended meaning. They need to know that modifiers must be placed as close as possible to the word they describe. To identify misplaced modifiers, students should locate the word or phrase being described and check whether the modifier is positioned directly next to it. They also need to identify key words in the sentence to attach modifiers correctly.
	Misplaced Modifier:
I almost listened to music for three hours while doing homework.

Corrected Misplaced Modifier:
I listened to music for almost three hours while doing homework.
	7
	Recognize
and correct dangling modifiers
	Students need to know that a dangling modifier is a descriptive word or phrase that does not clearly modify any word in the sentence, often leaving the sentence confusing or illogical. They need to know that every modifier must have a clear word it describes. To identify dangling modifiers, students should determine the intended subject or main noun for the modifier; if the intended noun is missing or unclear, the modifier is dangling and the sentence needs revision.
	Dangling Modifier:
Walking to school, the bus passed by.

Dangling Modifier Corrected:
While I was walking to school, the bus passed by.
	7

Phrase, Clause, & Cohesion Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use coordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet) connect words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance. They also need to recognize that coordinating conjunctions join ideas without changing the grammatical function of the connected elements.
	She is small but strong!
	1
	Use subordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, if, when, since, while, etc.) connect a dependent clause to an independent clause. They also need to recognize that the dependent clause cannot stand alone and functions to provide time, cause, condition, contrast, or reason.
	I stayed inside because it was storming.

If it snows, we will build a snowman.
	2
	Use prepositional phrases
	Students need to know that a prepositional phrase consists of a preposition and its object (noun or pronoun) and functions as an adjective or adverb. They also need to recognize how prepositional phrases provide information about location, time, direction, cause, or manner.
	The price of toys went up because of the high demand in the store.
	3
	Use correlative conjunctions
	Students need to know that correlative conjunctions work in pairs (e.g., either–or, neither–nor, both–and, not only–but also) to connect balanced elements of equal grammatical importance. They also need to recognize that each part of the pair must attach to similar structures (e.g., noun to noun, clause to clause).
	Both the mountains and the rivers affect where people build cities and farms in the United States.
	5
	Form and use verbal phrases for variety, precision, and nuance
	Students need to know that verbal phrases are built around verbals—gerunds (-ing forms functioning as nouns), participles (present -ing or past -ed/irregular forms functioning as adjectives), and infinitives (to + verb forms functioning as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs). They need to recognize that verbal phrases combine these verbals with other words (modifiers or objects) to create noun, adjective, or adverb phrases and that verbal phrases can appear at the beginning, middle, and end of sentences. Students also need to know that using verbal phrases allows sentences to vary in structure, add descriptive detail, and convey subtle nuances of meaning, such as timing, cause, or purpose and that verbal phrases can be layered with each other for increased sophistication.
	Gerund Phrase (as a noun):
Analyzing primary sources requires questioning assumptions and evaluating context.

Present Participial Phrase (as an adjective):
Clutching the letter in her trembling hands, she realized the truth had arrived too late.

Past Participial Phrase (as an adjective):
Worn by decades of use, the book still held its authority.

Infinitive Phrase (as an adverb):
The city invested in more efficient public transportation to reduce energy consumption.

Infinitive Phrase (as a noun):
To forgive after such betrayal felt impossible.

Layered Verbal Phrases (participial + infinitive):
Hoping to persuade undecided voters, the candidate delivered a speech designed to highlight economic stability.
	9-12
	Use parallel structure to emphasize parity between ideas
	Students need to know that parallel structure means using the same grammatical form for words, phrases, or clauses that are joined or compared. They need to know that elements that can be made parallel include lists of words or phrases, paired ideas connected by coordinating or correlative conjunctions, and clauses within a sentence. Students also need to recognize that maintaining parallel forms ensures clarity, readability, and equal emphasis across ideas in a sentence and that when combined with extended repetition, parallelism lends itself to emphasis of ideas.
	Parallel Verbs in a List:
The researcher collected, analyzed, and interpreted the data.

Parallel Gerund Phrases in a List:
Effective study habits include reviewing notes regularly, completing practice problems, and seeking feedback.

Parallel Infinitive Phrases in a List:
She wanted to leave quietly, to avoid conflict, and to preserve her dignity.

Parallel Structure with Correlative Conjunctions:
The policy aims not only to reduce costs but also to improve accessibility.

Parallel Dependent Clauses:
When the evidence is examined carefully and when the context is considered honestly, the argument becomes more convincing.
	9-12

Capitalization Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Capitalize the first word in a sentence
	Students need to know that the first word in every sentence begins with a capital letter, regardless of its part of speech. This signals the start of a complete thought.
	We like to play.
	K
	Capitalize the pronoun I
	Students need to know that the pronoun “I” is always capitalized, regardless of its position in a sentence, because it represents the speaker.
	I can jump.

Can I help?
	K
	Capitalize names of people
	Students need to know that proper nouns naming specific people begin with capital letters.
	Catie is my friend.

I sit next to John.
	1
	Capitalize dates
	Students need to know that the day and month in a date are capitalized.
	My birthday is on Monday, April 20.
	1
	Capitalize holidays
	Students need to know that names of holidays are capitalized.
	We wear red on Valentine’s Day.
	2
	Capitalize brand names
	Students need to know that specific brands or trademarks are capitalized.
	I drink Gatorade after every baseball game.
	2
	Capitalize names of places
	Students need to know that specific geographic locations are capitalized.
	We took a trip to the Greenville Zoo.

I used to live in Florida.
	2
	Capitalize titles of texts
	Students need to know that–generally–nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, and subordinating conjunctions in titles of books, articles, and other works are capitalized. Generally, articles, prepositions, and coordinating conjunctions are not unless they are the first or last word of a title.
	Charlotte is very caring and kind towards Wilbur in Charlotte’s Web.

In The One and Only Ivan, Ivan spends most of his life in a shopping mall.
	3
	Use capitalization appropriately in dialogue
	Students need to know that the first word of a speaker’s statement is capitalized, even within a sentence or paragraph.
	Continuous Dialogue: Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.

Interrupted Dialogue Response (Dialogue tag interrupts two sentences):  “Hard work pays off,” said Paul, “We’ll need to keep practicing every day!”

Interrupted Dialogue Response (Dialogue tag interrupts a single sentence):  “I knew you could do it,” said Marcus, “because you practice harder than anyone else.”
	3
	Capitalize abbreviations
	Students need to know that standard abbreviations for proper nouns or titles are capitalized (e.g., Doctor→Dr., Junior→Jr., United States of America →U.S.A, Street→St., frequently asked questions→FAQ, etc.).
	Dr. Jones says that all students need sufficient exercise.
	4
	Capitalize proper adjectives
	Students need to know that adjectives derived from proper nouns are capitalized.
	My love of Italian food comes from my childhood.
	4
	Capitalize formal titles
	Students need to know that official titles are capitalized when used with a person’s name.
	The bravery of Captain Adams saved the entire crew that day.
	4
	Use capitalization appropriately to indicate direct speech and text quotes
	Students need to know that the first word of a direct speech statement and the first word of a complete quoted sentence are capitalized. Quoted statements that cannot stand alone are not capitalized.
	Concluding With a Quote (quoting a complete sentence): The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting an incomplete sentence): The author explains that ocean currents are “powerful forces that shape Earth’s climate” and affect weather patterns around the world.

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting a complete sentence): The author states, “Ocean currents move warm and cold water around the Earth,” to explain how they affect climate.

Comma Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use commas in dates
	Students need to know that a comma separates the day and year in full dates.
	We moved on June 26, 2023.
	1
	Use commas to separate words in a series
	Students need to know that commas separate three or more items in a list.
	My favorite colors are pink, green, and purple.
	1
	Use commas before coordinating conjunctions in compound sentences
	Students need to know that a comma comes before coordinating conjunctions (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) when joining two independent clauses.
	I wanted to play outside, but it started to rain.
	1
	Use commas in addresses
	Students need to know that in addresses written in sentences, commas separate the street address, the city, and the state. When addresses are written on separate lines, a comma is generally only needed between the city and state.
	The birthday party is at 123 Happy Place, Cary, North Carolina.

123 Happy Place
Cary, North Carolina
	2
	Use commas in greetings and closings
	Students need to know that commas follow the greeting or closing in letters, emails, cards, etc.
	Example Greeting:
Dear Mom,

Example Closing:
Love, Mary
	2
	Use commas in dialogue
	Students need to know that commas separate the words of a speaker from dialogue tags.
	Continuous Dialogue: Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.

Interrupted Dialogue Response:  “I knew you could do it,” said Marcus, “because you practice harder than anyone else.”
	3
	Use commas to indicate direct address
	Students need to know that commas set off names or titles of people being spoken to. When direct addresses are at the beginning of a sentence, the comma comes after them. When direct addresses are at the end of a sentence, the comma comes before.
	John, I agree with what you said about the photograph helping readers understand deforestation better.

I agree with what you said about the photograph helping readers understand deforestation better, John.
	4
	Use commas to set off “yes” and “no”
	Students need to know that commas follow introductory yes or no in a sentence.
	Yes, I do believe that I included enough supporting evidence.
	4
	Use commas to indicate direct speech and quotations
	Students need to know that commas separate quoted material from the rest of the sentence unless the quoted material is integrated into the grammatical structure of the sentence. When quoted material concludes a sentence, a comma comes only before the opening quotation mark. When quoted material is introduced mid-sentence, a comma comes before the opening quotation mark and before the closing quotation mark.
	Concluding With a Quote (quoting a complete sentence): The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting a complete sentence): The author states, “Ocean currents move warm and cold water around the Earth,” to explain how they affect climate.

Integrating Quote into Grammatical Structure (quoting an incomplete sentence): The author explains that ocean currents are “powerful forces that shape Earth’s climate” and affect weather patterns around the world.
	5
	Use commas after introductory elements
	Students need to know that commas follow introductory words, phrases, or clauses.
	Single Word:
Finally, I believe that students should have longer recess because research shows that it leads to better mental health.

Prepositional Phrases as an Introductory Element:
In the second paragraph of the article, the author provides evidence that recycling helps reduce pollution.

Complex Sentence (When Dependent Clause is First):
Because of the growing list of grievances, the colonists decided to declare independence.
	5
	Use commas to set off tag questions
	Students need to know that commas separate a statement from a short question at the end of a sentence.
	The evidence for this is stated in the first paragraph, isn’t it?
	5
	Use commas in compound- complex sentences
	Students need to know that commas help separate clauses in sentences with multiple independent and dependent clauses for clarity.
	When I finished my homework, I texted my friends because I wanted to relax, and we planned our gaming session.
	6
	Use commas to separate coordinate adjectives
	Students need to know that commas separate two or more adjectives that equally modify a noun.
	She bought a trendy, oversized hoodie at the mall.
	7
	Use commas for nonrestrictive/parenthetical elements
	Students need to know that commas can set off extra information or details that are not essential to the core meaning of the sentence. This extra information often appears in recognizable structures, such as appositives, nonrestrictive relative clauses, and nonessential participial phrases.
	My best friend, who sits next to me in math class, helped me study for the test.

Quotations & Dialogue Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use quotation marks in dialogue
	Students need to know that spoken words are enclosed in quotation marks.
	Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.
	3
	Use quotation marks in titles (when appropriate)
	Students need to know that titles of short works (poems, articles, short stories, chapters in larger texts) are enclosed in quotation marks.
	In “Chapter 4” of Charlotte’s Web, Wilbur experiences loneliness.
	3
	Use quotation marks  to indicate direct speech and text quotations
	Students need to know that a speaker’s exact words go inside quotation marks and that any words copied directly from a text must also be placed inside quotation marks. When quoting text that includes dialogue, students should use single quotation marks to signal the dialogue.
	Quoting Text:
The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Quoting Dialogue:
In Bud, Not Buddy, Bud explains, “I remember what Momma always told me, ‘If you have to tell a lie, make it simple and believable.’”
	5
	Format block quotes appropriately
	Students need to know that long quotations (usually more than four lines) are formatted as block quotes. In MLA style, they are indented an additional ½”, double-spaced, use no quotation marks (unless quoted text is dialogue), and are often separated by line breaks before and after. For quoting verse/poems specifically, students should maintain the original line breaks in the source text. Unlike other in-text citations, students should include the appropriate citation after the period of the final sentence.
	Block Quote with Dialogue:
Steinbeck consistently highlights the responsibility George feels towards Lennie, showing how George attempts to insulate Lennie from the consequences of his own problematic behavior. Early in the novel, Steinbeck demonstrates how George guides Lennie’s behavior and thinking:
"’Course you did. Well, look Lennie - if you jus’ happen to get in trouble like you always done before, I want you to come right here an’ hide in the brush."
"Hide in the brush," said Lennie slowly.
"Hide in the brush till I come for you. Can you remember that?"
"Sure I can, George. Hide in the brush till you come." (Steinbeck 15)
Even in the first chapter of the novella, it is apparent that there is a significant mental load for George in his friendship with Lennie.

Dashes, Parentheses, Hyphens, & Ellipses Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use parentheses for nonrestrictive/ parenthetical elements
	Students need to know that parentheses enclose extra information or clarifications that are not essential to the main sentence meaning.
	To get to the park, turn right at the light (right next to the grocery store) and take the next left into the parking lot.
	7
	Use hyphens with affixes and compound words
	Students need to know that hyphens connect prefixes, suffixes, or compound words.
	She is a well-known social media creator at our school.
	8
	Use ellipses appropriately
	Students need to know that ellipses indicate omitted words within a quotation, especially when citing a source. They can also be used stylistically in writing to signal a pause or trailing thought.
	Ellipsis to Indicate an Omission:
In his speech, Martin Luther King Jr. stated, “"I have a dream that one day…little black boys and black girls will be able to join hands with little white boys and white girls as sisters and brothers".

Ellipsis to Indicate Pause in Thought:
I was going to hang out with my friends tonight… but Netflix won.

	Use colons to introduce lists
	Students need to know that colons follow independent clauses and introduce lists and explanations. Typically, lists that follow colons follow a parallel structure.
	Colon Introducing a List of Nouns:
For the camping trip, we need to pack several items: a tent, a sleeping bag, a stove, and a flashlight.

Colon Introducing a List of Gerundial Phrases:
Her weekend plans will keep her busy each day: going to the library, meeting friends for coffee, swimming at the lake, and finishing her art project.
	9-10
	Use colons to introduce quotations
	Students need to know that colons follow a complete sentence and introduce quotations, either direct (from a speaker) or text-sourced (from an authored text). Direct quotations that follow colons are indicated with an opening and closing quotation mark, and the end punctuation (e.g., period, question mark, exclamation point) is contained within the closing quotation mark. Text-sourced quotations are also indicated with an opening and closing quotation mark, but the end punctuation follows an appropriately formatted in-text citation, as needed.
	Colon Introducing a Direct Quotation:
Coach Stevens reminded the team of their golden rule: “Always give your best effort.”

Colon Introducing a Text-Sourced Quotation:
Elie Wiesel makes his opinion on indifference quite plain: “Indifference, after all, is more dangerous than anger and hatred” (Wiesel, par. 9).
	9-10
	Use em dashes for nonrestrictive or parenthetical elements
	Students need to know that em dashes can set off extra information or clarifications that are not essential to the main sentence meaning or indicate a break in thought. When parenthetical/nonrestrictive elements occur in the middle of a sentence, the content is enclosed in em dashes. When  parenthetical/nonrestrictive elements occur at the end of a sentence, the content is set-off by one em dash.
	Em Dash with Mid-Sentence Parenthetical:
Shakespeare’s depiction of Hamlet–the prince torn between duty and desire–reveals the enduring tension between action and contemplation.

Em Dash with End-of-Sentence Clarification:
The novel critiques societal inequality–through both subtle imagery and stark dialogue–highlighting the pervasive effects of class.

Em Dash with Break in Thought/Interruption:
The argument seemed sound–but upon closer inspection, the evidence revealed several inconsistencies.

Semicolons & Colons Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use semicolons to separate items in a series when items include internal commas
	Students need to know that semicolons separate complex list items that already contain commas to preserve clarity.
	For our day at the park, I packed snacks, including chips and candy; Ava made the playlists, selecting music we each like; and Leo brought the games, hoping to keep us all entertained.

I have lived in Omaha, Nebraska; Litchfield, Connecticut; and Mason City, Iowa.
	8
	Use semicolons with conjunctive adverbs or transitional phrases to link independent clauses
	Students need to know that semicolons can join two independent clauses, providing an additional way to connect complete ideas. This is especially useful when the clauses already contain internal commas or when using conjunctive adverbs (e.g., however, therefore, nevertheless, nonetheless, consequently, furthermore, moreover) or transitional phrases (e.g.,as a result, for this reason, in light of this, in contrast, on the other hand). When a conjunctive adverb or transitional phrase is used, the semicolon comes before it, and a comma always follows it, helping to maintain clarity in complex sentences.
	Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs, No Internal Punctuation:
The novel explores themes of ambition and betrayal; however, the protagonist’s personal growth remains central to the story.

Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs, Internal Punctuation:
Researchers have studied the effects of social media on teenagers, examining its impact on mental health, including anxiety and self-esteem, its role in shaping peer relationships and social norms, and its influence on academic motivation and time management; therefore, they argue that schools and families should guide responsible use while encouraging balance and critical thinking.

Semicolons with Transitional Phrases, No Internal Punctuation:
She completed the research experiment successfully; in the meantime, her classmates continued testing alternative hypotheses.

Semicolons with Transitional Phrases, Internal Punctuation:
The study examined social media use among teenagers, including time spent on messaging apps, participation in online communities, and exposure to trending challenges; as a result, researchers found significant correlations between frequent use, sleep disruption, and lower academic focus.
	9-12
	Use semicolons with coordinating conjunctions when independent clauses contain internal punctuation
	Students need to know that sometimes a semicolon can be used with a coordinating conjunction to join two sentences together and preserve clarity when the sentences are long and contain additional internal punctuation. They also need to know that this is an uncommon construction and is most helpful when one or both sentences already include several commas (e.g., complex sentences, series of phrases, etc.). Students should recognize that in this construction, the semicolon comes before the coordinating conjunction, and no additional comma is needed immediately after it.

	Recognize and correct frequently misused words
	Students need to know that some words are commonly confused or misapplied because they sound alike or have related meanings (e.g., their/there/they’re, affect/effect). Students need to know the distinct meanings and grammatical functions of these words in order to identify misuse in context. Students also need to know how to apply this knowledge when revising their own writing to ensure clarity and correctness.
	Misused Then/Than
According to the text, “he made quick progress,” and finished his invention quicker then his competitor.

Corrected with Than, Indicating Comparison
According to the text, “he made quick progress,” and finished his invention quicker than his competitor.

Misused Affect/Effect
The text says, “The storm destroyed the bridge,” and will drastically effect their ability to trade.

Corrected with Affect, Meaning to Influence
The text says, “The storm destroyed the bridge,” and will drastically affect their ability to trade.

Misused Accept/Except
I can relate to every character in the story accept Billy.

Corrected with Except, Meaning Excluding
I can relate to every character in the story except Billy.

	Correct sentence fragments
	Students need to know that a fragment is an incomplete thought lacking a subject, predicate, or both. Students need to know that the simple predicate is the main verb or verb phrase that shows the action or state of being and that the complete predicate includes the simple predicate and all the words that modify or complete it, such as objects, complements, and modifiers. Students need to know how to add missing subjects, predicates, or clauses to incomplete sentences to form grammatically complete statements.
	Fragment Missing a Subject
Rushed through the crowded hallway and down the stairs to the science lab.

Corrected with Added Subject
The student rushed through the crowded hallway and down the stairs to the science lab.

Fragment Missing a Complete Predicate
The exhausted students in the normally noisy cafeteria.

Corrected with Added Verb/Complete Predicate
The exhausted students in the normally noisy cafeteria collapsed quietly into their seats after a long day of testing.

Dependent Clause/Incomplete Thought
Because we studied all night for the difficult math test and reviewed our notes twice.

Corrected with Added Independent Clause
Because we studied all night for the difficult math test and reviewed our notes twice, we felt confident the next morning.

	Correct run-on sentences
	Students need to know that a run-on occurs when two or more independent clauses are joined improperly. Students need to know that fused sentences are a type of run-on where two or more independent clauses are joined with no punctuation. Students also need to know that comma splices are a type of run-on where two or more independent clauses are joined only with a comma. Students need to know how to separate  independent clauses correctly using end punctuation or join them using conjunctions and commas or semicolons appropriately to form grammatically correct sentences.
	Comma Splice Sentence Run-on
I forgot my homework at home, I tried to explain it to my teacher.

Corrected by Separating with a Period
I forgot my homework at home. I tried to explain it to my teacher.

Fused Sentence Run-on
We practiced after school every day we still lost the game.

Corrected by Joining with a Semicolon
We practiced after school every day; we still lost the game.

Fused Sentence Run-on
She stayed up late studying for the test she was exhausted the next morning.

Corrected by Joining with a Coordinating Conjunction and Comma
She stayed up late studying for the test, so she was exhausted the next morning.
