# Arts Education - Third Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Third Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 Dance standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed Visual Arts educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

3.CN.1.1 Describe dance forms found in the local community or region.
Glossary
* Community - A unified body of individuals: such as a group of people with a common characteristic or interest living together within a larger society; a body of persons of common and especially professional interests scattered through a larger society.14
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Region - Relating to or coming from a particular part of a country, such as a regional accent/dialect/newspaper.4
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students described how American dance can reflect the heritage, customs, and traditions of people in the United States, including various indigenous and cultural groups. At the current level, students describe the dance forms that exist in their local community or region. At the next level, students will describe styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* What is meant by local community and region
* How dance is present in multiple forms throughout the local community or region
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of the myriad of dance forms that may be present in the local community or region
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* How dance is experienced in the local community/region may differ from surrounding areas
* Some dancers only perform locally
* Multiple genres of dance may be featured regionally or locally
* Dance can be practiced, performed, and presented in a variety of settings and community venues
* Note the conjunction “or” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to describe dance forms found in the local community OR region. Depending on the learning activity, either may be considered.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Provide examples of dance forms that exist in the local community or region
* Model describing various dance forms that exist in the local community or region
* Guide student exploration in discovering dance forms that exist in their local community or region
* Design learning activities for students to summarize the characteristics of the dance forms that exist in their local community or region
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to describe dance forms found in the local community or region
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Field trip and guest artist opportunities for students
* Dance companies that are in the area
* Musical Theatre dance in community theatre performances
* Indigenous and other cultural dance celebrations and rituals
* Social dance outlets that are school appropriate - a Swing Dance Society, a Square Dance Group, local studios, colleges with dance programs, even other k-12 schools that have dance programs or after school dance opportunities

3.CN.1.2 Compare dance elements with elements from other arts disciplines.
Glossary
* Compare - To locate and/or list similarities and differences.14
* Dance Elements - The key components of movement; movement of the body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement; see Elements of Dance Organizer by Perpich Center for Arts Education (used with permission).17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified cross-curricular connections between dance and other content areas. At the current level, students compare dance elements with elements from other arts disciplines. Students will expand this comparison of dance concepts with other content areas in the next level.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that dance skills are transferable, enhancing performance and adaptability across diverse settings and disciplines.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The elements of dance (body, space, time, effort)
* The elements of other arts areas: theatre, music, and visual art
* The similarities and differences of the elements of dance with elements of music, theatre, and/or visual arts disciplines
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Identifying similarities and differences between the elements of dance and the elements of music, theatre, and/or visual arts
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Dance elements of body, space, time, and effort are specific to dance but can easily relate to other arts areas
* Art forms share a variety of concepts
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom instruction on the elements of dance
* Teach (and draw on student knowledge of) the elements used in other art forms - this would be an opportunity to collaborate instruction with other arts teachers at the school
* Demonstrate comparing similarities and differences in the elements of dance with those of music, theatre, and visual arts
* Guide student investigation of the similarities and differences between the various elements of multiple art forms and elements of dance
* Design activities for students to compare similarities and differences in the elements of dance with elements from other arts disciplines
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to compare dance elements with elements from other arts disciplines
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Music: Pitch, rhythm, harmony, dynamics, timbre, texture, form, and tempo
   * Understandings of how the elements of rhythm, dynamics, form, and tempo are used in dance and music
   * Explore moving to different tempos
   * Explore making movements of various sizes to explore dynamics
* Art: color, space, texture, line, shape, form
   * Making comparisons of line, form, shape, and space in visual art are also found in dance
* Theatre: plot, setting, character, thought, diction, song, spectacle
   * Comparisons of how movement used in theatre and in dance
   * Explore the difference between gesture, pantomime, and realistic and abstracted movement and when one may use these types of movements in theatre and dance
   * Explore how plot, setting, and character can be used in dance as compared to theatre

3.CR.1.1 Improvise movement based on one dance element.
Glossary
* Dance Element - The key components of movement; movement of the body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement; see Elements of Dance Organizer by Perpich Center for Arts Education (used with permission).17
* Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students generated spontaneous movement in response to musical cues and now improvise movement based on one dance element. In the next level, students will improvise movement based on multiple dance elements.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of dancers and choreographers to develop their creative aesthetic
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The basic dance elements and their components
* How to create spontaneous movement
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating spontaneous movement through improvisation that explores one of the basic dance element concepts
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Ensure students are informed of all the dance elements and the components in them. These should be taught from kindergarten so that by third grade, students have a clear beginning understanding for what each element includes
* Dance uses all the elements of dance but improvisation exercises can be explored by focusing on one of the elements at a time and mastering the components of the element
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain what it means to improv
* Teach and review the elements of dance
* Demonstrate and call on student volunteers to show movement improvisation based on one dance element
* Guide students through movement improvisation exercises based on one dance element
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to improvise movement based on one dance element
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* In the body element, students can demonstrate locomotor and axial/non-locomotor movements, different body shapes, use of isolation, simultaneous, sequential body movements or using movement initiation of different body parts
* With the space element, students can improvise movements using pathways, directions, level changes, size changes, and relationships with other dancers
* In the energy element improvisation can focus on qualities such as float, glide, wring, press, flick, dab, punch, and slash using the time, weight, and path in the energy qualities of movement
* For the time element, students can improvise movements using sudden or sustained time quality or different rhythms

3.CR.1.2 Combine simple dance sequences to create dance phrases.
Glossary
* Combine - To bring into such close relationship as to obscure individual characters : merge.14
* Dance Phrase - A sequence of movements that are ordered based upon a preconceived choreographic intent and contains a clear beginning, middle, and end that aligns with artistic intent. A movement phrase could be compared to a paragraph of movement and would contain two or more movement sentences.29
* Dance Sequence - A brief sequence of movements that are not necessarily ordered into a formal choreographic structure that communicates artistic intent but rather demonstrates a solution to a movement problem. A movement sequence could contain as few as two or movements.29
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students created short dance sequences with simple patterns and now combine simple dance sequences to create dance phrases. In the next level, students will create dance phrases incorporating abstraction of everyday movement to fulfill a given choreographic form.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are actualized into choreography by dancers and choreographers to intentionally fulfill specific purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Simple dance sequences
* How to structure dance sequences into longer movement phrases
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Merging short movement sequences into longer dance phrases
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Choreography is created through purposeful choices by the artist
* Students must have time to either create or learn short dance sequences that are to be used in mastery of this objective of creating longer phrases of movement
* Dance sequences can be created by the teacher, student, or peers
* Note that “phrases” is plural, implying more than one. Instruction of this objective will likely occur multiple times throughout the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain and demonstrate how short dance sequences can be combined into longer choreographic phrases of movement
* Provide practice for all students to learn and perform short dance phrases made from simple dance sequences
* Design learning activities in which students can create their own dance phrases by combining simple dance sequences
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to combine simple dance sequences to create dance phrases
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Create short prompts and choreographic structures for students to follow in group work to create original dance sequences to be combined into longer dance phrases
   * Technique based movements for different genres of dance: modern, jazz, ballet, tap, majorette, hiphop, etc.
   * Cross-curricular concepts including: weather patterns (ie: water cycle), life cycles (caterpillar or plant), daily sequences for morning noon and evening, the steps of the scientific process in movement, other daily sequences that are recognizable to all students

3.CR.1.3 Depict directions or spatial pathways in a dance phrase by drawing a picture, map, or symbol.
Glossary
* Depict - To represent or show something.4
* Spatial Pathways - Patterns of dance movement in and through space as in straight, zigzag, curvy, or wavy.29
* Symbol - An image, sign, or element, such as color, that is understood, by convention or context, to suggest some other meaning.22
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students depicted spatial levels in a variety of dance movements by drawing a picture or using symbols and now draw a picture, map, or symbol to depict directions or spatial pathways in a dance phrase. In the next level, students will depict the spatial relationships between two or more dancers in a dance phrase by drawing a picture or using symbols.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are recorded by dancers and choreographers through notation and symbolic documentation.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The eight basic directions
* The three spatial pathways (straight, circular, zig-zag)
* Movements that exemplify spatial directions or pathways
* How to visually represent spatial directions and pathways of movements
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Representing/Showing spatial directions and pathways through symbolic notation
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This thread of objectives is designed for students to record choreography they create, not observe. Pair with CR.1.2 and CR.2.1
* Teachers can use the Language of Dance Movement Symbols or have students create their own symbolic representation of movements
* When using student-created images, students need to have the opportunity to explain why they drew the symbols they did so that it can be determined if their reasoning is sound
* Note the conjunction “or” used twice in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to depict directions OR spatial pathways in a dance phrase by drawing a picture, map, OR symbol. Depending on the learning activity, it is up to the discretion of the teacher to decide which the student is to use or autonomy can be given to the student to make choices if depicting directions or spatial pathways and the manner in which they are notated
* A map could also be used to show spatial pathways with symbols or pictures used to show what to do along various stop points on the path
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Aid students in identifying the eight spatial directions (Forward, Backward, Right, Left, Forward Right Diagonal, Backward Right Diagonal, Forward Left Diagonal, Backward Left Diagonal)  and pathways (straight, curved, zig-zag) used in dance
* Demonstrate how pictures or symbols can be used to represent spatial pathways and directions
* Have students learn and perform dances that use a variety of spatial directions and pathways and have students draw symbols or pictures to represent them
* Encourage students to use a variety of spatial directions and pathways in their personal dance creations
* Design learning activities that task students to draw symbols, maps, or pictures of the directions or spatial pathways in choreography they learn or create
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to depict directions or spatial pathways in a dance phrase by drawing a picture, map, or symbol
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Use the Language of Dance Laban Symbols
* Pair this objective with CR.1.2 and have students combine simple dance sequences to create dance phrases then CR.2.1 in adapting the directions or pathways of that phrase. Students would use the symbols, pictures, or maps (or a combination of all three) to notate spatial directions or pathways of movement
* Teach movements for each of eight directions or three pathways and then create a class legend with symbols. Have students create a movement sequence and draw the symbols that represents the levels of the movements that they are executing

* How to alter the spatial directions or pathways of movement
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Modifying spatial directions or pathways used in original dance sequences to create new movement sequences
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.

* Students may struggle with finding "front" when facings are changed and will need some practice doing their dance phrase with a range of changing facings. Students may also find that adding a pathway changes the movement duration slightly or puts them in a different facing than they were before the pathway
* The eight basic directions are forward, backward, right and left, forward right diagonal, backward right diagonal, forward left diagonal, and backward left diagonal. The three pathways are straight, curved, and zig-zag
* Changing the direction or pathway of movements is one of the choreographic tools used to manipulate motifs to create new movement. Students can be challenged to change the direction or pathway of any movement. Pathways can be traveling or examining the pathway of a body part through space such as an arm moving on a straight or circular path in the dancer’s kinesphere
* Students may find that changing the direction or pathway of movements may impact the transitions and timing of the movement so the tempo may also need to change so that they can appropriately perform their movements
* This objective can be paired with other CR, PR, and RE objectives as students create, perform, and respond to dance
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Create classroom activities that support student creation and manipulation of choreography
* Demonstrate how to adapt the spatial directions or pathways of movements
* Ask for volunteer ideas for how a variety of motifs might be performed using different spatial pathways or directions
* Provide practice for all students to contribute ideas for how to adapt the spatial directions and pathways of movements used in choreography
* Create original choreography for students to adapt or guide students in generating original material to be used
* Design a learning activity in which students can adapt the spatial directions or pathways of movements in a pre-existing dance sequence
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to adapt a dance sequence by changing the directions or spatial pathways of the movements
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Show students how to adapt movements by making them face different directions or creating a specified pathway for a movement. A good starting place for changing direction and adding pathways may be social line dancing, where the dance has simple steps that go forward, sideways, and backward, but also change wall facings. This can be achieved easily with students by using a popular social line dance like the Cha Cha Slide. Students already know the movements, but asking them to change the pathways in the dance or the direction the dance is going will help them understand adapting a phrase to general spatial changes
* Teach students a dance sequence and task them to perform specific movements of the sequence by changing the direction or pathway. For example, “instead of skipping on a straight path, skip on a zig-zag path” or “instead of kicking your leg to the front, kick it in a different direction” (be mindful of spacing!). Other possibilities could include performing movements facing different directions in the general space or using different pathways of the body parts through space as if they have paint on them and are making lines around the dancer as they move from their starting to ending positions of movements

This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students explained possible meanings of various dance movements or choreographic works and now infer meaning from dance, citing evidence. In the next level, students will compare a personal interpretation of a dance with the interpretation of peers.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer’s interpretation of a work may align with or differ from the artist’s original intent.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * How meaning can be visually represented through dance movements or choreographic works
   * How to analyze the artistic choices used in dance movements or choreographic work
   * How to interpret meaning from artistic choices made in dance
   * How to reference evidence of meaning in choreography
   * How to linguistically describe dance
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Going beyond the available information to identify reasonable interpretations of dance movements or choreographic works with reference to supporting evidence
   * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Interpretations of meaning may vary based on the individual. Reinforce to students that there is not only one, “correct” answer
   * This objective does not specify if students are interpreting meaning from dance movements, sequences, phrases, or works therefore the length of the work is irrelevant. The student needs to however, provide evidence from their observations to support their interpretation
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Lead classroom discussion on how dance can communicate meaning
   * Demonstrate how to infer meaning and cite evidence from choreography that is either performed or observed (be mindful not to impart bias but allow students to have differing perspectives)
   * Provide examples of age-appropriate choreography for observation or physical embodiment that can be interpreted for meaning (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work)
   * Guide students on how to interpret meaning from dance
   * Provide sentence stems to help students cite evidence in support of their interpretation of possible meanings in dance observations or performance
   * Design activities for students to infer meaning, citing evidence from observations or performance of dance
   * Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to infer meaning from dance, citing evidence
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
   * Ask students to pair share or volunteer to describe their personal interpretations of meaning conveyed through dance citing evidence, in a teacher-led discussion
   * Provide sentence stems or vocabulary banks for students to write their interpretations of meaning of movements or works
   * Have students share their interpretation of the meaning of dance movement or work and give evidence supporting their interpretation as an exit ticket

3.RE.2.1 Explain preferences in observing various dance styles, forms, or genres.
Glossary
   * Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
   * Genre - Category of art or design identified by similarities in form, subject matter, content, or technique.17
   * Styles - Specific movement characteristics, qualities, or principles that give it distinctive identity; style may also refer to the unique artistic choices of a particular [artist].17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students explained preferences in performing various dance styles, forms, or genres and now explain observation preferences of various dance styles, forms, or genres. In the next level, students will contrast personal preferences for performing and viewing dance with those of peers.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with dance works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * How to identify and describe personal preferences when observing different dance styles, forms, or genres
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Describing the reasoning for personal preferences in the observance of different dance styles, forms, or genres
   * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * It is natural to have “preferences” but this objective challenges students to explain why or what they like or dislike when observing different dance styles, forms, and genres. It is important to understand personal bias to help develop how to view and appreciate dance objectively, regardless of personal preference
   * This objective can be paired with CN.1.1 when learning and observing dance forms found in the local community or region
   * Note the conjunction “or” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to explain preferences in observing various dance styles, forms, OR genres. It is up to teacher discretion as to which students are viewing
   * Note that this objective is plural - The student should be observing dance of multiple styles, forms, or genres over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Lead classroom discussion on how preferences in dance can vary based on the individual
   * Demonstrate explaining preferences in observing dances from a variety of styles, forms, or genres (be mindful not to impart bias but allow students to have differing perspectives)
   * Provide examples of dance performances in a wide array of styles, forms, or genres for observation (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work)
   * Guide student discovery of preferences when observing works from different styles, forms, or genres
   * Provide sentence stems to help students form descriptive sentences about their preferences  when they are observing various dance styles, forms, or genres
   * Design activities for students to explain personal preference in observing various dance styles, forms, or genres
   * Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to explain preferences in observing various dance styles, forms, or genres
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
   * Design activities that ask students to explain what they like and dislike about observing a style, form, or genre of dance
   * Students can pair-share or contribute to a larger group discussion
   * Four corners activity can have students grouping by level of preferences and then they can discuss in smaller groups
   * Students can write a short reflection explaining their preferences as an assignment or exit-ticket activity

3.RE.2.2 Identify positive feedback and areas for improvement for self and others.
Glossary
   * Feedback - A reaction or response to a particular design problem or design solution.29
   * Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
   * Improve - To enhance in value or quality : make better.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students used instructional support to identify positive feedback and areas for improvement for self and others and now master this same objective independently. In the next level, students will evaluate dances using established criteria to provide feedback for self and others.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * How to identify the positive aspects of personal work
   * How to identify areas for improvement in personal work
   * How to identify the positive aspects of another's work
   * How to identify areas for improvement in another's work
   * How to linguistically discuss movement performance and choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Recognizing, listing, or naming the strengths and areas for improvement in personal and peer work
   * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Students understand that they can use feedback to improve and also give feedback to help someone else improve. It is important to identify strengths when evaluating work so students build efficacy and can continue to build on their success. Identifying areas for growth is an important aspect of constructive criticism. Students may need support in understanding the positive intention of constructive criticism
   * Note the conjunction “and” used twice in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to identify positive feedback AND areas for improvement for self AND others with guidance. Both feedback that is positive and identifying areas of growth needs to be given for both self and others
   * This objective can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students improve choreography and dance skills by applying formal or informal feedback
   * This objective can also be paired with other CR and PR objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Lead classroom discussion on how dancers grow from constructive feedback
   * Guide students on how to use a simple rubric to identify qualities of work
   * Provide opportunities for students to compliment and identify areas in need of improvement in their work and the work of their peers. This can be through demonstration of skills learned or in performance of student-generated work
   * Provide examples of how to identify positive feedback and areas for improvement to self or others
   * Provide sentence stems to help students form positive feedback and suggestions of areas for improvement on aspects of dance creation or performance
   * Design activities for students to identify positive feedback and areas for improvement for self and others
   * Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to identify positive feedback and areas for improvement for self and others
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
   * Have students perform something they have learned or choreographed. Film the students performing and then have students give feedback on strengths and areas for improvement about their own performance and to each other by using a feedback form or sharing feedback verbally
