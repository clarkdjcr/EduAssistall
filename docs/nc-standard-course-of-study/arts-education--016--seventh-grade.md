# Arts Education - Seventh Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Seventh Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The 6-8 Dance standards and objectives are written for middle school students who are taught by a licensed Dance educator for at least 90 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

7.CN.1.1 Analyze how individuals and groups of dancers have influenced various cultures.
Glossary
* Analyze - Identifying and examining separate parts as they function independently and together in creative works and study of [the] arts.22
* Culture - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students explained how dance expresses and reflects values of civilizations around the world. At the current level, students analyze how individuals and groups of dancers have influenced various cultures. Students narrow that scope at the next level to identify the influence of North Carolinian styles and dancers on the broader dance community.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* About customs, traditions, and artistic expressions of various cultures
* Dance Artists (individuals and groups) who have influenced various cultures
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Identifying and examining the influence of individual and groups of dancers on various cultures
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Many popular dance styles and cultural trends have been influenced by individual dancers or dance groups
* Artists can have an impact on societal and cultural trends
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to analyze how individuals AND groups of dancers have influenced various cultures. Both individuals and groups need to be considered in mastery of this objective
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Provide examples of individual artists and dance groups that have had an influence on culture
* Guide student investigation into how various cultures have been influenced by individual artists or artistic groups
* Design learning activities for students to analyze how individuals and groups of dancers have influenced various cultures
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to analyze how individuals and groups of dancers have influenced various cultures
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Social Dances such as the Electric Slide, Cha Cha Slide, Wobble, etc,
* Breakdancing
* Dancers on television shows such as American Bandstand, Soul Train, Dance Moms, So You Think You Can Dance, etc.
* Dance Influencers on social media
* Athletes using dance in goal celebration (primarily football athletes)
* Popular dance moves like The Whoa, The Floss, Fortnight dances
* Artists who break stereotypes and change dance culture such as Misty Copeland

* Note the conjunction “and” used three times in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to identify dance skills AND processes that are both a part of, AND can be applied to, academic study AND daily life. Both dance skills and processes need to be considered for how they can be applied to and exist in both daily life and academic study. Depending on the learning activity this content can be addressed in one assignment or broken apart and addressed in multiple assignments over the course of study

* Design learning activities for students to identify how dance skills and processes are both a part of, and applicable to school and everyday life
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to identify dance skills and processes that are both a part of, and can be applied to, academic study and daily life
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students examine the creative design cycle and compare its use in dance and in usage in other academic and life experiences
* Have students brainstorm on skills using Portrait of a Graduate competencies that are often used in dance classroom, creation, and performance experience

Knowledge

* Anatomical challenges that impact dance performance
* Psychological stressors that impact dancers
* Strategies to improve anatomical challenges and address psychological challenges common to dancers
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Assessing the impact of strategies to improve anatomical alignment and decrease psychological stressors encountered by dancers
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* The effectiveness of strategies used to improve anatomical alignment and decrease psychological stressors may differ by individual. This objective was designed to not only have students examine how well strategies are working for themselves but additionally, which strategies have proven results among wider populations
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to evaluate strategies to improve anatomical alignment AND decrease psychological stressors encountered by dancers. Depending on the learning activity, strategies for both, anatomical alignment and psychological stressors can be considered simultaneously or separately in different assignments over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Activate prior knowledge and discuss the types of psychological stressors and anatomical concerns dancers may face when creating and/or performing dance
* Guide student investigation into physical and mental health and wellness strategies for improving anatomical alignment and decreasing psychological stressors in dance
* Create practices for students to evaluate how mobility and function approaches address anatomical challenges in their own body
* Support student use of strategies such as journaling and meditation, to address dance-related psychological challenges
* Design learning activities for students to evaluate the effectiveness of strategies used to improve anatomical alignment and decrease psychological stressors encountered by dancers
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to evaluate strategies to improve anatomical alignment and decrease psychological stressors encountered by dancers
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Students research different strengthening or practice exercises that address anatomical challenges in dance and evaluate how well certain strategies work over an established period of time
* Students learn of strategies that help with psychological challenges and make comparisons to evaluate how effective strategies could be in addressing the challenges dancers face
* Students could be provided with a list of various strategies for both anatomical and psychological challenges and have to evaluate which ones would seem most logical and effective in addressing the issue

7.CR.1.2 Create dance phrases using selected sequences of abstract movement and simple choreographic forms.
Glossary
* Abstract Movement - To remove movement from a particular or representative context and, by manipulating it through the elements of space, time, and energy, to create a new sequence or dance that retains the essence of the original.18
* Choreographic Form- The organization of choreography and movement to fulfill the artistic intent of a dance or dance study (for example, AB, ABA, or theme and variation); often referred to as choreographic form.2
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
* Dance Phrase - A sequence of movements that are ordered based upon a preconceived choreographic intent and contains a clear beginning, middle, and end that aligns with artistic intent. A movement phrase could be compared to a paragraph of movement and would contain two or more movement sentences.29
* Select - To choose a small number of things, or to choose by making careful decisions.4
* Sequence - to combine things in a particular order, or discover the order in which they are combined.4
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students used movement abstraction to create a short dance or dance sequence with a beginning, middle, and end. At the current level, students create dance phrases using selected sequences of abstract movement and simple choreographic forms. In the next level, students will organize dance phrases into simple dances using a variety of choreographic forms.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are actualized into choreography by dancers and choreographers to intentionally fulfill specific purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Choreographic forms
* How to abstract movement
* How to create dance phrases using abstraction and simple choreographic forms
* How to select dance sequences that use abstraction and simple choreographic forms for longer movement phrases
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Conceiving new dance phrases through the selection of shorter movement sequences that use abstraction and choreographic forms
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Choreography is created through purposeful choices by the artist
* Students must have time to either create or learn short dance sequences that are to be used in mastery of this objective of creating dance phrases
* Dance sequences can be created by the teacher, student, or peers
* The objective does not specify if the choreographic form is given or self-determined, therefore it is up to the discretion of the teacher
* Note that “phrases” is plural, implying more than one. Instruction of this objective will likely occur multiple times throughout the course of study
* Abstraction can include altering the time, space, dynamics, and/or body shapes of actions of people, animals, or things that move. Abstraction of ideas can also come from images, objects, feelings, or other concepts
* Students can identify their own ideas to be communicated through abstraction or the ideas can be assigned to them
* Use interdisciplinary approaches to strengthen learning of concepts from other subject areas
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - the student needs to demonstrate mastery of their ability to create dance phrases using selected sequences of abstract movement AND simple choreographic forms. The dance phrases must use a choreographic form and combine selected movement sequences
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain (or review) the concept of abstraction and demonstrate how various concepts can be abstracted in choreography
* Explain (or review) how choreographic forms are used in the design of choreography
* Provide practice for all students to learn and perform dance phrases that use abstract movement sequences and different choreographic forms
* Guide student development of abstract movement sequences to be used in choreography of dance phrases
* Select a choreographic form for students to use (AB, ABA, Rondo, Natural Form, etc) or have students select a form to use in their choreography
* Design learning activities in which students can use selected sequences of abstract movement and simple choreographic forms to create dance phrases
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to create dance phrases using selected sequences of abstract movement and simple choreographic forms
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Give students the option to create phrases using simple choreographic forms such as AB, ABA, Rondo, etc.
* Students could be tasked over several class periods or as homework to choreograph sequences of abstract movement expressing an idea and then using a choreographic form, select from their choreographed sequences to create a dance phrase
* Small groups could each develop an abstract movement dance sequence and teach their sequences to the class then each student must select a few sequences to be used in their choreography of a longer dance phrase that uses a given or self-determined choreographic form

7.CR.1.3 Document an original dance sequence using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology.
Glossary
* Dance Sequence - A brief sequence of movements that are not necessarily ordered into a formal choreographic structure that communicates artistic intent but rather demonstrates a solution to a movement problem. A movement sequence could contain as few as two or movements.29
* Digital Tools and Media - Anything in electronic form including photos, images, video, audio files, or artwork created or presented through electronic means; a gallery of artwork viewed electronically through any device.17
* Document - To create a record of (something) through writing, film, photography, etc.
* Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
* Symbol - An image, sign, or element, such as color, that is understood, by convention or context, to suggest some other meaning.22
* Technology - The mechanical aspects and contexts of arts production, including hardware, software, networks, code, etc.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students used pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology in the planning of a choreographic dance sequence and now are using these same methods to document an original dance sequence after it has been created. In the next level, students will reconstruct a given dance sequence documented in pictures, symbols, writing, or media technology.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are recorded by dancers and choreographers through notation and symbolic documentation.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to symbolically represent, describe in written text, or use media technology to record the actions of an original dance sequence
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating a record of an original dance sequence through/by using pictures or symbols, writing, or media technology
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students will need to develop a method to document their choreography, this could be through written journal, video recording, pictures or symbols to annotate movement, facings, dynamics, etc. The student will need to be assessed on their ability to document their original choreography
* This thread of objectives is designed for students to record choreography they create, not observe. Pair with CR.1.2 and CR.2.1
* Teachers can use the Language of Dance Movement Symbols or have students create their own symbolic representation of movements
* When using student-created images, students need to have the opportunity to explain why they drew the symbols they did so that it can be determined if their reasoning is sound
* Note the conjunction “or” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to document an original dance sequence using pictures OR symbols, writing, OR media technology. It is up to the discretion of the teacher to decide which the student is to use or autonomy can be given to the student to decide
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Teach (or review) how to use the elements of dance in choreography
* Teach (or review) the various methods for recording choreographic ideas through pictures, symbols, or writing
* Discuss how media technology can be used to support the creative process of designing and documenting choreography
* Demonstrate how pictures, writing, symbols, and media technology can be used to document choreography

In the next level, students will explain how the elements of dance and choreographic forms are used in various genres of dance.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that the techniques and elements in a dance work can be objectively identified and analyzed.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The elements of dance
* How to analyze dance for the use of dance elements
* How to recognize the elements of dance when observing or performing movement in various dance genres
* How to linguistically discuss choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the reasoning for how the elements of dance are used in the performance of various dance genres
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Having a video recording to observe will help facilitate conversation as it can be rewound and paused when discussing. A recording would also provide the opportunity for the student to watch it several times to help develop their analytical skills
* This could also be through physical instruction of dance that students embody and then must analyze rather than through observation of others in performance
* This objective shows how the elements are commonly used in all dance styles and genres
* Note that genres is plural. The student should be analyzing how dance elements are used in a variety of movement genres over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom instruction on the elements of dance
* Demonstrate explaining how the elements of dance are used in a variety of genres
* Provide examples of dance in various genres for observation or physical embodiment (these can be generated by the teacher, student, or professionally created and performed)
* Guide students on how to observe dance for evidence of the dance elements of body, space, time, and energy/effort
* Design activities for students to explain how the elements of dance are used in various dance genres
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to explain how the elements of dance are used in various dance genres
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Design a common checklist or graphic organizer for students to use when watching different dances from various genres over the course of study
* After observing a work of a specific genre, students can write a critique or record their analysis of how the elements of dance were used. This assignment would be repeated, having students analyze different dance genres in each assignment
* Students can be shown or guided to research and find examples of dances performed in a variety of dance genres and write one critique or record themselves explaining how the elements of dance were commonly used in each of the examples

7.RE.1.2 Describe how personal experiences and perspectives influence interpretations of dance works.
Glossary
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Interpret(ations) - When Responding - To explain or tell the meaning of: present in understandable terms.14
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
* Works - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified multiple meanings for choreographic works, citing evidence and now describe how personal experiences and perspectives influence interpretations of dance works. In the next level, students compare meanings interpreted from dance with the original artistic intent inferred from contextual information.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer’s interpretation of a work may align with or differ from the artist’s original intent.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How personal experiences shape interpretations of art
* How individual perspectives can vary in how dance is observed and interpreted
* Dance works can have multiple interpretations based on the personal experiences and perspectives of the viewer
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of how interpretations of dance works are shaped by personal experiences and individual perspectives
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective teaches students that interpretations of work are shaped by personal experiences. Awareness of how personal experiences and preferences influence interpretations promotes an understanding that how individuals respond to art is a unique experience
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to describe how personal experiences AND perspectives influence interpretations of dance works. Both experiences and perspectives need to be considered
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead discussion on how personal experience and perspectives can shape interpretation of art work
* Give examples of how personal experiences and perspectives influence interpretations of dance
* Provide examples of age-appropriate choreography for observation or physical embodiment that can be interpreted for meaning (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work)
* Create classroom activities to provide student practice in using personal experiences and perspectives to interpret choreographed works
* Design activities for students to describe how personal experiences and perspectives influence interpretations of dance works
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to describe how personal experiences and perspectives influence interpretations of dance works
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Lead a classroom discussion about how interpretations are individualistic and shaped by a person’s personal experiences and perspectives. Then have students observe a dance and write a critique that includes their interpretation of a work and then to add the prompt for them to respond in describing how their personal experiences or perspective informed their interpretation

7.RE.2.1 Explain how personal preferences for dance change when considering multiple viewpoints.
Glossary
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
* Viewpoint - The position from which something or someone is observed.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students explained how personal preferences for dance develop with increased exposure and now explain how personal preferences for dance change when considering multiple viewpoints. In the next level, students will explain how personal preferences for dance are influenced by experience, culture, and media.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with dance works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to explain personal preferences for dance
* How to consider multiple viewpoints for dance
* That consideration of multiple viewpoints can have an impact on personal preference
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the reasoning for the changes in personal preferences for dance when multiple viewpoints are considered
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective was designed to teach students that ignorance can sometimes negatively impact how dance is enjoyed. Appreciation for art improves with knowledge of purpose/reason of the work and an understanding of how to consider multiple viewpoints when evaluating work
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Provide examples of dance in a wide array of artists, styles, forms, or genres for observation and physical embodiment
* Create classroom activities that encourage students to share personal preferences about a work, style, or genre of dance
* Lead discussion and student investigation into the history, artistic intention, or personal preferences of others about the work, style, or genre
* Guide students in examining how preferences can change when there is a better understanding of the history, culture, artistic intent, purpose or when considering multiple viewpoints
* Design activities for students to explain how considerations of multiple viewpoints can influence personal preference in dance
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to explain how personal preferences for dance change when considering multiple viewpoints
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Students can compare multiple viewpoints to include, artistic intent, opinions of others, the historic or culture background, or the viewpoint of how the dance physically feels to perform versus emotionally feels, etc. then have the student write or verbally share how their personal preference can change when taking the other viewpoints into consideration.
* After a classroom discussion on how the consideration of multiple viewpoints can impact personal preference, have students reflect on when their preference for something may have changed after taking other viewpoints into consideration, then have them explain how this can apply to preferences for watching or performing dance of different styles, cultures, or genres

7.RE.2.2 Identify strengths and areas for improvement based on student-generated criteria when viewing dance performance and choreography.
Glossary
* Criteria - The rules or guidelines used for categorizing or judging; in arts assessment, the rules or guidelines used to judge the quality of a student’s performance.28
* Generate - To create by means of a defined process : produce.14
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
* Improve - To enhance in value or quality : make better.14
* Performance - Experience of engaging in the act of presenting [artistic skills] in a classroom or private or public venue.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identify strengths and areas for improvement based on established criteria when viewing dance performance and choreography. At the current level, students complete this same task using student-genered criteria. In the next level, students will provide guidance for the improvement of a dance performance using established criteria.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to generate criteria for the evaluation of observations in dance performance and choreography
* How to use student-generated criteria in the evaluation of strengths and areas for improvement in dance performance and choreography
* How to linguistically discuss movement performance and choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming strengths and areas for improvement in dance performance and choreography based on feedback from student-generated criteria
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students understand that they can use feedback to improve and also give feedback to help someone else improve. It is important to identify strengths when evaluating work so students build efficacy and can continue to build on their success. Identifying areas for growth is an important aspect of constructive criticism. Students may need support in understanding the positive intention of constructive criticism
* It is important that students are guided to develop criteria that is relevant to the work being evaluated
* Note the conjunction “and” used twice in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to identify strengths AND areas for improvement based on student-generated criteria when viewing dance performance AND choreography. Areas of both strengths and improvement need to be identified, however the evaluation can be made separately of performance and choreography when necessary
* This objective can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students use feedback from peers, instructors, and self to refine dance performance quality
* This objective can also be paired with other CR and PR objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how relevant criteria used in evaluation of choreography and performance helps dancers to improve their skills
* Demonstrate how to generate and use criteria in evaluation of how a dance is crafted and performed
* Brainstorm with students in identifying relevant criteria to be used in evaluation of a choreography or performance
* Guide students in crafting a rubric that has descriptive performance ratings for the criteria that is most essential to the dance performance and choreography being observed
* Assist students in using their criteria to evaluate dance choreography and performance
* Design activities for students to use student-generated criteria to identify strengths and areas for improvement in dance performance and choreography
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to identify strengths and areas for improvement based on student-generated criteria when viewing dance performance and choreography
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students examine the essential elements of the work that is to be evaluated. For example, in pairing this objective with CR.1.2, students would look for the use of simple choreographic forms and selection of abstract phrases in the construction of a movement phrase. If using PR.1.3 students’ criteria would include clarity of movement and demonstration of movement qualities in performance. Have students use rubrics to evaluate dances they create and/or perform to generate feedback for self and others
* Use video to record dances so students can watch themselves or refer back to the video to evaluate the work of their peers
* Students could also use this criteria to evaluate a professional work and write or record their suggestions of the strengths and where they could envision improvements to the choreography and performance
