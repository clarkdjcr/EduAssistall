# Science - Kindergarten

Kindergarten Science: 2023 Support Document
Kindergarten

Purpose:
The 2023 North Carolina K-12 Science Standards were officially approved by the State Board of Education on July 6, 2023. The Standard Course of Study is designed to help students continually grow their science knowledge and abilities. The standards are intended to foster conceptual understanding, develop scientifically literate students, and provide opportunities to build knowledge and practices within each grade band or course.

The K-12 Science Support Documents serve as resources for teachers, administrators, science specialists, instructional coaches, parents, and other stakeholders in understanding the standards and planning instruction. They do not serve as curricula nor as a means to limit instruction in the classroom, but rather are intended to serve as a guide for discerning and describing features of students and their work necessary to meet grade level proficiency.

Science Teaching and Learning:
A coherent and consistent approach throughout grades K-12 is key to realizing the vision for science education embodied in A Framework for K-12 Science Education.  Students should actively engage in science and engineering practices and apply crosscutting concepts in every grade level to deepen their understanding of each field’s disciplinary core ideas.

Science Safety:
It is the responsibility of teachers and school administrators to use appropriate legal standards and best professional practices under duty of care to make the science laboratory safe.  Follow the Public School Unit’s guidelines regarding safety in the classroom. For more information, review the National Science Teaching Association’s position statement on safety and school science instruction.

* Earth’s Place in the Universe

	Dimension 1: Science and Engineering Practice (SEP)
[Science and Engineering Practice included in Objective]
Practices refer to the things that scientists and engineers do and how they actively engage in their work. This section provides further clarification for what students should be doing to engage in this practice. There are various ways that each practice can be used, as articulated in A Framework for K-12 Science Education (NRC, 2012) and the NSTA’s SEP Matrix.

The Framework identifies a small number of disciplinary core ideas that all students should learn with increasing depth and sophistication, from Kindergarten through grade twelve. Key to the vision expressed in the Framework is for students to learn these disciplinary core ideas in the context of science and engineering practices. The importance of combining science and engineering practices and disciplinary core ideas is stated in the Framework as follows: Standards and performance expectations that are aligned to the framework must take into account that students cannot fully understand scientific and engineering ideas without engaging in the practices of inquiry and the discourses by which such ideas are developed and refined. At the same time, they cannot learn or show competence in practices except in the context of specific content (NRC Framework, 2012, p. 218).
The eight practices of science and engineering that the Framework identifies as essential for all students to learn and describes in detail are listed below:
1. Asking questions (for science) and defining problems (for engineering)
2. Developing and using models
3. Planning and carrying out investigations
4. Analyzing and interpreting data
5. Using mathematics and computational thinking
6. Constructing explanations (for science) and designing solutions (for engineering)
7. Engaging in argument from evidence
8. Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information
	Dimension 2: Crosscutting Concepts (CCC)
Crosscutting concepts are conceptual tools used along with the SEP and DCI to help students explain and predict science phenomena. They support students in making connections between disciplines and provide a context for sense-making. Teachers should encourage students to frame their thinking around the terminology of the CCC through questions and classroom discussions.
The most relevant Crosscutting Concepts may vary in light of the specific learning objectives and how the teacher structures the topic. Teachers should choose the one(s) that best support(s) their instructional goals and help(s) students make connections across different aspects of the content.
Suggested Crosscutting Concept(s): [Crosscutting Concept Name(s)] A Framework for K-12 Science Education identifies seven crosscutting concepts that bridge disciplinary boundaries and unite core ideas. Their purpose is to help students deepen their understanding of the disciplinary core ideas and develop a coherent and scientifically based view of the world (p. 83).
The seven crosscutting concepts of the Framework are:
1. Patterns
2. Cause and effect: Mechanism and explanation
3. Scale, proportion, and quantity
4. Systems and system models
5. Energy and matter: Flows, cycles, and conservation.
6. Structure and function
7. Stability and change

Matter and Its Interactions
	Standard and Objectives:
PS.K.1 Understand how objects are described based on their physical properties and how they are used.
PS.K.1.1 Analyze and interpret data to classify objects by physical properties (size, color, shape, texture, weight and flexibility).
PS.K.1.2 Engage in argument from evidence to summarize how different materials (clay, wood, cloth, paper, etc.) are used based on their physical properties.
	Objective: PS.K.1.1 Analyze and interpret data to classify objects by physical properties (size, color, shape, texture, weight and flexibility).
	Dimension 1: Science and Engineering Practice: Analyze and Interpret Data: With guidance and support, students record information (observations, thoughts, and ideas) and use observations (first hand or from media) to describe properties of objects (NSTA SEP Matrix).
	Dimension 2: Crosscutting Concepts: Patterns
Note: Crosscutting concepts (CCC) are used in combination with SEPs to help students with sensemaking in science. Please note teachers may choose one or more of the seven Crosscutting Concepts for a given standard (A Framework for K-12 Science Education).
	Dimension 3: Disciplinary Core Ideas:
* PS1.A Matter can be described and classified by its observable properties (e.g., visual, aural, textural), by its uses, and by whether it occurs naturally or is manufactured. Objects or samples of a substance can be weighed, and their size can be described and measured (A Framework for K-12 Science Education).
	What does it look like to demonstrate proficiency?

Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Classify - Determining that something belongs to a category based on relevant features or patterns

* Emphasis is on the properties of matter through direct observations using the five senses (sight, touch, hearing, smell and taste) in order to describe an object.
* Students collect data on physical properties of objects including size, color, shape, texture, weight and flexibility.
* Students accurately use vocabulary such as large, small, round, bumpy, rough, smooth, heavy, light, stiff, bendable etc. to describe objects.
* Students make statements about the data collected on the physical properties of objects such as, “there are more blue objects than red objects”.
* Analyze and Interpret Data - Students classify or organize the data collected into categories and explain their reasoning for the classification. Students use and share pictures, drawings, and/or writings of observations with teacher and peers.
	Academic Language
Question/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:
* What are some similarities between these objects? (e.g., same color, same size, etc.) What is the same about these objects? (Students identify how two or more objects have the same property such as two objects are blue or two objects are flexible)
* What are some differences between the objects? What is different about these objects? (Students identify how two or more objects are different such as one object is smooth and another object is rough.)
* What is one way you could sort (classify) or group these objects? How would you create groups of objects that are similar to each other? (Students sort objects into categories based on properties, such as objects that are smooth, objects that are yellow, objects that are flexible etc.)
* Describe the properties you are using to classify the objects (e.g., If students are given a rock they describe it as hard, rough, and gray. If they have a pipe cleaner they can describe it as flexible, fuzzy, and red.)
* Which group has more of a certain property (e.g., color, shape, size, texture, weight, flexibility)? (Students state which group has the most, e.g., There are more circles.)

Words to support student discourse: same, different, data, sort, physical properties, size, color, shape, texture, weight, flexibility, observation, large, small, round, bumpy, rough, smooth, heavy, light, stiff, bendable, senses, sight, touch, hearing, smell, taste

* Engage in Argument from Evidence- Make a claim about the use of material that is supported by the material’s properties.
	Academic Language
Questions/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:
* List three physical properties of this object. (e.g., When shown a piece of cloth, students could answer flexible, blue, soft, and light)
* How can this object be used? What do you notice about the object using your five senses that might give you an idea of how it could be used? How do you know? (Students describe how material is used; based on properties)
* List physical properties of an object that would be useful for e.g., building, clothing, cooking. (Possible answers include building materials need to be strong and hard and clothing needs to be flexible)
* Based on physical properties you have observed/explored, what do you think this object could be used for? Explain why. (e.g., paper is good for the pages of a book because you can print on them and they are flexible when you turn the page, but paper is not good for shoes, because it rips easily and absorbs water).

Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Compare - Finding similarities and differences between two or more objects, events, ideas, problems, or situations

* Emphasis is on positional words to demonstrate the location or place of an object in relation to another object.
* Students describe the relationship between objects using words such as in front of, behind, between, on top of, under, above, below, beside.
* Students use objects or drawings to demonstrate the location of an object.
* Students select which item is in front of, behind, between, on top of, under, above, below, or beside something else.
* Students demonstrate where an object is placed by using positional words to describe its location.
* Developing and Using a Model- With guidance and support, compare models representing positional relationships to identify common features and differences.
   * e.g., Describe a pond scene, either in real life, or a picture/diagram: e.g., the frog is in front of the grass. The grass is beside the pond, the frog is on top of the lily pad, above the water, etc. Identify which object is below the lilypad (fish, frog, etc.). Place the frog beside/next to the fish.... How would you describe the location of the frog/fish.... Where does the fish live? (under the water)...

* Students name different ways things move (e.g., straight, zigzag, round and round, back and forth, fast and slow)
* Students use vocabulary (e.g., straight, zigzag, round and round, back and forth, fast and slow) to describe how an object or organism is moving.

* Students know that Earth pulls down on all objects.
* Plan and Carry Out Investigations - With guidance and support, plan and conduct an investigation about how things move in collaboration with peers.

	Academic Language
Questions/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:
* Describe how the object moves when it is dropped (e.g., when dropping a tissue, or a feather, students could describe that they move slowly and move back and forth as they fall to the ground).
* When observing an organism moving (in person or video), how can you describe the motion? Can you use more than one word? (e.g., if students watch a video clip of vultures flying around prey, they could describe the motion as slow and round and round.)
* Can you give examples of things that move back and forth? (e.g., a swing, a rocking chair, a door)
   * Teacher note: To reinforce the concepts, continue to ask students for examples of other types of movement (straight, zigzag, round and round, back and forth, fast and slow)
* Discuss with students how they can investigate the movement of objects and/or organisms to answer a question.
   * What objects and/or organisms will we use? (e.g., look for objects in the classroom or natural world)
   * Make a prediction about how you think the objects will move.
   * Will we drop, toss, or roll the objects?
   * How will we collect data from our observations on how the objects move? (e.g., take a video, draw a picture, dictate observations for teacher to record)
   * Compare observations to students' initial predictions of how the objects will move.
   * Based on the observations, what is similar and different between how the objects and/or organisms move?
* Based on what you already know about how things move, what predictions can we make on how another object or organism will move? (e.g., If I know a pencil falls straight down when dropped, I can predict that a pen will also fall straight down when dropped.)

	What does it look like to demonstrate proficiency?

Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Summarize - Condensing larger information into a general theme or major point(s)

* Emphasis is on the characteristics that make something living or nonliving.
* Students know living things have the following characteristics:
   * Structure: Students know that living things are made of parts and each of the parts has a role to support life.
   * Growth and Changes: Students observe and compare how different organisms grow, develop and change over time.
   * Movement: Students know how various organisms move, noting similarities and differences.
   * Basic needs: Students know that all organisms have basic needs [e.g., air, water, food, (light for plants)].
* Students determine if something is living or nonliving based on its characteristics. Living things have all of the characteristics (structure, growth and change, movement, basic needs). Nonliving things do not have one or more of the characteristics.
* Engage in Argument from Evidence - With guidance and support, construct an argument with evidence to support a claim that living organisms have different characteristics than nonliving things
* Teacher Note: At this grade level, it is appropriate to define living things as anything that is alive or has ever been alive and nonliving as anything that is not now and has never been alive.
	Academic Language
Questions/Sentence Stems that utilize academic language:
* What makes something living? (e.g., it has structures that perform life processes; it grows and changes; it moves by itself; it has basic needs such as air, water, food, light for plants)
* What makes something nonliving? (e.g., it may have some, but not all of the characteristics of a living thing)
* Is this living or nonliving? How do you know? (e.g., if a student was shown a rock, they would state the claim that the rock is not living because it does not grow. If a student was shown a plant, they would state the claim that it is living because it has parts that support its life, grows over time, moves towards light, and needs water, air, food, and light.)

* When looking at and/or creating models, what different body parts do animals have to get food and/or resources? (e.g., dogs use their teeth to get food and resources, but birds use their beak)

Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Summarize - Condensing larger information into a general theme or major point(s)

	Academic Language
Sentence stems/Questions that utilize academic language:

* How does today’s weather compare to the weather from _________? It’s not hot today and has not been all month.”)
