# Arts Education - Sixth Grade

Parent Guide for Sixth Grade Dance

The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The grade 6-8 standards and objectives are written for middle school students who are taught by a licensed dance educator for at least 90 minutes per week.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students investigate how dance can express and reflect values of civilizations from around the world and how skills and concepts from other content areas can be used to develop dance ideas. Students also examine physical alignment concerns and mental stressors common to dancers.
CN.2 Explore - Students learn of the skills, characteristics, and behaviors necessary for success in various dance careers and examine how digital media has been used in a variety of dance performances.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students explore spontaneous movement invention using multiple dance elements in response to musical or verbal prompts and create dances with abstract movement that have a beginning, middle, and end. Students also plan a dance sequence by using pictures, writing, symbols, or media technology.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to adapt dance phrases by changing how slowly or quickly the movements are performed and learn terminology related to the copyright rules used to guide artists in the production of new works, including using musical accompaniment.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Perform - Students examine how feedback from instructors, peers, self-evaluation is used to improve performance and to demonstrate movement skills with strength, anatomical alignment, flexibility, stamina, and coordination. Students also use instructional support to help them demonstrate movement qualities and execute each step in a dance clearly and completely.
PR.2 Develop - Students compare themes, topics, or music choices for a group performance and use instructional support to demonstrate safe and respectful behaviors when performing and observing performances in various cultural settings. Students also learn terminology of production elements and identify their use in various performance situations.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students examine how the elements of dance (body, space, time, effort quality) are used in different dance techniques and also give reasoning in their multiple interpretations of choreographed works.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students examine how increased exposure to dance impacts personal preferences and use criteria to provide feedback in evaluation of performance and choreography.

How to Support Your Dance Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your dance student:
* Clear a little space so your child can move freely and safely.
* Watch dance from different cultures online or in your community and discuss the cultural values the dance might represent.
* Encourage your child to use ideas from other subject areas for a dance, such as making a dance about the weather cycle.
* Talk about how skills dancers use, such as discipline, focus, teamwork, help in dance-related careers.
* Put on a song and ask your child to create movements that match the rhythm or words.
* Have your child make up a short dance with a beginning, middle, and end that tells a simple story. Encourage them to sketch, write, or record videos of their own dance ideas as a starting point.
* Challenge your child to perform a dance phrase using different tempos.
* Talk about lighting, music, or costumes in a performance you watch together.
* Watch dance together and have your child identify how they see the elements of dance, then discuss several possible interpretations of the work.
