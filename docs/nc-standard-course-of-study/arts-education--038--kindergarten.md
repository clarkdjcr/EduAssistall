# Arts Education - Kindergarten

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Theatre Arts Standards Unpacking Kindergarten
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for theatre arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with theatre build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 theatre standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed theatre educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Theatre Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 theatre programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

K.CN.1.1 Identify types of theatre in local and global communities.
Glossary
* Identify - To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.27
* Community - A unified body of individuals: such as a group of people with a common characteristic or interest living together within a larger society; a body of persons of common and especially professional interests scattered through a larger society.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students are identifying types of theatre in local and global communities. In the next level they will compare theatre from a variety of cultures.
Enduring Understanding
A theatre artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of stories, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Various types of theatre found in the local community
* Various types of theatre found in global communities
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Recognizing, listing, or naming types of theatre
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Theatre exists in a variety of formats both in the local community and throughout the world.
* This objective states “local and global communities” so it is imperative that both are addressed.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare a list of theatre types from local and global communities
* Share examples with students, highlighting characteristics which makes each unique
* Demonstrate how recognizing and naming theatre types
* Provide opportunities for students to practice recognizing and naming theatre types as a group and individually, giving guidance as appropriate.
* Monitor individual students’ ability to recognize and name types of local and global theatre through formative and summative assessment, offering feedback as needed.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Folk Tales (from any nation)
* Appalachian Storytelling
* Japanese Kabuki Theatre
* Japanese Bunraku Puppets
* Italian Commedia dell’arte Masks
* Community Theatre
* Children’s Theatre
* NC Outdoor Drama

K.CR.1.1 Improvise stories and situations using dramatic play.
Glossary
* Dramatic Play - Spontaneous dramatic enactment often done by children pretending or imitating while playing.21
* Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14
* Situations - A combination of circumstances at a given moment.21
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students are improvising stories and situations using dramatic play. In the next level, they will improvise a variety of activities and scenarios using dramatic play.
Enduring Understanding
Exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of theatre artists to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The instructions of dramatic play
* Components of stories
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Spontaneously enacting stories and situations
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Dramatic play allows children to explore and express ideas, emotions, and experiences. By improvising stories, children develop their imagination and creativity. This type of play encourages them to become characters, explore different perspectives, and engage in problem solving.
* Dramatic play encourages children to think creatively and solve problems in the context of the stories they are improvising. This fosters a growth mindset and helps children gain confidence in their ability to create and innovate.
* Since this objective has the language “stories and situations” both must be considered to master this objective.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare materials which will inspire spontaneous storytelling and imaginative play.
* Demonstrate how to participate in dramatic play by modeling taking on a character, invent a story, or respond to imagined situations
* Guide students in exploring characters, emotions, and scenarios through prompts, encouraging collaboration, providing guidance when necessary, and allowing each child to contribute to the improvisation process
* Provide support and inspiration such as costumes, props, or story ideas to spark participation and foster imaginative play
* Monitor each students’ ability to spontaneously act out stories and situations through formative and summative assessment.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Story creation with props
* Role-Playing everyday situations
* "What happens next-" story completion activities
* Teacher-led scenarios for dramatic play (Examples: Superheros, Detectives, Restaurants, Animals, etc.)

K.CR.1.2 Communicate the main idea of an original story or scene using pre-writing activities with support.
Glossary
* Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
* Support - Assistance provided temporarily to enable a student to perform a task that would be difficult to perform unaided, best implemented in a manner that helps develop that student’s capacity to eventually perform the task independently.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students are communicating the main idea of an original story or scene using pre-writing activities with support. In the next level, they will create an original sequence of events by acting, drawing, or writing with support.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are recorded by theatre artists and playwrights through both written and visual documentation.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Pre-writing activities
* Main idea
* Components of a story
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Conveying the main ideas of a story or scene through pre-writing activities
Evidence of Mastery
* Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Kindergarten students are just beginning to understand that stories have a structure (beginning, middle, and end). Through pre-writing activities, students can start to recognize the parts of a story, such as characters, setting, and events, even if they can’t fully write them down yet.
* Since this objective has the language “story or scene” either original artifact may be created. Students are not required to do each to master this objective
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare engaging story prompts. (Example: A Day at the zoo, My Birthday Party, A Trip to the Beach etc.) and the materials for the visual storyboards to draw or arrange stories beginning, middle, and end.
* Demonstrate how to use the visual storyboards by drawing a beginning, middle, and end of a simple example story.
* Guide students in the creation of their original scene or story with thoughtful questions.
* Provide templates, sentence starters, visuals, and other pre-writing supports for planning stories.
* Monitor each students’ ability to convey the main ideas of a story through pre-writing activities through formative and summative assessment, documenting their use of dramatic play elements as evidence of mastery
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Show examples of original stories or scenes through picture books, illustrations, or oral storytelling.
* Provide visual prompts such as picture cards or story sequence charts to help students recognize the key parts of a story (beginning, middle, end).
* Offer sentence starters or prompts to help students express their main ideas.

K.CR.1.3 Create simple environments for dramatic play by arranging set pieces.
Glossary
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
* Environments - Physical surroundings that establish place, time, and atmosphere/ mood; the physical conditions that reflect and affect the emotions, thoughts, and actions of characters.21
* Dramatic Play - Spontaneous dramatic enactment often done by children pretending or imitating while playing.21
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because students are creating simple environments for dramatic play by arranging set pieces. In the next level, they will create simple costumes and props to enhance dramatic play using available resources.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are actualized by theatre designers and craftspersons intentionally to fulfill dramatic purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Components of simple environments
* Instructions for dramatic play
* Considerations for set placement
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating new environments
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Set pieces help students connect with the environment and story they are playing. By organizing set pieces these items reflect story and action. These pieces may be created by students or provided by the teacher
* This objective will be most meaningful when supporting improvisations (CR.1.1), original creation (CR.1.2), or as part of presenting works (PR.1.2). This objective has students creating the technical elements used in PR.2.3
* To assess each student's ability to create environments, give students opportunities to take turns participating in dramatic play while tracking their individual engagement
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare materials for set pieces (e.g., fabric, cardboard, scarves, paint, markers, yarn) or use pre-made items. Share images or videos of simple set designs from children’s theatre or familiar stories, and discuss how these elements help tell the story
* Demonstrate how set pieces can be used by arranging them in an initial dramatic play scenario
* Guide students in arranging set pieces for a follow-up dramatic play, gradually releasing responsibility so they arrange them independently next time
* Provide temporary support as needed. Encourage students to think through what items are needed in their environment (e.g., basket, doorway, tree) to support their storytelling
* Monitor individual students’ ability to create simple environments for dramatic play by arranging set pieces through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Discuss the purpose of sets in storytelling.
* Discuss types of set pieces
* Dramatic play: Pirate Adventure, Animal Characters, Storybook Characters
* Environments for CR.1.1, CR.1.2, or CR.2.1
* Environments for PR.1.2, PR.2.1, or PR.2.3

K.CR.2.2 Explain the difference between original and copied theatrical ideas or works.
Glossary
* Copying - An imitation, transcript, or reproduction of an original work (such as a letter, a painting, a table, or a dress).14
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Ideas - An entity (such as a thought, concept, sensation, or image) actually or potentially present to consciousness.14
* Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
* Work - Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort: artistic production.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline by establishing foundational knowledge of the difference between original and copied visual art ideas or works. At the current level, students explain the difference between copied and original ideas or works of art. In the next level, students will explain how and when to give credit to others when creating derivative work.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that respect for originality and ownership of intellectual property is shown through the ethical use of artistic works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Original ideas are new ideas not previously known or seen
* Copied theatrical arts ideas or works are created by doing the same thing as something or someone else
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the process of copying visual arts ideas versus creating new visual arts ideas or works
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students should be able to answer the question, “What is copied theatre, and what is original theatre?” to show mastery of this objective.
* This objective can be paired with CR.2.1 when students create their own original stories
* Note the conjunctions “and” “or” used in this objective. Because the student is being asked to differentiate between originality AND copying, both concepts need to be addressed; however, whether the student is explaining the difference between original and copied ideas OR original and copied works is dependent on the learning activity and up to the teacher.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare student friendly definitions and examples of original and copied works.
* Demonstrate describing how the process of copying musical ideas differs from creating new theatrical ideas or works. Tying this discussion to the works in CR.1.2 will maximize instructional time.
* Guide students to explain the differences between two prepared work samples. Lead a discussion exploring the value of origination over copying for developing themselves as artists.
* Provide sentence stems and word lists to scaffold student explanations.
* Monitor individual students’ ability to explain the difference between original and copied works while correctly using theatrical vocabulary through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Venn diagram comparing the process of origination and copying
* Student-created definitions/anchor chart
* Student interview
* Pair-share
* End-of-year work review, explaining the differences between original works and those created by copying
