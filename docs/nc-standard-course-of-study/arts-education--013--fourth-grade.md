# Arts Education - Fourth Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Fourth Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 Dance standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed Visual Arts educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

4.CN.1.1 Describe styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina.
Glossary
* Culture - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
* Customs - A usage or practice common to many or to a particular place or class or habitual with an individual; the whole body of usages, practices, or conventions that regulate social life.14
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Indigenous - Of or relating to the earliest known inhabitants of a place and especially of a place that was colonized by a now-dominant group.14
* Styles - Specific movement characteristics, qualities, or principles that give it distinctive identity; style may also refer to the unique artistic choices of a particular [artist].17
* Traditions - Pattern of practices and beliefs within a societal group.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students described the dance forms found in their local community or region. At the current level, students describe styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina. Students expand on this knowledge in the next level to describe this influence across the United States.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a dance artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shapes the creation of movements, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The customs and traditions of Indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina
* Dance Artists who have influenced the cultural customs or traditions of dance in North Carolina
* Dance Artists who have been influenced by the customs or traditions of cultural or Indigenous groups in North Carolina
* Styles of dance that reflect customs and traditions of various cultural groups from North Carolina
* How dance styles may have shaped customs and traditions of Indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of how dance styles or the work of dance artists has influenced or been influenced by North Carolinian Indigenous and cultural groups
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Many popular dance styles and artists are influenced by their local cultural dances
* Expose students to cultural diversity in North Carolina
* Artists and artistic styles can have an impact on the traditions and customs of cultural groups
* Note the conjunctions “and” and “or” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to describe styles AND dancers who have influenced, OR been influenced by, the customs AND traditions of indigenous AND other cultural groups in North Carolina. Depending on the learning activity, the teacher can decide if students are examining styles and dancers who have influenced or been influenced by. However, both styles and dancers as well as the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina must be considered. Given the scope of this objective, it may be helpful to break this objective down into multiple assessments over the course of study
* Collaborate with the Social Studies teacher to learn of units the students are studying
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Research and provide examples of cultural and indigenous dance styles in North Carolina
* Provide examples of artists and styles that have impacted or been impacted by customs and cultural traditions of Indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina
* Model describing styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina
* Guide student investigation into artists and styles that have influenced or been influenced by cultural customs and traditions of diverse cultural groups in North Carolina
* Design learning activities for students to summarize the characteristics of the styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to describe styles and dancers who have influenced, or been influenced by, the customs and traditions of indigenous and other cultural groups in North Carolina
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Appalachian Clogging
* Scottish Highland Dance in North Carolina
* Contemporary or social dance that reflects indigenous movement traditions seen in Powwows, Hoop Dancing, the Grass Dance
* NC Youth Tap Ensemble influenced by early tap dancers
* Square Dancing was influenced by European settlers doing reels and quadrilles
* Step Dance (common at HBCUs) was influenced by traditional African dance

* Identifying similarities and differences between dance concepts and concepts of other content areas
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form

Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Concepts found in dance can also be found in non-dance content areas
* Dance can be used as a way to further understand non-dance concepts
* Non-dance concepts can be used as a way to help us understand dance concepts
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom instruction on the elements and other concepts of dance and choreography
* Have knowledge, or draw on student knowledge, of concepts from other disciplines students may be exploring at this grade level
* Collaborate with teachers of other content disciplines
* Demonstrate comparing similarities and differences in the concepts used in dance with those found in other content areas
* Guide student investigation of the similarities and differences between the various concepts of other content areas and concepts used in dance
* Design activities for students to compare similarities and differences in concepts used in dance with concepts from other content areas
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to compare dance concepts with concepts from other content areas
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Research and integrate dance and non-arts/non-dance concepts so that true integration is present
* Math: Use choreographic tools in such a manner that the properties of multiplication can be applied to the movement sequence. For example, co-op to show associative property, retrograde to show commutative property. Students can also use the concept of duration and apply the distributive property to change the duration of their entire movement sequence
* Social Studies: Provide resources for students to research in class various NC landmarks and to create movements inspired by the characteristics and history of the landmarks to form a larger sequence. Use dance concepts like levels, pathways, types of shapes, to create movements
* Science: Use movement and the dance element of energy to represent the different stages of the rock cycle
* Literacy: Students can create dances based on themes of stories

Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:

4.CR.1.1 Improvise movement based on multiple dance elements.
Glossary
* Dance element - The key components of movement; movement of the body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement; see Elements of Dance Organizer by Perpich Center for Arts Education (used with permission).17
* Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students improvised movement based on one dance element and now use multiple elements in improvisation. In the next level, students will improvise movement to fulfill a simple movement score using one or more dance elements.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of dancers and choreographers to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The basic dance elements and their components
* How to create spontaneous movement
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating spontaneous movement through improvisation that explores two or more of the basic dance element concepts
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Elements work in tandem with one another in dance and are used to enhance choreography and performance
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain what it means to improv
* Teach and review the elements of dance
* Have a class discussion about how the elements are related to each other and can work in tandem with creating or improvising movement
* Demonstrate and call on student volunteers to show movement improvisation based on multiple dance element
* Guide students through movement improvisation exercises based on multiple dance elements
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to improvise movement based on multiple dance elements
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Play music and task students to improvise using multiple dance elements for a certain number of counts - this can be to improv when coming on and off the floor when dancing learned choreography in groups or as dedicated improv exercises (the elements can be pre-assigned by the teacher, student, or peer)
* Dancing dolls- have students working in pairs, one partner shows a dance shape for the other to begin and end in, on a cue the student uses spontaneous movement to come out of the shape and dance in a style the shape inspires and then return back to the shape when finished
* Improvisational activities that include the teacher pulling pre-fabricated cards from four piles; one for each element of dance. For example, from the space pile, a zig zag pathway is drawn, and from the body element a curved shape is drawn. Students then have to use curved shape and zig zag pathway in their improvisation exercise for a given number of counts or time. This can also be done in partners, where one partner draws cards and the other improvises. To begin, only pull two cards and then build up to including a card from each of the elements of dance
* Using the movement walk, every student then chooses some component from each element of dance to include in their walking exploration around the space. For example, a student may choose to isolate their lower body (body element), use bound energy (energy element), walk in a curvy pathway (space element), and make sure their walk follows a particular 4-beat rhythm (time element). As the exploration continues, students can change one or more of the elements to discover new bits of choreography that come from the elements exploration

4.CR.1.2 Create dance phrases incorporating abstraction of everyday movement to fulfill a given choreographic form.
Glossary
* Abstraction - To remove movement from a particular or representative context and, by manipulating it through the elements of space, time, and energy, to create a new sequence or dance that retains the essence of the original.18
* Choreographic Form - The organization of choreography and movement to fulfill the artistic intent of a dance or dance study (for example, AB, ABA, or theme and variation); often referred to as choreographic form.2
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
* Dance Phrases - A sequence of movements that are ordered based upon a preconceived choreographic intent and contains a clear beginning, middle, and end that aligns with artistic intent. A movement phrase could be compared to a paragraph of movement and would contain two or more movement sentences.29
* Given - Qualities or traits for assessing achievement level that are provided to students.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students combined simple dance sequences to create dance phrases and now use the abstraction of everyday movement to create dance dance phrases to fulfill a given choreographic form. In the next level, students will create short dances using basic choreographic forms that communicate abstract ideas.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are actualized into choreography by dancers and choreographers to intentionally fulfill specific purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The meaning of abstraction
* Choreographic forms
* How to abstract everyday actions into dance movement
* How to use a given choreographic form in the creation of a dance phrase
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Conceiving new movement phrases that exemplify abstraction of every day movements and satisfy a given choreographic form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Choreography is created through purposeful choices by the artist
* All movement can be considered dance. Abstraction of the time, space, dynamics, and/or body shapes of daily actions can lead to inventive choreography
* “Given” in this objective means students are provided directions on which choreographic form to use
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Explain the concept of abstraction and demonstrate how everyday movement can be abstracted in choreography
* Ask for volunteer ideas for everyday movements that could be abstracted - the teacher can demonstrate their ideas or have the volunteer or all students use improvisation to abstract ideas into abstract movement
* Provide practice for all students to learn and perform short dance sequences abstracting every day movement
* Select a choreographic form for students to use (AB, ABA, Rondo, Natural Form, etc)  and explain it
* Demonstrate how dance phrases of abstracted everyday movement can be applied to the selected choreographic form
* Design learning activities in which students can create dance phrases using abstraction of everyday movement for the given choreographic form
* Guide and support the creative process
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to create dance phrases incorporating abstraction of everyday movement to fulfill a given choreographic form
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Using choreographic forms such as AB, ABA, ABCD (natural form), ABACAD (Rondo)
   * Morning routines
   * School activities
   * Greetings
   * Evening routines
4.CR.1.3 Depict the spatial relationships between two or more dancers in a dance phrase by drawing a picture or using symbols.
Glossary

A movement phrase could be compared to a paragraph of movement and would contain two or more movement sentences.29

* Symbol - An image, sign, or element, such as color, that is understood, by convention or context, to suggest some other meaning.22
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students depicted directions or spatial pathways in a dance phrase by drawing a picture, map, or symbol and now are using symbolic representation of the spatial relationship between two or more dancers in a dance phrase. In the next level, students will symbolically represent the movement qualities in a dance phrase.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that unique ideas are recorded by dancers and choreographers through notation and symbolic documentation.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* What is meant by the term, spatial relationship
* How dancers can be arranged in different spatial relationships
* How to visually represent spatial relationships between dancers
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Representing/Showing spatial relationships through symbolic notation
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This thread of objectives is designed for students to record choreography they create, not observe. Pair with CR.1.2 and CR.2.1

The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that the techniques and elements in a dance work can be objectively identified and analyzed.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to linguistically describe dance phrases
* The elements of dance
* How to recognize the elements of dance when observing or performing dance phrases
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of dance phrases that are performed using an element of dance
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Having a video recording to observe will help facilitate conversation as it can be rewound and paused when discussing. A recording would also provide the opportunity for the student to watch it several times to help develop their analytical skills
* This could also be through physical instruction of dance phrases that students embody and then must analyze rather than through observation of others in performance
* This is a good objective to use with different dance genres, especially at this age as students are exploring different styles of dance according to their budding interests. It also shows how the elements are commonly used in all dance styles and genres
* Note that phrases is plural. The student should be analyzing how multiple dance elements are used in a variety of movement phrases over the course of study
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom instruction on the elements of dance
* Demonstrate describing dance phrases that exemplify multiple elements of dance
* Provide examples of dance phrases that exemplify multiple dance elements for observation or physical embodiment (these can be generated by the teacher, student, or professionally created and performed)
* Guide students on how to observe dance for evidence of the dance elements of body, space, time, and energy/effort
* Design activities for students to describe how they observed or used multiple elements in a dance phrase
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to describe dance phrases that exemplify multiple elements of dance
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* After students have learned one or more dance phrases that include multiple elements, the students must describe how the dance elements were exemplified in the phrase(s). Students can also share answers with peers
* Students can observe a dance performed by other students or artists. The teacher can direct their attention to specific phrases or students can select movement phrases to analyze. The students then describe how the movement phrases exemplified multiple elements of dance

4.RE.1.2 Compare a personal interpretation of a dance with the interpretation of peers.
Glossary
* Compare - To locate and/or list similarities and differences.14
* Interpret - To explain or tell the meaning of: present in understandable terms.14
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students inferred meaning from dance, citing evidence and now compare a personal interpretation of a dance with the interpretation of peers. In the next level, students will describe alternative meanings that could be interpreted from the same choreographic work.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer’s interpretation of a work may align with or differ from the artist’s original intent.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How meaning can be visually represented through dance movements or choreographic works
* That there can be multiple interpretations of the same artistic work
* How to analyze the artistic choices used in dance movements or choreographic work
* How to interpret meaning from artistic choices made in dance
* How to linguistically describe dance
* How to identify commonalities and differences between personal interpretations and the interpretations of others
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Identifying similarities and differences in a personal interpretation of a dance with the interpretations of peers
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery

Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective teaches students that there are often multiple interpretations of art and not just “one” correct answer
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how dance can communicate meaning
* Demonstrate how to interpret meaning from choreography that is either performed or observed (be mindful not to impart bias but allow students to have differing perspectives)
* Provide examples of age-appropriate choreography for observation or physical embodiment that can be interpreted for meaning (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work)
* Guide students on how to interpret meaning from dance
* Provide sentence stems to help students articulate their interpretations of dance observations or performance
* Design activities for students to compare similarities and differences in their interpretation of a dance with the interpretation of peers
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to compare a personal interpretation of a dance with the interpretation of peers
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Students write (or record a video of themselves speaking) individual interpretations after watching a piece of choreography and then switch with a partner. After giving each partner a moment to read what the other person said, partners can have a discussion about their comparisons.
* Students may need an alternative to writing, therefore making an option to do an audio or video recording would be helpful
* Hold a social “after party” in which students are encouraged to move about the room discussing their interpretation of a work with classmates. After the “party” ends, the students can identify one or two classmates who had similar and/or different interpretations of the work

4.RE.2.1 Contrast personal preferences for performing and viewing dance with those of peers.
Glossary
* Align - To be in or come into precise adjustment or correct relative position.14
* Contrast - Compare or appraise in respect to differences.14
* Perform(ing) - Process of realizing artistic ideas and work through interpretation and presentation.17
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students explained preferences in observing various dance styles, forms, or genres and now contrast personal preferences for performing and viewing dance with those of peers. In the next level, students will identify aspects of a dance that align and contrast with personal preference.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with dance works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to identify personal preferences for performing and viewing dance
* The preferences of peers for performing and viewing dance
* How to identify differences in preferences of an individual with preferences of peers
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Comparing differences of individual and peer preferences for performing and viewing dance
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* This objective asks students to look at the differing of preference between themselves and peers for performing or observing dance to promote the realization that not everyone shares the same aesthetic for art
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to contrast personal preferences for performing AND viewing dance with those of peers. An individual can have varying preferences for dance they perform versus observe, and preferences between two or more individuals can also be varied
* This objective may require more than one classroom session. Students will need time to first discover their own preferences in both performing and viewing, and then will need time to describe their preferences, and then time to compare with peers
* This objective can be met at the end of a unit of student discovery and exploration of different genres, elements, and styles of dance in order to decide their own preferences. Leading to the comparison, teachers should prepare movement phrases and a series of clips or films of a variety of dance genres, themes, elements, and styles. This way, students who have not had many opportunities for focused classes or live viewings of dance will have some idea of the realm of the dance world
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how preferences in dance can vary based on the individual
* Provide examples of dance in a wide array of styles, forms, or genres for observation (these can be generated by the teacher, student, peer, or professional work) and physical embodiment
* Guide student discovery of preferences when observing and performing works from different styles, forms, or genres
* Provide sentence stems to help students form descriptive sentences about their preferences  when they are observing and performing various dance styles, forms, or genres
* Design activities for students to contrast differences in their personal preferences for performing and viewing dance with those of peers
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to contrast personal preferences for performing and viewing dance with those of peers
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students write their own genre/style preferences for viewing dance and performing dance. Tell students these preferences may differ, for example, students may enjoy watching ballet rather than hiphop but may not enjoy performing ballet over hiphop. Then ask students to describe why they prefer the things they wrote. They should use dance terminology, especially in the way of the elements of dance, and vocabulary in their descriptions. Pair or team students in small groups to make comparisons with each other

4.RE.2.2 Evaluate dances using established criteria to provide feedback for self and others.
Glossary
* Criteria - The rules or guidelines used for categorizing or judging; in arts assessment, the rules or guidelines used to judge the quality of a student’s performance.28
* Evaluate - A judgment about the worth or quality of something. In education, data from tests, tasks, or performances are used to make judgments about the success of the student or program.28
* Feedback - A reaction or response to a particular design problem or design solution.29
* Provide - To supply or make available.14
* Use - To put into action or service.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous leve,l students identified positive feedback and areas for improvement for self and others and now evaluate dances using established criteria to provide feedback for self and others. In the next level students will generate criteria used to provide self and peer feedback.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to use established criteria to identify the positive aspects of personal work
* How to use established criteria to identify areas for improvement in personal work
* How to use established criteria to identify the positive aspects of another's work
* How to use established criteria to identify areas for improvement in another's work
* How to linguistically discuss movement performance and choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Offering feedback that is complimentary in nature for personal and peer work
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Students understand that they can use feedback to improve and also give feedback to help someone else improve. It is important to identify strengths when evaluating work so students build efficacy and can continue to build on their success. Identifying areas for growth is an important aspect of constructive criticism. Students may need support in understanding the positive intention of constructive criticism
* The objective does not specify how the criteria is established. It can be given to students by the teacher or created in collaboration with students. In the next level, students generate their own criteria to be used in evaluation
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to evaluate dances using established criteria to provide feedback for self AND others. Feedback needs to be given to both self and others
* This objective can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students set personal goals for improving dance performance based on instructor and peer feedback
* This objective can also be paired with other CR and PR objectives
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Lead classroom discussion on how dancers grow from evaluation and constructive feedback
* Provide students with a rubric that has descriptive performance ratings for the criteria that is most essential to the dance being observed
* Review the descriptors and provide exemplars whenever possible for each criteria rating
* Demonstrate using the rubric to evaluate students in performance
* Design activities for students to use established criteria to evaluate their work and the work of their peers.This can be through performance of teacher or student-generated work
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to evaluate dances using established criteria to provide feedback for self and others
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students use rubrics to evaluate dances they create and/or perform to generate feedback for self and others
* Use video to record dances so students can watch themselves or refer back to the video to evaluate the work of their peers
