# Science - Earth/Environmental Science

Earth/Environmental Science: 2023 Support Document
Earth & Environmental Science
Purpose:
The 2023 North Carolina K-12 Science Standards were officially approved by the State Board of Education on July 6, 2023. The Standard Course of Study is designed to help students continually grow their science knowledge and abilities. The standards are intended to foster conceptual understanding, develop scientifically literate students, and provide opportunities to build knowledge and practices within each grade band or course.

The K-12 Science Support Documents serve as resources for teachers, administrators, science specialists, instructional coaches, parents, and other stakeholders in understanding the standards and planning instruction. They do not serve as curricula nor as a means to limit instruction in the classroom, but rather are intended to serve as a guide for discerning and describing features of students and their work necessary to meet grade level proficiency.

Science Teaching and Learning:
A coherent and consistent approach throughout grades K-12 is key to realizing the vision for science education embodied in A Framework for K-12 Science Education.  Students should actively engage in science and engineering practices and apply crosscutting concepts in every grade level to deepen their understanding of each field’s disciplinary core ideas.

Science Safety:
It is the responsibility of teachers and school administrators to use appropriate legal standards and best professional practices under duty of care to make the science laboratory safe.  Follow the Public School Unit’s guidelines regarding safety in the classroom. For more information, review the National Science Teaching Association’s position statement on safety and school science instruction.

	Dimension 1: Science and Engineering Practice (SEP)
[Science and Engineering Practice included in Objective]
Practices refer to the things that scientists and engineers do and how they actively engage in their work. This section provides further clarification for what students should be doing to engage in this practice. There are various ways that each practice can be used, as articulated in A Framework for K-12 Science Education (NRC, 2012) and the NSTA’s SEP Matrix.

The Framework identifies a small number of disciplinary core ideas that all students should learn with increasing depth and sophistication, from Kindergarten through grade twelve. Key to the vision expressed in the Framework is for students to learn these disciplinary core ideas in the context of science and engineering practices. The importance of combining science and engineering practices and disciplinary core ideas is stated in the Framework as follows: Standards and performance expectations that are aligned to the framework must take into account that students cannot fully understand scientific and engineering ideas without engaging in the practices of inquiry and the discourses by which such ideas are developed and refined. At the same time, they cannot learn or show competence in practices except in the context of specific content (NRC Framework, 2012, p. 218).
The eight practices of science and engineering that the Framework identifies as essential for all students to learn and describes in detail are listed below:
1. Asking questions (for science) and defining problems (for engineering)
2. Developing and using models
3. Planning and carrying out investigations
4. Analyzing and interpreting data
5. Using mathematics and computational thinking
6. Constructing explanations (for science) and designing solutions (for engineering)
7. Engaging in argument from evidence
8. Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information

A Framework for K-12 Science Education identifies seven crosscutting concepts that bridge disciplinary boundaries and unite core ideas. Their purpose is to help students deepen their understanding of the disciplinary core ideas and develop a coherent and scientifically based view of the world (p. 83).

The seven crosscutting concepts of the Framework are:
1. Patterns - Observed patterns of forms and events guide organization and classification, and they prompt questions about relationships and the factors that influence them.
2. Cause and effect: Mechanism and explanation - Events have causes, sometimes simple, sometimes multifaceted. A major activity of science is investigating and explaining causal relationships and the mechanisms by which they are mediated. Such mechanisms can then be tested across given contexts and used to predict and explain events in new contexts.
3. Scale, proportion, and quantity - In considering phenomena, it is critical to recognize what is relevant at different measures of size, time, and energy and to recognize how changes in scale, proportion, or quantity affect a system’s structure or performance.
4. Systems and system models - Defining the system under study—specifying its boundaries and making explicit a model of that system—provides tools for understanding and testing ideas that are applicable throughout science and engineering.
5. Energy and matter: Flows, cycles, and conservation - Tracking fluxes of energy and matter into, out of, and within systems helps one understand the systems’ possibilities and limitations.
6. Structure and function - The way in which an object or living thing is shaped and its substructure determine many of its properties and functions.
7. Stability and change - For natural and built systems alike, conditions of stability and determinants of rates of change or evolution of a system are critical elements of study.

	ESS.EES.1 Explain how Earth's position relative to the sun influences conditions on Earth.
	ESS.EES.1.1 Use models to illustrate the formation of the solar system.
	Clarification Statement:
* It is theorized that the universe began with a period of rapid expansion over 13.7 billion years ago.
* It is theorized that our solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a giant cloud of gas and dust called a solar nebula.
   * The nebular hypothesis led to the formation of the solar system and the major steps of that process.
   * The solar nebula began to collapse under its own gravity, spinning faster and flattening into a disk shape.
   * As the planets formed, they swept up the remaining gas and dust in their paths, clearing their orbits and shaping the solar system.
   * The formation of the solar system is an ongoing process, with asteroids, comets, and other celestial bodies still being formed and influenced by gravitational interactions.
* There are distinguishing characteristics that help to identify the terrestrial, Jovian, and dwarf planets in our solar system.
   * Terrestrial planets and Jovian planets have differences in surface composition due to their proximity to the Sun.
   * The inner planets, including Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars, are rocky and dense.
   * The outer planets, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune, are gas giants composed mainly of hydrogen and helium.
* The role of gravity helps us to understand the formation of astronomical objects (sun, stars, planets, asteroids, and comets)  and motion in the solar system because these patterns explain many Earth phenomena, e.g. seasons, phases of the moon, tides, and day and night.
	Boundary Statement:
* Students are not expected to explore the various stages of planetary or star formation.
* Students are not expected to analyze the processes that led to the differentiation of planets and the evolution of their atmospheres (Earth’s changing atmosphere is covered in another objective. Changes in other planets' atmospheres do not need to be discussed).
* Students are not expected to create computer simulations or models that involve intricate calculations and in-depth programming knowledge.
* Students are not expected to know specific atmospheric compositions or conditions of Jovian Planets.
* Students know that dwarf planets exist, but do not need to know all their names, atmospheric compositions or conditions.

	ESS.EES.1.2 Use mathematics and computational thinking to analyze Earth’s motion through space.
	Clarification Statement:
* Earth’s rotation causes day and night.
* Earth’s revolution around the sun and axial tilt causes changing seasons. This leads to variations in sunlight intensity, day length, and affects weather patterns and climate zones on Earth.
* Kepler’s laws of planetary motion
   * Differences in eccentricity describe the different types of orbits (Earth’s orbital path is nearly circular while a comet’s orbital path is highly eccentric)
   * Kepler’s 2nd law explains the relationship between orbital speed and distance from the sun.
   * Kepler’s 3rd law explains that the square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis.
* The gravitational interactions of the Earth, Sun, and Moon cause tides.
   * Spring Tides occur with full moon and new moon.
   * Neap Tides occur with the first and third quarter moon phase.
* The Solar Nebular Theory explains how all planets in our solar system revolve around the Sun counterclockwise and rotate counterclockwise (with the exception of Venus and Uranus).
* In space, two or more objects orbiting each other also have a center of mass. It is the point around which the objects orbit. This point is the barycenter of the objects. The barycenter is usually closest to the object with the most mass.
	Boundary Statement:
* Students are not expected to utilize complex mathematical equations to calculate orbital eccentricity.
* Students are not expected to explore the effects of precession and nutation on Earth’s rotational axis or climate patterns.
* Students are not expected to calculate Kepler’s 3rd law.

	ESS.EES.1.4 Construct an explanation to infer how incoming solar radiation interacts with Earth systems to support life.
	Clarification Statement:
* Incoming solar radiation provides the energy that drives processes in the atmosphere, hydrosphere, lithosphere, and biosphere. These interactions support life through photosynthesis, temperature regulation, and sustaining ecosystems.
* Solar radiation influences climate by heating Earth’s surface, impacting temperature patterns, atmospheric circulation, and weather phenomena.
* The majority of light that reaches Earth’s surface is visible light. Other types of light are blocked by Earth’s magnetic field (gamma radiation) and the ozone layer (ultraviolet light).
* Certain molecules, organisms, and surfaces interact with specific wavelengths of light (e.g., Ozone reacting with UV light, IR light being trapped by greenhouse gases, visible light being used for photosynthesis) and these interactions are necessary to support life on Earth.
* The amount of direct sunlight and daylight an area receives is dependent on the area’s latitude, Earth’s axial tilt, and revolution (time of year).
	Boundary Statement:
* Students are not expected to complete multi-step conversions to calculate Earth’s energy budget (how much life can be supported based on the amount of incoming solar radiation).
* Students are not expected to use, comprehend, or develop intricate climate models or simulations that involve advanced mathematical concepts and extensive data analysis.
* Students are not expected to understand the relationships between wavelength and frequency of electromagnetic waves.
* Students are not expected to explain the detailed interactions between molecules, organisms, surfaces and specific wavelengths of light.
