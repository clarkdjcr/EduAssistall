# English Language Arts - Glossary

ELA NC SCOS GLOSSARY

North Carolina ELA Standard Course of Study Glossary
This glossary for the North Carolina Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) for English Language Arts, K–12 (2026) provides definitions of key terms used throughout the standards and objectives. While these terms also appear within the grade-level unpacking documents, this resource is also offered as a standalone reference to support quick access across grade-levels.
The glossary supports a shared understanding of the ELA standards by helping educators interpret their intent, clarify key terminology, and implement expectations consistently across grade levels and instructional contexts. Because student learning develops across grades, some terms may reflect developmental nuances, and their application may vary slightly by grade-level.
Please Note: These standards will be implemented in all North Carolina schools beginning in the 2027-28 school year.

 Letter A

Academic Words - Vocabulary that characterizes written and especially academic text—but are not so common in everyday conversation. Academic words appear in all sorts of texts: academic texts, technical writing, and literary texts. These words require particular instructional attention as they are often vital to comprehension, reappear in many texts, and frequently are part of a word family or semantic network. Also known as Tier II words (Beck, McKeown, & Kucan, 2008).

Accuracy/Accurate - The truthfulness and correctness of information.

Accuracy/Accurately (CD.F.4) - To read words correctly (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Adaptation - A new work of art using elements from a pre-existing work within the same or different medium (e.g., fairy tales as ballets, plays as operas, novels as stage plays, stage plays as novels or short stories, The Three Little Pigs vs The True Story of the Three Little Pigs, Beowulf vs Grendel, etc.) (Oxford University Press, n.d.).

Adjective - A part of speech that modifies a noun and answers questions like which one, what kind, or how many.

Adverb - A part of speech that modifies a verb, adjective, or other adverb and answers questions like how, when, where, or to what extent.

Argument - The position an author or speaker establishes in an attempt to persuade others to think or feel differently about an issue, to change how they act, or to resolve disagreements between themselves and other parties. Authors or speakers accomplish these ends by presenting claims supported with reasons, evidence, and appeals (Burke, 2013; Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Authoritative Source - An entity or work that is considered to be the primary or most reliable source for particular information. The authoritative data source is considered to be the most accurate, trusted, up-to-date, and held to the highest standard.

Automaticity/Automatically - The ability to read quickly and accurately without conscious effort (Moats & Tolman, 2019).

 Letter C

Call to Action - A concise prompt designed to spur an immediate response from an audience, guiding them toward a specific action and/or goal (Grammarly, n.d.).

Claim - A specific point or assertion that authors or speakers want the audience to accept as true and act on. Claims are debatable and require supporting evidence (Burke, 2013; Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Complex Sentence - A sentence with one independent clause and at least one dependent clause (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Compound Sentence - A sentence with multiple independent clauses but no dependent clauses (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Compound-Complex Sentence - A sentence with multiple independent clauses and at least one dependent clause (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Conjunction - A part of speech that connects words, phrases, or clauses.

Connected Text - Words that are linked in sentences, phrases, or paragraphs. A group of sentences that relate to one another (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Connotation - The range of further associations that a word or phrase suggests in addition to its straightforward dictionary meaning (Oxford University Press, n.d.).

Conventions - Established practices, rules, or standards for using language that are essential for clear communication.

Counterclaim - A challenge or objection to a point or set of points that identifies an issue, drawback, consequence, alternative interpretation, and/or unconsidered evidence (Harvard College Writing Center, n.d.).

Credible/Credibility - A source's trustworthiness and reliability.

Cursive - Handwriting that joins letters within a word. In North Carolina, House Bill 146 § 115C-81.75 requires public schools to provide cursive writing instruction (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

 Letter D

Decode - The process of translating a word from print to speech by using knowledge of sound–symbol (letter) correspondences.

Decodable Text - Text in which a high proportion of words comprise sound–symbol relationships that have already been taught (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Descriptive Text - A creative or artistic text that seeks to describe something — an object, person, place, experience, emotion, situation, etc. — to paint a vivid, sensory impression in the mind of the audience (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.; Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Determiners - A part of speech that introduces a noun and signals which one, how many, or whose (e.g., the, a, this, my).

Digital Tools - Technologies — including platforms, software, apps, devices, and digital resources — used to investigate, create, communicate, collaborate, analyze, problem-solve, and manage learning in meaningful ways (Joyner, 2019).

Domain-Specific Words - Words specific to a domain or field of study and key to understanding a new concept within a text. Also known as Tier III words (Liben, 2013).

 Letter E

Encode - The process of translating speech into print (writing) using knowledge of sound–symbol (letter) correspondences (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Evidence - Facts or information (e.g., quotes, examples, anecdotes, data, etc.) that can be referred to in order to support an opinion, point, or claim (Burke, 2013).

Evidence (Textual) - Text that can be referred to in order to support an idea, interpretation, or assertion (e.g., quote, passage, pictures or illustrations, etc.) (Burke, 2013).

Explanatory Text - A text that seeks to explain how or why something happens.

Expository Text - A text that seeks to inform, define, demonstrate, review, instruct, and/or explain a topic in detail. Expository texts require an investigation of the topic and evaluates evidence (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

 Letter F

Figurative Language - A type of descriptive language used to convey meaning in a way that differs from its literal meaning and is often used to add style to written or spoken language or to simplify a complex idea (Grammarly, n.d.).

Figure of Speech - A creative use of language to generate an effect, enhance an expression, or evoke an emotion (Grammarly, n.d.).

Fluency (CD.F.4) - The ability to read connected text with appropriate rate, accuracy, and expression (prosody).

Fluency (CW.4) - The ability to speak and write with ease and accuracy (International Literacy Association, n.d.).

Format/Form - The way a text is presented — such as print, non-print, static, or dynamic.  Static formats are nonmoving print, graphics, or images on screen. Dynamic formats include more than one mode (print, video, other) or nonlinear locations (hypertext link) (National Assessment Governing Board, 2025).

 Letter G

Graphemes - A letter or letter combination that represents a sound (phoneme) in a syllable or word. In English, a grapheme may be one, two, three, or four letters (e.g., e, ei, igh, or eigh) (Moats & Tolman, 2019; Reading Rockets, n.d.).

 Letter I

Idiom - A unique regional or individual expression that differs from the literal meaning of the words (e.g., Bite your tongue, piece of cake, rule of thumb, etc.) (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Informative Text - A nonfiction text that seeks to inform the reader about a particular topic (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Interjections - A part of speech that expresses sudden feeling or reaction (e.g., oh! wow! ouch!).

 Letter J

 Letter K

Key Details (K) - Important details that support, clarify, or convey the topic of a text, including answers to who, what, where, when, why, how (Burke, 2013).

Key Details (Grade 1) - Important details that support, clarify, or convey the main idea or theme of a text, including answers to who, what, where, when, why, how (Burke, 2013).

Key Details (Grades 2-12) - Important details that support, clarify, or convey the main idea or theme of a text (Burke, 2013).

 Letter L

Literary Elements - The core building blocks of a text (e.g., plot, setting, theme, characters, mood, allegory, conflict, etc.) (International Literacy Association, n.d.).

Literary Techniques - The stylistic choices the author makes to add depth, emotion, and meaning using literary elements and figurative language (e.g., foreshadowing, imagery, metaphor, etc.) (International Literacy Association, n.d.).

 Letter M

Main Idea - The central thought of or point made by a text or portion of text (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Manuscript - Handwriting that involves printing letters separate and unconnected within a word (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Media/Medium - The channel(s) or system(s) through which communication or expression is conveyed, especially of mass communication (e.g., books, newspapers, internet, and audio/video recordings). Media is the plural form of “medium” (International Literacy Association, n.d.).

Metaphor - A figure of speech that compares two unlike things (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Morphology - The study of word structure and how words are formed by looking at their meaningful units (morphemes) — prefixes, roots, and suffixes (e.g., mis-spell-ing) (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Multimodalities - The use of multiple modes of communication — such as images, text, audio, gestures, and video — to create meaning. There are five modes of communication: visual, linguistic, spatial, aural, and gestural (International Literacy Association, n.d.).

 Letter N

Narrative Text - A text that seeks to tell a story — real or imagined (Adlit.org, n.d.; Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Non-standard Sentence - A sentence that deviates from standard, prescriptive grammar rules (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Norms - A set of agreements used to interact, communicate, share, collaborate and coordinate.

Noun - A part of speech that names a person, place, thing, or idea.

 Letter O

 Letter P

Paraphrase - The process of restating essential information and ideas from a source in your own words (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Perspective - The way in which the author, narrator, speaker, individual, and/or character perceive and process what’s happening within the text, including how they think and feel (Burke, 2013; Writer’s Digest, n.d.).

Persuasive Text - A text that seeks to persuade, convince, influence, argue, recommend, change, advocate, urge, defend, justify, or support a researchable, non-fictional, and relevant topic (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Phoneme - The smallest parts of spoken language that combine to form words (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Phonics - Reading instruction that focuses on the alphabetic principle — the systematic, predictable relationship between spoken sounds (phonemes) and written letters (graphemes) — to allow readers to identify or “decode” words (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Poetic Text - A text that conveys a story, an idea, or an emotion through choosing and arranging language for meaning, sound, visuals, and/or rhythm (Academy of American Poets, n.d.).

Point of View - The position or vantage-point from which the events of a text seem to be observed, presented, or told to the audience (e.g., first person, second person, third person, etc.) (Burke, 2013; Colorín Colorado; Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.; Childs & Fowler, 2006).

Preposition - A part of speech that shows the relationship between a noun or pronoun and another word, often indicating location, time, or direction (e.g., on, before, toward).

Pronoun - A part of speech that replaces a noun or noun phrase (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Prosody - Oral reading expression. Prosody includes phrasing, attention to punctuation, pitch, tone, volume, emphasis, and rhythm in oral reading.

Purpose(s) - The reason(s), motivation(s), or intention(s) for writing or speaking (e.g., to persuade, to inform, to express, to explain, to entertain, to inspire, to critique, etc.) (Burke, 2013).

 Letter Q

 Letter R

Rate - The speed at which a person reads and speaks.

Reason(s) - Statement(s) of support for opinions, points, or claims, making them something more than mere assertions (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Recount/Recounting - A clearly sequenced ordering of narrative events (Keys to Literacy, n.d.).

Repeated Reading - A vital comprehension tool — leveraged by the teacher or students — that entails rereading text for multiple purposes. It is frequently used in the close reading of complex texts to deepen student comprehension (Fisher & Frey, 2020).

Recursive Process - A non-linear approach where authors/speakers cycle through stages (e.g., planning, drafting, revising, editing, producing, publishing, and presenting) constantly revisiting and refining work for improvement, rather than a strict, one-way path. This process emphasizes rethinking, adding, deleting, and polishing content at any stage, leading to deeper understanding and better product quality (Graham, et al.; 2016).

Rhetorical Elements - The appeals (ethos, pathos, logos, kairos) and related strategies (repetition, hyperbole, rhetorical questioning, etc.) used to persuade an audience (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Rhetorical Techniques - The stylistic choices the author makes specific to persuasion support an appeal: logos, pathos, or ethos. These may include: circular arguments, anaphora, antithesis, rhetorical questions, euphemism, repetition, hyperbole, understatement, etc.) (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

 Letter S

Self-Monitoring - The act of monitoring one’s own comprehension for what is understood, what is not understood, and what comprehension strategies would be appropriate to resolve issues with comprehension (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Sequence - The approach the author uses to order content, including chronological, temporal, developmental, easy to difficult, part to whole, simple to complex, thematic, and whole to part (International Literacy Association, n.d.).

Setting - The time, place, and/or surroundings where an individual or character is positioned or where an event takes place (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Simile - A figure of speech that compares two unlike things using “like” or “as” (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Simple Sentence - A sentence with one independent clause and no dependent clauses (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Source(s) - Materials from which information can be gathered. Types of sources can include, but are not limited to: books, journals, government reports, websites, primary sources, etc. View more types of sources (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Source Material - The original work from which new content is derived, serving as the basis for understanding, retelling, or building upon existing ideas or facts.

Structure - Texts can be organized in different ways depending on their purpose (e.g., narrative, descriptive, poetic, opinion/persuasion, explanatory, expository, informational). Within these broader types, authors use common text structures such as compare and contrast, problem and solution, cause and effect, sequence/chronology, and description. These structures may appear across an entire text or within sections, paragraphs, and individual sentences (Burke, 2013; Colorín Colorado, n.d.; International Literacy Association, n.d., Sedita, 2019, 2025).

Style - Any specific way of using language to convey ideas and purpose in writing and is comprised of an author’s diction, syntax, tone, characters, and other techniques. Style may also be associated with a specific author, genre, mode of writing, or time period (Oxford University Press, n.d.; Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Style Manual - Style guides include what writers need to know in order to ensure their work is written in a particular style and follows guidelines for a particular field including: grammar and language use, font, heading sizes, references, citations, etc (e.g., Associated Press, Chicago, MLA, etc.) (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Syllabication - The act of breaking words into syllables (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Syllable - The unit of pronunciation that is organized around a vowel. The vowel may or may not be preceded and/or followed by a consonant (e.g., o-ver, pa-per, bas-ket, or ba-na-na) (Moats & Tolman, 2019; Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Syntactical Structure/Syntax - The way in which words, phrases, and clauses are ordered and connected in the structure of sentences to create meaning and guide the reader’s interpretation (Childs & Fowler, 2006).

 Letter T

Talk Moves - A set of strategic moves that help support productive, accountable discussions (Keys to Literacy, n.d.).

Text(s) - Text(s) are generated by authors to communicate to readers. Text(s) take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres or modalities (e.g., visual, auditory, aural, linguistic, gestural, spatial, etc.). Text(s) may be printed on paper or published in digital forms (National Assessment Governing Board, 2025).

Text Complexity - Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task.

Text Feature(s) - An element of a text that aids in the organization or that supports meaning-making (e.g., title, page number, images, bold text, etc.).

Theme  (Grades 1-3) - A statement about a universal idea or truth that a text explores. Rather than just naming a topic, a theme expresses a deeper message or perspective about a topic (International Literacy Association, n.d.).

Theme (Grades 4-12) - A statement about a universal idea or truth that a text explores. Rather than just naming a topic, a theme expresses a deeper message, observation, or perspective about a topic.

Tone - A way of communicating a topic or subject (in writing, images, or sound) that conveys an attitude, mood, or atmosphere in a text. Tone can be established by a combination of word choice, imagery, perspective, punctuation, sentence structure, style, and subject matter (Grammarly, n.d.; Oxford University Press, n.d.; Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Topic - The general subject or idea a text explores, referring to what a text is mostly about (Burke, 2013).

Transcription - Converting spoken words or sounds to written text, including spelling, handwriting, keyboarding/word processing/typing, or speech-to-text conversion (Harris, et al. 2023; Kim, et al., 2025; Keys to Literacy, n.d.).

 Letter U

 Letter V

Validity - Refers to a source’s usefulness/strengths & weaknesses to answering a specific research question.

Verb - A part of speech that expresses an action, occurrence, or state of being.

 Letter W

Wide Reading - A wide variety of texts focused on a specific concept that connects new ideas and vocabulary (Fisher, Ross, & Grant, 2010).

Word Analysis - Instruction that focuses on examining words closely to understand how they are structured and how they convey meaning. It includes learning to decode complex words using knowledge of word parts (such as affixes and roots), building vocabulary, and applying spelling patterns (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Word Knowledge - The ability to recognize, understand, and use words effectively, aiding in making meaning from text (comprehension). Word knowledge includes vocabulary and morphological awareness (Frey, et al., 2025).

Word Recognition - The ability to automatically recognize a previously-learned word (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Figurative Language - A type of descriptive language used to convey meaning in a way that differs from its literal meaning and is often used to add style to written or spoken language or to simplify a complex idea (Grammarly, n.d.).

Dynamic formats include more than one mode (print, video, other) or nonlinear locations (hypertext link) (National Assessment Governing Board, 2025).

P
Paraphrase - The process of restating essential information and ideas from a source in your own words (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Perspective - The way in which the author, narrator, speaker, individual, and/or character perceive and process what’s happening within the text, including how they think and feel (Burke, 2013; Writer’s Digest, n.d.).

Persuasive Text - A text that seeks to persuade, convince, influence, argue, recommend, change, advocate, urge, defend, justify, or support a researchable, non-fictional, and relevant topic (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Phoneme - The smallest parts of spoken language that combine to form words (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Phonics - Reading instruction that focuses on the alphabetic principle — the systematic, predictable relationship between spoken sounds (phonemes) and written letters (graphemes) — to allow readers to identify or “decode” words (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Poetic Text - A text that conveys a story, an idea, or an emotion through choosing and arranging language for meaning, sound, visuals, and/or rhythm (Academy of American Poets, n.d.).

Point of View - The position or vantage-point from which the events of a text seem to be observed, presented, or told to the audience (e.g., first person, second person, third person, etc.) (Burke, 2013; Colorín Colorado; Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.; Childs & Fowler, 2006).

Preposition - A part of speech that shows the relationship between a noun or pronoun and another word, often indicating location, time, or direction (e.g., on, before, toward).

Pronoun - A part of speech that replaces a noun or noun phrase (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Prosody - Oral reading expression. Prosody includes phrasing, attention to punctuation, pitch, tone, volume, emphasis, and rhythm in oral reading.

Purpose(s) - The reason(s), motivation(s), or intention(s) for writing or speaking (e.g., to persuade, to inform, to express, to explain, to entertain, to inspire, to critique, etc.) (Burke, 2013).

R
Rate - The speed at which a person reads and speaks.

Reason(s) - Statement(s) of support for opinions, points, or claims, making them something more than mere assertions (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Recount/Recounting - A clearly sequenced ordering of narrative events (Keys to Literacy, n.d.).

Repeated Reading - A vital comprehension tool — leveraged by the teacher or students — that entails rereading text for multiple purposes. It is frequently used in the close reading of complex texts to deepen student comprehension (Fisher & Frey, 2020).

Recursive Process - A non-linear approach where authors/speakers cycle through stages (e.g., planning, drafting, revising, editing, producing, publishing, and presenting) constantly revisiting and refining work for improvement, rather than a strict, one-way path. This process emphasizes rethinking, adding, deleting, and polishing content at any stage, leading to deeper understanding and better product quality (Graham, et al.; 2016).

Rhetorical Elements - The appeals (ethos, pathos, logos, kairos) and related strategies (repetition, hyperbole, rhetorical questioning, etc.) used to persuade an audience (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Rhetorical Techniques - The stylistic choices the author makes specific to persuasion support an appeal: logos, pathos, or ethos. These may include: circular arguments, anaphora, antithesis, rhetorical questions, euphemism, repetition, hyperbole, understatement, etc.) (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

S
Self-Monitoring - The act of monitoring one’s own comprehension for what is understood, what is not understood, and what comprehension strategies would be appropriate to resolve issues with comprehension (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Sequence - The approach the author uses to order content, including chronological, temporal, developmental, easy to difficult, part to whole, simple to complex, thematic, and whole to part (International Literacy Association, n.d.).

Setting - The time, place, and/or surroundings where an individual or character is positioned or where an event takes place (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Simile - A figure of speech that compares two unlike things using “like” or “as” (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Simple Sentence - A sentence with one independent clause and no dependent clauses (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Source(s) - Materials from which information can be gathered. Types of sources can include, but are not limited to: books, journals, government reports, websites, primary sources, etc. View more types of sources (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Source Material - The original work from which new content is derived, serving as the basis for understanding, retelling, or building upon existing ideas or facts.

Structure - Texts can be organized in different ways depending on their purpose (e.g., narrative, descriptive, poetic, opinion/persuasion, explanatory, expository, informational). Within these broader types, authors use common text structures such as compare and contrast, problem and solution, cause and effect, sequence/chronology, and description. These structures may appear across an entire text or within sections, paragraphs, and individual sentences (Burke, 2013; Colorín Colorado, n.d.; International Literacy Association, n.d., Sedita, 2019, 2025).

Style - Any specific way of using language to convey ideas and purpose in writing and is comprised of an author’s diction, syntax, tone, characters, and other techniques. Style may also be associated with a specific author, genre, mode of writing, or time period (Oxford University Press, n.d.; Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Style Manual - Style guides include what writers need to know in order to ensure their work is written in a particular style and follows guidelines for a particular field including: grammar and language use, font, heading sizes, references, citations, etc (e.g., Associated Press, Chicago, MLA, etc.) (Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Syllabication - The act of breaking words into syllables (Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Syllable - The unit of pronunciation that is organized around a vowel. The vowel may or may not be preceded and/or followed by a consonant (e.g., o-ver, pa-per, bas-ket, or ba-na-na) (Moats & Tolman, 2019; Reading Rockets, n.d.).

Syntactical Structure/Syntax - The way in which words, phrases, and clauses are ordered and connected in the structure of sentences to create meaning and guide the reader’s interpretation (Childs & Fowler, 2006).

Talk Moves - A set of strategic moves that help support productive, accountable discussions (Keys to Literacy, n.d.).

Text(s) - Text(s) are generated by authors to communicate to readers. Text(s) take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres or modalities (e.g., visual, auditory, aural, linguistic, gestural, spatial, etc.). Text(s) may be printed on paper or published in digital forms (National Assessment Governing Board, 2025).

Text Complexity - Text complexity defines the level of difficulty in reading and understanding a text based on three measures that should be considered equally: quantitative, qualitative, and reader and task.

Text Feature(s) - An element of a text that aids in the organization or that supports meaning-making (e.g., title, page number, images, bold text, etc.).

Theme  (Grades 1-3) - A statement about a universal idea or truth that a text explores. Rather than just naming a topic, a theme expresses a deeper message or perspective about a topic (International Literacy Association, n.d.).

Theme (Grades 4-12) - A statement about a universal idea or truth that a text explores. Rather than just naming a topic, a theme expresses a deeper message, observation, or perspective about a topic.

Tone - A way of communicating a topic or subject (in writing, images, or sound) that conveys an attitude, mood, or atmosphere in a text. Tone can be established by a combination of word choice, imagery, perspective, punctuation, sentence structure, style, and subject matter (Grammarly, n.d.; Oxford University Press, n.d.; Purdue Online Writing Lab, n.d.).

Topic - The general subject or idea a text explores, referring to what a text is mostly about (Burke, 2013).

Transcription - Converting spoken words or sounds to written text, including spelling, handwriting, keyboarding/word processing/typing, or speech-to-text conversion (Harris, et al. 2023; Kim, et al., 2025; Keys to Literacy, n.d.).
