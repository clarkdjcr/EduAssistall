# Arts Education - First Grade

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Theatre Arts Standards Unpacking First Grade
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for theatre arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with theatre build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The K - 5 theatre standards and objectives are written for elementary students who are taught by a licensed theatre educator for at least 50 minutes per week.
The North Carolina Theatre Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 theatre programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

1.CN.1.1 Compare theatre from a variety of cultures.
Glossary
* Compare - To locate and/or list similarities and differences.14
* Culture - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified types of theatre in local and global communities, and now they are comparing theatre from a variety of cultures. In the next level, they will describe how American theatre reflects the heritage, customs, and traditions of people in the United States, including various indigenous and cultural groups.
Enduring Understanding
A theatre artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of stories, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* Characteristics of theatre from a variety of cultures
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Identifying similarities and differences in theatre types
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Theatre is a global art form that exists in and represents all cultures.
* The theatre examples examined could be the same ones used for 1.RE.1.1 or 1.RE.2.1
* Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Prepare materials from a variety of cultures using resources such as PBS Theatre.
* Model comparisons
* Guide students through the process of making comparisons in a respectful way
* Provide opportunities for students to practice identifying similarities and differences in theatre types, giving guidance as appropriate.
* Monitor individual students’ ability to compare various types of theatre while properly using theatre vocabulary through formative and summative assessment, offering feedback as needed.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
* Folk Tales (from any nation)
* Appalachian Storytelling
* Japanese Kabuki Theatre
* Japanese Bunraku Puppets
* Italian Commedia dell’arte Masks
* Community Theatre
* Children’s Theatre
* NC Outdoor Drama
* Greek Theatre
* World Theatre, such as: West African, Latino

1.CN.2.1 Describe the roles of the actor, director, and designers.
Glossary
   * Actor - The people in a play or dramatic work who portray the characters. This may be done through thought, action, dialogue, costumes, and makeup.29
   * Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
   * Designer - Responsible for creating the look and feel of a production.The team generally includes set, lighting, sound, costume, properties, and makeup designers.29
   * Director - The person who oversees the entire process of staging a production. The director is a member of the ensemble charged with interpreting the text and conveying a central concept or theme through the use of the elements of theatre.29
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students described the roles of the actor and the director. Now, they are describing the roles of the actor, director, and designers. In the next level, they will compare the roles of actors, directors, and designers.
Enduring Understanding
The roles, skills, and behaviors of a theatre artist are necessary for success in both artistic and other professions.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * What an actor does
   * What a director does
   * What designers do
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Summarizing the characteristics of actors, directors, and designers
   * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Actors, directors, and designers work individually and collaboratively to create theatrical works.
   * Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts.
   * This objective calls for “actor, director, and designer” so it is imperative that all three are covered.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Prepare student-friendly definitions and descriptions of each of the roles involved in putting on a production.
   * Begin with what students already know about actors, directors, and designers.
   * Provide opportunities for students to practice identifying actors, directors, and designers, then have them describe the roles they each play in theatrical experiences
   * Monitor individual students’ ability to describe the roles of actors, directors, and designers using disciplinary vocabulary through formative and summative assessment.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
   * Books such as “ABC’s of Broadway” & “Putting on a Play”
   * Differing roles of actors, directors, and designers through role-play
   * Creating a costume design for a character from a story
   * Creating a set design from a story
   * Mini-Performance: including all roles involved

Knowledge

   * Tools used by technical theatre designers
Skills

   * Recognizing, listing, or naming tools used by technical theatre designers

Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Many supplies are used in technical theatre, but students do not necessarily need to be able to identify every one.
   * Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts.

Teacher Actions

1.CR.1.1 Improvise a variety of activities and scenarios using dramatic play.
Glossary
   * Dramatic Play - Spontaneous dramatic enactment often done by children pretending or imitating while playing.21
   * Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students improvised stories and situations using dramatic play, and now they are improvising a variety of activities and scenarios using dramatic play. In the next level, they will improvise a variety of characters in given scenarios using dramatic play.
Enduring Understanding
Exploration, experimentation, and improvisation deepens the ability of theatre artists to develop their creative aesthetic.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * Instructions for dramatic play
   * Components of scenarios
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Spontaneously inventing responses to scenarios and other actors
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Students can move beyond simple role-playing and start improvising more complex scenarios.
   * Dramatic play allows children to explore different perspectives, practice empathy, and build social and emotional skills.
   * Students begin to understand and construct simple narratives. In dramatic play, they can practice structuring stories with a clear beginning, middle, and end and can expand on previously used ideas to build more sophisticated storylines.
   * Since this objective has the language “activities and scenarios” both must be considered to master this objective.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Prepare materials which will inspire a wide range of imaginative scenarios and dramatic activities.
   * Demonstrate how to start an improvisational scenario by establishing characters, a setting, and a problem
   * Guide students in creating their dramatic scenarios using structured prompts, maintaining safe and appropriate play as they take on roles, interact with peers, and develop more complex storylines
   * Provide plenty of opportunities for students to practice improvisation using supports such as character cards, setting ideas, and guiding questions to spark creativity and confidence in improvisation
   * Monitor each students’ ability to spontaneously invent responses to scenarios and other actors through formative and summative assessment, documenting their use of dramatic play elements as evidence of mastery.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
   * Definitions of improvisation
   * Teacher-led scenarios for dramatic play (Examples: Superheros, Detectives, Restaurants, Animals, etc.)

1.CR.1.2 Create an original sequence of events by acting, drawing, or writing with support.
Glossary
   * Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
   * Original - Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.14
   * Support - Assistance provided temporarily to enable a student to perform a task that would be difficult to perform unaided, best implemented in a manner that helps develop that student’s capacity to eventually perform the task independently.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students communicated the main idea of an original story or scene using pre-writing activities with support. Now, they are creating an original sequence of events by acting, drawing, or writing with support. In the next level, they will create an original scene using dialogue with support.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are recorded by theatre artists and playwrights through both written and visual documentation.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * Components of story sequences
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Creating a new sequence of events
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * Stories and events happen in a logical order: beginning, middle, and end. This is an essential skill for both oral and written communication.
   * By focusing on a simple structure of events (beginning, middle, end) and encouraging different ways of expressing ideas, students will build essential skills in narrative development and communication.
   * Since this objective has the language “acting, drawing, or writing” any of the listed methods may be used. Students are not required to do each to master this objective.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Prepare definitions and descriptions of sequencing events: Beginning, Middle, and End, as well as materials to aid in acting, drawing, and/or writing out sequences of events
   * Demonstrate an original story beginning, middle, and end through acting out, drawing, and writing (all three) to ensure students know what their options are.
   * Guide students to collaboratively build a story sequence as a whole class
   * Provide graphic organizers, story strips, word banks, or visuals to help students create their own story sequence.
   * Monitor each students’ ability to create their own sequence of events through formative and summative assessment.
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
   * Sequencing with Visual Aids: Use picture cards or storyboards to help students arrange the events of a story in the correct order.
   * Model Story Creation: Create a simple story with students by brainstorming ideas, identifying key events, and arranging them in a logical sequence. Emphasize the beginning, middle, and end structure.
   * Story Mapping: Provide graphic organizers like a "Story Sequence Chart" where students can draw pictures and write simple sentences for each event in their story.
   * Acting out a life skill that has a simplistic sequence of events (Ex. making a sandwich, putting your shoes on, brushing your teeth, etc.)

1.CR.1.3 Create simple costumes and props to enhance dramatic play using available resources.
Glossary
   * Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
   * Costumes - Clothing worn by actors to portray specific characters.29
   * Dramatic Play - Spontaneous dramatic enactment often done by children pretending or imitating while playing.21
   * Props - Anything that the actor uses on stage that is not scenery (i.e., a coffee cup or a cell phone).29
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students created simple environments for dramatic play by arranging set pieces. Now, they are creating simple costumes and props to enhance dramatic play using available resources. In the next level, they will create simple costumes, props, or masks to enhance a dramatic presentation.
Enduring Understanding
Unique ideas are actualized by theatre designers and craftspersons intentionally to fulfill dramatic purposes.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
   * Components of simple costumes
   * Instructions for dramatic play
   * Considerations for enhancing dramatic play
   * Props and costumes are used to help create a full picture for the audience to suspend belief and invest in the story being performed
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
   * Creating new costumes and props
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
   * The focus is on using "available resources," so students will be practicing how to repurpose everyday materials into useful theatrical tools. This promotes problem-solving and an understanding of sustainability in arts practices
   * Costumes and props help students connect with the characters they are portraying. By making their own costumes or props, students learn how these items reflect personality, role, and story
   * This objective will be most meaningful when supporting improvisations (CR.1.1), original creation (CR.1.2), or as part of presenting works (PR.1.2). This objective has students creating the technical elements used in PR.2.3
   * Since this objective has the language “costumes and props” both must be created to master this objective
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
   * Prepare materials for costumes and props (e.g., fabric, cardboard, scarves, paint, markers, yarn). Share pictures or videos of simple costumes and props used in children's theater or well-known stories and discuss how these items help tell the story
   * Demonstrate how to create a simple costume or prop, emphasizing freedom of creativity and imagination
   * Guide students in creating their own costumes
   * Guide students in creating their own props
   * Provide temporary support as needed, encouraging students to think through what items are needed in their story (e.g., crown, sword, unicorn, cape) to support their storytelling
   * Monitor individual students’ ability to create costumes and props through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
   * The purpose of costumes in storytelling (e.g., helping audiences understand the character).
   * The function of props (e.g., a basket for carrying items, a flower to show kindness, a walking stick for an elderly character).
   * Repurposing Materials: using old shirts to make capes, turning boxes into treasure chests, or transforming old magazines into masks, etc.
   * DIY Stories: Pirate Adventure, Animal Characters, Storybook Characters
   * Costumes and props for CR.1.1, CR.1.2, or CR.2.1
   * Costumes and props for PR.1.2, PR.2.1, or PR.2.3

Theatre artists balance inspiration and originality with ethics by creatively transforming or adapting other artistic ideas into new works.
Knowledge

   * Since this objective has the language “with support” students are able to master this objective even with temporary assistance

Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
      * Offering feedback naming correctly executed skills of self and others
      * Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
      * Feedback is about praise and noticing and reinforcing effort, creativity, and improvement
      * Students learn to focus on what others did well—whether it's acting, creating, or contributing to a group effort. This helps build confidence in themselves and others
      * This objective aligns with the kind of feedback students should be using in 1.PR.1.1
      * Ensuring that students are using disciplinary language is important for building proficiency in the theatre arts
      * Since this objective has the language “self and others” feedback must be given to both
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
      * Prepare a shared list of positive feedback phrases and anchor charts with examples
      * Demonstrate how to name something specific and kind about one’s own or another’s work
      * Guide students in using the shared language during turn-and-talk or small group reflection
      * Provide structured routines for offering feedback during class shares or performance circles
      * Monitor individual students' ability to provide positive feedback for self and others through formative and summative assessment
Possible Learning Activities
May include, but are not limited to:
      * Discuss how we can give positive feedback for creativity and effort. For example, in a skit, you can praise how someone created an interesting character or how they worked hard to learn their lines.
      * Giving Specific Feedback
      * Develop vocabulary to describe their feelings and reactions, such as "I liked the ... because ...,"
      * Use Positive Language
