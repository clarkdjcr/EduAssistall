# Arts Education - Advanced

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Dance Standards Unpacking Advanced Honors

The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a students’ choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed Dance educator. Credit earned from middle and high school courses meeting these minimums satisfies the Arts Education Graduation requirement and applies toward the Arts Education Diploma Endorsement. Courses that do not meet these minimums are not designed to build proficiency and are often used to spark interest in more intensive proficiency-based study later in a student’s education.
To account for variations in skill progression, as well as to embed multiple entry points across the K-12 spectrum, the objectives in the high school NCSCOS for Dance are organized by proficiency level, rather than grade level. Minimum proficiency outcomes are provided at each level to show what a student should know and be able to do by the end of a course or program when they create, present, respond to, and make connections with Dance. Courses using the Advanced High School standards are inherently honors.
The North Carolina Dance Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Dance programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

Glossary
* Improvise - To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.14

Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to create spontaneous movement
* How to cue movement inspiration when improvising with a partner or group
* How to collaborate on the creation or selection of a movement score with a partner or group
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Creating spontaneous movement through improvisation with a partner or group in response to a co-determined movement score
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Movement scores are "outlines" for the structure to be used in guiding the flow of improvisation. Basing improvisation off of movement scores keeps the student centered on exploring specific elements or concepts that exist in body movement. This helps to avoid the random strung together performance of pre-known "dance steps" or "tricks" but instead encourages a more organic exploration of movement possibilities
* The elements of dance or movement concepts can be used to inspire and improvise movement
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions

* Lead students through partnered or group improvisation activities using movement scores
* Share examples of movement scores with students
* Pair or group students
* Guide pairs or groups of students in their creation of a movement score
* Provide instructional time for students to use a co-determined movement score for improvisation with a partner or group
* Create formative and summative feedback on the student’s ability to improvise with a partner or group using a co-determined movement score
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Depending on the technique, improvisation with a partner or group may include contact improvisation at this level
* The movement score could include spatial patterns, the sequence of dance elements, or alignment to choreographic or musical structure such as AB, ABA, etc. Movement concepts could include suspend/release, expand/contract, spiral, free/bound flow, etc.

* Structure time for planning, student teaching, sharing, reflection, and revision of work
* Create formative and summative feedback on the student’s ability to create a dance for a partner or group using relevant movement choices; choreographic principles, forms, or processes; and production elements to fulfill choreographic intent
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students select relevant themes and identify the choreographic structure that best develops the theme (AB, ABA, Rondo, Natural form, etc)
* Students can use improvisation as a means of initially creating motifs relevant to the theme
* Use the creative design process with frequent opportunities for multiple forms of feedback and revision (PR and RE objectives)
* Use additional choreographic tools or processes such as chance, retrograde, motif manipulation, and the resequencing and transitioning of dance phrases to build content

AD.RE.2.1 Explain how kinesthetic empathy influences the way individuals experience and critique dance.
Glossary
* Critique - Individual or collective reflective process by which artists or designers experience, analyze, and evaluate a work[s] of art or design.17
* Explain - To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.4
* Kinesthetic Empathy - Kinesthetic empathy is the capacity to participate in somebody’s movement, or their sensory experience of movement. It makes use of mirroring of sensations and movements of the body to enhance connection and synchronicity between people.30
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students identified moments of kinesthetic empathy when viewing dance and now explain how kinesthetic empathy influences the way individuals experience and critique dance.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that an observer's personal experiences shape the way they perceive and engage with dance works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The meaning of kinesthetic empathy
* How to identify moments of kinesthetic empathy when observing dance
* How the experience of kinesthetic empathy influences critique
* How to linguistically describe dance choreography
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Describing the reasoning of how kinesthetic empathy influences the way individuals experience and critique dance.
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Kinesthetic empathy happens when performers are moving with clarity, dynamics, flexibility, and with full intentionality and projection. Dancing "full out"  creates the sense of kinesthetic empathy as performers and helps to increase the audience's experience with the work being performed
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to explain how kinesthetic empathy influences the way individuals experience AND critique dance
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined

* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to explain how kinesthetic empathy influences the way individuals experience and critique dance
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:

* Find several examples of dance recordings for students to observe and to discuss any moments of feeling kinesthetic empathy. Students can also perform for one another and identify moments where they feel kinesthetic empathy when observing their peers perform
* Showing examples of the same movement phrase performed at various levels of focus and intensity to see how kinesthetic empathy is impacted. Students can also critique peers in execution of the movement phrase at various intensity levels and compare how their critiques might differ from when they do not and do experience kinesthetic empathy
* Have students compare how their experiences and evaluations of dance might differ when watching professional works that evoke kinesthetic empathy and others that do not

* How to provide detailed feedback on strengths and areas for improvement when evaluating how works are crafted or performed
* How to use contextual information on the cultural background of a work in evaluation of artistic choices
* How to use contextual information on the artistic intent of a work in evaluation of artistic choices
* The cultural context and artistic intent of a work that is being evaluated
* How to linguistically provide detailed feedback in the description of movement performance and choreography

* Offering detailed feedback for performance and choreography considering the cultural context and stated artistic intent of the work.

Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Meaning is communicated through choreographic choices that are rooted in the personal, historical, and cultural contexts of the artist and their cultural environment. Considering the cultural context and artistic intent of the work informs observations and critique
* Students understand that they can use feedback to improve and also give feedback to help someone else improve. * Discuss why understanding the artistic intent and cultural context of a work should be considered when evaluating a work
* Provide, or have students generate a rubric that considers artistic intent and cultural context to be used in evaluation of a work
* Demonstrate using the rubric that is considerate of artistic intent and cultural context to provide detailed feedback of performance and choreography with students
* Create opportunity for student practice in considering the artistic intent and cultural context of when using criteria in their evaluation of work performed by self, peers, or in observations of professional works
* Design activities for students to provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography considering the cultural context and stated artistic intent of the work
* Create formative and summative assessment on the student’s ability to provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography considering the cultural context and stated artistic intent of the work
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students identify the cultural context and artistic intent of a work before evaluating it. Use a rubric that includes criteria relevant to the cultural context and artistic intent in evaluation of how a dance is created and/or performed. Include detailed feedback on areas of growth and suggestions for improvement
* Students can also critique works that have been created and/or performed by self and/or peers, keeping in mind the cultural context of the work and the artistic intent of the work
