# English Language Arts - Grade 2

Introduction
Unpacking the ELA Standard Course of Study
Grade 2

On January 8, 2026, the State Board of Education unanimously approved the 2026 North Carolina English Language Arts (ELA) Standard Course of Study (SCOS) for implementation in the 2027-2028 school year. To support successful implementation of these standards, the NCDPI ELA Team has created Unpacking Documents to deepen the understanding of the NC ELA SCOS and its Fundamental Principles: 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center, 2) Integrated Model of Literacy & Spiraling, and 3) Variety of Texts.

The Unpacking Documents include the ELA Standards and Objectives as well as a glossary, clarifications, standards-based question stems and sentence frames, classroom considerations, and supplemental resources.  Please note that these unpacking documents are not meant to be exhaustive.

Because North Carolina is a local-control state, all curricular decisions (e.g., texts, tasks, activities, etc.) are approved at the local level. Prior to implementing any of the materials in this resource, teachers should follow appropriate policies and procedures to have their curriculum vetted and approved by their school and district leaders.

Acknowledgements: The NCDPI ELA Team consulted with educators in the field as well as the Offices of Early Learning and Exceptional Children during the creation of this resource.

Questions? Contact the NCDPI ELA Team.

CT.1
Overview and Purpose
The Complex Text Strand (CT) ensures students are developing into proficient, independent listeners and readers, outlining essential skills that students should be practicing every day, in every lesson, and with every text. This strand supports students in becoming skilled, confident readers who build stamina and sustain attention as they engage with increasingly challenging texts. This could take place through independent reading or the use of interactive read alouds. During interactive read-alouds, students are exposed to complex texts that are two to three grade levels above their own, providing access to fluent modeling, rich content, academic vocabulary, and complex language structures through explicit teacher think-alouds.

In order to place text at the center of ELA teaching and learning, CT standards and objectives should be integrated across the other two strands of the NC ELA SCOS– Comprehension Development (CD) and Communication and Writing (CW)— when designing standards-aligned instruction.  This integration ensures that students practice skills within the context of grade-appropriate complex texts.  As students apply these skills across a variety of such texts, they demonstrate the ability to independently construct meaning across language domains.

Through the CT Strand, students engage with and refer to a range of high-quality, content-rich texts to build knowledge, deepen comprehension, and apply grade-level skills by:
* applying a range of strategies to monitor, support, and deepen comprehension
* drawing on a wide range of reading and listening experiences to build knowledge and vocabulary
* transferring techniques from the texts to their own evolving forms of communication and writing
* Independently and proficiently reading/listening to grade-level complex texts within the 2-3 text complexity band, applying skills from the grade 2 standards proficiently.

These CT standards and objectives reflect critical, research-based components of reading comprehension and reinforce rigorous grade-level expectations across all strands of the NC ELA SCOS. The Complex Texts strand serves as a foundation for standards-based, text-centered instruction in every ELA classroom.

Complex Text Strand and the Fundamental Principles

The Complex Text Strand standards and objectives ensure the fundamental principles of 1) Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center; 2) Integrated Model of Literacy; and 3) a Variety of Texts are enacted in the ELA classroom. Designed to be recursive, CT standards and objectives include both explicit and implicit connections to these fundamental principles to ensure that students are engaging with them routinely throughout instruction.
* Grade-Level Complex Texts at the Center | Grade-level complex text is the foundation of the CT.1 standard and the primary means through which students engage with the ELA NC Standard Course of Study (NCSCOS) to build essential academic competencies. Educators should carefully select complex texts using the three measures of text complexity and place them at the center of daily instruction to ensure standards are authentically introduced, taught, and assessed, avoiding skills being taught in isolation. In K–2, much of this explicit instruction occurs through interactive read-alouds that allow students to hear, comprehend, and discuss increasingly complex texts while their word-reading skills are still developing.
* Integrated Model of Literacy | CT.1 objectives support students’ ability to apply and integrate skills across language domains in various contexts and across a range of texts. For example, CT.1.1 requires students to return back to the texts they are reading to ground discussion and writing in textual evidence. CT.1.2 emphasizes repeated readings, writing in response to texts, and collaborative discussion as key drivers of comprehension. CT.1.4 promotes the reading-writing connection, ensuring students engage with language receptively through reading, and transfer that knowledge generatively to their own communication.
* Variety of Texts | Within the context of the NCSCOS, “texts” are defined as any materials students can read, write, speak, view, listen to, or explore. Texts take many forms, drawing on multiple genres and combinations of genres. They relay vastly different content to address many kinds of purposes. CT.1.3 encourages students to read widely to build knowledge and vocabulary across a range of topics, themes, and concepts.

Decodable texts serve an important role in advancing students’ word reading during explicit phonics instruction. Decodable texts are an onramp to independently reading complex texts. The development of oral reading fluency is the instructional transition point to shift from reading decodable texts to independently reading complex texts. Because children develop these skills at different rates, they may demonstrate readiness to independently read complex texts at different points within the K-2 grade band.

Complex texts play an essential role in helping students access grade-level standards. In K-2, complex texts are used during interactive read-alouds to build students’ background knowledge, vocabulary, language comprehension, and familiarity with rich text structures. These read-aloud experiences ensure all students—regardless of their current decoding abilities—engage with the rigorous content required by the standards. In addition, students encounter complex texts during independent reading as they develop the foundational skills necessary for reading increasingly complex texts on their own. Because students progress at different rates, their readiness to independently read and comprehend complex texts will emerge at different points within the K–2 grade band.

A note on text selection and local control

North Carolina is a local-control state. Because of this, the North Carolina Department of Public Instruction (NCDPI) does not provide a recommended text list, as curricular decisions are made at the PSU (public school unit) level. NCDPI cannot endorse specific curricula or instructional materials for local use. The standards do not restrict or preclude the teaching of specific modes, types, genres, mediums, and formats of text.

PSUs should carefully analyze potential  texts for each grade by examining the qualitative, quantitative, and reader and task measures to ensure students engage with  increasingly complex texts. Through text complexity analysis, educators can determine when a particular mode, type, genre, medium, format of text best supports the instructional purpose. Multiple approaches are appropriate and should be used in alignment with the standards to ensure students experience a range of texts and text types that prepare them for postsecondary opportunities.

Classroom Considerations
* Carefully select high-quality texts by ensuring they are appropriately complex for the grade-level. Determine overall text complexity using a variety of measures including qualitative, quantitative, and reader & task considerations. Educators should weigh all three measures equally, as no single measure is more important than another; the combination provides the most accurate determination of text complexity.
* Plan daily explicit reading instruction using increasingly complex texts within the 2-3 text complexity band, strategically incorporate intentional entry points that support student access while maintaining the full complexity of the text. Ensure students are exposed to a variety of texts.
* Use explicit think-alouds to model how proficient readers make meaning from grade-level complex texts, explicitly demonstrating how to navigate challenging vocabulary, text structures, and complex ideas. Follow this modeling with guided practice that allows students to apply these strategies with support, gradually releasing responsibility as they demonstrate increasing independence.
* Build students’ capacity to navigate grade-level complex texts independently by teaching them to recognize when meaning breaks down,  ask and answer questions as they read to monitor their comprehension, and reflect on what they have read. Provide regular and varied opportunities for students to practice these behaviors so they develop into  self-directed, strategic readers. Support students’ metacognition by engaging them before, during, and after reading to reflect on what they are learning and how they are making meaning from the text.

2.CT.1.1 Ask and answer questions about key details, referring explicitly to the text as the basis for the answers.

In grade 2, students build on their grade 1 learning by continuing to identify and use key details—who, what, when, where, why, and how—to actively engage with texts by asking and answering questions as they independently read and listen to texts within the 2–3 text complexity band, referring explicitly to the text as the basis for their answers.

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	* Which detail in the text best helps you understand this part?
* According to the text, what does this [word, sentence, or section] tell us?
* What specific detail from the text supports your thinking?
* How does the text help answer this question?
	* The detail that best helps me understand this part is ___.
* According to the text, this [word, sentence, or section] tells us ___.
* A specific detail from the text that supports my thinking is ___.
* The text helps answer this question by explaining ___.

Classroom Considerations
* Ground questioning in oral language development by providing frequent opportunities for structured talk (e.g., think-pair-share, sentence frames, and guided discussions) that allow students to rehearse questions and responses orally before applying these skills independently.
* Explicitly model close reading, using think-alouds and anchor charts, what qualifies as a key detail by showing how key details answer who, what, when, where, why, and how and explain what the text is mostly about. After students have practiced identifying and describing key details across a range of texts, model both asking and answering questions about those details during independent reading and interactive read-alouds. Through think-alouds, demonstrate how readers and listeners use pictures, words, and specific text evidence to generate questions, support answers, and make inferences—emphasizing that inferences are drawn by combining text clues with prior knowledge, not by guessing—to build early habits of text-based reasoning rather than reliance on background knowledge alone.
* Use text-dependent questions to support meaning-making by guiding students to notice and apply key details that build understanding of the text as a whole, rather than serving as isolated recall checks. To do this, differentiate and purposefully align questions across reading and listening contexts, and intentionally sequence them to increase in complexity. This progression allows students to develop a deeper, more coherent understanding of the text.
* Explicitly model, using think-alouds and anchor charts, simple text annotation practices to track key details. Show students how to point to or mark important parts of the text using pictures or symbols as annotations, and how to talk about key details as they read or listen for a pre-identified purpose, using talk moves, sentence frames, and other language supports, as needed.

2.CT.1.2 Use self-monitoring, repeated reading, recounting, writing in response to texts, and discussions with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension.

In grade 2, students build on their grade 1 learning by independently reading and listening to texts within the 2–3 text complexity band and continuing to use self-monitoring, repeated reading, recounting, writing in response to texts, and discussions with peers and adults to strengthen comprehension. These may include:
* noticing when meaning breaks down and independently applying appropriate comprehension strategies to repair understanding
* engaging in repeated readings and listenings for different purposes, including deepening overall understanding and attending closely to key details
* recounting key details to organize, clarify, and extend their thinking
* writing and speaking about texts through collaborative discussions, clearly expressing their ideas and consistently supporting their thinking by referring explicitly to what was read or heard
Together,  these practices support students in accessing and making meaning from grade-level complex texts with increasing independence.

Classroom Considerations
* Explicitly teach and support students to actively monitor their comprehension as they flexibly use a repertoire of comprehension strategies.
* Follow the principle of gradual release to ensure students use strategies independently.
* Provide explicit instruction and structured opportunities for collaborative discussions about text meaning through interactive read-alouds and close reading of complex texts.
* Support students in writing in response to reading for a variety of purposes, including to recount, interpret, and apply information from texts they have read.

In grade 2, students build on their grade 1 learning by continuing to build knowledge and vocabulary through wide reading, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 2. Students independently read and listen to multiple texts on the same subject or related topics—some selected by the teacher and, when appropriate, some selected by students—to deepen knowledge, strengthen comprehension, and expand vocabulary.

Students…
* draw on a wide range of texts to build knowledge
* draw on a wide range of texts to build vocabulary

Classroom Considerations
* Provide text sets connected by topic/theme and ensure they include a variety of text types, modes, and genres. Texts within the set should reflect a range of complexity levels so students can build the knowledge and vocabulary needed to access the anchor grade-level text being studied.
* To maximize the impact of wide reading, intentionally activate, connect, build, and extend students’ prior knowledge so they can link new learning to what they already know and have studied. Making these connections strengthens comprehension, supports vocabulary development, and increases engagement by making new content more accessible. Use writing and discussion strategies to uncover what students already know before introducing new texts or content (e.g. anticipation guides, cloze, concept sorts, ABC brainstorming, quick writes, etc).
* Create and maintain an inviting, well-resourced, and purposefully organized classroom library that offers a wide range of texts and media. Ensure the collection reflects students’ diverse interests, backgrounds, languages, and experiences; exposes students to academic language across content areas; and includes multiple genres that support knowledge-building around specific topics. Provide opportunities for students to self-select materials for independent reading at school, at home, and through digital access when available.
* Provide regular opportunities for wide reading so students can engage with multiple texts on the same topic/theme. When appropriate, offer choice within a shared topic/theme to increase motivation while supporting knowledge and vocabulary development. Model the purpose and habits of wide reading through brief book talks and by explicitly showing how reading across texts deepens understanding. Include time for students to discuss, write, or reflect about what they are learning from their reading.
* Collaborate with a librarian or media specialist to help identify digital resources, databases, and multimedia that broaden the range of texts students can draw on. Involve these specialists in helping students learn how to locate, evaluate, and select texts that support their knowledge building. Consider how research or independent reading tasks can be used to get students to read more widely on a topic being studied.
* Support students’ vocabulary development during reading by implementing a comprehensive, multifaceted vocabulary framework.  Explicitly teach high-impact Tier II and Tier III words connected to the text or topic of study. Equip students with strategies to determine the meaning of unfamiliar words independently. Foster curiosity about language by exploring how words work, how they are related, and how they change across contexts. Provide rich and varied language experiences so students encounter and use new vocabulary in meaningful ways. Together, these components build strong, flexible word knowledge that supports deeper comprehension.

2.CT.1.4 Recognize the choices and features used by authors and transfer those techniques to communication and writing.

In grade 2, students recognize the choices and features authors use in texts within the 2–3 text complexity band and apply those techniques in their own speaking and writing. Students listen to, view, and read a variety of texts and examine how authors use word choice, literary elements, text features, and embedded media to help shape meaning. As students notice how these features work in texts, they begin to use similar techniques in their own speaking and writing.

Students…
* recognize the choices and features used by authors
* transfer those techniques to communication and writing

Classroom Considerations
* Teach students to identify and reflect on the choices authors make and how those choices shape meaning, craft, and style. Guide students to analyze elements such as organization, text structure, literary elements, word choice, and figurative language, and discuss how these features influence the reader’s understanding and experience.
* Plan instruction that intentionally integrates skills across language domains– reading, writing, speaking, and listening–so students can notice techniques in the texts they read and apply those techniques in their own writing and speaking across contexts.
* Use mentor texts to help students study effective crafts moves (techniques) across genres and styles and then apply those craft moves in their own writing and speaking. Intentionally select texts that showcase a range of craft moves to build students’ understanding of how communication shifts based on purpose and audience.
* Plan instruction that gives students opportunities to both understand texts (interpretive) and create their own texts (expressive). Interpretive tasks/experiences help students make meaning from what they read, hear, or view. This might include recounting events, answering questions, noticing important details, etc. Expressive tasks/experiences provide students the opportunity to share their own ideas through speaking, acting, drawing or writing. Encourage students to try out techniques they notice in texts—such as strong word choices, patterns, text features, or story structure—when creating their own texts. Intentionally connect reading, writing, speaking, and listening so students see how authors and speakers make choices and can begin using those same craft moves (techniques) in their own work. For additional support and examples, explore The Writing Guidebook and The Discourse Playbook.

Classroom Considerations

2.CD.F.1.1 Initiate and maintain conversation through multiple exchanges.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Build on students’ experience with responding in conversations by explicitly teaching how to start a conversation with a peer. Model simple ways to begin an exchange by greeting a partner, asking a question, or making a related comment about a shared topic. Use think-alouds to demonstrate how speakers decide what to say to begin a conversation and how they invite a partner to respond. Provide opportunities for students to practice initiating conversations in structured settings so they become comfortable starting an exchange independently.
* Guide students in extending conversations beyond a single response by explicitly teaching and modeling talk moves that help sustain an exchange. Demonstrate how speakers can keep a conversation going by asking follow-up questions, adding related information, or responding to a partner’s idea with additional detail (e.g., “Can you say more about that?”, “I agree because…”, “That reminds me of…”). Encourage students to build on what their partner says rather than shifting topics. Provide guided practice where students use these talk moves with a partner to continue a conversation for several turns, helping them experience how conversations develop through multiple exchanges.
* Observe student conversations closely and provide immediate feedback that highlights successful initiation and sustained dialogue. Acknowledge when students independently begin conversations or continue an exchange across several turns. When conversations end prematurely, prompt students to consider how they might continue the interaction by asking another question or adding more information. Integrate opportunities for students to initiate and sustain conversations across instructional contexts so they develop confidence using these skills in a variety of settings.
	Example 1:
Talk Moves: This strategy uses sentence starter phrases that help students speak, listen, and build on one another’s ideas during discussions. Talk moves increase verbal participation by providing students with clear language structures for responding to and expanding on what others have said. Teachers may also pair these sentence starters with corresponding hand signals to help quickly identify who is engaged in the discussion and to support facilitation by indicating which students want to add information, agree with a previous comment, or share a different perspective. Please explore our Academic Discourse Playbook for example talk moves and other collaborative discussion strategies/protocols.

Example 2:
Partner Retell: This routine provides students with structured opportunities to initiate and maintain conversations through multiple exchanges with a peer.
1. One student begins the conversation by responding to a prompt while the partner listens attentively.
2. The partner then responds by asking a question, adding a related idea, or making a comment connected to what their partner shared.
3. Students continue the exchange for several turns before switching roles. Teachers may provide prompts, question stems, or sentence frames to support students, as needed.
This routine helps students practice starting conversations, listening closely, responding to a partner’s ideas, and sustaining multiple conversational turns during collaborative discussions.
Please Note: To ensure both students participate in multiple exchanges, teachers may set a 3–5 minute timer and give each student several chips or counters. Each time a student speaks, they move one chip to the center. The goal is for each partner to contribute at least two or more turns before the timer ends. This visual structure encourages balanced participation and helps students practice sustaining back-and-forth dialogue.

2.CD.F.1.2 Speak clearly using appropriate expression and inflection.

Considerations
	Examples
	* Begin by explicitly teaching the meaning of expression and inflection in speech. Model how speakers use their voice to show feeling, emphasize important words, and make their message more engaging and understandable. Demonstrate how vocal inflection can affect meaning by comparing examples spoken in a flat voice with examples that include natural expression and changes in pitch. Use think-alouds to demonstrate how speakers decide which words to emphasize and how/when to change their voice to help listeners better understand the message.
* Provide regular opportunities for students to hear and practice expressive speaking through reader’s theater and classroom discussions. Model how expression and inflection make speech more engaging and easier to understand, then invite students to repeat short phrases or sentences using varied expressions. Provide guided practice so students begin experimenting with how their voice can convey ideas more effectively.
* Help students understand how inflection changes the meaning of spoken language. Model how raising or lowering the voice can signal a question, emphasize an important idea, or convey emotions such as excitement, surprise, or curiosity. Provide opportunities for students to practice saying sentences with different inflections so they experience how vocal changes affect meaning. At the same time, explain that exaggerated or overly frequent changes in inflection can distract listeners and make the message harder to understand. Encourage students to use inflection purposefully based on what they are trying to communicate.
* Listen closely as students share ideas during classroom discussions and oral activities, and provide supportive feedback, highlighting effective use of expression and inflection. When students speak in a monotone or unclear manner, prompt them to try again using a voice that better matches the meaning of their message. Highlight examples of expressive speaking so other students can hear how voice can strengthen communication. Provide frequent opportunities for students to practice expressive speaking across classroom contexts so these skills become increasingly natural and automatic.
	Example 1:
Echo Speaking: This routine provides students with an opportunity to hear and practice expressive speech by repeating a modeled sentence.
1. The teacher first models a sentence using clear pronunciation while demonstrating appropriate expression and inflection to convey meaning.
   * Teacher: “Did you hear that loud thunder?”
2. Students then repeat the sentence together or with a partner, imitating the teacher’s tone, emphasis, and vocal expression.
   * Students: “Did you hear that loud thunder?”
This routine allows students to hear and imitate how changes in expression and inflection help communicate meaning to listeners.

Example 2:
Reader’s Theater: This strategy provides students with structured opportunities to practice speaking clearly while using appropriate expression and inflection. In Reader’s Theater, students read a script aloud, taking on the roles of different characters and focusing on how their voice conveys meaning. Rather than memorizing lines, students practice reading dialogue aloud with attention to pacing, tone, and character voice. As students rehearse and perform, they practice emphasizing important words, changing their tone to match the character, and using inflection to signal questions, excitement, or other emotions. Teachers can support this work by coaching students to think about how their voice communicates meaning to listeners. For example, a teacher might ask, “Which word should we emphasize?” or “How might this character sound?” Through repeated practice reading aloud for an audience, Reader’s Theater helps students develop confidence and fluency while strengthening their ability to speak clearly using appropriate expression and inflection.

2.CD.F.3.1 Decode and encode words with open and closed syllables and consonant -le.

Considerations

Begin by clearly explaining that formal language is used in situations such as writing to a principal, giving a presentation, or reading informational texts, while informal language is used in conversations with friends, personal narratives, or dialogue in stories. Through explicit think-alouds, model how language choices change based on audience, purpose, and situation. For example, “If I am asking my teacher for help, I might say, ‘Could you please help me?’ but if I am talking to a friend, I might say, ‘Can you help me?’ Both mean the same thing, but one sounds more formal.”
* When first introducing formal and informal language, select familiar, everyday situations students can easily relate to, such as speaking at home versus at school. Focus instruction on noticing differences in word choice, tone, and sentence structure rather than labeling language as right or wrong. Use short text excerpts, dialogue from stories, or modeled sentences to highlight contrasts (e.g., “I would like to apologize” vs. “I’m sorry”). Reinforce that different situations call for different kinds of language, and guide students to think about who they are communicating with and why they are communicating before choosing their words.
* Once students can identify examples of formal and informal language in a single context, introduce opportunities to compare language across multiple texts. Use explicit modeling and think-alouds to show how the same message can be communicated in both formal and informal ways depending on purpose (e.g., an invitation, a request, or an explanation). Support comparison with graphic organizers such as T-charts labeled Formal and Informal, or scenario cards that ask students to choose the most appropriate language for a given situation. Emphasize that authors and speakers intentionally select language to match their audience and purpose.
* After explicit modeling, provide structured and varied opportunities for students to apply their understanding of formal and informal English in speaking, listening, reading, and writing. Use routines such as role-playing different communication scenarios, revising sentences to sound more formal or more informal, or sorting examples of language by context and purpose. Provide partner or small-group discussions where students explain why a particular language choice is appropriate.

This is a continued emphasis from grade 1, but with texts appropriately complex for grade 2. Students apply this understanding to interpret and express meaning more effectively when reading, writing, speaking, and listening.

Students...
* distinguish shades of meaning among closely related verbs
* distinguish shades of meaning among closely related adjectives

Standards-Based Questions
	Sentence Frames
	* How do ___ and ___ differ in strength or intensity?
* What does the word ___ help the reader understand?
* How does the author’s word choice affect how you picture or understand this part of the text?
* If you were the author, which word would best match what you want the reader to picture, and why?
	* ___ is [stronger/more intense] than ___ because ___.
* The word ___ helps the reader understand ___.
* The author’s word choice helps me picture/understand ___ by ___.
* If I were the author, I would change ___ to ___ because ___.

Classroom Considerations
* Use a variety of increasingly complex interactive read-alouds to model and explicitly teach students how closely related verbs and adjectives differ in intensity, precision, and nuance. Begin by explaining that many words have related or similar meanings, but authors choose specific words to communicate precise actions, feelings, or levels of intensity. For example, two authors might describe a storm: one may write that the wind blew, while another says it howled. Both words describe wind, but howled communicates a stronger, more intense experience. Help students notice how these word choices affect how they picture and understand what is happening in the text. Through explicit think-alouds, model how readers attend to word choice and consider why an author might choose one word over another. For example, “The author says the character glanced instead of looked. That tells me the action was quick and careful, not long or focused.” Emphasize that precise word choice helps readers and listeners visualize events and understand characters and ideas more clearly.
* When introducing this concept, move beyond simple word pairs and work with clusters of closely related words drawn directly from interactive read alouds or text sets (e.g., said, whispered, murmured, exclaimed or big, huge, enormous, massive). Ensure students understand the core meaning of each word, then guide them to analyze subtle differences using context. Use sentence-level and paragraph-level examples to show how surrounding details clarify intensity and meaning. Create anchor charts or word continua that label differences using descriptive language (e.g., quiet → louder → loudest or small change → big change), reinforcing that shades of meaning exist along a spectrum rather than as fixed categories.
* Once students can analyze word intensity within a single text, extend learning by comparing word choices across multiple texts on the same or similar topic. Use think-alouds to examine how different authors convey similar actions, feelings, or descriptions using different word choices. For example, a teacher might say, “The author chose the word glared instead of looked. Both involve using your eyes, but glared shows a much stronger feeling and tells me more about how the character is feeling.” Support discussion with comparison charts that record the word used, its intensity, and the effect on meaning. Encourage students to justify their thinking using evidence from the text, reinforcing that word choice influences reader and listener understanding.
* After explicit modeling, provide structured and varied opportunities for students to apply their understanding of shades of meaning in both reading and writing. Use routines such as revising sentences by replacing general words with more precise ones and explaining how meaning changes, ranking words from least to most intense based on textual context, or collaborative “word debates” where students defend which word best fits a sentence or situation. Provide opportunities for students to intentionally apply precise verbs and adjectives in writing and speaking, prompting reflection with questions such as, “Why did you choose this word instead of another?” Reinforce that skilled readers and writers attend carefully to shades of meaning to communicate ideas clearly and effectively across reading, writing, speaking, and listening.

Noun Use Skills

Use abstract nouns

Concept
	Example Sentence

Students need to know that some nouns form their plural in ways that do not follow regular -s or -es rules (e.g., child → children, mouse → mice, foot → feet). Use reflexive pronouns

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Reflexive pronouns

Use relative pronouns

Students need to know that relative pronouns (who, whom, whose, which, that) introduce clauses that describe or give more information about a noun. 	Example Sentence

Use adjectives and adverbs

	Example Sentence

Concepts and Example Sentences

Sentence Production & Expansion Skills

Produce complete simple, compound, and complex sentences

In addition to producing simple and compound sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a complex sentence includes one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses, which do not express a complete thought on their own and are often introduced by subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, when, if) or relative pronouns. When the dependent clause comes before the independent clause, it is attached with a comma. When it comes after, a comma is not needed. To produce complex sentences, students should appropriately attach a dependent clause to an independent clause.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Simple Sentence
	The student solves the problem.
	Compound Sentence
	The student solves the problem, and she checks her work.
	Complex Sentence
	The student solves the problem because she wants to be sure it is correct.

Expand complete simple, compound, and complex sentences

Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional clauses to simple, compound, and complex sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-2 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.

Concept
	Example Sentence
	Expanded Simple Sentence
	The student reads a funny story.
	Expanded Compound Sentence
	The student reads a funny story, and then she talks about it with her friend.
	Expanded Complex Sentence
	The student reads a funny story because she wants to share it with her friend.

Classroom Considerations
* Provide explicit, systematic instruction to support the creation of readable texts by reinforcing legible handwriting while expanding expectations to include transcription, typing, and word processing. Continue to model correct letter formation and spacing as needed, with an emphasis on consistency, alignment, and readability across longer writing tasks and multiple modes (e.g., handwritten drafts and typed final products). Use think-alouds, exemplars, and anchor charts to show how clear spacing, consistent letter size, and appropriate formatting support the reader (e.g., “When I type my sentence, I still need spaces between words so my ideas are easy to read.”). Offer frequent opportunities for guided and independent practice using both manuscript writing and digital tools, providing immediate, specific feedback focused on clarity and presentation (e.g., “Your ideas are strong—check that your spacing and letter size help the reader follow your thinking.”). Intentionally connect transcription fluency to sentence production so students can focus more on meaning, organization, and sentence expansion rather than mechanics alone.
* Explicitly teach sentence construction through modeling, including think-alouds and anchor charts, to strengthen students’ understanding of complete simple and compound sentences and introduce complex sentences. Reinforce prior learning that complete sentences communicate whole ideas, then model how complex sentences are formed by expanding an independent clause with a dependent clause that adds meaning (e.g., time, cause, condition) using subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, when, after, if). Provide clear language students can use to describe sentence structure (e.g., “This part can’t stand alone—it depends on the rest of the sentence to make sense.”). Ensure students regularly hear, say, and orally rehearse simple, compound, and complex sentences before writing to support fluency, coherence, and control of increasingly complex structures.
* Use contrastive examples, sentence combining, and sorting tasks to support students in recognizing and producing complex sentences. Engage students in oral and written comparisons that distinguish simple, compound, and complex sentences, emphasizing that complex sentences include both an independent clause and a dependent clause. Through hands-on activities, have students sort sentence strips into Simple Sentences, Compound Sentences, and Complex Sentences, then identify and label the independent and dependent clauses within complex sentences. Extend this work by having students write two to three complex sentences and use color-coding or annotation to mark the independent clause, dependent clause, and subordinating conjunction. Provide repeated opportunities across lessons and content areas for students to combine sentences, revise sentence structure, and increase independence and accuracy over time.
* Integrate sentence production with phonics, spelling, grammar, and sentence expansion so students apply previously taught spelling patterns, morphology, and language conventions while composing. Support students in maintaining attention to meaning while encoding words accurately and efficiently (e.g., “I hear the base word play, and I’m adding -ed to show it already happened—played.”). Intentionally teach sentence expansion across simple, compound, and complex sentences by modeling how varying sentence types and adding precise, relevant details enhance clarity, cohesion, and reader engagement, introducing one type of expansion at a time (e.g., “My sentence is complete—The boy finished his work. Now I’ll add why by using ‘because’ to make it more specific.”). Emphasize that writers use a mix of sentence types to keep their writing interesting and clear for the reader. Require students to first produce a complete sentence before expanding it, and continue to use oral rehearsal, structured talk, and shared revision prior to writing to support organization and control of increasingly complex ideas.
* Develop writing fluency, stamina, and transfer by increasing the volume, frequency, and complexity of sentence-writing opportunities across the school day, content areas, and modes of writing. Provide short, meaningful writing tasks that gradually extend in length and sophistication, including opportunities to compose simple, compound, and complex sentences in response to texts, investigations, shared experiences, and content learning. Make expectations transparent by tracking explicitly taught sentence-writing skills on a growing anchor chart (e.g., “We’ve learned that complex sentences include an independent clause and a dependent clause—let’s add this to our What Great Writers Do chart.”). Prioritize clarity, cohesion, and readability over stylistic variety, refining sentence quality through immediate, targeted feedback (e.g., “You used because to add a reason—your complex sentence clearly explains your thinking.”). Reinforce transfer by expecting consistent application of handwriting, transcription, and sentence construction skills across subjects and writing purposes. Refer to Recommendation 1 of the Writing Guidebook  for additional guidance.

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use nouns with verbs to form complete thoughts
	Students need to know that a sentence requires a subject (noun) and a predicate (verb) to express a complete idea. The focus is on pairing a subject (noun) with an action or state (verb).
	The frog hops.
	K

	Students need to know that most nouns form plurals by adding -s, but nouns ending in s, x, z, ch, sh require -es. Students also need to know these patterns:

The buses stop.

	That box is empty.

	Use common nouns

	The teacher smiled.

Dogs make great pets.

	Use proper nouns

	Use possessive nouns
	Students need to know that adding an apostrophe and sometimes -s shows ownership (e.g., girl’s book, dogs’ collars). They also need to recognize that possessive nouns indicate relationships.
	My mom’s car is red.
	1
	Ensure noun–verb agreement
	Students need to know that a verb must match its subject in number: singular subjects take singular verbs, and plural subjects take plural verbs. They also need to recognize that intervening words or phrases do not change the required agreement.
	My sister plays with me.

The boys on the bus laugh.
	1
	Use abstract nouns
	Students need to know that abstract nouns name ideas, qualities, or concepts that cannot be perceived with the senses (e.g., freedom, courage, happiness). They also need to recognize that abstract nouns function as subjects, objects, or complements in a sentence, just like concrete nouns.
	Honesty is important.

She showed bravery during the storm.
	2
	Use collective nouns
	Students need to know that collective nouns name a group of people, animals, or things considered as a single unit (e.g., team, flock, committee). They also need to recognize that collective nouns may take singular or plural verbs depending on whether the group is acting as a unit or as individuals.
	The pack hunts at night.
	2
	Use irregular plural nouns
	Students need to know that some nouns form their plural in ways that do not follow regular -s or -es rules (e.g., child → children, mouse → mice, foot → feet). They also need to recognize the correct plural form for irregular nouns. They should understand that these nouns still represent more than one entity despite unusual spelling.
	The geese flew over the lake.

Three deer ran into the woods.

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use personal pronouns
	Students use personal pronouns to stand in for specific people or things (e.g., I, you, he, she, it, we, they). Personal pronouns function to simplify sentences and avoid repeating nouns.
	Dan is my friend. He sits next to me.
	1
	Use possessive pronouns
	Students use possessive pronouns to show ownership without repeating a noun (e.g., mine, yours, his, hers, theirs). These pronouns function to indicate possession clearly and concisely.
	This backpack is mine.
	1
	Use indefinite pronouns
	Students need to know that indefinite pronouns refer to non-specific people or things (e.g., someone, anything, everyone). They also need to recognize which are distinctly singular or plural and which can be either singular or plural for agreement purposes.
	Something smells good.

Everyone is here.
	1
	Use reflexive pronouns
	Students need to know that reflexive pronouns refer back to the subject of the sentence (e.g., myself, yourself, themselves). They also need to recognize that they often end in -self or -selves.Reflexive pronouns function to emphasize or reflect the action back on the subject.
	He hurt himself on the slide.

They built the fort themselves.
	2
	Use relative pronouns
	Students need to know that relative pronouns (who, whom, whose, which, that) introduce clauses that describe or give more information about a noun. They also need to know that the relative clause almost always follows immediately after the noun it modifies, and that the relative pronoun is the word that connects the clause to that noun.
	I have a teacher who helps me.

I met a boy whose sister is in my class.
	2
	Ensure pronoun-
antecedent agreement
	Students need to know that a pronoun must match the number (singular or plural) and person of the noun it replaces. They also need to know that to determine agreement, they should first identify the antecedent–the specific noun the pronoun refers to–before choosing the correct pronoun form.
	Thomas Edison spent a lot of time perfecting his invention.

The miners dug for coal, and they carefully brought it to the surface.
	3
	Ensure correct agreement with indefinite pronouns
	Students need to know which indefinite pronouns are singular, plural, or sometimes both (e.g., everyone → singular; some → singular or plural), so verbs agree correctly.
	Everyone in the science class has finished their experiment on ecosystems.

Both of the strategies are helpful for solving the word problem.

	Use verb tense to convey various times, sequences, states, and conditions
	Students need to know that verb tense changes the form of a verb to indicate when an action or state occurs, how actions relate in time, or under what conditions they happen. They also need to recognize that choosing the correct tense ensures clarity about the sequence and timing of events.
	Past: The three branches of government worked together to create and enforce laws for the country.

Present: Communities make rules to keep people safe and help everyone live peacefully together.

Future: Scientists will study ways to reduce pollution in the oceans.

	Use possessive pronouns

	Use intensive pronouns

	6
	Use subjective pronoun case

	They finished the experiment before the bell rang.
	6
	Use objective pronoun case
	Students need to know that objective pronouns (me, him, her, us, them) function as the object of a verb or preposition. They also need to recognize that the object receives the action or is the target of the preposition.

Verb Use Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Form and use action verbs with -ing, -ed, -s
	Students need to know that -ing shows ongoing action, -ed shows past action, and -s shows third-person singular present.
	She is jumping.

They played outside.

My dad cooks lunch.
	K
	Use verbs to express past, present, future
	Students need to know that verbs change form to indicate when an action occurs (e.g., walked, walk, will walk).
	He jumped on my back.

He jumps high.

He will jump far.
	K
	Form and use irregular past tense verbs
	Students need to know that regular verbs form the past with -ed, while irregular verbs have unique past forms..
	We ate lunch outside.
	2
	Use regular and irregular verbs in simple tenses
	Students need to know that regular verbs form their past tense by adding -ed (e.g., walk → walked), while irregular verbs have unique past tense forms that do not follow a predictable pattern (e.g., go → went, eat → ate, am/is/are → was/were). They also need to recognize that simple tenses–past, present, and future–express actions or states at a single point in time.
	Past: We went outside to observe how much the plants had grown.

Present: She goes outside every morning to observe how much the plants have grown.

Future: We will go outside tomorrow to measure the growth of the plants.
	3
	Ensure subject-verb agreement
	Students need to know that singular subjects take singular verbs and plural subjects take plural verbs, even when intervening words or phrases appear between the subject and verb. They also need to know how agreement works with:
* Compound subjects (“and” usually = plural; “or/nor” = verb agrees with nearest subject)
* Indefinite pronouns (singular: everyone, each; plural: few, many; some can be singular or plural depending on meaning)
* Collective nouns (singular when acting as a unit, plural when acting as individuals)
* Inverted sentences (identify the true subject first, e.g., There are many books)
* Titles, sums, or amounts (usually singular, e.g., The United States is large)
	Singular Subject:
The teacher needs to know where you found evidence of this in the text.

Plural Subject:
They need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.

Indefinite Singular:
Everyone needs to know where you found evidence of this in the text.

Indefinite Plural:
A few (of us) need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.

Compound Subject:
John and Jane need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.

Singular Collective Noun:
The class needs to know where you found evidence of this in the text.

Plural Collective Noun:
The students need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.
Inverted Sentence:
Does the class need to know where you found evidence of this in the text?

Amount:
Those two (students) need to know where you found evidence of this in the text.
	3
	Form and use progressive aspect
	Students need to know the progressive aspect is formed with a past, present, or future form of “be” (i.e., state of being) + a progressive verb “-ing” to show ongoing actions (e.g., is running, were eating, will be fishing). Progressive verbs function to indicate actions in progress.
	Environmentalists are cleaning up the trash that is polluting our oceans.
	4
	Use modal auxiliaries
	Students need to know modal verbs (e.g., can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would, ought to, need to, used to, have to) show ability, possibility, necessity, obligation, or permission. Modals are a specific type of auxiliary verb that add meaning to, and are always followed by, the base form of a main verb.
	People should clean up the trash that is polluting our oceans.
	4
	Form and use perfect aspect
	Students need to know the perfect aspect is formed with a past, present, or future form of “have” + a past participle to show completed actions related to another point in time (e.g., has walked, had eaten, will have fished). Perfect aspect functions to indicate actions that are finished relative to the present or past.
	Oceans currents have greatly affected our weather.
	5
	Form and use perfect progressive aspect
	Students need to know the perfect progressive aspect is formed with a past, present, or future tense of “have” + been + a progressive verb (-ing) to show an action that was ongoing for a period of time leading up to a specific point in time (e.g., has been running, had been eating, will have been fishing). This aspect emphasizes duration in relation to completion or a reference moment, highlighting both the ongoing nature of the action and its connection to another time.
	She has been texting her friend group about this evening's school dance since yesterday.
	6
	Recognize and correct inappropriate tense shifts
	Students maintain consistent verb tense within sentences or related sentences. Students need to know that verbs in a sentence or related sentences should maintain consistent tense unless the timeline changes.
	Last night he studied for the quiz, reviewed his notes, and completed the practice questions.
	6
	Distinguish between active and passive voice
	Students need to know that active voice emphasizes the subject performing the action, while passive voice emphasizes the subject receiving the action. They also need to know that passive constructions typically use a form of “be” + past participle.
	Active Voice:
The students designed the posters for Spirit Week.

Passive Voice:
The posters were designed by the students for Spirit Week.
	7
	Use active and passive voice appropriate to purpose, audience, task
	Students select active or passive voice to match their writing goal (e.g., active for directness, passive for emphasis on the action). Students need to know which form emphasizes the subject or action.
	Active Voice:
Our dedicated team won the championship game after months of practice.

Passive Voice:
The championship game was won by our dedicated team after months of practice.
	8
	Use indicative, imperative, and interrogative moods
	Students need to know that verb mood indicates the function or purpose of a sentence. In the indicative mood, verbs express statements or facts. In the imperative mood, verbs give commands or instructions. In the interrogative mood, verbs form questions. Students also need to recognize that verb forms, word order, and auxiliary verbs often signal the mood of a sentence.
	Indicative Mood:
We are taking the bus home from school tomorrow.

Imperative Mood:
Finish your meal before you can have dessert.

Interrogative Mood:
Did you upload your homework to the class website?
	8
	Use conditional and subjunctive moods
	Students need to know that the conditional mood expresses hypothetical situations and possible, but not guaranteed, outcomes by often using if/if-were clauses and modal auxiliaries. The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, demands, or possibilities that are not factual. Students need to recognize that the subjunctive often appears in clauses following verbs expressing desire, recommendation, or uncertainty and that the verb in the subjunctive is usually the base form (i.e., no -s, -ed, or -ing ending). The past subjunctive, especially with the verb to be (e.g., were), can sound unusual or “formal” because it does not follow normal past-tense patterns and is often replaced by was in casual speech; however, using were correctly signals a hypothetical or non-factual situation, which is essential for expressing wishes, contrary-to-fact statements, or imagined scenarios.
	Conditional Mood:
If the weather improves, we will go for a hike tomorrow.

If the data were more complete, the results would be more reliable.

Subjunctive Mood:
Members of the book club wished their next read were available for pick-up.

The committee recommended that the proposal be revised before approval.

	9-12
	Use verbals (gerunds, participles, infinitives)
	Students need to know that verbals are verb forms that function in ways other than as the main verb of a clause. Gerunds are formed by adding -ing to a verb and act as nouns. Participles act as adjectives: present participles are formed with -ing , and past participles often end in -ed for regular verbs or take an irregular form. Infinitives are formed with to + base verb and can function as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs. Students also need to recognize that verbals allow sentences to vary in structure, provide additional detail, and express actions, qualities, or purposes in different grammatical roles.
	Gerund (as a noun):
Writing improves with consistent practice.
She values listening in difficult conversations.

Past Participle (as an adjective):
The exhausted runner collapsed at the finish line.

Present Participle (as an adjective):
The emerging theory challenged earlier assumptions.

Infinitive (as a noun):
To learn requires patience and persistence.

Infinitive (as an adverb of purpose):
She stayed late to revise.

Infinitive (as an adjective):
The committee proposed a plan to implement.

Adjective & Adverb Use Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use adjectives
	Students need to know that adjectives change or clarify the meaning of a noun. They also need to recognize that adjectives usually come directly before the noun or after certain verbs like be or seem.
	I saw black clouds in the sky.
	K
	Use adjectives and adverbs
	Students need to know adjectives modify nouns, while adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Students need to know the distinction between the two types of modifiers. They also need to recognize the questions each answers: adjectives answer which one, what kind, how many; adverbs answer how, when, where, or to what extent.
	The girl drew three boxes on her paper.

She drew them quickly.
	2
	Choose appropriate modifier based on what is being modified
	Students need to know modifiers must match the type of word they modify: adjectives with nouns and adverbs with verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Students need to know that placing the wrong type of modifier changes or confuses the meaning. They also need to understand basic placement rules: modifiers should be close to the word they describe.
	We saw a beautiful sunset.

The girl spoke softly.
	2
	Form and use comparative adjectives/ adverbs
	Students need to know comparatives are forms of adjectives or adverbs that show ranges of difference between two things (e.g., taller, more quickly). Students need to know that comparatives often use “-er” or “more” depending on word structure. They also need to recognize how to compare actions or qualities accurately using these forms.
	Comparative Adjective:
The area of the large rectangle is the sum of the two smaller rectangles.

Comparative Adverb:
The area of the large rectangle is found more easily by adding the two smaller rectangles.
	3
	Form and use superlative adjectives/
adverbs
	Students need to know superlatives are forms of adjectives or adverbs that show the highest or extreme degree among three or more things (e.g., tallest, most quickly). Students need to know that superlatives often use “-est” or “most” depending on word structure. They also need to recognize how to identify or form the “most extreme” quality in a set.
	Superlative Adjective:
Heat always moves from the hottest object to the cooler one.

Superlative Adverb:
Heat moves from a warmer object to a cooler one the fastest when they touch each other.
	3
	Use relative adverbs
	Students need to know that relative adverbs (where, when, why) introduce clauses that provide additional information about a noun or verb. They need to know that these clauses follow the word they describe and function like adjectives or adverbs, explaining location (where), time (when), or reason (why). Students also need to recognize that these relative adverb clauses cannot stand alone and always link their clause to a specific noun or verb in the main clause.
	I need to use a pictorial model when showing my work for the word problem.
	3
	Order adjectives according to conventional patterns
	Students need to know that when multiple adjectives modify a noun, they usually follow a specific sequence (e.g., quantity, opinion, size, age, color, origin, material). Students need to know the conventional order of adjectives to avoid confusion. They also need to recognize how each adjective type answers a different descriptive question.
	Opinion → Age → Material → Noun:
The beautiful old wooden box contained many valuables.
	4
	Recognize
and correct misplaced modifiers
	Students need to know that a misplaced modifier is a word or phrase that is too far from the word it describes, which can create confusion or an unintended meaning. They need to know that modifiers must be placed as close as possible to the word they describe. To identify misplaced modifiers, students should locate the word or phrase being described and check whether the modifier is positioned directly next to it. They also need to identify key words in the sentence to attach modifiers correctly.
	Misplaced Modifier:
I almost listened to music for three hours while doing homework.

Corrected Misplaced Modifier:
I listened to music for almost three hours while doing homework.
	7
	Recognize
and correct dangling modifiers
	Students need to know that a dangling modifier is a descriptive word or phrase that does not clearly modify any word in the sentence, often leaving the sentence confusing or illogical. They need to know that every modifier must have a clear word it describes. To identify dangling modifiers, students should determine the intended subject or main noun for the modifier; if the intended noun is missing or unclear, the modifier is dangling and the sentence needs revision.
	Dangling Modifier:
Walking to school, the bus passed by.

Dangling Modifier Corrected:
While I was walking to school, the bus passed by.
	7

Phrase, Clause, & Cohesion Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use coordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet) connect words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance. They also need to recognize that coordinating conjunctions join ideas without changing the grammatical function of the connected elements.
	She is small but strong!
	1
	Use subordinating conjunctions
	Students need to know that subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, if, when, since, while, etc.) connect a dependent clause to an independent clause. They also need to recognize that the dependent clause cannot stand alone and functions to provide time, cause, condition, contrast, or reason.
	I stayed inside because it was storming.

If it snows, we will build a snowman.
	2
	Use prepositional phrases
	Students need to know that a prepositional phrase consists of a preposition and its object (noun or pronoun) and functions as an adjective or adverb. They also need to recognize how prepositional phrases provide information about location, time, direction, cause, or manner.
	The price of toys went up because of the high demand in the store.
	3
	Use correlative conjunctions
	Students need to know that correlative conjunctions work in pairs (e.g., either–or, neither–nor, both–and, not only–but also) to connect balanced elements of equal grammatical importance. They also need to recognize that each part of the pair must attach to similar structures (e.g., noun to noun, clause to clause).
	Both the mountains and the rivers affect where people build cities and farms in the United States.
	5
	Form and use verbal phrases for variety, precision, and nuance
	Students need to know that verbal phrases are built around verbals—gerunds (-ing forms functioning as nouns), participles (present -ing or past -ed/irregular forms functioning as adjectives), and infinitives (to + verb forms functioning as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs). They need to recognize that verbal phrases combine these verbals with other words (modifiers or objects) to create noun, adjective, or adverb phrases and that verbal phrases can appear at the beginning, middle, and end of sentences. Students also need to know that using verbal phrases allows sentences to vary in structure, add descriptive detail, and convey subtle nuances of meaning, such as timing, cause, or purpose and that verbal phrases can be layered with each other for increased sophistication.
	Gerund Phrase (as a noun):
Analyzing primary sources requires questioning assumptions and evaluating context.

Present Participial Phrase (as an adjective):
Clutching the letter in her trembling hands, she realized the truth had arrived too late.

Past Participial Phrase (as an adjective):
Worn by decades of use, the book still held its authority.

Infinitive Phrase (as an adverb):
The city invested in more efficient public transportation to reduce energy consumption.

Infinitive Phrase (as a noun):
To forgive after such betrayal felt impossible.

Layered Verbal Phrases (participial + infinitive):
Hoping to persuade undecided voters, the candidate delivered a speech designed to highlight economic stability.
	9-12
	Use parallel structure to emphasize parity between ideas
	Students need to know that parallel structure means using the same grammatical form for words, phrases, or clauses that are joined or compared. They need to know that elements that can be made parallel include lists of words or phrases, paired ideas connected by coordinating or correlative conjunctions, and clauses within a sentence. Students also need to recognize that maintaining parallel forms ensures clarity, readability, and equal emphasis across ideas in a sentence and that when combined with extended repetition, parallelism lends itself to emphasis of ideas.
	Parallel Verbs in a List:
The researcher collected, analyzed, and interpreted the data.

Parallel Gerund Phrases in a List:
Effective study habits include reviewing notes regularly, completing practice problems, and seeking feedback.

Parallel Infinitive Phrases in a List:
She wanted to leave quietly, to avoid conflict, and to preserve her dignity.

Parallel Structure with Correlative Conjunctions:
The policy aims not only to reduce costs but also to improve accessibility.

Parallel Dependent Clauses:
When the evidence is examined carefully and when the context is considered honestly, the argument becomes more convincing.
	9-12

Organized by skill/concept, the charts demonstrate the progression of skills across grade levels, helping identify what students learned in prior grades, what they are expected to learn in the current grade, and how those skills progress in future grades. Example sentences, where included, demonstrate convention concepts and provide potential models for instructional use.

Capitalization Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Capitalize the first word in a sentence
	Students need to know that the first word in every sentence begins with a capital letter, regardless of its part of speech. This signals the start of a complete thought.
	We like to play.
	K
	Capitalize the pronoun I
	Students need to know that the pronoun “I” is always capitalized, regardless of its position in a sentence, because it represents the speaker.
	I can jump.

Can I help?
	K
	Capitalize names of people
	Students need to know that proper nouns naming specific people begin with capital letters.
	Catie is my friend.

I sit next to John.
	1
	Capitalize dates
	Students need to know that the day and month in a date are capitalized.
	My birthday is on Monday, April 20.
	1
	Capitalize holidays
	Students need to know that names of holidays are capitalized.
	We wear red on Valentine’s Day.
	2
	Capitalize brand names
	Students need to know that specific brands or trademarks are capitalized.
	I drink Gatorade after every baseball game.
	2
	Capitalize names of places
	Students need to know that specific geographic locations are capitalized.
	We took a trip to the Greenville Zoo.

I used to live in Florida.
	2
	Capitalize titles of texts
	Students need to know that–generally–nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, and subordinating conjunctions in titles of books, articles, and other works are capitalized. Generally, articles, prepositions, and coordinating conjunctions are not unless they are the first or last word of a title.
	Charlotte is very caring and kind towards Wilbur in Charlotte’s Web.

In The One and Only Ivan, Ivan spends most of his life in a shopping mall.
	3
	Use capitalization appropriately in dialogue
	Students need to know that the first word of a speaker’s statement is capitalized, even within a sentence or paragraph.
	Continuous Dialogue: Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.

Interrupted Dialogue Response (Dialogue tag interrupts two sentences):  “Hard work pays off,” said Paul, “We’ll need to keep practicing every day!”

Interrupted Dialogue Response (Dialogue tag interrupts a single sentence):  “I knew you could do it,” said Marcus, “because you practice harder than anyone else.”
	3
	Capitalize abbreviations
	Students need to know that standard abbreviations for proper nouns or titles are capitalized (e.g., Doctor→Dr., Junior→Jr., United States of America →U.S.A, Street→St., frequently asked questions→FAQ, etc.).
	Dr. Jones says that all students need sufficient exercise.
	4
	Capitalize proper adjectives
	Students need to know that adjectives derived from proper nouns are capitalized.
	My love of Italian food comes from my childhood.
	4
	Capitalize formal titles
	Students need to know that official titles are capitalized when used with a person’s name.
	The bravery of Captain Adams saved the entire crew that day.
	4
	Use capitalization appropriately to indicate direct speech and text quotes
	Students need to know that the first word of a direct speech statement and the first word of a complete quoted sentence are capitalized. Quoted statements that cannot stand alone are not capitalized.
	Concluding With a Quote (quoting a complete sentence): The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting an incomplete sentence): The author explains that ocean currents are “powerful forces that shape Earth’s climate” and affect weather patterns around the world.

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting a complete sentence): The author states, “Ocean currents move warm and cold water around the Earth,” to explain how they affect climate.
	5

End Punctuation Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Recognize and name end punctuation marks
	Students need to know that sentences end with one of three marks: period (.), question mark (?), or exclamation point (!). Students need to know the names of the end punctuation marks and their basic functions.
	I see a cat.

Do you like dogs?

That was fun!
	K
	Use correct end punctuation for sentences
	Students need to know how to choose the correct end punctuation based on sentence type: periods for declarative, question marks for interrogative, or exclamation points for exclamatory sentences.
	My sister plays soccer.

How old are you?

We won our last game!
	1

Comma Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use commas in dates
	Students need to know that a comma separates the day and year in full dates.
	We moved on June 26, 2023.
	1
	Use commas to separate words in a series
	Students need to know that commas separate three or more items in a list.
	My favorite colors are pink, green, and purple.
	1
	Use commas before coordinating conjunctions in compound sentences
	Students need to know that a comma comes before coordinating conjunctions (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) when joining two independent clauses.
	I wanted to play outside, but it started to rain.
	1
	Use commas in addresses
	Students need to know that in addresses written in sentences, commas separate the street address, the city, and the state. When addresses are written on separate lines, a comma is generally only needed between the city and state.
	The birthday party is at 123 Happy Place, Cary, North Carolina.

123 Happy Place
Cary, North Carolina
	2
	Use commas in greetings and closings
	Students need to know that commas follow the greeting or closing in letters, emails, cards, etc.
	Example Greeting:
Dear Mom,

Example Closing:
Love, Mary
	2
	Use commas in dialogue
	Students need to know that commas separate the words of a speaker from dialogue tags.
	Continuous Dialogue: Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.

Interrupted Dialogue Response:  “I knew you could do it,” said Marcus, “because you practice harder than anyone else.”
	3
	Use commas to indicate direct address
	Students need to know that commas set off names or titles of people being spoken to. When direct addresses are at the beginning of a sentence, the comma comes after them. When direct addresses are at the end of a sentence, the comma comes before.
	John, I agree with what you said about the photograph helping readers understand deforestation better.

I agree with what you said about the photograph helping readers understand deforestation better, John.
	4
	Use commas to set off “yes” and “no”
	Students need to know that commas follow introductory yes or no in a sentence.
	Yes, I do believe that I included enough supporting evidence.
	4
	Use commas to indicate direct speech and quotations
	Students need to know that commas separate quoted material from the rest of the sentence unless the quoted material is integrated into the grammatical structure of the sentence. When quoted material concludes a sentence, a comma comes only before the opening quotation mark. When quoted material is introduced mid-sentence, a comma comes before the opening quotation mark and before the closing quotation mark.
	Concluding With a Quote (quoting a complete sentence): The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Mid-sentence Quotes (quoting a complete sentence): The author states, “Ocean currents move warm and cold water around the Earth,” to explain how they affect climate.

Integrating Quote into Grammatical Structure (quoting an incomplete sentence): The author explains that ocean currents are “powerful forces that shape Earth’s climate” and affect weather patterns around the world.
	5
	Use commas after introductory elements
	Students need to know that commas follow introductory words, phrases, or clauses.
	Single Word:
Finally, I believe that students should have longer recess because research shows that it leads to better mental health.

Prepositional Phrases as an Introductory Element:
In the second paragraph of the article, the author provides evidence that recycling helps reduce pollution.

Complex Sentence (When Dependent Clause is First):
Because of the growing list of grievances, the colonists decided to declare independence.
	5
	Use commas to set off tag questions
	Students need to know that commas separate a statement from a short question at the end of a sentence.
	The evidence for this is stated in the first paragraph, isn’t it?
	5
	Use commas in compound- complex sentences
	Students need to know that commas help separate clauses in sentences with multiple independent and dependent clauses for clarity.
	When I finished my homework, I texted my friends because I wanted to relax, and we planned our gaming session.
	6
	Use commas to separate coordinate adjectives
	Students need to know that commas separate two or more adjectives that equally modify a noun.
	She bought a trendy, oversized hoodie at the mall.
	7
	Use commas for nonrestrictive/parenthetical elements
	Students need to know that commas can set off extra information or details that are not essential to the core meaning of the sentence. This extra information often appears in recognizable structures, such as appositives, nonrestrictive relative clauses, and nonessential participial phrases.
	My best friend, who sits next to me in math class, helped me study for the test.
	7

Apostrophe Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use apostrophes to form contractions
	Students need to know that apostrophes replace omitted letters in contractions.
	It’s time for lunch.

	1
	Use apostrophes to form singular possessive nouns
	Students need to know that an apostrophe + s shows ownership for singular nouns.
	The bird’s nest is in that tree.
	2

	Students need to know that an apostrophe after s shows ownership for plural nouns.
	Regular Plural: Pioneers’ wagons often broke down on the road.

Irregular Plurals (not ending in s): It was the people’s right to abolish the government.

Same Plural and Singular: The scissors’ blades are very sharp.

Quotations & Dialogue Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use quotation marks in dialogue
	Students need to know that spoken words are enclosed in quotation marks.
	Billy rushed over to his friends and cried, “I made the team!”  “That’s great!” replied Jane.
	3
	Use quotation marks in titles (when appropriate)
	Students need to know that titles of short works (poems, articles, short stories, chapters in larger texts) are enclosed in quotation marks.
	In “Chapter 4” of Charlotte’s Web, Wilbur experiences loneliness.
	3
	Use quotation marks  to indicate direct speech and text quotations
	Students need to know that a speaker’s exact words go inside quotation marks and that any words copied directly from a text must also be placed inside quotation marks. When quoting text that includes dialogue, students should use single quotation marks to signal the dialogue.
	Quoting Text:
The article states, “All students require adequate and sufficient exercise in order to be healthy.”

Quoting Dialogue:
In Bud, Not Buddy, Bud explains, “I remember what Momma always told me, ‘If you have to tell a lie, make it simple and believable.’”
	5
	Format block quotes appropriately
	Students need to know that long quotations (usually more than four lines) are formatted as block quotes. In MLA style, they are indented an additional ½”, double-spaced, use no quotation marks (unless quoted text is dialogue), and are often separated by line breaks before and after. For quoting verse/poems specifically, students should maintain the original line breaks in the source text. Unlike other in-text citations, students should include the appropriate citation after the period of the final sentence.
	Block Quote with Dialogue:
Steinbeck consistently highlights the responsibility George feels towards Lennie, showing how George attempts to insulate Lennie from the consequences of his own problematic behavior. Early in the novel, Steinbeck demonstrates how George guides Lennie’s behavior and thinking:
"’Course you did. Well, look Lennie - if you jus’ happen to get in trouble like you always done before, I want you to come right here an’ hide in the brush."
"Hide in the brush," said Lennie slowly.
"Hide in the brush till I come for you. Can you remember that?"
"Sure I can, George. Hide in the brush till you come." (Steinbeck 15)
Even in the first chapter of the novella, it is apparent that there is a significant mental load for George in his friendship with Lennie.

Dashes, Parentheses, Hyphens, & Ellipses Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use parentheses for nonrestrictive/ parenthetical elements
	Students need to know that parentheses enclose extra information or clarifications that are not essential to the main sentence meaning.
	To get to the park, turn right at the light (right next to the grocery store) and take the next left into the parking lot.
	7
	Use hyphens with affixes and compound words
	Students need to know that hyphens connect prefixes, suffixes, or compound words.
	She is a well-known social media creator at our school.
	8
	Use ellipses appropriately
	Students need to know that ellipses indicate omitted words within a quotation, especially when citing a source. They can also be used stylistically in writing to signal a pause or trailing thought.
	Ellipsis to Indicate an Omission:
In his speech, Martin Luther King Jr. stated, “"I have a dream that one day…little black boys and black girls will be able to join hands with little white boys and white girls as sisters and brothers".

Ellipsis to Indicate Pause in Thought:
I was going to hang out with my friends tonight… but Netflix won.
	8
	Use colons to introduce lists
	Students need to know that colons follow independent clauses and introduce lists and explanations. Typically, lists that follow colons follow a parallel structure.
	Colon Introducing a List of Nouns:
For the camping trip, we need to pack several items: a tent, a sleeping bag, a stove, and a flashlight.

Colon Introducing a List of Gerundial Phrases:
Her weekend plans will keep her busy each day: going to the library, meeting friends for coffee, swimming at the lake, and finishing her art project.
	9-10
	Use colons to introduce quotations
	Students need to know that colons follow a complete sentence and introduce quotations, either direct (from a speaker) or text-sourced (from an authored text). Direct quotations that follow colons are indicated with an opening and closing quotation mark, and the end punctuation (e.g., period, question mark, exclamation point) is contained within the closing quotation mark. Text-sourced quotations are also indicated with an opening and closing quotation mark, but the end punctuation follows an appropriately formatted in-text citation, as needed.
	Colon Introducing a Direct Quotation:
Coach Stevens reminded the team of their golden rule: “Always give your best effort.”

Colon Introducing a Text-Sourced Quotation:
Elie Wiesel makes his opinion on indifference quite plain: “Indifference, after all, is more dangerous than anger and hatred” (Wiesel, par. 9).
	9-10
	Use em dashes for nonrestrictive or parenthetical elements
	Students need to know that em dashes can set off extra information or clarifications that are not essential to the main sentence meaning or indicate a break in thought. When parenthetical/nonrestrictive elements occur in the middle of a sentence, the content is enclosed in em dashes. When  parenthetical/nonrestrictive elements occur at the end of a sentence, the content is set-off by one em dash.
	Em Dash with Mid-Sentence Parenthetical:
Shakespeare’s depiction of Hamlet–the prince torn between duty and desire–reveals the enduring tension between action and contemplation.

Em Dash with End-of-Sentence Clarification:
The novel critiques societal inequality–through both subtle imagery and stark dialogue–highlighting the pervasive effects of class.

Em Dash with Break in Thought/Interruption:
The argument seemed sound–but upon closer inspection, the evidence revealed several inconsistencies.
	9-12

Semicolons & Colons Skills

Concept
	Description
	Example Sentence
	Grade
	Use semicolons to separate items in a series when items include internal commas
	Students need to know that semicolons separate complex list items that already contain commas to preserve clarity.
	For our day at the park, I packed snacks, including chips and candy; Ava made the playlists, selecting music we each like; and Leo brought the games, hoping to keep us all entertained.

I have lived in Omaha, Nebraska; Litchfield, Connecticut; and Mason City, Iowa.
	8
	Use semicolons with conjunctive adverbs or transitional phrases to link independent clauses
	Students need to know that semicolons can join two independent clauses, providing an additional way to connect complete ideas. This is especially useful when the clauses already contain internal commas or when using conjunctive adverbs (e.g., however, therefore, nevertheless, nonetheless, consequently, furthermore, moreover) or transitional phrases (e.g.,as a result, for this reason, in light of this, in contrast, on the other hand). When a conjunctive adverb or transitional phrase is used, the semicolon comes before it, and a comma always follows it, helping to maintain clarity in complex sentences.
	Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs, No Internal Punctuation:
The novel explores themes of ambition and betrayal; however, the protagonist’s personal growth remains central to the story.

Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs, Internal Punctuation:
Researchers have studied the effects of social media on teenagers, examining its impact on mental health, including anxiety and self-esteem, its role in shaping peer relationships and social norms, and its influence on academic motivation and time management; therefore, they argue that schools and families should guide responsible use while encouraging balance and critical thinking.

Semicolons with Transitional Phrases, No Internal Punctuation:
She completed the research experiment successfully; in the meantime, her classmates continued testing alternative hypotheses.

Semicolons with Transitional Phrases, Internal Punctuation:
The study examined social media use among teenagers, including time spent on messaging apps, participation in online communities, and exposure to trending challenges; as a result, researchers found significant correlations between frequent use, sleep disruption, and lower academic focus.
	9-12
	Use semicolons with coordinating conjunctions when independent clauses contain internal punctuation
	Students need to know that sometimes a semicolon can be used with a coordinating conjunction to join two sentences together and preserve clarity when the sentences are long and contain additional internal punctuation. They also need to know that this is an uncommon construction and is most helpful when one or both sentences already include several commas (e.g., complex sentences, series of phrases, etc.). Students should recognize that in this construction, the semicolon comes before the coordinating conjunction, and no additional comma is needed immediately after it.
	Semicolons with Coordinating Conjunctions, Internal Punctuation:
Since he speaks French, Spanish, and Italian, he assumed he would have no trouble traveling in southern Europe, especially since many people in the region now speak English; but when he arrived in Sicily, his unfamiliarity with the Sicilian dialect made communication difficult.

The treaty established new boundaries, clarified trade agreements, and outlined military obligations; and although it was celebrated in the capitals, many local leaders opposed its terms.
	9-12

Sentence Production & Expansion Skills

Sentence Type
	Description
	Examples
	Grade
	Simple Sentence
	Students need to know that a simple sentence is made up of one independent clause, which includes a subject and a predicate and expresses a complete thought. In most simple sentences, the subject comes before the predicate. To produce simple sentences, students should write an independent clause with a clear subject–predicate structure that expresses a complete thought.
	Simple Sentence
The dog ran.
	K
	Expand complete simple sentences
	Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional information to a simple sentence while keeping the sentence grammatically correct.  Students should flexibly use K grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.
	Expanded Simple Sentence
The big dog ran fast.
	K
	Produce complete simple and compound sentences
	In addition to producing simple sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a compound sentence consists of two or more independent clauses joined by coordinating conjunctions (e.g., for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) with commas. To produce compound sentences, students should join independent clauses using a coordinating conjunction and a comma.
	Simple Sentence
Plants need water.

Compound Sentence
Plants need water, so they can grow.
	1
	Expand complete simple and compound sentences
	Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional information to simple and compound sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., simple → simple; compound → compound), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound). Students should flexibly use K-1 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.
	Expanded Simple Sentence
Plants need fresh water every day.

Expanded Compound Sentence
Plants need fresh water every day, so they can grow big and strong.
	1
	Produce complete simple, compound, and complex sentences
	In addition to producing simple and compound sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a complex sentence includes one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses, which do not express a complete thought on their own and are often introduced by subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, when, if) or relative pronouns. When the dependent clause comes before the independent clause, it is attached with a comma. When it comes after, a comma is not needed. To produce complex sentences, students should appropriately attach a dependent clause to an independent clause.
	Simple Sentence
The student solves the problem.

Compound Sentence
The student solves the problem, and she checks her work.

Complex Sentence
The student solves the problem because she wants to be sure it is correct.
	2
	Expand complete simple, compound, and complex sentences
	Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional clauses to simple, compound, and complex sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-2 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.
	Expanded Simple Sentence
The student reads a funny story.

Expanded Compound Sentence
The student reads a funny story, and then she talks about it with her friend.

Expanded Complex Sentence
The student reads a funny story because she wants to share it with her friend.
	2
	Produce complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in a paragraph
	In addition to producing simple and compound sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a complex sentence includes one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses, which do not express a complete thought on their own and are often introduced by subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, when, if) or relative pronouns. When the dependent clause comes before the independent clause, it is attached with a comma. When it comes after, a comma is not needed. To produce complex sentences, students should appropriately attach a dependent clause to an independent clause.
	Simple Sentence
Light bends slightly in clear water.

Compound Sentence
Light goes through the water, and it changes direction.

Complex Sentence
When light passes through water, it changes direction.
	3
	Expand complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in a paragraph
	Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional clauses to simple, compound, and complex sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K- 3 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.
	Expanded Simple Sentence
Light bends slightly in clear water, making objects look different.

Expanded Compound Sentence
Light passes through the water most clearly, and it changes direction the fastest.

Expanded Complex Sentence
When bright light passes through water where it is clear, it bends and changes direction quickly.
	3
	Produce complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in paragraphs
	In addition to producing simple and compound sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a complex sentence includes one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses, which do not express a complete thought on their own and are often introduced by subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, when, if) or relative pronouns. When the dependent clause comes before the independent clause, it is attached with a comma. When it comes after, a comma is not needed. To produce complex sentences, students should appropriately attach a dependent clause to an independent clause.
	Simple Sentence
The wind blows gently through the towering trees.

Compound Sentence
The wind blows through the swaying trees, and it shakes the leaves.

Complex Sentence
When the strong, chilly wind blows through the trees, it shakes the leaves.
	4
	Expand complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in paragraphs
	Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional clauses to simple, compound, and complex sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-4 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.
	Expanded Simple Sentence
Ah, how the wind whispers through the towering, swaying trees!

Expanded Compound Sentence
The wind whispers through the swaying trees, and it shakes the leaves like tiny dancers on a stage.

Expanded Complex Sentence
When the strong, chilly wind blows through the trees, it is a conductor, making the leaves create a silent performance.
	4
	Rearrange complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in paragraphs

Students need to know how to move modifiers, phrases, and clauses within simple, compound, and complex sentences to improve flow, coherence, or emphasis and without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that rearranging parts of sentences can improve flow, coherence, and emphasis without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by requiring adjustment to clause structure (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-4 grammar and convention knowledge to rearrange sentences appropriate to the grade-level.
	Rearranged Simple Sentence
Ah, how the towering, swaying trees are whispered through by the wind!

Rearranged Compound Sentence
The wind whispers, and it shakes the leaves on the swaying trees like tiny dancers on a stage.

Rearranged Complex Sentence
The strong, chilly wind is a conductor, making the leaves create a silent performance.
	4
	Produce complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in paragraphs
	In addition to producing simple and compound sentences based on prior learning, students also need to know that a complex sentence includes one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses, which do not express a complete thought on their own and are often introduced by subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, when, if) or relative pronouns. When the dependent clause comes before the independent clause, it is attached with a comma. When it comes after, a comma is not needed. To produce complex sentences, students should appropriately attach a dependent clause to an independent clause.
	Simple Sentence
Recess should be extended for all students to relax and enjoy their day more.

Compound Sentence
Recess should be longer and students should have more time to play.

Complex Sentence
If recess is extended, students will enjoy school more.
	5
	Expand complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in paragraphs
	Students need to know how to add modifiers, phrases, or additional clauses to simple, compound, and complex sentences without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that expansion can increase sentence detail without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by adding detail in new clauses (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-5 grammar and convention knowledge to expand sentences to be appropriate to the grade-level.
	Expanded Simple Sentence
Recess should be extended to both help students relax and enjoy their day more and give them a chance to run and play.

Expanded Compound Sentence
Either recess should be longer, or students will miss out on important playtime, and Dr. Smith says, “Children who engage in daily physical activity perform better academically and socially.”

Expanded Complex Sentence
If recess is extended, students will enjoy school more, because a little fun goes a long way.
	5
	Rearrange complete simple, compound, and complex sentences in paragraphs
	Students need to know how to move modifiers, phrases, and clauses within simple, compound, and complex sentences to improve flow, coherence, or emphasis and without creating fragments or run-ons. Students should understand that rearranging parts of sentences can improve flow, coherence, and emphasis without changing the sentence type (e.g., compound → compound; complex → complex), or it can change the sentence type by requiring adjustment to clause structure (e.g., simple → compound; compound → complex). Students should flexibly use K-5 grammar and convention knowledge to rearrange sentences appropriate to the grade-level.
	Rearranged Simple Sentence
An extended recess would allow all students to relax and enjoy their day more.

Rearranged Compound Sentence
Dr. Smith says, “Children who engage in daily physical activity perform better academically and socially,” so either recess should be longer or students will miss out on important playtime.

Rearranged Complex Sentence
Because a little fun goes a long way, students will enjoy school more if recess is extended.
	5
	Produce complete simple, compound, complex, and compound- complex sentence structures
	In addition to producing simple, compound, and complex sentences based on prior learning, students need to know that compound sentences can also be formed using a semicolon. Students also need to know that a compound-complex sentence combines multiple independent clauses with at least one dependent clause. To produce compound-complex sentences, students should join independent clauses using a coordinating conjunction and a comma or a semicolon and attach at least one dependent clause within a single sentence.
	Simple Sentence
The student checked her phone for messages after finishing her homework.

Compound Sentence
He studied for the history test, and he reviewed his math notes one last time.

Complex Sentence
Although the game wasn’t close, the team stayed focused until the final buzzer.

Compound-Complex Sentence
After completing the draft of our group presentation, we shared our work online, and our classmates gave helpful feedback.

Students need to know that fused sentences are a type of run-on where two or more independent clauses are joined with no punctuation.
