# Arts Education - Eighth Grade

Parent Guide for Eighth Grade Dance

The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
The grade 6-8 standards and objectives are written for middle school students who are taught by a licensed dance educator for at least 90 minutes per week.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students explore influential North Carolinian dance styles and artists and examine how skills and information from dance can be used to solve a variety of problems. Students also practice healthy habits to prevent injury and manage stress in dance.
CN.2 Explore - Students explore how creativity prepares them for college and career choices and make comparisons of effective use of technology in dance performances.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students explore spontaneous movement invention using multiple dance elements in response to prompts from dance score and use dance phrases and simple structures to create a short dance. Students also use pictures, writing, symbols, or media technology to recreate a dance sequence.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to adapt dance phrases by changing a dance element and also explain how to legally and ethically share dance, including musical accompaniment, both physically and digitally.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Perform - Students use feedback from instructors, peers, and self-assessment to improve performance and demonstrate movement skills with precision, strength, anatomical alignment, flexibility, stamina, and coordination. Students also work on refining movement qualities and the execution of movements in a dance.
PR.2 Develop - Students refine theme or topic, including music choices for a group performance, and demonstrate safe and respectful behaviors for dance in a variety of cultural settings. Students also create a production element design that would strengthen the meaning of a dance as it is performed in various situations.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students explain how the elements of dance (body, space, time, effort quality) and choreographic structures are used in different dance techniques, and also examine how interpretations of dances compare with what the choreographer originally intended based on the background and context of the artist or work.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students examine how experiences, culture, and media can influence personal preferences in dance and use criteria in evaluation of dance performance to provide suggestions for improvement.

How to Support Your Dance Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your dance student:
* Clear a little space so your child can move freely and safely.
* Watch a short clip of clogging, hip-hop, or African dance groups from NC and discuss their influence on the dance community.
* Encourage your child to use strategies such as staying hydrated, stretching, breathing deeply, and taking short breaks when stressed to stay healthy both physically and mentally.
* Ask your child how creativity in dance could prepare students for college and careers, including careers outside the arts.
* Watch a recorded performance online and ask how lighting, sound, or video editing made the performance more engaging.
* Create a movement score that sequences different dance elements of body, space, time, and effort, and have them improvise movement according to what is prescribed in the score.
* Have your child create multiple dance phrases, then put them together to make a longer routine.
* Encourage practice to develop strength, endurance, flexibility, and coordination.
* Compare the use of dance elements in different dance styles, such as in hip-hop versus ballet.
* Watch a performance with your child, then compare your interpretations of the dance to what the choreographer may have intended.
