# Arts Education - Advanced

Parent Guide for Advanced Dance
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for dance is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The ability to create, present, respond to, and make connections with dance builds competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a student’s choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed dance educator.
For additional questions, please contact Dr. Laura Stauderman, K-12 Dance and Visual Arts Consultant
Connect
Explore and relate artistic ideas and works to past, present, and future societies and cultures.
CN.1 Relate - Students explore the impact of the global expansion of dance from the 19th century to the present and examine how the skills learned in the creation and performance of dance art are relevant across different professions. Students also create a plan to help others with physical and mental wellness in dance.
CN.2 Explore - Students explore the training, skills, and action plans necessary for a successful career and examine how new innovations and technologies are impacting dance.
Create
Create and adapt new artistic ideas and work individually or collaboratively.
CR.1 Originate - Students generate spontaneous movement in improvisation with a partner or group using a movement score and use choreographic principles, structures, and processes along with production elements, to create a dance for a partner or group. Students also use pictures, writing, symbols, or media technology to document choreography.
CR.2 Adapt - Students learn how to create an adaptation of a significant work by selecting multiple key elements to use and others to modify. Students also explore how a choreographer gets permission to use another artist’s work, including musical accompaniment.
Present
Present, perform, produce, and develop artistic ideas and works.
PR.1 Perform - Students apply feedback from a variety of sources to improve personal performance when dancing alone and with others and learn to manage their energy, body control, and awareness of movement to improve technique and performance. Students improve their ability to execute each step in a dance clearly and completely with focus and full-body energy to express ideas.
PR.2 Develop - Students make improvements to a theme, topic, or musical selection for a live or recorded group performance and determine a plan to encourage safe and respectful behavioral expectations for formal and informal performances in a variety of cultural contexts. Students also create production element designs that would strengthen the meaning of a group dance.
Respond
Analyze and evaluate how the arts communicate.
RE.1 Analyze - Students examine similarities and differences of dance elements used in different sections of the same choreographic work and describe how dancers and choreographers make creative choices to express their ideas.
RE.2 Evaluate - Students explain how being able to physically feel or emotionally connect with the movement influences how people view and critique dance. They also provide detailed feedback for performance and choreography, taking into consideration the cultural background and artist’s intent.

How to Support Your Dance Student at Home
The following strategies can help support your dance student:
* Clear a little space so your child can move freely and safely.
* Encourage your child to connect the skills learned in making and performing dance to other subjects and professions.
* Develop a family plan to include stretching, hydration, balanced meals, and strategies for handling stress.
* Have your child research the training and skills required for a specific dance career they might be interested in.
* Examine together how new innovations and technologies are impacting the dance industry.
* Invite your child to use a movement score to improvise movement with a sibling, friend, or family member.
* Support your child in documenting choreography using sketches, notes, or videos.
* Ask your child what feedback they’ve received from teacher, peer, and self-evaluation, and how they are using it to improve their skills.
* Watch dance together and have your child explain how the elements of dance are used in different sections of the dance.
* Observe and discuss how dancers and choreographers make decisions to communicate meaning in different dance performances.
* Watch dance together and discuss how feeling the movement in your own body while watching impacts how the dance is experienced.
