# Arts Education - Accomplished

North Carolina Standard Course of Study 2024 Visual Arts Standards Unpacking Accomplished Honors
The 2024 North Carolina Standard Course of Study for Visual Arts is intended to provide a Comprehensive Arts Education while developing students as artists. The abilities to create, present, respond to, and make connections with Visual Arts build competence by expanding foundational knowledge and skills within each grade band and proficiency level. The standards are organized within the four artistic practices of Connect, Create, Present, and Respond, which are supported by the National Arts Education Standards created by the National Coalition for Arts Standards.
Levels Beginning through Advanced courses for high school credit are intended to develop proficiency in the artform of a students’ choice and to support a well-rounded education. These courses meet for as long as other high school courses meet and are also taught by a licensed Visual Arts educator. Credit earned from middle and high school courses meeting these minimums satisfies the Arts Education Graduation requirement and applies toward the Arts Education Diploma Endorsement. Courses that do not meet these minimums are not designed to build proficiency and are often used to spark interest in more intensive proficiency-based study later in a student’s education.
To account for variations in skill progression, as well as to embed multiple entry points across the K-12 spectrum, the objectives in the high school NCSCOS for Visual Arts are organized by proficiency level, rather than grade level. Minimum proficiency outcomes are provided at each level to show what a student should know and be able to do by the end of a course or program when they create, present, respond to, and make connections with Visual Arts. Courses using the Accomplished High School standards are inherently honors.
The North Carolina Visual Arts Standards maintain the respect for local control of each Public School Unit (PSU). These standards and objectives are not intended to be the curriculum, nor do they indicate the whole of a curriculum which will be written by a PSU or school. The standards for this course have been developed to serve as the framework which will guide each PSU in the development of the curriculum for K-12 Visual Arts programs, and the proficiency level objectives show the minimum student outcomes for each skill.

AC.CN.1.1 Describe how the visual arts reflect society, culture, and politics.
Glossary
* Culture - Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.17
* Describe - To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.4
* Society - An enduring and cooperating social group whose members have developed organized patterns of relationships through interaction with one another; a community, nation, or broad grouping of people having common traditions, institutions, and collective activities and interests.14
* Visual Arts - As defined by the National Art Education Association, include the traditional fine arts, such as, drawing , painting, printmaking, photography, and sculpture; media arts, such as, animation, video and emerging digital technologies; design, such as, communication, product, and interactive; environmental, such as, architecture, landscape architecture, interior and urban planning; conceptual, performance, participatory, street, and folk arts and works of art in clay, glass, metal, wood, fiber, paper and other materials. (Revised March 2017).17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students explained the impact of historical, societal, and cultural perspectives on the visual arts. At the current level, students describe how society, culture, and politics are reflected in visual arts and at the next level, describe how the visual arts are impacted by history, society, culture, and personal life.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that a visual artist’s perception of events, cultures, and society shape the creation of techniques, styles, and genres; equally worthy of study, expression, and celebration.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How visual arts draws inspiration, meaning, and purpose from society, culture, and politics
* Movements, Artists, and Artistic works that reflect various societal, cultural, and political events
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Summarizing the characteristics of how visual arts reflect society, culture, and politics
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Art plays an integral role in documenting history as it reflects society, culture, and community perspectives on key issues and human experiences
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students need to demonstrate mastery of their ability to describe how the visual arts reflect society, culture, AND politics. Depending on the learning activity all three; society, culture, and politics can be addressed in one assessment or explored separately over multiple assignments over the course of study
* This objective can be paired with CN.2.2 in which students analyze the influence of digital media and technology on creating, editing, and presenting visual arts and other CR, PR, and RE objectives for creation, presentation, analysis, and evaluation
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Provide examples of art that reflects society, culture, and politics
* Guide student exploration and investigation in how society, culture, and politics are represented in a variety of artworks throughout history
* Lead classroom discussions on how art reflects perspectives about society, culture, and politics
* Provide learning activities in which students can describe how society, culture, and politics are reflected in visual works of art
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to describe how the visual arts reflect society, culture, and politics
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Examination of how art captures iconic figures or important elements of culture, politics, or society
* Examination of how art is used to reflect perspectives about religious and philosophical beliefs, social injustice, political issues, and other cultural or societal concerns throughout history
* Examination of art along a timeline of key art movements across various cultures and geographic locations
* Describe culturally relevant art through critique, turn-and-talks, journals, sketches, etc.

AC.CR.2.1 Demonstrate awareness of the implications of appropriation and appreciation when creating personal artwork.
Glossary
* Appropriation - Intentional borrowing, copying, and alteration of preexisting images and objects.17
* Create - Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.17
* Personal - Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person and are influenced by personal life, family, habits, interest, and/or preferences.17
Vertical Alignment
This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level students learned to apply knowledge of appropriation to create new ideas for personal artwork and now demonstrate awareness of the implications of appropriation and appreciation when creating personal artwork. In the next level, students will demonstrate how appropriation and appreciation applies to creating personal artwork with new tools and media.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is to have students know that visual artists balance inspiration and originality with ethics by creatively transforming or adapting other artistic ideas into new works.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* The difference between drawing inspiration from a culture or another artist's work (appreciation) and exploiting or misrepresenting it without context or permission (appropriation)
* The implications of using images or ideas of others without giving proper credit
* The importance of attributing sources and ensuring respectful representation
* The creative design process
* How to use materials and tools safely and appropriately
* The elements of art
* The principles of design
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Showing/Execute/Modeling awareness of responsible use of images and ideas of others in personal artwork
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* It is important for artists to know how to be respectful and ethical when using cultural references in personal art
* This objective can be paired with CR.2.2 in which students compare how parody, adaptation, and artificial intelligence relate to copyright and fair use of art as well as with CN objectives as students learn about art in personal, societal, cultural, historical, and daily life contexts and other CR, PR, and RE objectives for creation, presentation, analysis, and evaluation
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective- students should be able to show mastery of their ability to demonstrate awareness of the implications of appropriation AND appreciation when creating personal artwork. Awareness of the implications of both appropriation and appreciation can be assessed simultaneously in the creation of one work or assessed separately in the creation of multiple works.
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Provide examples of the differences between appropriation and appreciation in visual art
* Guide student examination of how cultural elements were used respectfully and when they were misrepresented in visual arts
* Lead student discussion on how artists can respectfully create art that reflects various cultures and when using cultural images could be problematic
* Provide opportunities for students to demonstrate awareness of the implications of appropriation and appreciation when creating personal artwork
* Provide clear expectations in the appropriate and safe use of tools and materials
* Guide student ideation and creation
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to demonstrate awareness of the implications of appropriation and appreciation when creating personal artwork

This objective develops the EQ-throughline because in the previous level, students critiqued personal artwork or the works of others with self-generated criteria and now critique a personal body of work using given and self-generated criteria. In the next level, students will critique personal art considering relevant traditional and contemporary criteria as well as personal artistic vision.
Enduring Understanding
The Enduring Understanding from this thread of objectives is providing feedback through objective critique is imperative in improving artistic practice.
Knowledge
To master this objective, students need to KNOW:
* How to generate criteria to analyze and evaluate art
* How to use given and self-generated criteria to analyze and identify strengths and areas for improvement in a personal body of work
Skills
To master this objective, students will need to be SKILLED at:
* Analyzing and evaluating a personal body of work for how well it meets given and self-generated criteria
* Using disciplinary and other academic vocabulary in verbal or written form
Evidence of Mastery
Given that the Arts Standards are mastery-based, teachers must collect evidence on every student, not just the class as a whole. Mastery is demonstrated when observable and/or documented student work shows each individual has met the expectations of this objective. Use the Knowledge and Skills listed above as the specific criteria for what each student must demonstrate—regardless of how well or artistically it is done.
Key Insights
* Note the distinction in the definition of “critique” - students should be objectively ANALYZING and EVALUATING the quality of any given aspect of the work
* Criteria should be appropriate to the work and determined based on the cultural context, purpose, and goal of the work being evaluated
* This can be paired with PR.1.1 in which students use formative or summative feedback from this RE objective to justify revisions made to personal artwork
* This objective can also be paired with other CR, PR, and RE objectives and should be assessed towards the end of the course to give the student time to complete a body of work for evaluation
* Note the conjunction “and” used in this objective - students should be able to demonstrate mastery of their ability to use both given AND self-generated criteria to critique a personal body of work. Both sets of criteria can be used simultaneously or separately on different occasions
* Specificity is the hallmark of an effective critique
In the Classroom - Suggestions Only, this content is locally determined
Teacher Actions
* Have students select a body of personal work for critique
* Design the given criteria to be used in evaluation of artwork (this can also be done in collaboration with the class)
* Guide students in self-generation of criteria for the critique of their work
* Provide learning activities in which students can use given and self-generated criteria to critique a personal body of work
* Guide students in using visual literacy skills to evaluate their body of art based on self-generated and given criteria
* Create formative and summative assessments on the student’s ability to critique a personal body of work using given and self-generated criteria
Possible Learning Activities may include, but are not limited to:
* Have students practice using given criteria as a class to evaluate a professional body of work as an example
* Students can write or audio/video record themselves using given and self-generated criteria in the critique of a personal body of work
